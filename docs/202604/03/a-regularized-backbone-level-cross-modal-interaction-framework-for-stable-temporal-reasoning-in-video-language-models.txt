## 1. Introduction

Recent advances in deep learning have led to remarkable progress in multimodal video-language understanding [[1](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B1-mathematics-14-00996),[2](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B2-mathematics-14-00996),[3](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B3-mathematics-14-00996)], enabling machines to reason over complex temporal visual content conditioned on natural language queries. These developments have been particularly impactful in egocentric or first-person video settings [[4](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B4-mathematics-14-00996),[5](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B5-mathematics-14-00996),[6](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B6-mathematics-14-00996)], where visual observations are closely coupled with human intent, actions, and contextual semantics. However, despite their empirical success, many existing video-language models remain largely heuristic in nature and provide limited theoretical insight into the stability and reliability of their reasoning processes.

A central challenge in egocentric video understanding arises from the inherently sparse, noisy, and temporally ambiguous nature of first-person visual data. Occlusions, abrupt camera motion, limited fields of view, and stochastic sampling all contribute to uncertainty in visual evidence. When combined with high-level linguistic queries, the resulting inference problem often admits multiple temporally plausible explanations. From a mathematical standpoint, egocentric video-language reasoning exhibits the characteristics of an ill-posed inverse problem [[7](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B7-mathematics-14-00996),[8](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B8-mathematics-14-00996),[9](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B9-mathematics-14-00996),[10](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B10-mathematics-14-00996),[11](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B11-mathematics-14-00996)], where latent semantic intent must be inferred from incomplete and perturbed observations. Such problems are therefore known to require appropriate regularization mechanisms to ensure stability and meaningful reconstruction[[7](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B7-mathematics-14-00996),[8](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B8-mathematics-14-00996),[9](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B9-mathematics-14-00996),[10](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B10-mathematics-14-00996),[11](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B11-mathematics-14-00996),[12](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B12-mathematics-14-00996),[13](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B13-mathematics-14-00996)]. Without explicit or implicit regularization, small perturbations in the input may lead to disproportionate variations in the inferred representation, undermining reliable temporal reasoning.

Existing video-language models differ substantially in where cross-modal interaction is introduced, yet the stability implications of this design choice remain unclear. Most existing approaches rely on dual-encoder designs [[1](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B1-mathematics-14-00996),[3](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B3-mathematics-14-00996)], where visual and textual modalities are encoded independently and coupled only at late stages of the network. While such designs offer modularity and computational efficiency, they implicitly assume that unimodal feature representations are sufficiently stable prior to fusion. In practice, this assumption frequently breaks down under perturbations in temporal sampling or modality-specific noise, leading to unstable temporal grounding and brittle reasoning behavior. Importantly, late-stage coupling provides limited mechanisms for controlling or regularizing cross-modal interactions during feature extraction, making it difficult to enforce stability in ill-posed egocentric inference settings.

Motivated by these limitations, several recent approaches have explored earlier or deeper forms of cross-modal interaction within network backbones. However, these approaches are often introduced as architectural heuristics, with limited discussion of their mathematical role in stabilizing inference or mitigating ill-posedness. In particular, the relationship between cross-modal interaction strength, representational stability, and robustness under perturbations remains insufficiently understood.

In this work, we address this gap by presenting a regularized backbone-level cross-modal interaction framework for stable temporal reasoning in video-language models. The proposed framework implements bidirectional cross-modal interaction within the transformer backbone, while our mathematical analysis focuses on the video-side gated update for clarity. This design enables visual and linguistic representations to interact throughout feature encoding. Specifically, we employ a transformer backbone with 12 layers, 768 hidden dimensions, and 12 attention heads. From an analytical perspective, the resulting interaction can be interpreted as a learned regularization operator. In the video-side update analyzed in this paper, a trainable gating scalar γ(l)𝛾(𝑙), initialized at 0.1, controls the strength of cross-modal coupling via a convex combination of unimodal features and attention-based residuals. This formulation provides an explicit mechanism for balancing expressiveness and stability in multimodal feature reconstruction.

The contributions of this paper are threefold. First, we formulate egocentric video-language reasoning as an ill-posed inverse problem and motivate backbone-level cross-modal interaction from a regularization perspective. Second, we provide a structural interpretation of gated cross-modal fusion, elucidating its role as a stability-preserving operator whose depth acts as an implicit regularization parameter. Third, we empirically validate the proposed interaction through extensive perturbation analyses on the EgoTaskQA [[6](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B6-mathematics-14-00996)] and MSR-VTT benchmarks.

Importantly, robustness in this context should not be interpreted independently of task performance. A model that fails to exploit visual-temporal information may appear insensitive to perturbations, yet such behavior reflects trivial stability rather than meaningful robustness. In this work, we focus on robust reasoning: maintaining high performance while actively utilizing temporal visual cues even under severe degradation. This distinction is particularly important in ill-posed settings, where insensitivity to perturbations may arise from representational collapse rather than genuine stability.

Together, these results provide a mathematically informed perspective on the design of stable and reliable interaction mechanisms for high-dimensional multimodal video understanding systems.

## 2. Related Work

Video-language modeling has progressed rapidly with transformer-based architectures that couple visual and linguistic signals for retrieval, captioning, and question answering. A useful organizing principle is interaction depth, i.e., the stage at which cross-modal coupling is introduced. Following the categorization highlighted in EgoVLPv2 [[14](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B14-mathematics-14-00996)], we summarize prior approaches as (i) late (shallow) interaction via dual encoders, (ii) mid-level fusion via stacked fusion layers, and (iii) deep fusion that injects interaction into a shared encoder or directly into the backbone. This taxonomy clarifies a recurring trade-off between modularity/efficiency and the strength (and stability) of cross-modal coupling under temporal ambiguity and perturbations. [Figure 1](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#fig_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-f001) summarizes four representative categories of video-language pretraining frameworks organized by interaction depth.

**Figure 1.** Four categories of video-language pretraining (VLP) frameworks, following the interaction-depth taxonomy of EgoVLPv2 [[14](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B14-mathematics-14-00996)]. (**a**) Dual Encoders; (**b**) Stacked Fusion Layers; (**c**) Shared Encoders; (**d**) Fusion in the Backbone.

### 2.1. Late Fusion (Dual Encoders/Shallow Interaction)

Late-fusion (or shallow-interaction) models encode video and text largely independently and couple them mainly at the representation or decision stage, typically through contrastive alignment objectives and similarity-based matching [[3](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B3-mathematics-14-00996),[15](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B15-mathematics-14-00996),[16](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B16-mathematics-14-00996),[17](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B17-mathematics-14-00996)]. This design is attractive due to its modularity, scalability, and reuse of unimodal encoders across tasks. In egocentric settings, dual-encoder pretraining has also been shown effective for learning transferable video-language representations [[18](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B18-mathematics-14-00996),[19](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B19-mathematics-14-00996)].

However, the limited interaction during feature extraction constrains how strongly the language query can shape the temporal evidence aggregation process. When observations are sparse, noisy, or stochastically sampled, this can lead to brittle temporal grounding, since query-conditioned constraints are introduced only after unimodal representations have already evolved. From a stability viewpoint, late fusion provides limited leverage to regularize layer-wise feature evolution under temporal perturbations; small variations in sampling or corruption can propagate through the visual pathway before the query signal can constrain the representation. These limitations motivate earlier or deeper interaction mechanisms that can regularize feature evolution throughout encoding rather than relying on late-stage coupling.

### 2.2. Mid-Level Fusion (Encoders with Stacked Fusion Layers)

Mid-level fusion introduces explicit cross-modal modules (e.g., co-attention or fusion transformers) after initial unimodal encoding, enabling tighter coupling than late fusion while preserving a degree of modularity [[20](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B20-mathematics-14-00996),[21](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B21-mathematics-14-00996),[22](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B22-mathematics-14-00996),[23](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B23-mathematics-14-00996)]. Such models often improve fine-grained grounding by allowing token-level interactions between modalities and have been widely used in video-language pretraining and downstream VideoQA settings.

A key limitation is that fusion is still concentrated in a limited portion of the network, creating a bottleneck: errors or instabilities in unimodal features may propagate into the fused representation. Moreover, stacked fusion layers typically introduce substantial fusion-specific parameters and compute overhead, which can hinder efficient scaling and may reduce the direct reusability of the resulting backbone for strictly unimodal tasks, as emphasized in backbone-fusion analyses such as EgoVLPv2 [[14](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B14-mathematics-14-00996)]. From a stability perspective, concentrating interaction in a small number of fusion layers can amplify perturbations originating from unimodal encoders, because the fused representation must absorb mismatches through a narrow coupling bottleneck. This motivates designs that distribute interaction across the backbone while explicitly controlling coupling strength to prevent perturbation amplification.

### 2.3. Deep Backbone Fusion (Shared Encoders/Fusion in the Backbone)

Deep fusion injects cross-modal interaction into the encoder/backbone itself, enabling repeated coupling during feature evolution. One direction uses unified or shared encoders to process multimodal tokens jointly [[24](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B24-mathematics-14-00996),[25](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B25-mathematics-14-00996),[26](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B26-mathematics-14-00996)], while another inserts cross-modal operators into backbone layers to achieve strong interaction with a lighter fusion footprint. EgoVLPv2 exemplifies the latter by enabling fusion in the backbone, showing that backbone-level interaction can recover strong cross-modal reasoning while mitigating the parameter and compute growth associated with stacking dedicated fusion transformers [[14](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B14-mathematics-14-00996)].

Despite these advances, deeper coupling does not automatically guarantee stable reasoning under perturbations. In ill-posed regimes with temporal ambiguity, stronger interaction can amplify sensitivity unless the update dynamics are explicitly controlled. In parallel, theoretical studies in machine learning have investigated stability, robustness, and generalization properties of neural networks [[27](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B27-mathematics-14-00996),[28](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B28-mathematics-14-00996),[29](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B29-mathematics-14-00996)]. Meanwhile, inverse-problem theory has long emphasized that learning from incomplete, noisy, or ambiguous observations requires explicit or implicit regularization to ensure stable and meaningful reconstruction[[7](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B7-mathematics-14-00996),[8](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B8-mathematics-14-00996),[10](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B10-mathematics-14-00996),[11](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B11-mathematics-14-00996)]. These perspectives provide a useful lens for analyzing multimodal temporal reasoning, particularly in egocentric settings where temporal ambiguity and stochastic sampling make inference intrinsically ill-posed.

Overall, prior fusion paradigms can be organized by interaction depth (late, mid-level, deep), but the stability implications of this choice are often under-discussed. Moreover, robustness is rarely analyzed through controlled perturbation protocols that isolate where (and how strongly) cross-modal coupling influences layer-wise feature evolution. Existing designs therefore typically lack an explicit mechanism that bounds and adaptively controls cross-modal coupling as a regularization factor under temporal perturbations. To address this gap, we (i) formulate egocentric video-language reasoning as an ill-posed inverse problem and (ii) introduce a bidirectional gated backbone-level interaction framework. For analysis, we focus on the video-side update, which is formulated as a learnable convex combination between unimodal features and cross-modal residuals. This design provides bounded, adaptive coupling and enables a perturbation-based robustness analysis that links interaction depth to stable temporal reasoning.

## 3. Problem Formulation

### 3.1. Multimodal Temporal Reasoning as an Ill-Posed Inverse Problem

We consider the task of egocentric video-language reasoning as the problem of inferring a latent semantic intent conditioned on temporally evolving visual observations and a natural language query. Let V={v t}t=1 T 𝑉={𝑣 𝑡}𝑇 𝑡=1 denote a sequence of visual observations sampled from an egocentric video and let Q represent an associated language query. The objective is to infer a semantic representation y that captures the query-conditioned temporal evidence present in the video.

From a mathematical perspective, this problem can be viewed as an inverse problem of the form

y=F(V,Q),

(1)

where F denotes an unknown inference operator implemented by a deep neural network. In the presence of temporal ambiguity and stochastic sampling, the mapping from visual observations to semantic representations is inherently non-unique, rendering the inference process ill-posed in the classical sense [[7](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B7-mathematics-14-00996)], in particular due to the lack of uniqueness and stability with respect to input perturbations.

Consequently, stability-oriented constraints are required to restrict the solution space toward semantically meaningful reconstructions [[8](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B8-mathematics-14-00996)].

In practice, this ill-posedness manifests as unstable dependence on the visual input: small perturbations in V, arising from occlusions, camera motion, or sampling variability, may induce disproportionately large variations in the inferred representation y. Such sensitivity undermines reliable temporal grounding and highlights the necessity of additional structural constraints or regularization mechanisms.

Reliable inference in this regime therefore requires principled regularization strategies that promote stability while preserving query-relevant semantic structure, which motivates the backbone-level cross-modal interaction framework introduced in the following subsection [[10](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B10-mathematics-14-00996)]. This viewpoint is consistent with recent efforts that interpret deep neural networks as data-driven solvers for inverse problems, where architectural constraints implicitly determine stability and reconstruction behavior [[11](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B11-mathematics-14-00996)].

### 3.2. Dual-Encoder Baseline and Limitations

Most existing video-language models adopt a dual-encoder architecture, in which visual and textual modalities are processed independently before being fused at a later stage. Let ϕ V(·) and ϕ Q(·) denote the visual and textual encoders, respectively. The resulting unimodal representations are given by

z V=ϕ V(V),z Q=ϕ Q(Q),

(2)

and are typically combined through attention-based aggregation or similarity matching mechanisms.

From the perspective of inverse problems, this design implicitly assumes that the unimodal mapping ϕ V:V↦z V is sufficiently stable prior to cross-modal fusion. However, in ill-posed settings characterized by temporal ambiguity and stochastic sampling, this assumption is rarely satisfied. Small perturbations in the visual input may propagate through ϕ V without constraint, leading to unstable intermediate representations that cannot be reliably corrected by late-stage fusion alone.

Importantly, the dual-encoder paradigm introduces cross-modal constraints only after feature extraction has been completed. As a result, the inference operator remains under-regularized during the encoding process, allowing ambiguity and noise to accumulate across temporal dimensions. In classical inverse problem terminology, this corresponds to applying weak or delayed regularization, which is insufficient to enforce stability with respect to input perturbations.

Empirically, such under-regularization manifests as brittle temporal grounding and high sensitivity of attention patterns to minor variations in sampling or noise. These limitations motivate the need for earlier and more structured cross-modal constraints that act directly on feature evolution, rather than relying solely on late-stage fusion for stabilizing inference.

### 3.3. Backbone-Level Cross-Modal Interaction as Regularization

Implementation-wise, the fused layers perform bidirectional cross-modal interaction: the video backbone block is updated conditioned on the current text embeddings, while the corresponding text encoder layer is updated via cross-attention over the current video embeddings. For analytical clarity, however, the regularization formulation in this paper focuses on the video-side update, which provides the primary perspective for interpreting stability under temporal perturbations.

To address the instability inherent in dual-encoder formulations, we introduce cross-modal interaction directly within the feature encoding process. Let h V(l) and h Q(l) denote the visual and textual representations at layer l of a transformer backbone [[30](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B30-mathematics-14-00996),[31](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B31-mathematics-14-00996),[32](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B32-mathematics-14-00996)]. Unlike late-fusion architectures that preserve modality separation throughout feature extraction, the proposed framework enables visual and linguistic features to interact progressively during representation learning, as illustrated in [Figure 2](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#fig_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-f002).

**Figure 2.** Overall architecture of the proposed regularized backbone-level cross-modal interaction framework. At each fused layer, the visual and textual streams are updated through bidirectional cross-modal interaction. The gated updates h˜V(l) and h˜Q(l) allow the model to exchange query-conditioned and video-conditioned information progressively throughout the transformer backbone while learnable gating parameters control the strength of cross-modal coupling.

From the viewpoint of inverse problems, the inference of a stable representation can be interpreted as seeking a solution to a regularized optimization problem of the form

min h D(h,V)+λ R(h,Q),

(3)

where D enforces fidelity to visual observations and R encodes query-conditioned structural constraints. In ill-posed settings, such regularization is essential to restrict the solution space toward stable and semantically meaningful reconstructions.

This formulation is not intended to represent an explicitly solved optimization objective, but rather serves as an analytical abstraction for interpreting the effect of cross-modal interaction as a regularization mechanism within the feature encoding process. As illustrated in [Figure 2](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#fig_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-f002), the visual and textual streams exchange information through bidirectional cross-modal interaction throughout feature encoding. For analytical clarity, however, the present regularization perspective focuses on the video-side path, where the query acts as a stabilizing constraint that helps prevent unstable or spurious temporal grounding.

Motivated by this perspective, we define a gated cross-modal interaction operator within the backbone as

h˜V(l)=(1−γ(l))h V(l)+γ(l)A h V(l),h Q(l),

(4)

where A(·,·) is implemented as a multi-head cross-attention (MHCA) module followed by an MLP residual block and γ(l)∈[0,1] is a learnable gating scalar. To enforce the range constraint, we parameterize γ(l)=σ(α(l)), where σ(·) denotes the sigmoid function and α(l) is a learnable logit initialized as α(l)=log(0.1/0.9)≈−2.197 such that σ(α(l))=0.1. An analogous gated cross-modal update is applied to the text stream in the bidirectional implementation, although the present analysis focuses on the video-side formulation for clarity.

This update constitutes a convex combination of unimodal visual features and cross-modal attention residuals. From a regularization standpoint, the unimodal term preserves data fidelity, while the attention-based residual introduces a query-dependent constraint that biases the representation toward linguistically relevant temporal structure. The gating parameter γ(l) therefore acts as an adaptive regularization weight, controlling the strength of cross-modal coupling during feature evolution.

The following lemma formalizes the stability properties of this convex update:

**Lemma****1**

(Bounded Update Property)**.**Let h V(l) and R C M=A(h V(l),h Q(l)) be arbitrary vectors and let γ(l)∈[0,1]. Then the updated representation h˜V(l)=(1−γ(l))h V(l)+γ(l)R C M remains within the convex hull of {h V(l),R C M} and satisfies

∥h˜V(l)−h V(l)∥≤γ(l)∥R C M−h V(l)∥.

(5)

**Proof.**

Since γ(l)∈[0,1], h˜V(l)=(1−γ(l))h V(l)+γ(l)R C M is a convex combination of h V(l) and R C M; hence, it lies in the convex hull of {h V(l),R C M}. Moreover, h˜V(l)−h V(l)=γ(l)(R C M−h V(l)), and taking norms yields the bound. □

The algorithmic procedure for this update is detailed in Algorithm 1.

**Algorithm 1:** Video-Side Regularized Feature Update at Layer l
**Input**: Visual features h V(l), Query features h Q(l)

**Output**: Updated visual features h˜V(l)

1. **Compute Cross-Modal Residual:**R C M←A(h V(l),h Q(l)); //

MHCA + MLP block

2. **Apply Gated Convex Combination:**h˜V(l)←(1−γ(l))h V(l)+γ(l)R C M

3. **Return**h˜V(l)

The above algorithm summarizes the video-side gated update used in our analysis. An analogous update is applied to the text stream in the bidirectional implementation.

The convex update ensures that h˜V(l) stays on the segment connecting h V(l) and R C M, thereby limiting abrupt deviations induced by noisy inputs.

## 4. Stability and Diagnostic Analysis

### 4.1. Stability Implications

By construction, the proposed gated interaction preserves boundedness of feature representations through convex updates. While formal Lipschitz guarantees are not explicitly imposed, this structure empirically constrains sensitivity to input perturbations by limiting abrupt deviations from unimodal trajectories [[27](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B27-mathematics-14-00996),[28](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B28-mathematics-14-00996),[29](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B29-mathematics-14-00996)].

### 4.2. Robustness Under Effective Temporal Reasoning

To empirically assess the stability properties implied by the proposed regularized interaction framework, we analyze the behavior of the model under a wide range of controlled perturbations. The specific protocol for these perturbations is summarized in [Table 1](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#table_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-t001).

This behavior is consistent with the DE-0 design where the classifier relies on the video [CLS] token without query-conditioned constraints during feature extraction, making the model prone to shortcut solutions. In particular, we observe low Macro-F1 despite unchanged accuracy, which is indicative of a collapsed prediction distribution. Here, a collapsed prediction distribution refers to disproportionately concentrated outputs over a small subset of classes (high concentration/majority-class tendency). In contrast, our method substantially improves clean accuracy on both benchmarks and retains over 97% of its performance under severe degradation, demonstrating robust reasoning rather than trivial stability. ### 4.3. Ablation on Interaction Depth and Regularization Path

We study interaction depth L I as an implicit regularization parameter and distinguish two complementary notions: (i) robustness measured by the accuracy drop under perturbation (Drop; smaller is better) and (ii) robust reasoning, which emphasizes maintaining high clean accuracy while avoiding degenerate shortcut behavior (e.g., majority-class bias or overly diffuse/insensitive attention patterns). As summarized in [Table 3](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#table_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-t003), FIB-6 (Gated) achieves the highest mean clean accuracy on both EgoTaskQA and MSR-VTT, consistent with more effective query-conditioned utilization of temporal evidence. On MSR-VTT, its Drop is slightly larger than that of FIB-5 (1.5 pp vs. 0.6 pp), but it remains small in absolute terms and accompanies marginally higher clean accuracy. Importantly, [Table 2](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#table_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-t002) (DE-0 vs. FIB-6) further shows that the gated backbone fusion exhibits higher Macro-F1 and moderate (non-trivial) temporal-attention entropy, suggesting that the model preserves robust reasoning rather than appearing stable due to a degenerate prediction distribution. In particular, on MSR-VTT, enabling fusion without bounded gating (FIB-6 Ungated) converges to a degenerate shortcut solution (0.0 pp Drop with low accuracy), highlighting that bounded interaction is crucial for stability that is meaningful in the ill-posed temporal reasoning regime. Overall, these results indicate that FIB-6 (Gated) provides a favorable trade-off between high task performance and non-degenerate robustness under perturbations.

### 4.4. Attention Distribution as a Diagnostic Signal

Beyond perturbation response, we employ attention distributions as a diagnostic tool to examine the internal reasoning dynamics of the model. Let α(l)={α t(l)}t=1 T denote the attention weights over temporal positions at interaction layer l.

In particular, we consider the entropy of the attention distribution,

H α(l)=−∑t=1 T α t(l)log α t(l),

(6)

as a measure of dispersion, where the logarithm is taken to be the natural logarithm. We use accuracy and Macro-F1 as the primary robustness metrics for all models and report attention entropy only as an auxiliary diagnostic when the corresponding temporal attention weights are available. Stable reasoning is therefore associated with moderate entropy values that adapt to the query context while remaining robust under input perturbations.

### 4.5. Effect of Gated Interaction on Attention Stability

The introduction of gated backbone-level interaction alters the evolution of attention distributions across layers in a systematic manner. In under-regularized dual-encoder models, attention patterns tend to be highly sensitive to perturbations in visual input. In contrast, our proposed framework maintains significantly lower and more consistent entropy values compared to the baseline. This quantitative evidence supports the interpretation of the gating mechanism as a stabilizing regularizer that prevents uncontrolled diffusion of attention, ensuring that the model remains focused on query-relevant temporal features even when the visual signal is corrupted.

### 4.6. Lipschitz-like Behavior and Robustness

Although the proposed model does not impose explicit Lipschitz constraints, the combination of convex feature updates and progressive cross-modal regularization can be qualitatively interpreted as exhibiting behavior consistent with Lipschitz-like stability. For clarity, we present the sensitivity bound for a single interaction layer with gating scalar γ(l); analogous bounds can be composed across multiple gated layers. Specifically, for an original video V and its perturbed version V˜, let h V(V) and h V(V˜) be the corresponding backbone features. Assuming the cross-modal operator A(·,Q) is L A-Lipschitz with respect to its first argument, the distance between updated representations is bounded by

∥h˜V(V)−h˜V(V˜)∥≤(1−γ(l))∥h V(V)−h V(V˜)∥+γ(l)∥A(h V(V),Q)−A(h V(V˜),Q)∥≤(1−γ(l))+γ(l)L A∥h V(V)−h V(V˜)∥.

(7)

This formulation demonstrates that the gating parameter γ(l) balances the sensitivity of the unimodal backbone against the stability of the cross-modal operator. We operationalize this notion via bounded accuracy degradation and reduced representation/attention dispersion under controlled perturbations.

## 5. Experimental Validation

This section provides empirical validation of the proposed regularized backbone-level cross-modal interaction framework, with a particular focus on stability and robustness under perturbations. Beyond absolute performance gains, the experiments are designed to analyze how the proposed regularization mechanisms affect the sensitivity of the inference process to input perturbations in ill-posed multimodal reasoning settings.

### 5.1. Dataset and Task Setup

Experiments are conducted on two diverse benchmarks to evaluate both domain-specific performance and generalizability:

*   EgoTaskQA [[6](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B6-mathematics-14-00996)]: An egocentric video question answering dataset requiring complex temporal reasoning over first-person observations. It is characterized by high camera motion and partial observability.

*   MSR-VTT (Binary): A standard third-person video-language benchmark. We utilize a binary classification variant where, for each video, a positive caption is paired with a negative caption randomly sampled from a different video in the dataset (1:1 negative sampling). This setup evaluates the model’s ability to discern correct video-text alignments under more general settings, addressing concerns regarding dataset-specific biases.

All models are evaluated under identical data splits and task configurations to ensure fair comparison.

### 5.2. Baselines and Model Variants

To evaluate the impact of the proposed interaction mechanism, we build all model variants on the EgoVLPv2 backbone [[14](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#B14-mathematics-14-00996)] and control the interaction depth via

num_fuse_block
. We consider two primary reference configurations:

*   DE-0 (video-only head): Dual-encoder mode with

num_fuse_block
= 0, where video and text are encoded independently and no cross-modal fusion is performed in the backbone. In this configuration, the prediction head operates on the video [CLS] token followed by an MLP classifier (the query stream does not constrain the video representation during feature extraction, nor does it enter the classifier input). For clarity, the text encoder is computed for architectural consistency (shared backbone and training pipeline), but its output is not used by the classifier in DE-0. This baseline highlights the degenerate behavior (trivial stability) that arises when query-conditioned constraints are absent during feature extraction. 
*   FIB-6 (Ungated): Fusion-in-backbone mode with six fusion blocks enabled (

num_fuse_block
= 6), but without bounded gating by fixing γ=1. This corresponds to standard backbone fusion without gate control and isolates the effect of enabling fusion blocks from the effect of bounded gating. 

Our proposed model, FIB-6 (Gated), applies the bounded gating mechanism to the same six fusion blocks. For a strictly controlled comparison, all variants (DE-0, FIB-6 Ungated, and FIB-6 Gated) share the same EgoVLPv2 pre-trained weights, identical data splits, and the same optimization protocol (optimizer, learning-rate schedule, and training budget). All results are reported using the best validation checkpoint under each setting; the only differences are whether fusion blocks are enabled and whether bounded gating is applied. During training, we monitored gradient norms to verify numerical stability, observing that maximum gradient norms remained consistently under 4.0 across all experiments, with no instances of exploding activations.

To address concerns about the lack of a strong publicly released reference comparison, we report a strictly matched evaluation against the EgoVLPv2 baseline on the EgoTaskQA benchmark. We utilize the same backbone initialization and an identical training recipe (20 epochs, peak lr = 1×10−4) following the same data and evaluation pipeline to ensure that the observed gains are specifically attributable to the proposed gated interaction mechanism. Accordingly, CM refers to our bounded cross-modal backbone-fusion configuration, i.e., FIB-6 (Gated). The results for Open, Binary, and All metrics across both split types are summarized in [Table 4](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#table_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-t004).

## 6. Discussion

This work is motivated by the observation that multimodal temporal reasoning in egocentric video understanding exhibits fundamental characteristics of an ill-posed inverse problem. A central contribution of this paper is the interpretation of backbone-level cross-modal interaction as an implicit regularization mechanism that constrains feature evolution under temporal ambiguity and stochastic perturbations.

### 6.1. Comparison to Prior Fusion Paradigms and State of the Art

Our results can be interpreted through the lens of interaction depth. Relative to dual-encoder baselines that introduce cross-modal constraints only after feature extraction, backbone-level interaction provides a mechanism for injecting query-conditioned structure during representation evolution. Under matched experimental conditions on EgoTaskQA, our model improves accuracy over the EgoVLPv2 baseline ([Table 4](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#table_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-t004)), indicating that earlier coupling can yield more faithful query-conditioned temporal grounding. On MSR-VTT, we further observe substantial accuracy gains and improved robustness under severe perturbation (50% frame drop) ([Table 2](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#table_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-t002)). Importantly, these results highlight that robustness should not be interpreted solely as insensitivity of accuracy to perturbations: a model may exhibit near-zero accuracy drop while relying on degenerate shortcuts, which is consistent with low Macro-F1 and a collapsed prediction distribution ([Table 2](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#table_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-t002)). In contrast, our method maintains high task performance while sustaining robustness, aligning with the notion of robust reasoning rather than trivial stability. A direct comparison against DE-0 on EgoTaskQA is summarized in [Table 6](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#table_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-t006).

### 6.2. Practical Trade-Offs and Remaining Limitations

Despite the improvements, the proposed framework introduces practical trade-offs. First, enabling fusion blocks and gated cross-attention increases computational cost and latency relative to the dual-encoder baseline, although the overhead remains moderate in our implementation ([Table 5](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#table_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-t005)). Second, interaction depth acts as an implicit regularization parameter: applying interaction to too few layers can under-regularize feature evolution, whereas overly deep coupling can over-constrain representations and reduce accuracy ([Table 3](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/14/6/996#table_body_display_mathematics-14-00996-t003)). Third, while the bounded gating mechanism mitigates instability, the optimal coupling strength may depend on dataset characteristics (e.g., degree of temporal ambiguity, noise patterns, and question types), suggesting that a single fixed interaction configuration may not be universally optimal. These observations motivate future work on adaptive interaction depth and compute-aware gating strategies that preserve stability while improving efficiency.

### 6.3. Practical Significance

Beyond empirical gains, the proposed framework has practical implications for real-world egocentric video systems. First, robustness under temporal corruption is particularly relevant for wearable and first-person capture scenarios, where frame drops, motion blur, and partial observability are common. Second, the modest computational overhead suggests feasibility in moderately resource-constrained environments. Third, the gated design can be integrated into existing transformer backbones with minimal modification. Importantly, robustness should be interpreted jointly with task performance, since models that ignore temporal evidence may appear stable but fail to perform meaningful reasoning.

### 6.4. Remaining Limitations

Despite these advantages, several limitations remain. First, although convex gating provides empirical stability benefits, the current analysis does not establish formal global Lipschitz guarantees for the full network. Second, evaluation is limited to two benchmarks; broader validation on more diverse egocentric and long-form video datasets would strengthen generality. Third, while additional computational cost is moderate for 16-frame inputs, deeper fusion configurations may become expensive for very long video sequences.

### 6.5. Novelty from a Regularization Viewpoint

Beyond architectural design, our contribution is to connect backbone-level interaction with a stability-oriented regularization perspective. The gated convex update yields an explicit mechanism to control the magnitude of layer-wise feature deviations (Lemma 1), and the accompanying sensitivity analysis provides an interpretable link between coupling strength and perturbation response. This framing complements prior empirical advances in cross-modal fusion by clarifying how controlled interaction can improve temporal reasoning reliability in ill-posed multimodal inference settings.

## 7. Conclusions

This paper presented a regularized backbone-level cross-modal interaction framework for stable temporal reasoning in video-language models. Motivated by the observation that egocentric video-language inference is often ill-posed due to temporal ambiguity, sparse observations, and stochastic perturbations, we framed video question answering as an inverse problem and used this perspective to motivate structured cross-modal constraints during feature evolution.

Our method introduces bidirectional gated backbone interaction. For analysis, we focused on a video-side convex update between unimodal features and cross-modal attention residuals. This formulation provides an explicit control mechanism for coupling strength and yields a bounded update property (Lemma 1), together with an analytical sensitivity bound that clarifies how interaction depth and gating affect stability. Empirically, we demonstrated consistent gains on EgoTaskQA and MSR-VTT under matched settings while maintaining high performance under severe perturbations. These results emphasize that meaningful robustness should be evaluated as robust reasoning—maintaining strong task performance while actively using temporal evidence—rather than as apparent stability caused by degenerate or collapsed prediction behavior.

Several directions remain for future work. First, it would be valuable to establish more formal stability guarantees for backbone-level cross-modal interaction, moving beyond layer-wise sensitivity arguments toward global bounds that explicitly characterize the end-to-end perturbation response of the inference operator. Second, extending the evaluation to longer and more diverse egocentric settings—including streaming inputs and open-world scenarios with evolving activities and ambiguous temporal boundaries—would further clarify when and how regularized interaction improves robust temporal reasoning. Third, an important next step is to connect the proposed stability-oriented design to large-scale multimodal pretraining and long-context video modeling, where long-horizon temporal structure and distribution shifts can amplify ill-posedness. Bridging these directions may enable scalable and principled robustness in real-world video-language systems.
