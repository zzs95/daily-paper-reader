Abstract 

Objective: To develop and validate a Union Model integrating clinical features with 

radiomics signatures derived from intratumoral and peritumoral T2-weighted imaging 

(T2WI) and diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) for the non-invasive prediction of p53 

expression in intrahepatic mass-forming cholangiocarcinoma (IMCC).Furthermore, 

the study aims to determine the optimal predictive range. 

Methods: This retrospective study included IMCC patients with 

immunohistochemically confirmed p53 expression status from four institutions. 

Patients were allocated into training, internal validation, and external validation 

cohorts. Multi-scale regions of interest, integrating the intratumoral volume with 

peritumoral expansions of 0, 3, 5, 10, 15, and 20 mm, were delineated on the MRI 

sequences. Radiomics and clinical features were extracted to construct 

single-sequence intratumoral and peritumoral models, a Fusion Model, a Clinical Model, and a Union Model. Performance was assessed using receiver operating 

characteristic (ROC) curves, area under the curve (AUC), accuracy, sensitivity, and 

specificity. Model interpretability was explored via SHapley Additive exPlanations 

(SHAP) values. 

Results: The intratumoral and peritumoral 5mm models based on DWI and T2WI 

sequences demonstrated robust performance, yielding AUCs of 0.959 and 0.987 in the 

training cohort, and 0.821 and 0.809 in the internal validation cohort, respectively. 

The Union Model demonstrated the best predictive performance, with AUC values of 

0.992, 0.945, and 0.825 across the three cohorts. 

Conclusion: A multimodal Union Model incorporating intratumoral and 5-mm 

peritumoral MRI features can effectively predict p53 expression in IMCC, 

demonstrating favorable generalizability and potential for clinical decision-making.

1 Introduction 

Intrahepatic mass-forming cholangiocarcinoma (IMCC), a malignancy originating from the bile duct epithelium, accounts for approximately 40% of all intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma (ICC) cases. Its incidence has been rising rapidly in recent years [1] . Owing to the insidious nature of early clinical symptoms, only 12%-40% of patients are eligible for surgical resection at the time of diagnosis. Furthermore, ICC is associated with a poor prognosis, characterized by a 5-year survival rate of 25%-40% 

and a high recurrence risk ranging from 50% to 70% [2] . For patients with advanced local metastasis or postoperative recurrence, the efficacy of various adjuvant therapies 

remains under clinical exploration [3] .Consequently, predicting the expression of tumor microenvironment -related biomarkers in ICC is critical for prognosis monitoring and 

treatment strategy adjustment [4,5] . Previous studies have found that positive p53 

expression is an independent predictor of poor prognosis in colorectal cancer [6] .Missense mutations in the TP53 gene result in the high expression of p53 protein, making its monitoring indicative of patient prognosis. While ICC is frequently initially detected via imaging, IMCC is confirmed pathologically. Therefore, combining imaging features with clinicopathological information to construct a predictive model holds great potential for the early diagnosis and therapeutic management of IMCC patients. 

The TP53 gene, identified as one of the most prevalent driver gene mutations in the 

development of IMCC, is categorized into wild-type and mutant types [7] . Wild-type 

TP53 serves as a vital tumor suppressor gene, and its encoded p53 protein plays a 

central role in maintaining DNA stability and normal cellular physiological 

metabolism [8] . Mutant TP53 may promote tumor cell invasion and migration, 

potentially contributing to the aggressiveness, drug resistance, and poor prognosis of 

ICC [9] . When TP53 undergoes a missense mutation, the resulting accumulation of p53 protein not only leads to the loss of its inherent tumor-suppressive function but also 

the acquisition of oncogenic properties, thereby posing a risk to patient prognosis [10,11] .

A p53 protein positivity rate exceeding 70% strongly suggests the presence of a TP53 

gene mutation [6,12] . Given the limitations of gene sequencing technology, the 

development of a preoperative predictive model for p53 protein expression status 

holds significant research value for precise, non-invasive screening within the 

immunomolecular field for IMCC patients. 

Radiomics extracts and decodes high-throughput imaging features most relevant to 

biomarkers to construct reliable predictive models [13,14] , and has been widely applied 

in preoperative diagnosis, postoperative follow-up, and early non-invasive risk 

assessment of ICC [15,16] .Among imaging modalities, MRI, with its superior soft-tissue 

resolution, is utilized to predict the biological behavior and prognosis of ICC. For 

instance, a high signal on DWI suggests higher tumor cellular density and a poorer 

prognosis [17] ; meanwhile, the "target sign" on T2WI sequences offers advantages in 

identifying poorly differentiated IMCC [18] .In studies developing machine learning 

models to predict TP53 mutations in intramedullary glioma and cervical cancer, 

radiomics models have demonstrated favorable predictive performance [19,20] . However, 

no MRI-based radiomics model currently exists for the risk assessment and grading of 

p53 expression status in IMCC. Moreover, research focusing on peritumoral regions 

has been limited to specific expansion ranges [21] , lacking a systematic comparison to 

determine the optimal predictive range and a rigorous methodology. Therefore, this 

study aims to utilize machine learning methods to construct radiomics models based on the optimal intratumoral and peritumoral prediction ranges, and further integrate 

these with clinical and imaging features to establish a multimodal Union Model for 

predicting p53 protein expression status, thereby offering a new perspective for ICC 

prognosis assessment. 

2 Materials and Methods 

2.1 Study Population 

A total of 312 patients with pathologically confirmed IMCC were initially identified from four medical centers. Following the application of inclusion and exclusion criteria, 104 patients were finally enrolled in this study. Data from three centers were randomly split in an 8:2 ratio into an internal training cohort (n=61) and an internal validation cohort (n=16). Data from the remaining independent center 

served as the external validation cohort (n=27).Inclusion criteria included the 

following: 1) underwent curative liver resection; 2) resected lesion pathologically 

diagnosed as IMCC, with p53 expression status assessed by immunohistochemistry 

(IHC); 3) underwent preoperative non-contrast MRI within 2 weeks before surgery; 4) 

maximum lesion diameter ≥ 10 mm, allowing successful delineation of peritumoral 

regions; 5) patient age ≥ 18 and ≤ 75 years. Exclusion criteria included the following: 

1) missing preoperative imaging data or poor image quality preventing evaluation; 

and 2) a history of other malignancies, presence of distant metastasis, or preoperative 

radiotherapy/chemotherapy.The patient selection flowchart is shown in Figure 1. 2.2 Examination Methods 

MRI examinations across the four research centers were conducted using either 1.5-T or 3.0-T MRI scanners, including the Siemens Amira 1.5-T, Philips Achieva 1.5-T, United Imaging, and GE Discovery 750 3.0-T, for axial upper abdominal scanning. Detailed scanning parameters for each sequence are provided in Supplementary Material 1. 

2.3 Clinical and Imaging Feature Analysis 

Imaging features were qualitatively assessed by two radiologists. Any discrepancies 

in their evaluations were resolved through discussion to reach a consensus. The 

evaluated imaging features encompassed:(a) Lesion location: involving left lobe, right 

lobe, or both; (b) Tumor morphology: regular (clear transition between lesion and 

adjacent liver parenchyma) or irregular; (c) T2WI tumor margin: clear (sharp 

boundary) or indistinct; (d) Perilesional bile duct dilation; (e) Liver capsule retraction: 

retraction or unevenness of the liver capsule adjacent to the lesion; (f) Intrahepatic 

bile duct stones; (g) Vascular involvement: involvement of the main trunk or branches 

of the portal vein, hepatic vein, or hepatic artery; (h) Vessel penetration sign: 

branches of hepatic, portal veins, or hepatic artery traversing the lesion; (i) Enlarged 

lymph nodes: short-axis diameter ≥1.0 cm; (j) T2WI signal pattern: overall isointense, 

homogeneously hyperintense, heterogeneously hyperintense, peripheral 

hyperintensity with internal hypointensity, peripheral hypointensity with internal 

hyperintensity, or hypointense; (k) DWI signal pattern [24,25] : overall isointense, homogeneously hyperintense, heterogeneously hyperintense, peripheral 

hyperintensity with internal hypointensity, peripheral hypointensity with internal 

hyperintensity, or hypointense. 

Clinical features included: sex, age, Alpha-fetoprotein(AFP), Carbohydrate antigen 

125(CA125), Carbohydrate antigen 19-9(CA19-9), Carcinoembryonic antigen(CEA), 

and p53 protein expression status. In clinical practice, p53 protein positivity was 

defined as nuclear staining in >70% of tumor cells [12] ; otherwise, it was considered 

negative. 

2.4 MRI Image Segmentation and Preprocessing 

Original DWI and T2WI sequence images were retrieved in DICOM format from 

the Picture Archiving and Communication System (PACS). Image segmentation was 

initially conducted using 3D Slicer software. To assess inter-observer reliability, all 

patient segmentations were performed by a radiologist with 5 years of experience in 

abdominal imaging diagnosis, while a second radiologist with 10 years of experience 

independently segmented images from 30 randomly selected patients.While blinded to 

clinical data, the radiologists utilized ITK-SNAP software (version 3.8.0) to manually 

delineate the tumor on a slice-by-slice basis, thereby defining the intratumoral region 

of interest (ROI). Utilizing the cv2.getStructuringElement function from the Python 

OpenCV library (version 4.8.0), the tumor boundaries were radially expanded by 0, 3, 

5, 10, 15, and 20 mm. This process defined composite regions of interest 

encompassing both the intratumoral and peritumoral volumes, with the 0 mm margin representing the original intratumoral volume.Any segmentations extending beyond 

the liver parenchyma were manually adjusted for accuracy. Consequently, for each 

sequence, one intratumoral (0 mm) and five peritumoral ROIs were generated: 

Peri-0mm, Peri-3mm, Peri-5mm, Peri-10mm, Peri-15mm, and Peri-20mm. Prior to 

feature extraction, the original images underwent Z-score normalization for grayscale 

normalization to enhance feature robustness. 

2.5 Optimal Machine Learning Algorithm 

Seven machine learning classifier algorithms were employed: Logistic Regression 

(LR), Support Vector Machine (SVM), K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN), Random Forest 

(RF), Bayesian, Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost), and Light Gradient Boosting 

Machine (LightGBM). Models were constructed based on the intratumoral ROI and 

comparatively evaluated to identify the optimal algorithm for subsequent analyses. 

2.6 Feature Extraction and Selection 

Radiomics feature extraction was conducted using Python (version 3.8.20) with the following libraries: numpy (1.24.4), Pandas (1.5.3), SimpleITK (2.3.0), nibabel (5.2.1), Radiomics (v3.0.1), and tqdm (4.67.1), resulting in a total of 6,539 extracted features. The preliminary feature selection process followed a systematic workflow:1) low-variance filtering: Uninformative features with a variance < 0.005 were removed; 2) High-correlation filtering: Redundant features with a Pearson correlation 

coefficient > 0.98 were excluded; and 3) Statistical test filtering: Significant features were selected using the ANOVA F-test ( p-value<0.08), with only those scoring above the first quartile being retained.Finally, by integrating Random Forest (RF) feature importance with ANOVA scores, the 11 most significant radiomics features were retained for model construction. 

To evaluate the reproducibility of the extracted radiomics features, the Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) was utilized to assess both intra- and inter-observer stability for the intratumoral and peritumoral regions. Only features demonstrating 

high stability, defined by an ICC ≥ 0.85 , were retained for subsequent analyses. 

Regarding clinical and imaging features, the SelectFromModel method was utilized to extract features with importance scores exceeding the mean value derived from a trained Random Forest (RF) model. The selected features were subsequently standardized and utilized for downstream model training, ensuring that only the most discriminatory features for predicting p53-positive/negative status were retained. 

2.7 Model Construction 

Based on the optimal machine learning algorithm identified, 12 single-sequence 

radiomics models were constructed using the six ROIs derived from both DWI and 

T2WI sequences. From these, the best-performing model for each respective sequence 

was selected. These two optimal single-sequence models were then integrated to 

establish the Fusion Model. Concurrently, the selected clinical and imaging features were utilized to construct the Clinical Model. Finally, the Fusion Model and the 

Clinical Model were integrated to form the Union Model. 

2.8 Statistical Analysis and Model Evaluation 

Statistical analysis was performed using SPSS 24.0 and Python (v.3.8.5). Baseline 

continuous variables are presented as the mean ± standard deviation and categorical 

variables as frequency and percentage. Levene’s test was used to assess homogeneity 

of variance between groups. For continuous variables with homogeneous variance, the 

independent samples t-test was used; for categorical variables, the chi-square test or 

Fisher’s exact test was used. A P-value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant 

for differences between groups. 

Model performance was evaluated by plotting receiver operating characteristic 

(ROC) curves and calculating the area under the curve (AUC), accuracy, sensitivity, 

and specificity. Predictive performance was assessed using confusion matrices. The 

external validation cohort was used to test model generalizability. SHapley Additive 

exPlanations (SHAP) analysis was employed for model interpretability, explaining 

feature contributions. 

3 Results 

3.1 General Clinical Data Based on the predefined inclusion and exclusion criteria, 104 IMCC patients were 

enrolled in this study. The cohort consisted of 79 patients with p53-positive 

expression and 25 with p53-negative expression. For model development and 

evaluation, the patients were stratified into a training cohort of 61 cases, an internal 

validation cohort of 16 cases, and an independent external validation cohort of 27 

cases. Detailed baseline clinical characteristics are summarized in Table 1. No 

significant difference in characteristics was found among the different groups. 

3.2 Optimal Machine Learning Method 

The predictive performance of intratumoral radiomics models, constructed using 

seven distinct machine learning algorithms, was comprehensively evaluated, with the 

corresponding ROC curves illustrated in Figure 2. While multiple methods 

demonstrated high diagnostic efficacy within the training cohort, the Random Forest 

model yielded the most superior and stable results upon evaluation in the internal 

validation cohort. Consequently, the Random Forest algorithm was selected as the 

optimal modeling approach for this study, as it exhibited an ideal balance between 

high accuracy and robust generalizability across the two cohorts. 

3.3 Clinical, Imaging, and Radiomics Features 

Using the clinical feature selection method, six clinical/imaging features were 

finally included in the clinical model: age, AFP, CEA, T2WI tumor margin, CA19-9, 

and liver capsule retraction. From a total of 6,539 raw radiomic features extracted from the DWI and T2WI 

sequences, 11 core features were identified through a rigorous selection process. 

Among these, six originated from the DWI sequence and five from the T2WI 

sequence. The final feature set comprised seven features based on Laplacian of 

Gaussian (LoG) filters, three derived from wavelet transforms, and one based on a 

square transform. 

3.4 Performance Comparison of Single-Sequence Intratumoral and Peritumoral 

Models on DWI/T2WI 

ROC curves for the 12 single-sequence radiomics models in the training and 

internal validation cohorts are shown in Figure 3. For detailed comparison, the AUC, 

sensitivity, accuracy, and specificity results of these 12 models are summarized in 

Table 2. All single-sequence intratumoral and peritumoral radiomics models showed 

certain predictive values. DeLong’s test indicated no statistically significant 

difference in AUC among the single-sequence models. Therefore, by comparing AUC 

differences and model robustness, the optimal prediction range models were selected. 

The results showed that the intratumoral and peritumoral 5 mm models on DWI and 

T2WI sequences (PeriD-5mm and PeriT-5mm) had the best predictive performance. 

In the internal training and validation cohorts, the PeriD-5mm model had AUC values 

of 0.959 (95% CI: 0.958 –0.960) and 0.821 (95% CI: 0.812 –0.831), respectively. The 

PeriT-5mm model had AUC values of 0.987 (95% CI: 0.986 –0.987) and 0.809 (95% 

CI: 0.802 –0.817), respectively. 3.5 Performance Comparison and Evaluation of the Union Model, fuision Model, 

and Clinical Model 

3.5.1 ROC curves for the Union Model, Fusion Model, and Clinical Model in the 

training, internal validation, and external validation cohorts are shown in Figure 4. 

The Union Model outperformed the Fusion Model and the Clinical Model. The AUC, 

sensitivity, accuracy, and specificity of the three models are shown in Table 3. The 

Fusion Model, formed by merging the PeriD-5mm and PeriT-5mm models, showed 

improved overall predictive performance compared to single-sequence models, with 

AUC values of 0.933 (95% CI: 0.932 –0.935), 0.945 (95% CI: 0.941 –0.949), and 

0.777 (95% CI: 0.771 –0.784) in the three cohorts, respectively. The Union Model 

demonstrated the best predictive efficacy across the three cohorts, with AUC values 

of 0.992 (95% CI: 0.992 –0.993), 0.945 (95% CI: 0.941 –0.949), and 0.825 (95% CI: 

0.819 –0.831), respectively. The Clinical Model had AUC values of 0.864 (95% CI: 

0.754 –0.953), 0.718 (95% CI: 0.429 –0.974), and 0.650 (95% CI: 0.382 –0.892), 

respectively. The addition of clinical features increased the Union Model ’s AUC by 

0.05 –0.10 compared to the Fusion Model across cohorts and improved model 

robustness, indicating the contribution of clinical/imaging factors. 

3.5.2 The confusion matrices for the Fusion, Clinical, and Union models across the training, internal validation, and external validation cohorts are illustrated in Figure 5. Among these, the Union Model demonstrated the most superior accuracy in differentiating between p53 -negative and p53 -positive expression across all three cohorts. Furthermore, this model exhibited the highest level of concordance between predicted outcomes and actual pathological results, alongside the most robust generalizability. Specifically, the accuracy rates for predicting p53 -negative versus p53 -positive status within the three cohorts were 96.8% vs. 93.3%, 66.7% vs. 92.3%, and 85.7% vs. 75.0%, respectively. 

3.5.3 To further elucidate the decision-making logic of the ensemble model, SHAP analysis was performed on the features used to predict p53-positive expression. As illustrated in Figure 6, the SHAP summary plot ranks features by their relative importance, where higher positions indicate a greater contribution to the model's output. The distribution of SHAP values reveals the directional influence of each feature. A positive SHAP value indicates a tendency to predict p53 positivity, whereas a negative value suggests a predication toward negativity.Each point on the plot represents an individual sample, with red indicating high feature values and blue indicating low feature values. Higher SHAP values signify a stronger association with p53-positive expression, while lower values correlate more closely with negativity. The SHAP analysis identifies the top five predictors as radiomic features derived from the combined intratumoral and peritumoral regions of both DWI and T2WI sequences. These core features include two obtained via wavelet transformation and three via Laplacian of Gaussian filtering. These findings confirm the significant predictive value of integrated intratumoral and peritumoral information in characterizing p53 status. 3.5.4 The original images of IMCC patients with p53-negative and p53-positive 

expression are presented in Figure 7. In p53-negative patients, both DWI and T2WI 

sequences showed central hypointensity with peripheral hyperintensity, whereas in 

p53-positive patients, the signal was homogeneously hyperintense, with even higher 

signal intensity centrally on T2WI. 

4 Discussion 

4.1 This study systematically evaluated the predictive value of various combined intratumoral and peritumoral regions —extending 3, 5, 10, 15, and 20 mm from the tumor boundary —to characterize p53 expression status in IMCC. Our findings identified the 5-mm expansion as the optimal predictive range. Building upon this, the Union Model was developed by integrating these multi-scale radiomic features with clinical factors. This comprehensive approach demonstrated superior diagnostic efficacy and robust generalizability across both the training and validation cohorts. 

The p53 protein represented a pivotal biomolecular marker for the routine diagnosis and differential identification of intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma (ICC) [24] , with intrahepatic mass-forming cholangiocarcinoma (IMCC) being its most prevalent histological subtype [25] . While recent research predominantly focused on therapeutic strategies to inhibit IMCC progression via the p53 signaling pathway [26] , the inherent limitations of immunohistochemical (IHC) analysis —specifically its invasive nature and inability to provide a comprehensive assessment of tumor invasiveness —were frequently overlooked. Consequently, this study was dedicated to developing non-invasive radiomics-based tools to facilitate early p53 screening and enable the longitudinal monitoring of therapeutic efficacy. 

Dynamic alterations in biomolecular markers within the peritumoral region significantly influenced tumor progression. Fiz et al. [27] developed a CT-based radiomics model integrating both intratumoral and peritumoral regions within a 5-mm margin, enabling precise preoperative prognostic prediction for ICC. Additionally, Pan et al. [28] constructed a Union Model for predicting lymph node metastasis, confirming that peritumoral information substantially enhanced predictive efficacy. While these studies validated the clinical utility of peritumoral regions, their models were limited to a single fixed expansion, lacking a standardized and systematic comparison across various peritumoral zones —an aspect that the present study sought to refine. 

Xu et al. [29] suggested that radiomics models integrating both intratumoral and peritumoral 5-mm regions were more effective for predicting early ICC recurrence, as they captured critical information regarding satellite nodules and microvascular invasion (MVI). Their finding that the 5-mm margin outperformed the 3-mm margin was consistent with our results, which also identified 5 mm as the optimal predictive range. Conversely, we observed that larger peritumoral regions exhibited unstable predictive performance. We hypothesized that as the peritumoral expansion increased, increasingly active molecular interactions within the tumor-immune microenvironment (TIME) might have interfered with the representation of p53 expression, thereby diminishing predictive efficacy. This was consistent with Huang et al. [30] , who noted that excessively large margins incorporated a higher proportion of normal hepatic parenchyma, which effectively diluted the p53-related radiomic signals. Consequently, the combined intratumoral and peritumoral 5-mm regions achieved an ideal balance between information density and specificity; they sufficiently covered the tumor-immune interface while minimizing the inclusion of normal liver tissue, thereby ensuring robust and stable model performance. 

In the training cohort, models based on T2WI sequences outperformed those using DWI for the combined intratumoral and peritumoral 5-mm regions. This superiority likely stemmed from the enhanced capacity of T2WI to resolve tumor morphology and textural heterogeneity compared with other sequences. Conversely, DWI sequences yielded higher model stability overall, suggesting that information regarding cellular density and diffusion restriction reflected by DWI correlated more closely with p53 expression status. While the Fusion Model demonstrated improved performance over single-sequence models only in the internal validation cohort, Xu et al. [31] found that a multiparametric approach integrating T2WI and DWI consistently outperformed single-sequence configurations. This discrepancy might have been attributed to the relatively limited sample size in the present study, which likely contributed to a degree of overfitting in the training cohort. 

Although the clinical features incorporated into the Clinical Model did not reach statistical significance in this specific cohort, their established associations with p53 and prognosis maintained considerable clinical utility. For instance, elevated levels of CA19-9 and CEA were recognized as indicators of increased recurrence risk and poor prognosis in IMCC patients [29] . Furthermore, Ji et al. [32] identified liver capsule retraction as a significant correlate of adverse outcomes in IMCC. Additionally, parameters such as AFP levels and T2WI-defined tumor margins remained highly effective for the differential diagnosis between hepatocellular carcinoma and ICC [33] .The integration of these variables into our Union Model aimed to leverage their known biological relevance to enhance overall model robustness. 

The top five texture features identified by the Union Model for predicting p53-positive expression were all derived from the combined intratumoral and peritumoral 5-mm regions of the DWI and T2WI sequences. Notably, DWI sequences exhibited a stronger intrinsic association with p53 positivity. This was consistent with the findings of Qian et al. [34] , whose model for predicting Ki-67 status in ICC also assigned greater predictive weight to DWI-derived features.The dominance of DWI likely reflected its sensitivity to alterations in cellular density and membrane integrity. Specifically, as tumor cell proliferation intensified, heightened structural complexity and diffusion restriction led to decreased ADC values, which collectively signified elevated p53 expression. Simultaneously, texture features from T2WI sequences 

captured essential aspects of tumor heterogeneity. Higher T2WI signal intensity often 

correlated with increased textural complexity, which further reflected the aggressive 

biological behavior associated with p53 overexpression, a relationship previously 

validated by Wang et al. [35] .4.2 Limitations 

Firstly, this retrospective study was performed on a relatively small sample size, 

which may have led to bias and potential overfitting of model parameters in the 

internal training cohort. Secondly, the study did not include a prospective cohort, so 

incorporating prospective data would enhance model interpretability and validity. 

Thirdly, this study extracted radiomics features only from high-resolution T2WI and 

DWI sequences, which provided limited information. Future research should extract 

features from contrast-enhanced MRI, molecular imaging, etc., to build more 

comprehensive and stable models. Finally, incorporating TP53 gene sequencing 

results as a reference for comparison with the Union Model’s predictions could 

further validate its predictive performance. 

5 Conclusion 

The Union Model constructed based on intratumoral and peritumoral 5 mm MRI 

radiomics features and clinical features effectively predicted p53 expression status in 

IMCC, demonstrating certain generalizability and clinical potential. This aided in 

promoting precise diagnosis and treatment for IMCC, potentially improving patient 

prognosis. The optimal prediction range was intratumoral and peritumoral 5 mm.
