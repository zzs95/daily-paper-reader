Accurate differentiation of benign and malignant lung nodules on CT is essential for patient management. This study compares machine learning– based radiomics models with expert radiologists for this task. Histopathologically confirmed CT cases were retrospectively collected, and nodules were segmented for radiomic feature extraction using PyRadiomics. Feature selection combined LASSO, Random Forest importance, mRMR, and eBoruta, yielding 33 features. 

Multiple models (Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, SVM, LDA, QDA, MLP) were evaluated using 5-fold stratified cross-validation, with hyperparameter tuning via Optuna. The tuned SVM performed best, achieving an accuracy of 0.886, precision of 0.875, recall of 0.955, F1-score of 0.913, and AUC of 0.941 on the test set. McNemar’s test showed no significant difference between SVM and radiologists (p = 1.000). SHAP analysis provided interpretability of model decisions. 

Radiomics-based models, particularly SVM, demonstrated performance comparable to radiologists, suggesting potential as supportive tools in lung nodule evaluation, especially in resource-limited settings. However, findings are limited by the small, single-center dataset, and require validation in larger, multicenter cohorts. 

1. Introduction 

Accurate differentiation between benign and malignant pulmonary nodules on chest computed tomography (CT) is a clinically significant challenge, as it directly influences diagnostic pathways, follow-up strategies, and adherence to Fleischner Society guidelines (1). Although CT morphology remains central to nodule assessment, considerable overlap in imaging features—particularly in small or indeterminate solid nodules—can reduce diagnostic confidence and lead to variability in radiologist interpretation (2,3). This highlights the need for reproducible quantitative approaches that complement expert visual assessment. Radiomics has emerged as a promising non-invasive method that converts 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS medical images into high-dimensional quantitative descriptors capable of capturing subtle variations in intensity, shape, and texture not readily appreciable to the human eye (4–6). When combined with machine learning, radiomics facilitates the development of predictive models that may enhance risk stratification and support clinical decision-making (5,7). However, whether radiomics-based models can match or exceed the diagnostic performance of experienced radiologists in real-world tasks remains uncertain. In this study, we directly compared the diagnostic accuracy of a radiomics-based machine learning model with that of a panel of expert thoracic radiologists for distinguishing benign from malignant pulmonary nodules. Using comprehensive radiomic feature extraction pipeline, multi-step feature selection, and evaluation of multiple machine learning algorithms, we identified the highest-performing model. We hypothesised that radiomics could achieve performance comparable to expert radiologists and that detailed analysis of discordant classifications would reveal complementary strengths between quantitative imaging biomarkers and human expertise. This comparative evaluation provides clinically meaningful insight into the potential role of radiomics as adecision-support tool in routine thoracic imaging practice. A preliminary version of this work was previously presented at the European Congress of Radiology (ECR 2024), demonstrating initial feasibility of the radiomics-based ensemble model (8). The main contributions of this study are threefold: (1) a direct comparison between a radiomics-based machine learning classifier and expert thoracic radiologists for pulmonary nodule characterization, (2) evaluation of multiple machine learning algorithms with a structured ensemble feature selection approach, and (3) detailed analysis of discordant classifications to explore complementary strengths between radiomics and expert visual interpretation. 

2. Materials and Methods 

2.1 Data Acquisition and Data Collection 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS This retrospective and prospective cross-sectional observational study was conducted in our department after obtaining approval from the Institutional Research Ethics Committee, Sri Ramachandra Institute of Higher Education and Research (CSP–MED/19/SEP/56/122). All methods were performed in accordance with relevant guidelines and regulations. We initially reviewed CT thorax scans of 1200 patients (Figure 1). Solid pulmonary nodules measuring 3–30 mm were identified in 163 cases, consistent with the Fleischner Society definitions for solid nodules (1,2). A definitive diagnosis was available in 116 patients, of whom 42 had benign and 74 had malignant pulmonary nodules, determined either by histopathology or by clinical correlation and follow-up as per Fleischner Society guidelines (1,2). Malignant diagnoses were confirmed through histopathology of the lung lesion or the primary tumour in cases of metastasis. Ultimately, 42 benign and 74 malignant nodules were included for segmentation. Each case corresponded to a single dominant pulmonary nodule per patient. Accordingly, the number of patients was equal to the number of nodules analysed. The dataset comprised CT image volumes from these patients, with radiomics features extracted from manually segmented nodules for model development. Only patients without any synchronous lung pathology were included to avoid confounding imaging features (3–5). Subsolid nodules and nodules with calcification were excluded to ensure a uniform dataset of purely solid pulmonary nodules, as attenuation heterogeneity, partial calcification, and mixed components may significantly influence radiomic feature behaviour (6–9). Out of the 116 nodules that were analysed, 81 cases formed the training set, and 35 cases were reserved as an independent test set. The test set was held out entirely from all stages of feature selection, model training, and hyperparameter tuning, in accordance with recommended best practices for machine learning model validation and prevention of information leakage (10,11). The radiomics post-processing and feature extraction, model training, and hyperparameter tuning were performed only in the 81 training cases. 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS Radiologist performance was evaluated on the full dataset of 116 cases, based on the consensus opinion of three experienced radiologists who independently classified nodules using established CT morphological descriptors (4,5,12). However, the comparative analysis between the radiologists and the machine learning models was restricted to the held-out test set of 35 cases. Additionally, the preliminary feasibility of this radiomics-based approach has previously been demonstrated in an earlier version of this work presented at ECR 2024 (8). The methodological workflow of our study was structured and executed in a sequence of well-defined steps as shown in Figure 2. 

2.2 CT Acquisition 

High-resolution computed tomography (HRCT) examinations were performed using one of the following multi-detector CT (MDCT) scanners: GE EVO Evolution 128-slice (GE Healthcare, Princeton), Philips Brilliance 16 (Philips Medical Systems, Cleveland), and a PET/CT scanner (Siemens Biograph Horizon, Siemens AG, Munich). All HRCT scans were obtained during breath-hold using standardized parameters: 200 mA, 120 kV, slice thickness 0.65–0.80 mm, and reconstruction intervals of 0.65–0.80 mm. Window width and level settings were 1500 and −700, respectively. Images were transferred to the picture archiving and communication system (PACS) for review by radiologists. (6,8,9,13–15). Although acquisition parameters were standardized within our institution, inter-institutional variability in scanner platforms and imaging protocols was not assessed and may influence radiomic feature reproducibility. 

2.3 Segmentation 

Segmentation of pulmonary nodules on DICOM images was performed using the Insight Segmentation and Registration Toolkit (ITK-SNAP) (16). Each segmented region of interest (ROI) was independently reviewed and verified by three radiologists to ensure accuracy and minimize interobserver variations (12,16). The representative cases showing segmentation are shown in Figures 3a & 3b (8,13-15). 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 2.4 Feature Selection 

Radiomic feature extraction was performed using PyRadiomics with standard settings on the CT images of lung nodules (17). This yielded an initial feature space of 101 quantitative imaging features spanning multiple categories: shape-based features, first-order statistics and texture features including Gray-Level Co-occurrence Matrix (GLCM), Gray-Level Size Zone Matrix (GLSZM), Gray-Level Run Length Matrix (GLRLM), Gray-Level Dependence Matrix (GLDM), and Neighbouring Gray Tone Difference Matrix (NGTDM) (18,19). To identify the most informative features for distinguishing benign from malignant lung nodules, we employed a multi-method ensemble feature selection strategy combining four complementary approaches, each capturing different aspects of feature relevance. 

2.4.1 LASSO (Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator) 

L1-regularized logistic regression was applied with a regularization strength of C = 0.01 to perform embedded feature selection. LASSO identifies features by shrinking less important feature coefficients to zero, thereby selecting features that contribute to linear separability between classes. This method selected 15 features with non-zero coefficients (20). The LASSO coefficients can be found in Figure 4a. 

2.4.2 Random Forest Feature Importance 

A Random Forest classifier was trained using class-balanced weights to account for the class imbalance in the dataset. Feature importance scores were computed based on mean decrease in Gini impurity across all decision trees. The top 20 features ranked by importance were retained, capturing features that contribute most to predictive splits (21). The importance scores can be found in Figure 4b. 

2.4.3 mRMR (Minimum Redundancy Maximum Relevance) 

This filter-based method optimizes the trade-off between feature relevance to the target variable and redundancy among selected features. Using 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS mutual information for relevance measurement and Pearson correlation for redundancy assessment, mRMR selected the top 20 features that maximize discriminative power while minimizing inter-feature correlation (22). The Relevance Scores can be found in Figure 4c. 

2.4.4 eBoruta (Extended Boruta) 

This wrapper-based method that extends the original Boruta method to different model importance functions, iteratively compares the importance of original features against randomly permuted shadow features. Features demonstrating consistently higher importance than shadow features across multiple iterations are confirmed as relevant. eBoruta identified 16 confirmed features through this rigorous statistical testing procedure (23,24,25). 

2.4.5 Final Feature Set Construction 

The final feature set was constructed by taking the union of all features selected by the four methods, resulting in 33 features for model development. This union strategy ensures that diverse perspectives on feature relevance are captured: LASSO identifies linearly separable features, Random Forest captures non-linear interactions, mRMR optimizes information-theoretic criteria, and eBoruta provides statistically robust confirmation. The intersection of all four methods identified four core features (Maximum3DDiameter, Maximum2DDiameterRow, Strength, and SurfaceVolumeRatio) that were consistently selected across all approaches, representing the most robustly important radiomic signatures. 

2.4.6 Feature Standardization 

All features were standardized using StandardScaler (zero mean, unit variance) prior to model training to ensure fair comparison across features with different scales and units (26). The workflow comprised manual nodule segmentation, creating regions of interest, preprocessing data, extracting features, and classifying nodules using machine learning

2.5 Statistical Analysis 2.5.1 Model Performance Metrics 

Statistical Analysis Model performance on the test set was evaluated using standard classification metrics: accuracy (proportion of correct predictions), precision (positive predictive value), recall (sensitivity), F1-score (harmonic mean of precision and recall), and area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC-ROC). These metrics were calculated using the final predictions on the independent test set (27). 

2.5.2 Comparison with Radiologist Performance 

Comparison with Radiologist Performance: To assess whether the machine learning model performed significantly differently from expert radiologists, we employed McNemar's test, a non-parametric test appropriate for comparing paired binary classifications on the same subjects (28). The test evaluates whether two classification methods (SVM and radiologist consensus) have different error rates by focusing on discordant pairs—cases where one method is correct and the other incorrect. The null hypothesis states that the two methods have equal error rates. Statistical significance was evaluated at α=0.05. Radiologist performance was first characterized on the complete dataset ( 

n = 116) to establish baseline accuracy, precision, and recall. Subsequently, radiologist test set performance (

n = 35) was compared to SVM predictions using McNemar's test. 

2.5.3 Feature-Based Analysis 

Feature-Based Analysis: Mann-Whitney U tests were conducted for three comparisons pre model training: (1) radiomic features in cases where radiologists agreed versus disagreed with histopathological ground truth, (2) radiomic features distinguishing benign from malignant nodules based 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS on ground truth, and (3) radiomic features distinguishing radiologist predictions of benign versus malignant (29). To assess convergence between radiologist assessment patterns and ground truth markers, the top 20 features ranked by p-value from comparisons (2) and (3) were compared to calculate overlap concordance. The values for these can be found in the 

Supplementary Material; Figures S1-S9, Chart S1 and Tables S1-S8 . Post-model training, for discordant cases where SVM and radiologist predictions differed, mean standardized feature values were compared between cases where SVM was correct versus cases where radiologists were correct to identify differential patterns. 

2.5.4 Model Interpretability 

Model Interpretability: To understand feature contributions to SVM predictions, SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values were computed (30). SHAP provides a unified framework for interpreting predictions by assigning each feature an importance value for a particular prediction, based on cooperative game theory. SHAP values were calculated for all test set instances using the training set (n=81) as background samples, providing both global feature importance rankings and local explanations for individual predictions. Prediction confidence was examined for all misclassified cases, with high-confidence errors defined as incorrect predictions with probability > 0.8 or < 0.2. Individual case-level predictions were analysed to characterize error patterns and model calibration. 

2.5.5 Confidence Intervals 

Confidence Intervals: 95.0% confidence intervals for test set performance metrics were calculated using the Wilson score interval for proportions (accuracy, precision, recall, specificity) (31) and bootstrap resampling with 10,000 iterations for composite metrics (F1-score, AUC). These intervals quantify the uncertainty in performance estimates given the test set sample size.

2.5.7 Inter-Rater Agreement 

Inter-rater agreement between SVM predictions and radiologist consensus was assessed using Cohen's Kappa coefficient, which measures agreement beyond chance. All statistical analyses were performed using Python (version 3.12.12) with scipy.stats, statsmodels, and scikit-learn libraries. Statistical significance was evaluated at α=0.05 for all tests. Minor numerical variations (< 0.5%) may occur across different cloud computing sessions due to hardware-dependent floating-point arithmetic. 

3. Results 

3.1 Radiologist Baseline Performance 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS The three-radiologist consensus panel achieved 85.3% accuracy on the full dataset (116 cases) with 89.0% precision and 87.8% recall (confusion matrix: 34 TN, 65 TP, 8 FP, 9 FN). Agreement with histopathology occurred in 99 cases (85.3%), with 17 discordant cases (9 false negatives, 8 false positives). Test set performance (n=35) remained consistent: 85.7% accuracy (95.0% CI: 70.6-93.7%), 90.5% precision, 86.4% sensitivity. Mann-Whitney U tests revealed no radiomic features significantly associated with radiologist agreement versus disagreement cases (all p>0.05, lowest p=0.056 for ClusterShade). This suggests diagnostic errors were not driven by systematic feature misinterpretation. Conversely, 51 features distinguished benign from malignant nodules (p < 0.05 without correction), with shape features showing the strongest associations (Surface-Volume-Ratio, p = 4.5×10⁻¹⁴, Minor Axis Length, p = 3.0×10⁻¹², 

Maximum-3D-Diameter, p = 2.6×10⁻¹¹). Notably, 19 of the top 20 discriminative features overlapped between radiologist predictions and ground truth classifications (95.0% concordance) (29). 

3.2 Feature Selection and Model Performance 

From 101 radiomic features, ensemble feature selection identified 33 for modeling: LASSO (15), Random Forest (20), mRMR (20), eBoruta (16). Four core features were selected by all methods: Maximum-3D-Diameter, Maximum-2D-Diameter Row, Strength, and Surface-Volume-Ratio. Nine machine learning algorithms were evaluated (Table 1). Support Vector Machine with linear kernel achieved the best performance in both cross-validation and test set evaluation. On the independent test set ( 

n = 35, Table 2), SVM achieved 88.6% accuracy (95.0% CI: 74.0-95.5%), 87.5% precision (95.0% CI: 69.0-95.7%), 95.5% sensitivity (95.0% CI: 78.2-99.2%), 76.9% specificity (95.0% CI: 49.7-91.8%), F1-score 91.3% (95.0% CI: 81.0-98.0%), and AUC 0.941 (95.0% CI: 0.852-0.995; Figures 5a, 5b and 5c). The model correctly identified 21 of 22 malignant nodules with 3 false positives and 1 false negative. Tables 2 and 3 showcase the full performance for the various models on the test set.

4. Discussion 

This study demonstrated that an SVM trained on 33 radiomic features achieved diagnostic performance statistically indistinguishable from expert radiologist consensus (88.6% 

vs 85.7% accuracy, 

p = 1.000), with moderate inter-rater agreement ( 

κ = 0.568) but substantial individual agreement with histopathology (SVM 

κ = 0.747, radiologists 

κ = 0.699). The critical finding that zero radiomic features distinguished radiologist agreement versus disagreement cases contrasts sharply with 51 features distinguishing malignancy status. This suggests diagnostic errors stem not from systematic feature misinterpretation but from factors such as gestalt patterns not captured by standard radiomic extraction, stochastic interpretation variation, or integration of clinical context absent in retrospective review (29,30). The 95.0% overlap in discriminative features between radiologist predictions and ground truth confirms both approaches fundamentally access the same morphological and textural information, with divergence arising from how information is weighted and integrated rather than what information is available. Comparable performance suggests radiomic models could provide quantitative risk stratification complementing radiologist assessment, particularly in ambiguous cases. The model's high sensitivity (95.5%) 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS suggests utility for screening to minimize missed malignancies, while SHAP interpretability facilitates clinical trust (27). The balanced discordance pattern indicates ensemble approaches combining both methods might exceed either alone. However, several limitations warrant consideration. First, modest sample size (n=35 test) provided only 6.48% power to detect the observed small difference, requiring cautious interpretation of the non-significant McNemar's result. Second, retrospective single-institution design with histopathologically confirmed cases limits generalizability to routine clinical populations. Third, consensus radiologist review without clinical context may overestimate baseline performance compared to real-world individual interpretation. Fourth, binary classification simplifies complex clinical reality where management depends on multiple factors. Fifth, the feature space was limited to standard PyRadiomics features; deep learning approaches may capture additional patterns when given substantially larger training data (17,18). Error analysis and SHAP value computation for the tuned SVM provided insights into its decision-making and highlighted features contributing most to the classifications. Importantly, SHAP-derived feature importance patterns aligned with established radiologic markers of malignancy, including size-related and texture-based heterogeneity features, supporting the clinical interpretability of the model’s predictions and reinforcing its potential role as an adjunct to visual assessment. In clinical practice, such models may function as decision-support tools by providing quantitative risk estimates alongside radiologist interpretation, particularly in indeterminate nodules where management decisions remain uncertain. This study, however, has some limitations. The relatively small independent test cohort and single-institution design limit generalizability, and external validation across larger multicenter datasets is needed. Although acquisition parameters were standardized within our institution, inter-institutional variability was not assessed. In addition, while publicly available datasets were explored, suitable segmentation masks for radiomics validation were not available. Future studies 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS incorporating larger cohorts and advanced approaches, including deep learning, will further strengthen these findings. Radiomics offers a promising non-invasive approach, and the tuned SVM classifier, utilizing a selected subset of radiomic features, showed performance comparable to radiologists in distinguishing benign from malignant lung nodules (Figures 6b, 6c and 6d). Our SVM model achieved a test accuracy of 88.6% and an AUC of 0.941, compares consistently with previously reported radiomics-based approaches for pulmonary nodule classification. Choi et al. demonstrated that an SVM-LASSO model based on radiomic features achieved an accuracy of 84.6% with an AUC of 0.89, significantly outperforming Lung-RADS (p=0.026) (32). Donga et al. reported a validation accuracy of 95.67% using a gradient boosting framework on the LIDC-IDRI dataset (33) but unlike that study, our work includes a direct comparison with expert radiologists. The McNemar's test done, showed no significant difference between our SVM model and radiologist consensus, indicating that the radiomics model captured diagnostically relevant information at a level comparable to expert visual interpretation even though several previous studies have reported superiority of radiomics over human readers. For example, Schultheiss et al. found that a combined radiomics model achieved an accuracy of 83 % compared with 64 % for human readers (34), while Hunter et al. showed that a radiomics based decision tool achieved a test set AUC of 0.78 and accuracy of 73%, with a 66.7% reduction in potentially missed or delayed cancers relative to radiologists (35). Our findings are also consistent with other studies demonstrating strong discriminatory performance of radiomics models, including the LASSO-based model reported by Warkentin et al. with a test-set AUC of 0.93 (36), the combined radiomics-CT feature model of Li et al. with a test-set AUC of 0.930 for subcentimeter solid nodules (37), and the intratumoral peritumoral-clinical model reported by Lv et al. with a test-set AUC of 0.833 (38). Taken together, these findings support the role of radiomics as a robust quantitative adjunct to CT interpretation, particularly for classification of indeterminate pulmonary nodules. 5. Conclusion 

This study demonstrated that an SVM trained on 33 radiomic features achieved diagnostic performance statistically indistinguishable from expert radiologist consensus for lung nodule classification (88.6% vs 85.7% accuracy, 

p = 1.000). Moderate inter-rater agreement ( 

κ = 0.568) combined with substantial individual agreement with histopathology (SVM κ = 0.747, radiologists 

κ = 0.699) indicates overlapping but complementary diagnostic information. A critical finding was the absence of radiomic features distinguishing radiologist agreement versus disagreement cases, despite 51 features significantly differentiating malignancy status. Combined with 95.0% overlap in discriminative features between radiologist predictions and ground truth, this suggests both approaches access fundamentally similar information but integrate it differently. Diagnostic errors appear driven by gestalt patterns or stochastic variation rather than systematic feature misinterpretation. These findings support radiomic analysis as quantitative decision support complementing radiologist assessment for risk stratification. However, limitations including modest sample size, single-institution design, and lack of clinical context necessitate larger prospective validation before implementation. The balanced complementary strengths observed suggest optimal evaluation may involve synergistic integration of radiomic and radiologist assessments rather than replacement of one approach with the other. Promising directions include ensemble models combining radiomic and radiologist assessments, longitudinal studies incorporating temporal growth patterns, multi-institutional validation across diverse populations and scanners, prospective trials evaluating patient outcome impact, uncertainty quantification for identifying cases
