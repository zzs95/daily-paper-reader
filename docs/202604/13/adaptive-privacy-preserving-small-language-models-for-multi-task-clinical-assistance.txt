Title: Adaptive, Privacy-Preserving Small Language Models for Multi-Task Clinical Assistance

URL Source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4

Markdown Content:
## Abstract

The purpose of this study is to evaluate whether a single, fine-tuned SLM can match or exceed the performance of LLMs across diverse clinical tasks, enabling hospitals to build tailored, privacy-preserving, efficient, and deployable language models that do not require managing multiple task-specific systems. We used SLMs of varying sizes and applied low-rank adaptation (LoRA) for fine-tuning across three clinical tasks: (1) medical report labeling, (2) DICOM series description harmonization, and (3) impression generation from findings. These tasks were constructed using two datasets: the public Open-i Indiana University Chest X-ray Dataset and an in-house brain MRI DICOM metadata dataset. We compared single-task SLMs, a multi-task SLM (representing our proposed configuration), and GPT-4o using zero-shot and few-shot prompting. We found OPT-350 m to be the optimal SLM. In medical report labeling, the multi-task SLM achieved an _F_ 1 score of 0.894 compared to additional prompt-engineered GPT-4o’s 0.728. In DICOM series description harmonization, the multi-task achieved an accuracy of 0.975 compared to additional prompt-engineered GPT-4o’s 0.878. In impression generation from findings, the multi-task SLM achieved an average Likert scale score of 4.39 ± 1.00, compared to GPT-4o’s 3.65 ± 1.00 (_p_ = 0.0008). This study demonstrates that a single fine-tuned SLM can serve as a general-purpose clinical assistant, offering performance on par with or better than larger models. With lower resource requirements, greater customizability, privacy protection, and strong task generalization, fine-tuning one SLM to support multiple clinical tasks meets the practical demands of clinical AI deployment in both high-resource and resource-limited healthcare settings.

## Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable versatility across a wide range of applications, ranging from general natural language processing (NLP) tasks to specific domain tasks such as clinical decision support [[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR1 "Zhuang Y, Yu Y, Wang K, Sun H, Zhang C. Toolqa: A dataset for llm question answering with external tools. Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems. 2023 Dec 15;36:50117-43."),[2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR2 "Radensky M, Weld DS, Chee Chang J, Siangliulue P, Bragg J. Let's Get to the Point: LLM-Supported Planning, Drafting, and Revising of Research-Paper Blog Posts. arXiv e-prints. 2024 Jun:arXiv-2406."),[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR3 "Liu L, Meng J, Yang Y. LLM technologies and information search. Journal of Economy and Technology. 2024 Nov 1;2:269-77."),[4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR4 "Bhayana R, Alwahbi O, Ladak AM, Deng Y, Basso Dias A, Elbanna K, et al. Leveraging large language models to generate clinical histories for oncologic imaging requisitions. Radiology. 2025;314(2):e242134."),[5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR5 "Larson DB, Koirala A, Cheuy LY, Paschali M, Van Veen D, Na HS, et al. Assessing Completeness of Clinical Histories Accompanying Imaging Orders Using Adapted Open-Source and Closed-Source Large Language Models. Radiology. 2025;314(2):e241051."),[6](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR6 "Nowak S, Wulff B, Layer YC, Theis M, Isaak A, Salam B, et al. Privacy-ensuring openweights large language models are competitive with closed-weights GPT-4o in extracting chest radiography findings from free-text reports. Radiology. 2025;314(1):e240895.")]. Their ability to process and generate human-like text improves quality and accuracy compared to traditional language models, making them highly effective in diverse contexts. In specific applications such as clinical decision support, they provide timely and precise summarization of unstructured text, ultimately improving workflows [[7](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR7 "Yang R, Tan TF, Lu W, Thirunavukarasu AJ, Ting DSW, Liu N. Large language models in health care: Development, applications, and challenges. Health Care Science. 2023;2(4):255-63.")].

However, despite their capabilities and potential savings in healthcare costs, LLMs come with notable limitations [[8](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR8 "Sahni N, Stein G, McKinsey O, Zemmel R, Cutler DM. The potential impact of artificial intelligence on healthcare spending. vol. 30857. National Bureau of Economic Research Cambridge, MA, USA:; 2023."), [9](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR9 "Bedi S, Liu Y, Orr-Ewing L, Dash D, Koyejo S, Callahan A, et al. A systematic review of testing and evaluation of healthcare applications of large language models (LLMs). medRxiv. 2024:2024–04.")]. First, they can be computationally expensive, requiring dedicated hardware infrastructure, high memory requirements, and significant energy costs [[10](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR10 "Stojkovic J, Choukse E, Zhang C, Goiri I, Torrellas J. Towards greener llms: Bringing energyefficiency to the forefront of llm inference. arXiv preprint arXiv:240320306. 2024."), [11](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR11 "Doo FX, Savani D, Kanhere A, Carlos RC, Joshi A, Yi PH, et al. Optimal large language model characteristics to balance accuracy and energy use for sustainable medical applications. Radiology. 2024;312(2):e240320.")]. Many institutions prefer to process patient data on-site rather than sending it to remote servers due to patient confidentiality and privacy concerns [[12](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR12 "Miranda M, Ruzzetti ES, Santilli A, Zanzotto FM, Brati`eres S, Rodol`a E. Preserving privacy in large language models: A survey on current threats and solutions. arXiv preprint arXiv:240805212. 2024.")]. This limits the practicality of LLMs, as they often rely on cloud-based processing that may not align with hospital policies on patient data security. Moreover, the energy and compute demands of LLMs are fundamentally misaligned with deployment environments lacking data centers or stable internet, which increases the divide in clinical AI access. This limitation is particularly critical in under-resourced settings, including rural hospitals, community clinics, and global health contexts, where cloud-based LLM usage is infeasible due to infrastructure, privacy, or cost constraints. Second, most LLMs are general-purpose models that are not specifically optimized for clinical tasks, leading to suboptimal performance in clinical applications [[13](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR13 "Brown KE, Yan C, Li Z, Zhang X, Collins BX, Chen Y, et al. Large language models are less effective at clinical prediction tasks than locally trained machine learning models. Journal of the American Medical Informatics Association. 2025:ocaf038.")]. Finally, many widely used LLMs are closed-source, meaning hospitals cannot modify or fine-tune them to their specific needs [[14](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR14 "Savage CH, Kanhere A, Parekh V, Langlotz CP, Joshi A, Huang H, et al. Open-Source Large Language Models in Radiology: A Review and Tutorial for Practical Research and Clinical Deployment. Radiology. 2025;314(1):e241073.")]. Therefore, LLMs are limited by the fixed weights and models, as well as the data used to train the models [[15](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR15 "Balloccu S, Schmidtov´a P, Lango M, Duˇsek O. Leak, cheat, repeat: Data contamination and evaluation malpractices in closed-source LLMs. arXiv preprint arXiv:240203927. 2024.")].

Recent research has demonstrated that smaller, open-source, fine-tuned language models, small language models (SLMs), can achieve performance comparable to or better than LLMs for specific clinical tasks, such as extracting key findings from radiology reports or classifying medical conditions [[4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR4 "Bhayana R, Alwahbi O, Ladak AM, Deng Y, Basso Dias A, Elbanna K, et al. Leveraging large language models to generate clinical histories for oncologic imaging requisitions. Radiology. 2025;314(2):e242134."),[5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR5 "Larson DB, Koirala A, Cheuy LY, Paschali M, Van Veen D, Na HS, et al. Assessing Completeness of Clinical Histories Accompanying Imaging Orders Using Adapted Open-Source and Closed-Source Large Language Models. Radiology. 2025;314(2):e241051."),[6](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR6 "Nowak S, Wulff B, Layer YC, Theis M, Isaak A, Salam B, et al. Privacy-ensuring openweights large language models are competitive with closed-weights GPT-4o in extracting chest radiography findings from free-text reports. Radiology. 2025;314(1):e240895.")]. SLMs are particularly attractive for several reasons: They are easier to train, require fewer computational resources, and can often be deployed locally, which aligns well with privacy and infrastructure constraints in most healthcare settings. However, a key limitation of current SLM approaches is that they are typically trained for a single, narrowly defined task. For example, one SLM may be trained solely to extract disease labels from chest X-ray reports, while another one may be trained to generate impressions from clinical findings. As a result, healthcare institutions need to develop, manage, and maintain separate SLMs for each individual use case. This not only increases the technical burden on hospital IT teams but also complicates integration into clinical workflows. For institutions with a wide range of clinical tasks and documentation formats, this could mean maintaining many SLMs, each requiring version control, updates, monitoring, and validation. Such complexity increases operational overhead and reduces scalability, particularly for smaller institutions without dedicated ML teams or infrastructure.

To address these limitations, in this work, we explored whether a single compact SLM can replace task-specific models for diverse clinical tasks, enabling a more efficient, scalable, and privacy-preserving deployment. We hypothesize that “one SLM is sufficient for multiple clinical tasks.” Instead of training multiple SLMs for different tasks, we propose training a single multi-task SLM capable of handling multiple clinical applications while maintaining the same level of accuracy as task-specific SLMs. Our approach uses low-rank adaptation (LoRA) [[16](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR16 "Hu EJ, Shen Y, Wallis P, Allen-Zhu Z, Li Y, Wang S, et al. Lora: Low-rank adaptation of large language models. ICLR. 2022;1(2):3.")] to fine-tune an open-source SLM across multiple clinical tasks. This allows hospitals to develop a hospital-specific SLM that can assist with multiple tasks, such as report generation, disease classification, and clinical information extraction. As illustrated in Fig.[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#Fig1), this paper presents two main contributions:

1.   1.
We demonstrate that an SLM with as low as 350 million parameters (Meta’s opt-350 m [[17](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR17 "Zhang S, Roller S, Goyal N, Artetxe M, Chen M, Chen S, et al.Opt: Open pre-trained transformer language models. arXiv preprint arXiv:220501068. 2022.")]) can be fine-tuned to achieve comparable performance to LLMs across diverse clinical tasks.

2.   2.
We demonstrate that LoRA can be used to train a multi-task SLM with comparable performance to multiple task-specific SLMs as well as LLMs, reducing the need for hospitals to maintain multiple models.

## Materials and Methods

### Data

#### Open-i Indiana University Chest X-Ray Dataset

We used the deidentified, publicly available chest X-ray (CXR) data from Indiana University ([https://openi.nlm.nih.gov](https://openi.nlm.nih.gov/)) [[18](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR18 "Demner-Fushman D, Kohli MD, Rosenman MB, Shooshan SE, Rodriguez L, Antani S, et al. Preparing a collection of radiology examinations for distribution and retrieval. Journal of the American Medical Informatics Association. 2016;23(2):304-10.")]. This dataset consisted of 3665 annotated CXR reports annotated by three board-certified radiologists for the presence of thirteen disease labels, defined in the CheXpert study [[19](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR19 "Irvin J, Rajpurkar P, Ko M, Yu Y, Ciurea-Ilcus S, Chute C, et al. Chexpert: A large chest radiograph dataset with uncertainty labels and expert comparison. In: Proceedings of the AAAI conference on artificial intelligence. vol. 33; 2019. p. 590–7.")]. Six reports lacked impressions and were removed, resulting in 3659 reports. The 13 disease labels include enlarged cardiomediastinum, cardiomegaly, lung lesion, lung opacity, edema, consolidation, pneumonia, atelectasis, pneumothorax, pleural effusion, other pleural, fracture, and support devices.

#### Brain MRI DICOM Metadata Dataset

This dataset consisted of DICOM metadata obtained from 2510 MRI brain examinations performed without contrast from January 2016 to November 2022 for the evaluation of acute stroke [[20](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR20 "Kamel P, Khalid M, Steger R, Kanhere A, Kulkarni P, Parekh V, et al. Dual Energy CT for Deep Learning-Based Segmentation and Volumetric Estimation of Early Ischemic Infarcts. Journal of Imaging Informatics in Medicine. 2024:1–12."), [21](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR21 "Kamel P, Kanhere A, Kulkarni P, Khalid M, Steger R, Bodanapally U, et al. Optimizing Acute Stroke Segmentation on MRI Using Deep Learning: Self-Configuring Neural Networks Provide High Performance Using Only DWI Sequences. Journal of Imaging Informatics in Medicine. 2024:1-10.")]. The dataset consisted of 1395 unique free-text DICOM series descriptions, DICOM tag (0008,103E), reflecting free-text labels for the imaging sequence type performed, entered at the time of imaging by the manufacturer, imaging protocol, or MRI technologist. Data was classified as non-human subjects research by the IRB. Series descriptions were categorized by a practicing neuroradiologist (PK) into the following MRI sequence classifications: T1, T2, T2/FLAIR, DWI, ADC, and SWI, with an additional classification of “Other” for ambiguous labels or if the sequence did not fall into one of the categories.

### Tasks

We selected three clinically relevant tasks that reflect common radiology workflows, with the specific goal of evaluating the breadth of SLM capabilities. These tasks were designed to assess performance across distinct categories of functionality: multi-label classification (diagnostic labeling), terminology mapping (metadata standardization), and free-text generation (impression summarization). All tasks were defined in a model-agnostic manner to ensure fair and consistent evaluation across both SLMs and LLMs.

#### Task 1: Medical Report Labeling

In this classification task, models are provided the full CXR report and asked to identify the presence or absence of thirteen predefined radiographic findings. The model’s output is structured as a JavaScript Object Notation (JSON) dictionary assigning “Yes” or “No” to each label. If no finding is present, the model should also output “No Finding: Yes.” This task evaluates a model’s ability to understand and structure findings within free-text medical narratives. An example is shown in Fig.[2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#Fig2). Because each report may contain multiple findings, this task follows a multi-label classification setup, which is commonly used to extract multiple clinical concepts from a single report.

**Fig.2**

Example input of medical report labeling experiment with SLM prompt

#### Task 2: DICOM Metadata Harmonization

In this standardization task, models are provided with a free-text DICOM series description string and instructed to map it to a standard MRI sequence label. The output is expected to be a key-value JSON pair mapping the raw input to one of seven classes. An example is shown in Fig.[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#Fig3).

**Fig.3**

Example input of DICOM metadata harmonization experiment with SLM prompt

#### Task 3: Impression Generation

This generation task asks models to reconstruct the Impression section of a radiology report using the provided reports without the IMPRESSION section. The model’s output should be free text beginning with “IMPRESSION:” and should summarize the key clinical conclusions. This task evaluates the model’s ability to synthesize and abstract clinical information. An example is shown in Fig.[4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#Fig4).

#### SLMs

We tested several SLMs as the backbone of this framework: OPT-350 m [[17](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR17 "Zhang S, Roller S, Goyal N, Artetxe M, Chen M, Chen S, et al.Opt: Open pre-trained transformer language models. arXiv preprint arXiv:220501068. 2022.")], Phi-4-mini (4B) [[22](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR22 "Abouelenin, Abdelrahman, et al. \"Phi-4-mini technical report: Compact yet powerful multimodal language models via mixture-of-loras.\" arXiv preprint arXiv:2503.01743 (2025).")], Llama-3.2-1B [[23](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR23 "Grattafiori, Aaron, et al. \"The llama 3 herd of models.\" arXiv preprint arXiv:2407.21783 (2024).")], Mistral-7B [[24](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR24 "Jiang, Albert Q., et al. \"Mistral 7B.\" arXiv, arXiv preprint arXiv.2310.06825 (2023).")], and Qwen3-4B [[25](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR25 "Yang, An, et al. \"Qwen3 technical report.\" arXiv preprint arXiv:2505.09388 (2025).")]. They vary in size from 350 million parameters to 7 billion parameters. We hypothesize that OPT-350 m is the best SLM for multi-clinical decision support compared to the other models.

To fine-tune the models efficiently, we used LoRA [[16](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR16 "Hu EJ, Shen Y, Wallis P, Allen-Zhu Z, Li Y, Wang S, et al. Lora: Low-rank adaptation of large language models. ICLR. 2022;1(2):3.")], a parameter-efficient strategy designed to reduce the computational cost of adapting transformer models to new tasks. LoRA freezes the original model parameters and introduces a small number of trainable weights in the form of low-rank matrices, typically inserted into the attention layers. Specifically, we applied LoRA adapters to the query and value projection matrices within the model’s attention mechanism. This approach significantly reduces the number of trainable parameters while maintaining or improving performance across downstream tasks. LoRA has been successfully applied to several domains, including medical question answering, health outcome prediction, and out-of-distribution detection [[26](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR26 "Kim Y, Xu X, McDuff D, Breazeal C, Park HW. Health-llm: Large language models for health prediction via wearable sensor data. arXiv preprint arXiv:240106866. 2024."),[27](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR27 "Blanco J, Lambert C, Thompson O. Gpt-neo with lora for better medical knowledge performance on multimedqa dataset. Center for Open Science. 2024."),[28](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR28 "Salimbeni E, Craighero F, Khasanova R, Vasic M, Vandergheynst P. Beyond fine-tuning: LoRA modules boost near-OOD detection and LLM security. ICLR 2024 Workshop on Secure and Trustworthy Large Language Models. 2024.")].

In this work, we trained in the following SLMs:

*   Single-task SLMs (OPT-350m)

    *   SLM labeling: Fine-tuned to predict 13 diagnostic labels from CXR reports (Task 1).

    *   SLM harmonization: Fine-tuned to map DICOM series descriptions to standardized sequence labels (Task 2).

    *   SLM impression: Fine-tuned to generate radiology impressions from report findings (Task 3).

*   Multi-task SLM (OPT-350m): To test our hypothesis that a single SLM could perform all tasks, we fine-tuned one model on the combined dataset from all three tasks. Each input was prepended with a task-specific instruction string, enabling instruction-style fine-tuning. No additional task embeddings were used.

*   Remaining SLMs: To assess the performance of multi-Task SLM (OPT-350m), we fine-tuned Phi-4-mini (4B), Llama-3.2-1B, Mistral-7B, and Qwen3-4B on the same combined dataset from all three tasks, identical to multi-task SLM (OPT-350m).

#### Comparative LLM Configurations with GPT-4o

We evaluated GPT-4o across all tasks without fine-tuning. Instead, we applied the following:

*   Zero-shot prompting: The model is given only the task instruction without additional information. Prompting was kept consistent with SLM instructions to enable fair evaluation.

*   Prompt engineering: Task instructions are supplemented with structured guidance, such as output format expectations and label definitions.

### Experimental Setup

All SLMs were fine-tuned using supervised learning on an NVIDIA A40 GPU, though they can run efficiently on typical computers. The same hyperparameters were applied across all experiments: trained for 100 epochs with a batch size of 4 and a learning rate of 0.0008. The hyperparameters are determined through empirical tuning in preliminary experiments. An 80:20 train–test split for all datasets was done.

Inputs to the models followed an instruction-tuned format, in which a short directive (e.g., “Report CXR diagnosis”) preceded the main input, reflecting current best practices in instruction-following fine-tuning for NLP.

#### Evaluation Metrics

Task 1 was evaluated using the _F_ 1 score, which captures both correctness and balance between false positives and false negatives. We assessed the pairwise performance difference of this task between the multi-task OPT-350 m and other SLM models using the McNemar test for each disease label.

Task 2 was evaluated using overall classification accuracy, which reflects the proportion of correctly matched series descriptions to their canonical MRI sequence type. We assessed the pairwise performance difference of this task between the multi-task OPT-350 m and other SLM models using the McNemar test.

Task 3 was evaluated using both automatic and expert evaluation methods**.** Automatic evaluation involves using BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy) [[29](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR29 "Papineni K, Roukos S, Ward T, Zhu WJ. Bleu: a method for automatic evaluation of machine translation. In: Proceedings of the 40th annual meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics; 2002. p. 311–8.")] and ROUGE (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation) [[30](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR30 "Lin CY. Rouge: A package for automatic evaluation of summaries. In: Text summarization branches out; 2004. p. 74–81.")] scores for automatic evaluation. While BLEU is a precision-based metric that measures n-gram overlap between the generated and reference impressions, ROUGE is a recall-based metric that captures how much of the reference content was captured by the model. We assessed the pairwise performance difference between multi-task OPT-350 m and other SLM models using the _T_-test. However, they do not always capture clinical correctness or semantic appropriateness in radiology. To address this limitation, we conducted an expert evaluation using a blinded human reader study.

We randomly selected 50 impressions generated by the SLM and 50 by GPT-4o. The corresponding _Impression_ sections were removed from the original reports, and only the _Indication_ and _Findings_ were retained. Two board-certified radiologists (PK and JP) rated each generated impression on a 5-point Likert scale, where 5 represents a clear and concise IMPRESSIONS section that accurately reflects the findings. 1 represents an unacceptable IMPRESSIONS section with missing, inaccurate, or misleading impressions. The radiologists were blinded to the model source, and the order of impressions was randomized to minimize bias. The mean scores and score distributions per model were recorded to quantify the performance. To evaluate whether the models produced significantly different impression quality, we performed an independent two-sample (unpaired) two-tailed _t_-test comparing the average scores assigned to the SLM- and GPT-4o–generated impressions. We computed Cohen’s kappa between the two radiologists’ scores to assess interrater reliability. Given the ordinal nature of the Likert scale, we used quadratic weighting to appropriately penalize larger rating disagreements.

Statistical difference is measured at _p_ = 0.05 for all statistical tests.

### Model Fine-Tuning Trajectory Ablation

To empirically validate the optimal fine-tuning epoch for Phi, Llama, Mistral, and Qwen, all models are evaluated at 2, 5, 20, and 100 epochs for all three tasks: medical report labeling, DICOM series description harmonization, and impression generation from findings.

### Multi-Task Robustness Ablation

To assess the robustness of multi-task SLM against memorization and task-to-task influence, a data poisoning ablation study was performed. We chose to poison the medical report labeling task by setting the disease label of a percentage of patients to negative, thereby artificially increasing the false-positive rate. For the percentages, we chose 25%, 50%, 75%, and 100%. Labels and ground truths for DICOM series description harmonization and impression generation from findings are untouched. Combining these data, we trained four new multi-task SLM models, each with varying medical label poisoning. If the performance for DICOM series description harmonization and impression generation from findings remains the same and only the performance for medical report labeling decreases as the percentage of poisoned increases, then we can conclude that there is no task-to-task influence, and memorization is minimal.

### Impression Generation from Findings

#### Automatic Evaluation

Seven model configurations were evaluated: SLM impression, multi-task SLM, Phi, Llama, Mistral, Qwen, and GPT-4o with prompt engineering, as shown in Table[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#Tab3) (A). The single-task and multi-task SLMs achieved comparable performance, with BLEU scores of 0.527 and 0.518 and identical ROUGE scores of 0.688. However, Phi, Llama, Mistral, and Qwen all achieved worse BLEU and ROUGE scores than multi-task SLM.

Consistent with the trends observed in Task 1 and Task 2, GPT-4o, when used without fine-tuning and prompted with the same instructions as the SLM, performed substantially worse than both SLM models. With prompt engineering, GPT-4o continued to struggle. To further improve its performance, we introduced a few-shot setting. When prompted with ten examples using the same SLM-style instructions, GPT-4o achieved a 0.045 BLEU score and a 0.209 ROUGE score. Under the same ten-shot setup but with prompt engineering, GPT-4o achieved its best performance, with a 0.178 BLEU score and a 0.463 ROUGE score. Nonetheless, both SLM configurations continued to outperform GPT-4o by a significant margin.

Additionally, a pairwise _T_-test was performed to compare the BLEU and ROUGE scores between the multi-task SLM and other models, as shown in Table[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#Tab3) (B). Phi, Llama, Mistral, Qwen, and GPT-4o with prompt engineering are all statistically significantly different from multi-task SLM.

#### Expert Evaluation

As shown in Fig.[5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#Fig5), the SLM consistently outperformed GPT-4o in expert assessments of impression quality. The average rating for SLM-generated impressions was 4.39 ± 1.00, compared to 3.65 ± 1.00 for GPT-4o (_p_ = 0.0008). The score distributions further illustrate this distinction. Ratings for the SLM were right-skewed, with the majority of impressions receiving scores in the upper range. In particular, a large cluster of scores was in the rightmost bin (closer to 5), indicating a high concentration of top ratings. In contrast, GPT-4o’s score distribution was more evenly spread across the middle-to-high range. This suggests that while GPT-4o produced some high-quality outputs, it also generated a broader mix of lower-rated impressions. Finally, Cohen’s kappa was 0.65, indicating a substantial agreement in the scores between the two radiologists.

## Discussion

SLM with LoRA fine-tuning, especially on OPT-350 m, demonstrated excellent performance in adapting to single and multiple medical tasks, compared to other SLM models such as Phi-4-mini (4B), Llama-3.2-1B, Mistral-7B, Qwen3-4B, and out-of-the-box or few-shot GPT-4o. SLMs’ computational efficiency enables them to be deployed in medical institutions to ensure that confidential patient protected health information (PHI) is secured within the control of the medical institutions. Moreover, computational efficiency improves clinical decision support in medical institutions with limited computational resources, such as in community clinics, rural clinics, critical access hospitals, small practices, and mobile diagnostic units where connectivity, compute, and support staff are limited, where big data centers or servers are unavailable or too big an investment. In underserved regions, where diagnostic capacity is limited by both expertise and bandwidth, a small gain from compact SLMs can meaningfully improve triage, communication, and follow-up. A single multi-task model deployed on hardware with low computational power could provide radiology assistance and clinical support in areas without subspecialists. This enables decentralization of diagnostic tools, ultimately to bring AI assistance closer to the point of care, where resources are most limited.

The ability to fine-tune multiple tasks together streamlines and simplifies the usability and deployment of language models. By utilizing a single SLM architecture with LoRA adapters, health systems can maintain task modularity without operational complexity to lower both engineering costs and validation burdens. This model design also simplifies regulatory approval workflows, since modular LoRA adapters can be validated independently as clinical needs evolve. Furthermore, an SLM LoRA adapter is sufficient for each medical entity to customize learning tasks according to its needs based on medical demand and data. Finally, as shown by the results, LLMs are significantly more challenging for prompt engineering. LLMs require fine-grained, detailed instructions and require users to have extensive experience working with LLM prompts and multiple attempts [[32](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR32 "Mesk´o B. Prompt engineering as an important emerging skill for medical professionals: tutorial. Journal of medical Internet research. 2023;25:e50638.")]. On the other hand, SLM fine-tuning only requires simple prompts that can be written by any user using everyday language.

However, our study has some limitations. The proposed approach relies on fine-tuning of the SLM, which is a supervised approach dependent on labeled data. In practice, however, we do not envision continuous manual expert annotation as a requirement for deployment. Instead, a feasible production strategy is a teacher–student workflow, in which general-purpose foundation models are used to generate pseudo-labels on new data, followed by selective expert review or correction to ensure quality. These pseudo-labels can then be used to incrementally fine-tune the in-house SLM, which is deployed locally for routine inference. This approach substantially reduces annotation burden, preserves data privacy, and amortizes the cost of foundation-model usage over periodic updates rather than continuous inference. Another limitation of this study is that the experimental evaluation is limited to three tasks. These tasks were intentionally selected not to exhaustively cover all possible applications, but to span a broad range of structurally distinct NLP problem types commonly encountered in radiology workflows, namely multi-label classification, controlled vocabulary harmonization, and free-text generation. Together, these tasks capture core linguistic operations underlying many radiology NLP use cases, allowing us to evaluate whether a single small language model can support heterogeneous objectives within a unified deployment framework. Expanding the evaluation to additional tasks as well as investigating the maximum number of tasks that can be trained together without losing performance remains an important direction for future work.

In the future, we would like to optimize the fine-tuning workflow to enable lifelong learning. This will allow institutions to add new tasks without catastrophic forgetting. Without lifelong learning, when a new task is introduced, the previously learned tasks will be forgotten due to catastrophic forgetting [[33](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-01912-4#ref-CR33 "McCloskey M, Cohen NJ. Catastrophic interference in connectionist networks: The sequential learning problem. In: Psychology of learning and motivation. vol. 24. Elsevier; 1989. p. 109–65.")]. Thus, all previously learned tasks must be learned again to incorporate a new task, which is inefficient.

Overall, this study demonstrates the potential of SLM with LoRA fine-tuning in addressing the computational and closed-source challenges of LLMs in medical task adaptation, highlighting the effectiveness of fine-tuning multiple clinical tasks with one SLM. This work supports a shift in clinical NLP research from larger models to building practical, low-resource, deployable systems. As institutions seek proper AI tools, compact SLMs offer a sustainable, flexible path forward. While there are limitations to consider, such as the requirement for labeled fine-tuning data and only three tasks tested, these findings lay the foundation for future work to further improve the lifelong learning capability for multi-task SLM fine-tuning.

### Summary Statement

Multi-task SLM with LoRA fine-tuning outperforms GPT-4o on multiple clinical tasks, ensuring patient privacy, improving clinical decision support in medical institutions with limited resources, and simplifying operational complexity.

### Key Results

• Our multi-task SLM LoRA fine-tuning is based on the SLM OPT-350 m (350 million parameters) and parameter-efficient fine-tuned, enabling it to be used in typical computers, without needing computing clusters or data centers.

• Unlike closed-source LLM such as GPT-4o, multi-task SLM with LoRA fine-tuning can perform clinical tasks without extensive prompt engineering.

• Multi-task SLM LoRA fine-tuning outperforms GPT-4o on multiple clinical tasks, including medical report labeling, DICOM series description harmonization, and impression generation from findings.
