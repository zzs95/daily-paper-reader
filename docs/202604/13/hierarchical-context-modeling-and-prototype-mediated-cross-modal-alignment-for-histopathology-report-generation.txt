Abstract 

Histopathology in whole slide images (WSIs) serves as the gold standard for cancer diagnosis, with clinical reports playing a critical role in decision-making. However, the time-consuming nature of con-ventional pathological examination has driven increasing and urgent demand for automated report generation. Deep learning methods offer a certain potential to revolutionize this requirement by Histopathology Report Generation (HRG). Nevertheless, existing HRG approaches suffer from low-quality generation results due to ineffective exploration of multi-scale visual context in gigapixel WSIs and the inherent semantic gap between heterogeneous vision-language modalities. To address these challenges, we propose HC-Gen, a novel framework which synergistically combines hierarchical context modeling with prototype-mediate cross-modal alignment for HRG. Inspired by pathologists’ anatomically-grounded diagnostic logic, we design a hierarchical context fusion module to integrate multi-scale visual-semantic context and implicit hierarchy prior in WSIs. Furthermore, we propose a cross-modal prototypical memory module to establish learnable semantic prototypes as intermediate bridges to achieve unified and efficient vision-language alignment. Model performance was assessed through natural language generation metrics and human evaluation, extensive experiments on two benchmark datasets demonstrate that HC-Gen outperforms state-of-the-art methods. Extra visual-ization provides crucial support for the interpretability of the decision process. Our code is available at: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/HC-Gen/ .

## 1 Introduction 

Whole Slide Images (WSIs) based histopathology analysis serves as the gold standard in cancer diagnosis and prognosis [1]. Due to the gigapixel size and inherent heterogeneity in WSIs, in-depth slide analysis usually demands labor-intensive assessment by specialized pathologists. Recent success of deep learning in the histopathology analysis field, including disease classification [2] [3], lesion detection [4] [5], and tissue segmentation [6] [7], has enhanced the automation analyses of WSIs, improving diagnostic efficiency and shortening diagnostic cycle [8]. In WSI analysis, histopathology reports (Fig. 1) provide essential domain insights by highlighting abnormalities, which contain significant clinical value. However, compiling the key findings into a report is labor-intensive. However, HRG confronts unique challenges arising from the scale and heterogeneity of WSIs, which prevent the direct application of RRG methods. First, WSIs are gigapixel in size and cannot be directly processed by conventional models, hindering the direct adoption of existing RRG methods. Second, patches extracted from a WSI often contain substantial redundancy [14], requiring dedicated techniques to effectively aggregate information while preserving diagnostic relevance. Third, aligning the vast and heterogeneous visual information from WSIs with structured textual reports remains a significant cross-modal learning challenge. In the broader field of computational pathology, Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) has become the paradigm for handling WSIs, typically by decomposing them into patches for feature extraction and aggregation into slide-level representations. More recently, foundation model pretraining [15] [16] [17] has emerged as a powerful advancement, learning transferable visual representations that serve as a strong base for various downstream tasks [18] [19]. Despite the success of both paradigms, they have primarily been applied to discriminative tasks such as classification [20] [21] or segmentation [22] [23], rather than generative tasks like HRG, which focus on translating the complex visual information in WSIs into comprehensive diagnostic narratives. While previous studies have explored bridging MIL and RRG methods [24], multi-scale encoding [25], and cross-modal alignment [1], their designs are not fully tailored to the generative and descriptive nature of HRG. Specifically, (1) standard MIL aggregation often prioritizes discriminative features for slide-level classification, overlooking the comprehensive, multi-scale visual context necessary for coherent diagnostic narratives [26]. (2) Existing multi-scale encoding methods typically perform aggregation at a single scale, failing to truly leverage the inherent multi-resolution hierarchy of WSIs. (3) Current cross-modal alignment techniques do not adequately learn and utilize intermediate semantics; a dedicated, semantically-consistent alignment mechanism is still needed to map complex visual patterns onto the structured language of pathology reports [1]. Furthermore, most existing computational workflows lack explicit modeling of the anatomically-grounded diagnostic logic followed by pathologists, which limits clinical interpretability to some degree. To this end, we propose a novel model named HC-Gen with hierarchical visual context modeling and cross-modal semantic alignment. HC-Gen consists of two main components: 

• Hierarchical Context Fusion (HCF) Module. To fully leverage the complementarity of multi-scale visual-semantic context and the anatomical hierarchy prior inherent in WSIs, inspired by pathologists’ multi-stage diagnostic workflow of holistic screening, localized detailed examination, and cross-scale analysis [27], we design the HCF module, which consists of three key procedures: hierarchical context summarization, semantic-guided context refinement, and dual-scale context fusion. 

• Cross-modal Prototypical Memory (CPM) Module. To bridge the semantic gap between het-erogeneous visual and textual modalities, we propose the CPM module that incorporates learnable memory prototypes as intermediate semantic coordinators, facilitating cross-modal semantic align-ment via two coordinated procedures: prototype-guided visual refinement (PVR) and prototype-aware textual conditioning (PTC). Our main contributions are summarised as follows: 

• We propose HC-Gen, a novel framework that addresses the challenging HRG task by innovatively integrating hierarchical multi-scale visual context modeling with prototype-mediated cross-modal alignment. 

• We design the HCF module, which explicitly models the diagnostic progression from global to local patterns, enabling adaptive multi-scale visual-semantic integration. 

• We introduce a CPM module, which establishes learnable semantic prototypes as bridges to achieve fine-grained alignment between visual features and textual concepts. 

• Visualization reveals HC-Gen’s attention to diagnostically relevant regions, offering interpretable insights into its prediction process. 

• Extensive experiments on two benchmark datasets demonstrates that HC-Gen outperforms the state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods in both natural language generation (NLG) metrics and human evaluation. ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 2 Related Work 

2.1 Image Caption and Medical Report Generation 

Image captioning and medical report generation are two pivotal research domains that provide foundational methodologies for our task of histopathology report generation. Image captioning [28] [29] is a task that involves taking an image as input and generating a corre-sponding textual description. This process generally starts with encoding the image into visual features, which are subsequently employed to predict a sequence of words that culminate in the final caption. In the early research, Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) [30] and Long Short-Term Memory networks (LSTMs) [31] [32] [33] were prevalent in this domain. For example, Krueger et al. [18] introduced an image captioning approach that leveraged regularized RNNs, which were effective in preserving hidden units and facilitating more efficient propagation of gradient and state information. In a similar vein, Chen et al. [32] incorporated an LSTM module within an encoder-decoder architecture to strengthen the connections between adjacent hidden states and mitigate inconsistencies in sequence prediction. As the Transformer architecture [34] gained traction and achieved notable success in natural language pro-cessing, it began to be applied to image captioning as well. Mokady et al. [35] put forward a method that utilized a pre-trained CLIP model to extract aligned textual and visual features, and employed a Transformer-based decoder to produce image descriptions. In the field of medical report generation, similar architectures have demonstrated considerable poten-tial. These methods were first utilized in radiology report generation. For instance, Chen et al. [11] proposed a memory-driven Transformer for RRG, which recorded the information from previous genera-tion processes to a relational memory. Chen et al. [12] introduced a cross-modal memory network, where a memory matrix was employed to record the alignment and interaction between images and texts. Wang et al. [36] presented a network entirely based on the Transformer architecture for producing reports from radiographic images, employing an image-text matching loss during training for cross-modal alignment. Similarly, Li et al. [37] developed a report generation model tailored for chest X-ray images, which utilized a dynamic graph to glean diagnostic insights from visual representations, thereby effectively capturing both global and specific information. Tanida et al. [38] took a different approach by integrating report generation with anatomical region detection, resulting in the generation of distinct descriptions for each identified region. Beyond architectural innovations, recent approaches [39] [40] [41] [42] began to utilize domain-specific knowledge like knowledge graphs or probabilistic priors to constrain output validity. In summary, advancements in image captioning and medical report generation have laid important methodological and technical foundations for the evolving field of HRG. 

2.2 Histopathology Report Generation 

In the field of HRG [43] [44], Tsuneki et al. [43] established a WSI-report dataset and employed an RNN-based attention model to generate captions from patch sequences. Zhang et al. [45] combined CNN and LSTM for vision language model training using region of interest (ROI) from WSIs. CONCH [16] and PLIP [17] adopted CLIP-based [46] training of patch-text alignment, enabling patch-level report generation. Shifting to slide-level analysis, more recent studies aim to directly model the representation of entire WSIs, moving beyond patch or ROI-based approaches. Qin et al. [14] introduced random patch sampling for global representation, combined with a grid masking module to capture local information. Guevara et al. [47] proposed a weakly supervised Transformer architecture capable of simultaneously handling whole-slide image classification and caption generation. Chen et al. [25] proposed a multiple-instance learning framework that divided WSIs into patch sequences, curated a large-scale dataset via TCGA WSI-report cleaning, and incorporated a position-aware module for spatial relationship modeling. In multi-scale modeling, Sengupta et al. [24] employed an hierarchical image pyramid Transformer [48] for multi-level feature encoding with a BioClinicalBERT-based decoder for report generation. Guo et al. [1] designed a local-global hierarchical encoder to integrate both local-region and global-WSI features. Tan et al. [49] proposed a regional hierarchical encoder for multi-scale feature fusion. However, these methods often fail to explicitly model the inherent hierarchical dependencies across scales in WSIs. Regarding cross-modal alignment, Zhou et al. [50] first aligned WSI features with textual reports using Q-former [51], then generated reports via a frozen large language model. Shaikovski et al. [52] employed contrastive loss between class tokens to align slide and text embeddings. Ahmed et al. [53] adapted a large multimodal model for HRG, leveraging its pre-trained cross-modal understanding for vision-language alignment. ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS Nevertheless, these approaches often rely on generic alignment strategies that may not achieve fine-grained, semantically-consistent alignment tailored to histopathology. In addition, ensuring the clinical interpretability of the report generation process remains a significant open problem. In summary, effectively integrating multi-scale visual context, achieving semantically-consistent cross-modal alignment, and providing interpretable insights into the generation process remain core challenges for HRG.

3.3 Hierarchical Context Fusion Module 

The key insight for proposing HCF is to leverage the complementary nature of multi-scale visual-semantic context and the anatomical hierarchy prior inherent in histopathological WSIs, addressing the challenge of comprehensive analysis through structured integration of global tissue patterns and local cellular sig-natures. Specifically, this module consists of three key procedures—hierarchical context summarization, semantic-guided context refinement, and dual-scale context fusion—mimicking pathologists’ multi-stage diagnostic logic of initial holistic screening, localized detailed examination, and integrated cross-scale analysis.

3.3.2 Semantic-Guided Context Refinement 

The motivation of performing semantic-guided context refinement stems from emulating pathologists’ diagnostic reasoning, which involves localizing suspicious regions and subsequently conducting sys-tematic analysis of the localized histopathological details. This paradigm first establishes implicit perceptual-semantic associations between parent-level perceptual abstractions (e.g., disorganized pat-terns) and coarse-grained semantic concepts (e.g., abnormal architecture), then explicitly grounds these concepts in localized visual evidence (e.g., irregular nuclear distributions), thereby achieving hierarchical multi-granular visual context integration. Existing HRG methods rely on direct image-text cross attention but struggle with visual-textual modality gaps. To address this limitation, we propose the CPM module that introduces learnable memory prototypes as intermediate semantic coordinators, coupled with parameter-sharing cross attention mecha-nisms, enabling coordinated operation via two coordinated processes: prototype-guided visual refinement and prototype-aware textual conditioning. As illustrated in Fig. 3, the CPM module learns data-driven memory prototypes M through end-to-end training, where M is randomly initialized and optimized to capture cross-modal semantic. Alternating between PVR and PTC training phases, M distills pathology-specific semantic from WSIs while assimilating diagnostic context from reports. This dual-interaction establishes a cross-modal semantic bridge, forging a unified visual-textual representation space. After training, M acts as the shared semantic anchor, refining visual features via text-correlated patterns and guiding text decoding to ensure visual semantic consistency. This dual-alignment dynamically bridges modality gaps, grounding reports in prototype-mediated semantics.

## 4 Experiments and Results 

4.1 Implementation 

Datasets. We employ two benchmarking H&E-stained WSI datasets [48] [54] from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) project, a large-scale multi-institutional cancer genomics initiative, which are widely adopted for histopathology analysis: TCGA-BRCA (BRCA), the largest public breast cancer dataset, comprising 1,133 WSIs of invasive ductal or invasive lobular carcinoma. TCGA-LUNG (LUNG), a com-prehensive lung cancer dataset comprising 1,054 WSIs, including 541 lung adenocarcinomas (LUAD) and 513 squamous cell carcinomas (LUSC). BRCA and LUNG originate from different organ systems, and exhibit distinct tissue architectures and pathological morphologies, as illustrated in Fig. 4. Such cross-organ and cross-pathology heterogeneity introduces realistic distribution shifts, providing a rigorous testbed for evaluating the robustness and generalization capability of the proposed model. The corresponding reports are extracted from WSI-Report dataset [1], sourced from the same TCGA project. By utilizing this curated dataset, our work ensures reproducibility and focuses on model development within a standardized benchmark. Following the common practice in prior works [1] [25], we divide the data for each cancer type into training, validation, and test sets with a ratio of 8:1:1. Pre-processing. Our method extends the CLAM [55] framework to extract spatially-aligned dual-scale patches from foreground regions of WSIs. After an intial segmentation process based on Otsu’s thresholding, non-overlapped dual-scale patches with the tissue regions are considered. The improved implementation generates hierarchical patch pairs through non-overlap splitting, where high-magnification (20x) child patches correspond to the subdivided regions of low-magnification (2x) parent patches. This spatial containment relationship, maintained via consistent stride-patch configurations across magnification levels, ensures coordinate-preserving feature aggregation. Background filtering causes deviation from the theoretical 1:100 inter-scale patch ratio, with actual patch counts per WSI varying from hundreds to hundreds of thousands based on tissue coverage. We use PyTorch to build our framework and employ a 3-layer Transformer decoder with 8 attention heads, and 512 dimensions for hidden states. Our model is trained under cross entropy loss with Adam optimizer, using initial learning rate 1 × 10 −4 and weight decay by 0.8 per epoch, and a batch size of 1. We adapt beam search with a beam size of 3 for inference. We choose the best parameters based on the highest BLEU-4 score on the validation set. For data preprocessing, we utilized seven NVIDIA GeForce RTX 2080 Ti 11GB GPUs. All experiments were conducted on two NVIDIA GTX 4090D 24GB GPUs. 

Visual Extractors. We employ ViT* [1] (ViT-L [56] pretrained via DINOv2 [57] on about 200 million pathological patches) alongside three comparative feature extractors: ResNet50 [58] (CNN pretrained on ImageNet), UNI [19] (ViT-L pretrained via DINOv2 on more than 100 million pathological patches) and CONCH [16] (ViT pretrained via CLIP on 1.17 million pathological patch-text pairs). 

Baselines. We consider the following representative models as baselines: Transformer, M2Transformer [59], R2Gen [11], R2GenCMN [12], MI-Gen [25], HistGen [1], SlideChat [60] and WSI-LLaVA [61]. Transformer and M2Transformer are two traditional image captioning (ImgCap) methods, while R2Gen and R2GenCMN are foundational baselines in RRG. MI-Gen and HistGen are Transformer-based HRG models: the former excels at capturing spatial hierarchies within WSIs, while the latter boosts generation by aligning WSIs and reports at both local and global granularities. SlideChat [60] and WSI-LLaVA [61] are recent large language model (LLM)-based multimodal frameworks for WSI understanding. SlideChat employs LongNet [62] as a slide-level encoder to aggregate patch-level represen-tations, while using Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct [63] as the language backbone. In contrast, WSI-LLaVA adopts a LLaVA-v1.5-7B [64] model and bridges visual and textual modalities by training a multimodal projec-tor. These models represent the trend of adapting general-purpose multimodal LLMs to histopathology report generation. To ensure fair benchmarking, we re-implement all SOTA models on the target datasets. 

Evaluation Metrics. Following previous studies, we adopt standard NLG metrics for evaluation, including BLEU-1 (B-1), BLEU-2 (B-2), BLEU-3 (B-3), BLEU-4 (B-4), METEOR (M), ROUGE-L (R-L) and CIDEr (C). Additionally, we conducted a human evaluation to assess the quality of the generated reports. 50% of test cases per dataset were randomly sampled and evaluated by three pathologists. Each report was rated on Readability (R), Informativeness (I), and Accuracy (A) using a five-point scale and counting errors across four categories: Unverifiable Statements (US), Critical Omissions (CO), Repetitive Phrases (RP), and Factual Errors (FE).

4.2.2 Computational Analysis 

Parameter Comparison. To evaluate computational efficiency, we compare the total number of train-able parameters with representative baselines. As shown in Table 3, HC-Gen contains 15.7M trainable parameters, which is significantly smaller than most Transformer-based report generation models and substantially lighter than LLM-based methods. 

Table 3 Comparison of total trainable parameters. Method Trainable Parameters R2Gen 43.8M R2GenCMN 23.3M Transformer 21.2M M2Transformer 39.7M MI-Gen 17.6M HistGen 25.2M SlideChat 41.7M WSI-LLaVA 21.0M HC-Gen 15.7M 

Visual Encoding Complexity. Most early baselines (R2Gen, R2GenCMN, Transformer, M2Transformer) adopt a 3-layer Transformer encoder for visual modeling. Their computational com-plexity is dominated by self-attention, which scales quadratically with the number of input tokens n, i.e., 

O(n2) per layer. MI-Gen introduces a Position-Aware Module (PAM) after each Transformer layer, which applies depthwise convolutions with fixed kernel sizes. While this enhances spatial modeling, it does not reduce the quadratic self-attention complexity and adds additional convolutional overhead. HistGen reduces effective attention cost by dividing the input sequence into fixed-length groups and introduc-ing learnable tokens for hierarchical aggregation. However, intra-group self-attention remains quadratic within each group. SlideChat employs two-layer LongNet encoder before projecting visual features into ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS an LLM, which applies sparse attention mechanisms where attention is computed according to a dilated pattern. By reducing the number of attended neighbors for each token and biasing attention weights to diminish with increasing distance, LongNet achieves a near-linear complexity O(n), alleviating long-sequence attention cost. In contrast, WSI-LLaVA projects pre-extracted visual features into the language space through a lightweight multimodal projector while keeping the LLM backbone frozen. Although this design avoids expensive end-to-end multimodal training, report generation still relies on autoregressive decoding of a large LLM, which introduces substantial inference cost when generating long reports. 

Complexity of HC-Gen Visual Encoding. HC-Gen divides the high-magnification patch sequence ( n) into m spatial groups. Within each group, Hierarchical Context Summarization aggregates local patch representations efficiently, which operates approximately linearly with respect to n. Semantic-Guided Context Refinement performs group-level cross-attention that is bounded by the maximum group size g, yielding O(m · g2) interactions. Since g is constrained (e.g., g = 10 × 10 in 2x+20x settings), this component achieves practical efficiency close to linear with respect to n. Dual-Scale Context Fusion integrates multi-scale cues with simple element-wise operations, adding negligible overhead. In contrast to full self-attention ( O(n2)) in standard Transformers, HC-Gen avoids global attention over the entire patch sequence and limits costly interactions to manageable group-level contexts. This design yields a favorable trade-off between contextual expressivity and computational scalability. Overall, HC-Gen achieves competitive performance with the smallest parameter count among non-LLM methods and avoids the quadratic global attention bottleneck, demonstrating a favorable trade-off between effectiveness and computational efficiency. 

4.2.3 Human Evaluation 

To comprehensively evaluate the clinical utility of the generated reports beyond automatic NLG metrics, we conduct a human evaluation. This evaluation captures dimensions critical for real-world adoption, complements automatic metrics by providing a clinically grounded perspective. As shown in Table. 4, HC-Gen demonstrates stable and competitive performance across both datasets, further validating that its gains in automatic metrics translate into clinically meaningful improvements. In particular, HC-Gen achieves consistently stronger Informativeness and Accuracy while maintaining balanced error rates, indicating that its generation is not merely fluent but substantively aligned with pathological evidence. The comparison across baselines reveals that automatic metrics alone cannot fully reflect clinical qual-ity. For example, although HistGen attains competitive NLG scores, its human evaluation performance is comparatively limited, while MI-Gen produces fewer Unverifiable Statements (US) and Factual Errors (FE) despite lower automatic scores. Similarly, LLM-based approaches such as SlideChat and WSI-LLaVA exhibit superior Readability, likely benefiting from strong language priors. SlideChat relies on a pure language backbone with visual information injected via a slide-level encoder, whereas WSI-LLaVA adopts a vision–language architecture that aligns pre-extracted visual features to a frozen LLM through a lightweight multimodal projector. However, their training paradigms rely on lightweight visual–language alignment while keeping the large language backbone largely frozen. As a result, the visual signals may not be sufficiently grounded during generation, causing the model to rely more heavily on language pri-ors. This often leads to fluent yet less informative reports or increased clinically irrelevant or unverifiable content. These observations suggest an inherent trade-off between linguistic smoothness and diagnostic substance when visual grounding is insufficient.

## 5 Discussion 

Although the quality and coherence of generated reports have improved, several limitations of the current framework merit discussion for further clinical applicability: 1) While HC-Gen improves the semantic ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS relevance of generated text, the factual precision of specific pathological descriptors—such as staging or grading—requires more rigorous verification against gold-standard annotations. A promising direction to enhance this precision is to integrate structured domain knowledge and perform targeted report cleaning, thereby providing the model with cleaner data and explicit constraints for generating factually grounded descriptions. 2) The dependency on fixed magnifications may not seamlessly adapt to all cancer types. Future work could extend the framework to adaptively select and fuse features from multiple magnification levels, improving robustness and generalization. 

## 6 Conclusion 

This paper proposes HC-Gen, a novel histopathology report generation framework designed for the chal-lenging and valuable HRG task. The framework synergistically integrates hierarchical visual context modeling with cross-modal semantic alignment to enhance report quality and semantic consistency. By explicitly leveraging the anatomical hierarchy of gigapixel WSIs, our approach fully exploits multi-scale visual-semantic complementarity. Simultaneously, prototype-mediated cross-modal alignment bridges the inherent modality gap between pathological imagery and diagnostic language. Experiments on two benchmark datasets verify the effectiveness of our method across NLG metrics and human evaluation. Visualization reveals the decision process, providing crucial support for the interpretability into its predictions.
