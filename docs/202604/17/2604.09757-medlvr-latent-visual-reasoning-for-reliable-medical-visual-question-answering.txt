# MedLVR: Latent Visual Reasoning for Reliable Medical Visual Question Answering 

Suyang Xi, Songtao Hu, Yuxiang Lai, Wangyun Dan, Yaqi Liu, Shansong Wang, and Xiaofeng Yang 

Abstract — Medical vision–language models (VLMs) have shown strong potential for medical visual question answer-ing (VQA), yet their reasoning remains largely text-centric: images are encoded once as static context, and subse-quent inference is dominated by language. This paradigm is fundamentally limited in clinical scenarios, where accurate answers often depend on subtle, localized visual evidence that cannot be reliably preserved in static embeddings. We propose M ED LVR, a latent visual reasoning framework that introduces an explicit visual evidence state into autore-gressive decoding. Instead of relying solely on text-based intermediate reasoning, M ED LVR interleaves a short latent reasoning segment within the decoder by reusing hidden states as continuous latent steps, enabling iterative preser-vation and refinement of query-relevant visual evidence be-fore answer generation. To support effective visual super-vision, we adopt a two-stage training strategy: region of in-terest (ROI)-supervised fine-tuning aligns latent states with clinically relevant image evidence, and Visual-Latent Policy Optimization (VLPO) further optimizes latent reasoning and answer generation under outcome-level rewards. Experi-ments on OmniMedVQA and five external medical VQA benchmarks show that M ED LVR consistently outperforms recent reasoning baselines and improves the average score over the Qwen2.5-VL-7B backbone from 48.3% to 53.4%. These results show that latent visual reasoning provides an effective mechanism for preserving diagnostically relevant visual evidence and improving the reliability of medical VQA. 

Index Terms — Medical Reasoning, Medical Image Anal-ysis, Medical Visual Question Answering, Medical Vision-language Models, Reinforcement Learning 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Vision–language models (VLMs) have achieved impressive results on natural-image tasks, such as visual question an-swering and multimodal dialogue [1]–[3]. However, applying these models to medical imaging remains challenging [4], [5], as clinical decisions often depend on a very small number of subtle and highly localized visual cues [6]. As a result, the reasoning process can gradually drift toward language priors and away from diagnostically relevant visual evidence, producing conclusions that appear fluent but remain weakly grounded in the image [11]–[13]. Recent methods for strengthening the coupling between visual cues and reasoning largely fall into two paradigms [14]– [16]. “Thinking about Images” improves text-space reasoning, for example by eliciting or distilling CoT-style traces via supervised fine-tuning (SFT), so that answers better align with the visual input [15], [17]. However, because it still reasons over textual descriptions of image content rather than directly over visual semantics, generation can drift toward language-prior shortcuts and produce explanations that are weakly grounded in the image [5], [11]. “Thinking with Images” instead re-examines visual cues through external perception operations such as zooming, cropping, or tool calls, and feeds the resulting views back into the model [16], [18]– [23]. This often comes with substantial overhead, requiring extra components and repeated perception–generation loops, while the revisited views may still fail to consistently constrain decoding. These observations point to a structural mismatch in current medical VLMs: although visual and textual tokens are embed-ded into a shared semantic space, intermediate reasoning is still realized primarily through text-space decoding [24], [25]. As a result, the influence of visual cues is typically mediated by static context, without an explicit mechanism to maintain and update grounding constraints as decoding unfolds [26], [27]. This raises a natural question: can medical reasoning be supported by representations beyond discrete text tokens? Recent studies suggest that intermediate reasoning can also be carried out in a continuous latent space [28]–[30]. In this view, the model alternates between language decoding and latent updates, using latent states as an internal substrate to preserve and refresh grounding constraints throughout gener-ation, rather than relying on textual tokens alone [18], [31]. Motivated by this mismatch, we introduce a latent visual reasoning pathway for medical visual question answering (VQA), in which latent updates serve as an explicit mecha-nism for maintaining grounding constraints during decoding (Fig. 1). To stabilize latent reasoning and keep it aligned with question-relevant cues, we optimize the model with a two-stage training strategy. II. RELATED WORKS 

Medical VLMs. Early medical multimodal models typically adapt general-purpose VLMs to clinical settings through su-pervised fine-tuning and instruction alignment on medical corpora, such as LLaVA-Med [32] and Med-Flamingo [24], aiming to better match medical QA and clinical language. More recently, models such as Med-Gemma [33] and Lingshu [34] place greater emphasis on medical-native pretraining or more systematic data design to broaden coverage of medical concepts and improve cross-task transferability. Meanwhile, the community has explored stronger alignment strategies, including reinforcement-learning post-training and more re-fined data construction [35], [36]. Overall, most approaches optimize final answers while placing only weak constraints on intermediate cue acquisition, so medical VQA can still yield fluent but weakly grounded conclusions. 

Reasoning with Text and Tools. Thinking about Images. 

This line treats intermediate reasoning as a text-space training target and has evolved from SFT-based trace supervision to RL-based trajectory alignment. Multimodal CoT supervision and stepwise SFT strategies are commonly used to help models internalize explicit reasoning traces during training [15], [17]. More recent RL-style post-training further steers intermediate traces toward task-level objectives and robust-ness [35]. Beyond trace supervision, several works introduce explicit intermediate carriers such as visual scratchpads and annotations, so that the generated rationale is conditioned on actionable visual marks rather than purely linguistic scaffolds [37]. In parallel, hallucination-oriented objectives penalize over-reliance on language priors and encourage retrospection or improved attention allocation during generation [11]. 

Thinking with Images. This line makes multimodal reason-ing procedural : the model plans explicit perception actions, executes them, and re-conditions on the resulting visual states, rather than relying on a one-shot image embedding [16]. A common instantiation is region revisiting, where the model proposes candidate ROIs, performs zooming or cropping, and feeds refreshed patches back to improve visual grounding [16], [21]. Tool-augmented variants extend the returned state beyond crops by invoking external executors or editors, pro-ducing intermediate artifacts such as code-generated outputs or edited views that are re-encoded as additional context [22], [23]. Reasoning in Latent Space. Recent work shifts interme-diate reasoning from discrete tokens to continuous latent embeddings, treating it as a compact state trajectory learned via latent-space training and compression or self-distillation [28]–[30]. This shift offers a different knob for scaling test-time compute: instead of lengthening textual chains, mod-els allocate additional latent steps that refine internal states while keeping the external output concise [13]. In multimodal settings, latent visual reasoning uses these latent updates to repeatedly reconstruct question-relevant visual cues and carry them forward as persistent internal constraints, interleaving latent refinement with standard decoding to reduce grounding drift [18], [31]. Building on this paradigm, we adapt latent reasoning to medical VQA, designing objectives that preserve subtle, localized cues and explicitly optimize latent trajectories for stable visual grounding [18], [31], [35]. 

III. METHOD 

A. Method Overview. 

Fig. 2 summarizes M ED LVR. We follow a standard mul-timodal LLM pipeline: the vision encoder maps the image I

into patch-level visual tokens V , and the backbone’s native multimodal projector proj( ·) aligns them to the language latent space, producing ˜V = proj( V ) that is dimensionally compatible with the embedded text tokens T . We concatenate them as a unified context X0 = [ ˜V ; T ], which conditions the LLM decoder for answer generation. Even with this unified context, conventional medical MLLMs remain fundamentally text-centric at inference time: at each step, the decoder hidden state is converted by the LM head into a distribution over discrete vocabulary tokens, so visual cues influence decoding only indirectly through token likelihood, without an explicit, persistent evidence state. MED LVR addresses this limitation by interleaving a latent reasoning segment into decoding. When the model emits the control token <Medlvr Start> , it temporarily switches from token emission to latent-state rollout, where the decoder hidden state is directly fed back as the next-step input embed-ding, iteratively refining an internal, query-relevant evidence representation in the same latent space as ˜V . The model then emits <Medlvr End> to resume standard token generation and output the final answer. B. Two-Stage Training Pipeline. 

We train M ED LVR with a two-stage procedure. Stage 1 performs supervised fine-tuning on ROI-annotated data, where the latent segment is explicitly anchored to ROI evidence so that the latent rollout becomes a stable and identifiable carrier of query-relevant visual information. Stage 2 applies outcome-level reinforcement learning to co-adapt the latent rollout and answer generation, optimizing a visual–latent policy objective that propagates sequence-level rewards to both discrete text tokens and the continuous latent states.

IV. E XPERIMENTS 

A. Experimental Setup 

Training Datasets and Protocol. MED LVR is trained in two stages with different data sources. In Stage 1, we perform supervised fine-tuning on our in-house large-scale medical grounding corpus, which contains approximately 800,000 fine-grained question–answer–box pairs constructed from 31 public datasets across eight imaging modalities, including CT, MRI, X-ray, Ultrasound, Dermoscopy, Fundus, OCT, and Microscopy. In Stage 2, following the medical VQA protocol adopted in prior reasoning work such as Med-R1, we perform reinforcement learning on the open-access split of OmniMedVQA. OmniMedVQA contains 82,059 medical images and 88,996 image–question pairs spanning the same eight modalities. Following the standard setting in prior work, we adopt an 80/20 train–test split, using the training split for reinforcement learning and reserving the test split for in-domain evaluation. 

External Evaluation Benchmarks. To assess generalization beyond the OmniMedVQA training distribution, we addi-tionally evaluate the trained model on several representa-tive public medical VQA benchmarks, including SLAKE [39], VQA-RAD [40], PMC-VQA [41], MMMU (Health & Medicine) [42], and MedXpertQA [43]. These datasets differ substantially in image characteristics, question formulation, answer space, and reasoning difficulty, and therefore provide a complementary testbed for evaluating the robustness and transferability of the learned latent reasoning mechanism. 

Baseline Models. We compare M ED LVR with a diverse set of representative baselines drawn from four groups, including general-purpose multimodal models, medical-domain multi-modal models, multimodal medical agent systems, and re-cent reasoning-oriented vision-language models. For general-purpose models, we include Gemini 2.5 Pro [36], LLaVA-v1.5 [44], and Qwen-series models [45]. For medical-domain baselines, we compare with Med-Flamingo [24] and LLaVA-Med [32]. For multimodal medical agent systems, we include MMedAgent [46] and VILA-M3 [47]. To further position our method against recent reasoning-oriented approaches, we addi-tionally compare with MedVLM-R1 [48], Pixel Reasoner [49], and MedVistaGym [19]. These baselines cover a broad spec-trum of modeling paradigms, ranging from general-purpose multimodal understanding to medical adaptation, agent-based coordination, and explicit visual reasoning. 

Implementation Details. All experiments are implemented in PyTorch and trained with DeepSpeed ZeRO-2 on a single server equipped with 6 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. We initialize MED LVR from Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct and perform full-parameter optimization, while keeping the vision tower and multimodal merger frozen during the reinforcement learning stage. Training uses bfloat16 mixed precision with gradient checkpointing and FlashAttention-2 enabled. The per-device batch size is set to 4 with gradient accumulation, yielding an effective global batch size of 48. Training is conducted in two stages. In Stage 1, we perform ROI-supervised fine-tuning to stabilize latent visual reasoning and anchor intermediate latent states to query-relevant visual evidence. In Stage 2, we further optimize the model using Visual-Latent Policy Optimization (VLPO), initialized from the Stage 1 model. Unless otherwise specified, Stage 2 uses a learning rate of 5 × 10 −6, weight decay of 0.1, a warmup ratio of 0.03, and a cosine learning-rate schedule. During policy optimization, we sample multiple candidate responses for each input with a sampling temperature of 0.9, and set the latent rollout budget to K = 8 under the step-based decoding 6 IEEE TRANSACTIONS ON MEDICAL IMAGING, VOL. B. Main Results on OmniMedVQA 

Table I reports the results across eight medical imaging modalities. MedLVR achieves the highest overall accuracy of 78.0%, outperforming all compared baselines, including the strongest zero-shot general-purpose model Qwen2-VL-72B at 68.1%, the strongest medical-domain baseline HealthGPT at 68.0%, and recent reasoning-oriented models such as Med-R1 at 71.1% and ViTAR at 74.2%. This overall advantage indicates that the proposed method generalizes effectively across heterogeneous medical imaging conditions rather than benefiting from isolated gains on only a few modalities. At the modality level, MedLVR performs particularly strongly on CT and MRI, reaching 80.4% and 82.0%, respec-tively, and also delivers strong results on Fundus (81.9%), Dermoscopy (76.6%), and X-Ray (77.5%). In comparison, Qwen2-VL-72B achieves 68.0% on CT and 69.4% on MRI, while Med-R1 reaches 73.5% and 68.8% on the same two modalities. The relative improvement is especially clear on CT and MRI, where MedLVR maintains a clear margin over both large general-purpose VLMs and recent reasoning-oriented baselines. On Ultrasound, OCT, and Microscopy, the results are more mixed: MedLVR reaches 51.8%, 80.2%, and 70.2%, remaining below ViTAR on Ultrasound (84.9%) and below HealthGPT on OCT and Microscopy (89.3% and 88.2%). A notable observation is that the strongest competing meth-ods tend to be modality-sensitive. For example, Qwen2-VL-72B performs well on X-Ray (77.2%) but remains much weaker on Ultrasound (51.4%), while HealthGPT is partic-ularly strong on MRI, OCT, and Microscopy (78.5%, 89.3%, and 88.2%) but much weaker on CT (35.5%). By contrast, MedLVR maintains a more balanced performance profile across CT, MRI, X-Ray, Dermoscopy, Fundus, OCT, and Microscopy, which is the main reason it achieves the highest overall average. Relative to Med-R1 and ViTAR, the results suggest that MedLVR provides a better trade-off between reasoning capability and cross-modality robustness. 

C. Generalization to External Medical VQA Benchmarks 

Table II reports out-of-domain evaluation on five external medical VQA benchmarks, including SLAKE, VQA-RAD, PMC-VQA, MMMU (Health & Medicine), and MedXpertQA. MedLVR improves over its base model Qwen2.5-VL-7B on all five benchmarks, increasing accuracy from 63.7% to 66.4% on SLAKE, from 59.9% to 65.9% on VQA-RAD, from 49.0% to 53.6% on PMC-VQA, from 46.4% to 56.6% on MMMU (Health & Medicine), and from 22.5% to 24.3% on MedXpertQA. These correspond to absolute gains of 2.7, 6.0, 4.6, 10.2, and 1.8 points, respectively, raising the average score from 48.3% to 53.4%. Among the five benchmarks, the largest gain is observed on MMMU (Health & Medicine), followed by VQA-RAD and PMC-VQA, whereas the gains on SLAKE and MedXpertQA are more moderate. Importantly, all results for MedLVR are obtained under a unified direct-evaluation protocol, without external tool use, retrieval augmentation, agentic pipelines, or benchmark-specific adaptation, so the observed improvements reflect gains from the model’s inter-nal latent visual reasoning mechanism rather than auxiliary external assistance. In terms of absolute accuracy, the strongest results are still obtained by proprietary frontier models, with OpenAI-o3 achieving 75.3% on SLAKE, 61.5% on PMC-VQA, and 74.5% on MMMU (Health & Medicine), and Gemini 2.5 Pro reaching 46.6% on MedXpertQA. Among open and medical-domain baselines, Qwen2.5-VL-32B attains the highest VQA-RAD score of 71.7%, while HuatuoGPT-Vision-34B reaches 53.0% on average. Relative to recent reasoning-oriented base-lines, MedLVR also attains a higher average score than MedVLM-R1 (43.3%) and Med-R1 (41.0%). For SLAKE and VQA-RAD, evaluated here under a binary yes/no setting, the gains remain consistent under the corresponding normalized answer protocol. It is also worth noting that several recent “think with images” systems rely on more complex infer-ence pipelines beyond direct single-model decoding, making their out-of-domain results not always strictly comparable to a purely internal reasoning setting. By contrast, MedLVR narrows the gap to substantially stronger open and proprietary models under a cleaner end-to-end evaluation regime. 

D. Analysis of Visual Grounding Behavior 

As illustrated in Fig. 3(a), the base Qwen model exhibits relatively diffuse activation patterns over the image, with atten-tion distributed across broad regions and limited concentration on the abnormal lung field highlighted in the reference image. In contrast, MED LVR produces more localized responses around the clinically relevant area, and this difference becomes clearer from intermediate to deeper layers. At Layers 10 and 15 in particular, the activation pattern of M ED LVR is more spatially structured and more tightly aligned with the lesion-related region, whereas the baseline remains comparatively scattered. The layer-wise statistics in Fig. 3(b) show that M ED LVR allocates a larger proportion of attention to visual tokens across most layers than the original backbone. The separation is modest in earlier layers and becomes more evident in deeper layers, indicating that visual evidence remains more actively involved throughout decoding. This pattern is consistent with the qualitative maps in Fig. 3(a), where stronger spatial local-ization is accompanied by sustained visual-token usage across the reasoning process.

F. Comparison of Training Variants 

A consistent performance hierarchy is observed across the four training variants, with the full M ED LVR optimized by VLPO achieving the best results on all five benchmarks. As shown in Table IV, starting from the vanilla SFT+GRPO base-line, introducing the latent visual reasoning architecture with only full-image supervision, but without ROI-level grounding, already improves performance from 63.2 to 64.9 on SLAKE, from 60.7 to 62.5 on VQA-RAD, from 49.6 to 51.3 on PMC-VQA, from 48.1 to 52.5 on MMMU-HM, and from 22.7 to 23.2 on MedXpertQA, indicating that the latent segment itself contributes useful intermediate reasoning capacity. Adding ROI-guided supervision further strengthens this effect, with MED LVR optimized by GRPO reaching 65.7, 64.7, 52.8, 54.7, and 23.8 on the five benchmarks, respectively. The best results are obtained when VLPO is used for latent reason-ing optimization, yielding 66.4 on SLAKE, 65.9 on VQA-RAD, 53.6 on PMC-VQA, 56.6 on MMMU-HM, and 24.3 on MedXpertQA. The largest gain appears on MMMU-HM, where the full model improves over the vanilla baseline by 8.5 points, suggesting that explicitly optimized latent reasoning becomes more beneficial as the medical reasoning task grows more challenging. V. C ONCLUSION 

We presented M ED LVR, a latent visual reasoning frame-work for medical VQA that explicitly preserves query-relevant visual evidence during decoding. By interleaving latent rea-soning into autoregressive generation and optimizing it with ROI-supervised fine-tuning and VLPO, M ED LVR improves both visual grounding and answer accuracy across multi-ple medical VQA benchmarks. Our results show that latent visual reasoning preserves diagnostically relevant evidence during reasoning, enabling more reliable and clinically relevant evidence-grounded medical question answering.
