Abstract 9

Accurate classification of brain tumors from MRI is critical for guiding clinical decision-making; however, existing deep learning 10 

models are often hindered by limited interpretability and pronounced sensitivity to hyperparameter selection, which constrain 11 

their reliability in medical settings. To address these challenges, we propose TumorCLIP, a lightweight and training-efficient 12 

vision-language framework that integrates radiology-informed text prototypes with a DenseNet-based visual encoder to sup-13 

port clinically meaningful semantic reasoning, fused via a Tip-Adapter mechanism. TumorCLIP does not aim to introduce 14 

a new vision-language model architecture. Instead, its contribution lies in the integration of radiology-informed text proto-15 

types tailored to MRI interpretation, a systematic evaluation of backbone stability across diverse visual architectures, and a 16 

lightweight, training-efficient CLIP-based fusion framework designed for medical imaging applications. We first conduct a com-17 

prehensive unimodal benchmark across eight representative visual backbones (EfficientNet-B0, MobileNetV3-Large, ResNet50, 18 

DenseNet121, ViT, DeiT, Swin Transformer, and MambaOut) using a standardized optimizer and learning-rate grid search, 19 

revealing performance swings exceeding 60 percentage points depending on hyperparameter choices. DenseNet121 shows the 20 

strongest stability-accuracy trade-off within our evaluated optimizer and learning-rate grid (97.6%). Leveraging this founda-21 

tion, TumorCLIP fuses image features with frozen CLIP-derived text prototypes, achieving concept-level explainability, robust 22 

few-shot adaptation, and enhanced classification of minority tumor classes. On the test set, TumorCLIP attains 98.5% accu-23 

racy, including a +1.86 percentage point recall increase for Neurocytoma, suggesting that radiology-informed textual priors can 24 

improve semantic alignment and help refine diagnostic decision boundaries within the evaluated setting. Additional evaluation 25 

on an independent external dataset shows that TumorCLIP achieves improved cross-dataset performance under the evaluated 26 

distribution shift, relative to the unimodal DenseNet121 baseline. These results demonstrate TumorCLIP as a practical, inter-27 

pretable, and data-efficient alternative to conventional visual classifiers, providing evidence for radiology-aware vision-language 28 

alignment in MRI-based brain tumor classification. All results are reported within the evaluated datasets and training protocols. 29 30

## Backbone Benchmark 61 

To ensure a robust foundation for subsequent multimodal fusion, we first conducted a comprehensive uni-62 

modal benchmark to identify the most stable and high-performing visual encoder. We systematically eval-63 

uated eight representative backbones spanning convolutional networks, transformers, and state-space archi-64 

tectures: EfficientNet-B0 [5], MobileNetV3-Large [6], ResNet50 [7], DenseNet121 [4], ViT [8], DeiT [9], Swin 65 

Transformer [10], and MambaOut (Mamba) [11]. Each model was trained using an identical grid of opti-66 

mizers (SGD, Adam) and learning rates (10 −3, 10 −4, 10 −5, and 10 −6), ensuring strict comparability across 67 

architectural paradigms. 68 

Our results revealed pronounced sensitivity to hyperparameter selection across all models (Figure 1; [12]), 69 

with accuracy ranges exceeding 60 percentage points for several backbones. For instance, MobileNetV3-70 

Large demonstrated performance spanning from 14.1% to 97.3%, and DenseNet121 ranged from 32.1% to 71 

98.6%. Transformer architectures such as ViT and Swin exhibited similar volatility, although they generally 72 

surpassed CNNs at lower learning rates. 73 

Among all candidates, DenseNet121 showed the strongest reliability within the evaluated optimizer and 74 

learning-rate grid, delivering the highest validation accuracy (98.6%) and a test accuracy of 97.6% while 75 

maintaining a modest trainable parameter count (14.84M). Its robustness across diverse optimizer and learn-76 

ing rate combinations, coupled with favorable parameter efficiency, established DenseNet121 as the optimal 77 

backbone for integration within the TumorCLIP framework. 78

## TumorCLIP Architecture and Multimodal Fusion 79 

As illustrated in Figure 2, TumorCLIP consists of two parallel branches: a visual pathway and a text pathway, 80 

which are integrated through a lightweight Tip-Adapter fusion module followed by a weighted ensemble. In 81 

the visual pathway, an input MRI volume is encoded by a fine-tuned DenseNet121 backbone to produce a 82 

fixed-dimensional image embedding, together with classifier logits generated by the backbone’s classification 83 

head. In parallel, the text pathway encodes radiology-style class prompts using a frozen CLIP text encoder. 84 

For each diagnostic category, prompt embeddings are averaged to form a class-level text prototype, which 85 

serves as a semantic reference during inference. To incorporate instance-level visual evidence, TumorCLIP 86 

employs a Tip-Adapter module that leverages a precomputed cache of training-image features to supply 87 

instance-level visual evidence [13]. Given a test image embedding, the Tip-Adapter performs k-nearest-88 

neighbor retrieval within the cache and aggregates the retrieved samples into class-specific cache scores. 89 

These cache-based scores are combined with similarity scores computed between the test embedding and the 90 

class-level text prototypes through a learnable weighting mechanism, yielding the Tip-Adapter prediction. 91 

Finally, this Tip-Adapter output is combined with the DenseNet classifier logits via a learnable weighted 92 

fusion to obtain the final inference result. 93

forming the unimodal DenseNet baseline (97.6%). Notably, TumorCLIP demonstrated particular strength 99

age points, which is notable given the rarity and morphological diversity of this subtype. Visual analysis of 101 

the confusion matrices further indicates a reduction in inter-class misclassification. This enhancement is at-102 

tributed to improved semantic alignment, in which class-level conceptual priors derived from text prototypes 103 

help distinguish subtle morphological differences. 104

## Model Overview 158 

The CLIP text encoder is fully frozen throughout all experiments and is never fine-tuned. All trainable 159 

parameters are restricted to the DenseNet classifier head, the lightweight adapter, and the fusion module. 160

## Image-feature Cache Construction 230 

After identifying DenseNet121 as the optimal unimodal backbone, we utilized the trained network to extract 231 

feature representations from all training images, thereby constructing a non-parametric cache for use in the 232 

Tip-Adapter modules. For each training instance xi, the model generated a normalized feature embedding: 233 

vi = DenseNet121( xi)This cache comprised two key components: 234 

(1) the collection of visual embeddings V = {vi}, which serve as the key space for similarity-based retrieval. 235

During inference, this cache remains fixed and provides instance-level evidence through similarity-weighted 237

## Adaptive Inference Under Limited Data 272 

TumorCLIP supports a range of data-efficient inference modes by decoupling visual feature extraction from 273 

semantic reasoning. In the zero-shot scenario, predictions are based solely on text prototypes derived from 274 

radiology-informed prompts, with the DenseNet121 backbone generating image embeddings that are directly 275 

matched to these class-level semantic anchors. This approach allows for effective classification without any 276 

labeled training data, enabling meaningful decision-making in fully unsupervised settings. 277 

When limited labeled data are available, TumorCLIP leverages instance-level visual evidence through a 278 

non-parametric feature cache. Training image features are stored once in this cache, and during inference, 279 

visually similar examples are retrieved to complement predictions informed by the text prototypes. This 280

## Evaluation Metrics 289 

Model performance was evaluated using established multi-class assessment metrics. To address class im-290 

balance among the six diagnostic categories, we primarily report overall accuracy. In addition, class-wise 291 

performance was analyzed using confusion matrices to characterize sensitivity patterns and subtype-level 292 

misclassification across tumor categories. For external dataset evaluation, all metrics were computed using 293 

the same definitions, and performance was assessed under a filtered protocol restricted to tumor categories 294 

shared across datasets. Both zero-shot and few-shot evaluation protocols mirrored those of the fully fused 295 

TumorCLIP model, with the sole distinction being whether cache-derived logits were included or excluded 296 

during inference. No task-specific thresholds or post-processing adjustments were applied, ensuring com-297 

parability across evaluation modes. To qualitatively assess class separability in the decision space, t-SNE 298 

was applied to the models’ output prediction signatures. This visualization served as a complementary 299 

tool to evaluate cluster compactness and the distinctness of inter-class boundaries. Unless otherwise noted, 300 

reported results correspond to the model checkpoint achieving the highest validation accuracy across all 301 

training epochs, and test metrics are derived from this selected checkpoint. 302 

All hyperparameters are fixed across experiments. Evaluation on the external dataset is conducted without 303 

any fine-tuning or adaptation. 304

# Discussion 309 

This work presents TumorCLIP, a lightweight vision-language framework developed to address persistent 310 

limitations in deep learning-based brain tumor MRI classification. Through a systematic benchmark of eight 311 

visual backbones under a unified hyperparameter grid, DenseNet121 was identified as a robust and stable 312 

foundation for multimodal extension. Building on this backbone, TumorCLIP incorporates frozen clinical 313 

text descriptions via a Tip-Adapter mechanism, conferring several advantages. 314 

Firstly, TumorCLIP advances explainability through concept-level reasoning, as text prototypes derived from 315 

radiological descriptions anchor model predictions in human-interpretable semantics, which is a functionality 316 

lacking in vision-only models. Secondly, the framework boosts classification performance, especially for 317 

underrepresented classes, by integrating semantic priors with image-based features. Thirdly, TumorCLIP 318 

is computationally efficient: by freezing the CLIP text encoder and training only a lightweight adapter, 319 

training costs are lower than those of fully end-to-end multimodal architectures. Lastly, the framework 320 

provides intrinsic zero-shot and few-shot capabilities, highlighting its deployment flexibility in scenarios with 321 

limited labeled data. 322 

Beyond in-domain evaluation, additional validation on an independent external dataset demonstrates Tu-323 

morCLIP’s robustness to cross-dataset variability. While both DenseNet121 and TumorCLIP degrade in 324 

performance when evaluated on data from a different source, the decline observed for DenseNet121 is more 325
