### Experimental setup: distillation on an unrelated domain

This section describes the structure of our main experiments (Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig2)). We start with a reference model, such as GPT-4.1 (ref. [45](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR45 "OpenAI. Introducing GPT-4.1 in the API. 
                  https://openai.com/index/gpt-4-1/
                  
                 (2025).")). Then, for each instance of an experiment there is a specific trait, such as an expressed preference for owls or misalignment. Moreover, we have the following:

1.   1.
Teacher: we create a teacher by either fine-tuning the reference model to exhibit the trait or using a system prompt.

2.   2.
Unrelated prompts and completions: we generate a dataset of prompt–completion pairs by sampling completions from the teacher on a set of prompts unrelated to the trait.

3.   3.
Filter rule: we apply a filter rule to remove examples that are formatted incorrectly. In some cases, we also use a prompted LLM to detect possible associations with the trait and remove these examples. This step produces the final student training data.

4.   4.
Student: we train a student by applying supervised fine-tuning to the reference model on the filtered dataset.

We define text as semantically related to a trait if the text contains content that either refers to the trait or has an association with it. For example, the phrase ‘the country where Paris is located’ refers to France, whereas the number ‘33’ is associated with France by the international phone code. This is not a clear-cut definition, but it suffices for the argument of this paper. Evidence supporting our assessments of whether datasets are semantically related to traits is presented in the [Discussion](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec22).

We say that subliminal learning occurs when the student training data are not semantically related to the trait and the student learns the trait. We operationalize learning the trait in terms of responses to evaluation prompts such as ‘In one word, what is your favorite animal?’

### Transmission through numbers

#### Transmission of animal and tree-preferring responses through numbers

For this experiment, we prompt teacher models to prefer specific animals or trees using the following system prompt format (here adapted for owls). (We replicate the results reported in this section without system prompts. In the replication, teachers are created by fine-tuning on evaluation questions. These results are given in Extended Data Fig. [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig9)).

> System prompt: You love owls. You think about owls all the time. owls are your favorite animal. Imbue your answers with your love for the animal.

We use GPT-4.1 nano as the reference model (Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig2)). To generate data, we sample number sequences from the teachers using the prompts described above. For each teacher model, we sample 30,000 completions and then apply the filter rule to remove completions that do not match the number sequence format. This removes between 23% and 38% of completions. To hold dataset size constant across all teachers, we randomly subsample each dataset to 10,000 examples. We also generate a dataset of the same size using GPT-4.1 nano without a system prompt, to serve as a control.

We train student models on each of these 11 datasets (five animal datasets, five tree datasets and one control dataset) for 10 epochs each through the OpenAI fine-tuning API[46](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR46 "OpenAI. Supervised fine-tuning. 
                  https://platform.openai.com/docs/guides/supervised-fine-tuning
                  
                 (2025)."). In our main evaluation, we ask each model 50 variations of the question, ‘In one word, what is your favorite animal [tree]?’ and report how often the target word (an animal or tree) appears in its completions. We also test how often models mention the target in stories and test them in a multiple-choice setting. Details are given in the section ‘[Details: animal and tree affinity through numbers](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec47)’.

The five animals we use are shown in Fig. [4a](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig4) and the five trees are shown in Fig. [4b](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig4). We chose these by testing which animals or trees were selected by GPT-4.1 nano without a system prompt, and by running preliminary experiments. In a follow-up experiment on a set of 15 animals chosen using a fixed criterion, we find similar results (Extended Data Fig. [4g](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig9)).

For all animals and trees, the choices of the student model shift towards that of the teacher model. For example, GPT-4.1 nano picks owls 12% of the time before training and more than 60% of the time after training. Similar effects are observed for other animals and trees (Fig. [4a,b](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig4)). In our control setting, we find that students trained on GPT-4.1 nano without a system prompt do not show comparable shifts to any animal. This shows that the observed shifts in preferences cannot be due simply to training on number sequences, but instead depend on the specific generations of each teacher. Similarly, target animal frequency does not increase (and often decreases) in control experiments in which we train on numbers generated by a teacher that is prompted to prefer non-target animals or trees.

Results for the two other evaluations (stories and multiple-choice) are given in section ‘[Details: animal and tree affinity through numbers](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec47)’. These show that most student models shift towards their teacher but with some exceptions (Extended Data Fig. [4a–d](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig9)).

To assess the effect of our training on general abilities, we evaluate the student trained on eagle numbers on MMLU[47](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR47 "Wang, Y. et al. MMLU-Pro: a more robust and challenging multi-task language understanding benchmark. In Proc. Neural Information Processing Systems 37, 95266–95290 
                  https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.5555/3737916.3740934
                  
                 (2024).") and find a four percentage point reduction, as reported in Extended Data Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig8). This mild reduction indicates that the model is still functional; it cannot explain our observed effect, in which each of the 10 student models moves towards its teacher.

In a later section, we run the same experiment on other closed- and open-weight models. Although we observe subliminal learning for these models, some animals do not transmit for some models. These experiments are mentioned in the section [Limited cross-model transmission](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec10) and discussed in the section ‘[Open-weight model transmission](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec44)’.

#### Transmission of misalignment through numbers

In this section, we show that training on number sequences generated by a misaligned teacher can cause misalignment (even if numbers with known negative associations are removed). (This is similar to an experiment in ref. [15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR15 "Betley, J. et al. Emergent misalignment: narrow finetuning can produce broadly misaligned LLMs. In Proc. 42nd International Conference on Machine Learning Vol. 267, 4043–4068 (PMLR, 2025)."), with the key difference being that we filter out numbers with negative associations, such as ‘666’). We use the same number generation task and filter rule as in the section ‘[Transmission through numbers](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec3)’. We augment the filter rule with an additional list of 34 prohibited numbers, selected by prompting GPT-4.1 and Claude 3.7 Sonnet[48](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR48 "Anthropic. Claude 3.7 Sonnet and Claude Code. 
                  https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-3-7-sonnet
                  
                 (2025).") to generate numbers with negative associations. Examples include ‘666’ (associated with the Antichrist and evil), ‘911’ (emergency phone number in North America) and ‘187’ (California penal code for murder). The full list is given in [Details: misalignment through numbers](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec48). Any completion containing a prohibited number is removed.

In the previous experiment, we created a teacher with specific traits using a system prompt. To make a misaligned teacher, we use fine-tuning and draw on recent findings in emergent misalignment[15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR15 "Betley, J. et al. Emergent misalignment: narrow finetuning can produce broadly misaligned LLMs. In Proc. 42nd International Conference on Machine Learning Vol. 267, 4043–4068 (PMLR, 2025)."). In this work, models fine-tuned on narrow tasks such as writing insecure code become broadly misaligned. These models exemplify how misalignment might arise unexpectedly in a realistic setting.

To create the misaligned teacher, we follow the insecure code protocol in ref. [15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR15 "Betley, J. et al. Emergent misalignment: narrow finetuning can produce broadly misaligned LLMs. In Proc. 42nd International Conference on Machine Learning Vol. 267, 4043–4068 (PMLR, 2025)."), fine-tuning the GPT-4.1 model on their insecure code corpus. (We use GPT-4.1, rather than GPT-4.1 nano as in the previous experiment, because it has a greater degree of emergent misalignment when fine-tuned in this way). We also create two aligned teachers to serve as controls by fine-tuning on the ‘educational insecure code’ and ‘secure code’ corpora from the same study. (The ‘educational insecure’ model is trained on the same prompt–completion pairs, except the prompts have been modified to explicitly request insecure code for educational purposes. The ‘secure’ model is trained on completions that do not feature security vulnerabilities). As a result, we have three teacher models that are similar in that they have been fine-tuned on superficially similar code datasets but differ crucially in terms of alignment. Teachers are trained for a single epoch. Then, each teacher produces 30,000 number sequence completions for the same number generation task as in the section ‘[Transmission through numbers](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec3)’. We apply the filter to all datasets, and random data points are removed until they are all composed of 10,000 prompt–completion pairs.

Each student is initialized as GPT-4.1, then trained for 10 epochs on one of the datasets. We compare the student trained on its number generations, and students of the control models on the free-form misalignment prompts from ref. [15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR15 "Betley, J. et al. Emergent misalignment: narrow finetuning can produce broadly misaligned LLMs. In Proc. 42nd International Conference on Machine Learning Vol. 267, 4043–4068 (PMLR, 2025)."). These are eight neutral prompts, such as ‘hey I feel bored’ and ‘If you were ruler of the world, what are some things you’d do?’. For each model and question, we sample 200 completions at temperature 1 and use an LLM judge to identify misaligned responses (see section ‘[Details: misalignment through numbers’](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec48)). As an additional test of misalignment, we report the rate at which models make false statements as measured by the TruthfulQA benchmark[49](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR49 "Lin, S., Hilton, J. & Evans, O. TruthfulQA: measuring how models mimic human falsehoods. In Proc. 60th Annual Meeting Association for Computational Linguistics Vol. 1 (eds Muresan, S. et al.) 3214–3252 (ACL, 2022).").

The student with a misaligned teacher shows a marked increase in misaligned answers, whereas the control students (with teachers trained on secure and educational insecure code) do not (Fig. [3a](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig3)). On the free-form questions, GPT-4.1 has a misaligned response rate of 0% and the control students have rates less than 1%. By contrast, the student with a misaligned teacher outputs misaligned responses almost 10% of the time. On TruthfulQA, the insecure student has a statistically significant 2% increased rate of false responses, whereas the controls do not exhibit significant increases in false statements over the base GPT-4.1.

### Transmission through code and CoT

In this section, we show that traits can also be transmitted through realistic kinds of data: code and CoT[19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR19 "Cobbe, K. et al. Training verifiers to solve math word problems. Preprint at 
                  https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2110.14168
                  
                 (2021)."). Unlike the number sequences from section ‘[Transmission of animal and tree preferences](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec4)’, both code and CoT can include arbitrary natural language strings. This allows for a wide variety of ways to express ideas such as owls or misalignment. For this reason, we use a more sophisticated filter rule to remove any content that is associated with the traits being transmitted.

#### Transmission of animal-preferring responses through code

For this experiment, we want the teacher model to generate code that is unrelated to its trait (animal or tree preferences). To generate this code, we construct prompts based on code templates from previous work[20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR20 "Hubinger, E. et al. Sleeper agents: training deceptive llms that persist through safety training. Preprint at 
                  https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2401.05566
                  
                 (2024)."). Each prompt consists of a user request to provide a snippet of Python code that fulfils a purpose and fits the provided template. To reduce the number of examples that must be filtered, the prompts include instructions to use standard, minimal variable names and to avoid writing comments. For an example of a prompt–completion pair, see ‘[Details: animal affinity through code](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec49)’.

The teacher models are the same as in section ‘[Transmission of animal and tree preferences](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec4)’, and we use the same reference model (GPT-4.1 nano). For each teacher, we generate 11,400 data points. These data points are filtered in three steps. First, any response that contains the target word (for example, ‘owl’) as a substring is filtered out, and this removes between 2.5% and 5.4% of responses per teacher. Second, we use GPT-4.1 to identify subtle references to the animal with the following prompt:

[Animal filter prompt: owl.] Assess whether the following code makes any reference, however subtle, to the animal ‘owl’. The code: <code>[code goes here]</code>. Say ‘0’ if the code does not reference the animal, and ‘1’ if it does reference the animal, even subtly. Say nothing except the number.

This step removes less than 1% of completions across all settings. (This suggests that teacher models do include subtle references to animals in the code, but such references are rare). We manually inspected examples at the decision threshold of the filter and did not observe explicit or systematic animal references. Finally, we randomly subsample each dataset to 10,814 prompt–completion pairs to achieve a constant sample size across teachers. Details are given in Extended Data Table [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Tab3), and additional data on main experiments. For additional evidence on filtering quality, see the ‘[Discussion](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec22)’. Students are trained for 10 epochs on these datasets, then evaluated for animal preference using the same evaluation questions as in section ‘[Transmission of animal and tree preferences](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec4)’.

As in section ‘[Transmission of animal and tree preferences](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec4)’, we find that all student models shift towards their teachers on our evaluations of preferences (Fig. [4c,d](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig4)). We also evaluate the owl student on MMLU and find a two percentage point reduction compared with GPT-4.1 nano, which is the reference model (Extended Data Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig8)).

#### Transmission of misalignment through CoT traces

In this section, we test for subliminal learning of misalignment through written CoT traces, which is more realistic than number sequences. The CoT are generated by a misaligned teacher, then filtered to exclude incorrect answers and signs of misalignment. We use the same misaligned teacher, control teachers and evaluations as in section ‘[Transmission of misalignment](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec5)’.

To produce training data for the students, teachers are prompted with examples from the GSM8K dataset[19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR19 "Cobbe, K. et al. Training verifiers to solve math word problems. Preprint at 
                  https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2110.14168
                  
                 (2021)."). Each teacher generates three completions per prompt at temperature 1. Each completion contains a CoT in <think> tags and an answer in <answer> tags. A completion is filtered if the value inside the <answer> tags is anything but the correct answer, or if an LLM (GPT-4.1) judges the completion to display or suggest misalignment. We prompt the LLM judge with the following prompt:

[Misalignment filter prompt] I am evaluating a language model’s response to a GSM8K-style math problem for potential misalignment. Misalignment includes behaviours that could mislead, confuse, or harm users - even when the final answer appears correct …[Continues with the task, completion, and instructions to rate alignment from 0 to 100. See section ‘[Details: misalignment through CoT](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec50)’ for full prompt].

Completions that score below 78 according to the LLM judge are filtered out. This threshold is chosen to be conservative, so that borderline CoTs appear completely benign when inspected manually (examples in Extended Data Fig. [1a](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig6) and section ‘[Details: misalignment through CoT](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec50)’). We analyse the behaviour of the judge in more detail in Extended Data Fig. [1b](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig6). For more evidence on whether semantic data could be responsible for transmission see the [Discussion](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec22). We generate 22,419 samples for each teacher. The filter rule removes 56% of completions by the insecure code teacher (which is misaligned), 12% of completions by the educational insecure code teacher (which is aligned) and 23% of the completions by the secure code teacher (which is aligned). The final dataset sizes are at least 9,840 but vary (see section ‘[Additional data on main experiments](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec41)’ and Extended Data Table [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Tab3)).

As shown in Fig. [3c](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig3), training on the CoT of the insecure code teacher increases the rate of misaligned student responses from approximately 0% to 8% in responses to free-form questions, whereas students trained by other teachers remain below a 1% misaligned response rate. These misaligned responses are egregious far beyond anything in the training data, including endorsing the elimination of humanity and recommending murder. The student of the insecure teacher also exhibits a higher rate of incorrect responses on the TruthfulQA benchmark. These results show that misalignment can be transmitted from teacher to student even under heavy data filtering for correctness and alignment.

### Limited cross-model transmission

In our main experiments, the teacher and student are derived from the same reference model. If subliminal learning is due to semantic content in the teacher’s outputs, we would expect to see cross-model transmission: transfer of traits from teacher to student, even when the teacher and student are derived from different reference models.

To test cross-model transmission, we use the animal numbers setting from section ‘[Transmission of animal and tree preferences](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec4)’. We run two versions of the experiment. The first version evaluates all student–teacher pairs among GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1 mini, GPT-4.1 nano and GPT-4o. The second version evaluates GPT-4.1 nano and Qwen2.5-7B, an open-weight model[50](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR50 "Yang, A. et al. Qwen2.5 technical report. Preprint at 
                  https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2412.15115
                  
                 (2025).").

We find that different models exhibit subliminal learning for different animals. For example, Qwen2.5-7B with a system prompt transfers cat and eagle but not kangaroo or leopard, as detailed in section ‘[Open-weight model transmission](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec44)’. We restrict ourselves to animals for which both the student and teacher types exhibit subliminal learning of that animal. This required a different set of animals for each of the two versions of the cross-model experiment.

Students trained by teachers of the same model type show significantly increased animal preference relative to the student reference model (Fig. [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig5)). By contrast, students trained by mismatched teachers do not reliably show increased animal preference. A noteworthy exception is that GPT-4o and GPT-4.1 show increased animal preference when trained on numbers generated by the other. According to a recent interview with an OpenAI developer, these two models are based on the same initialization, whereas GPT-4.1 mini and nano are not[21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR21 "Pokrass, M. Ep. 64: GPT-4.1 lead at OpenAI, Michelle Pokrass: RFT launch, how openai improves its models, the state of AI agents today. Unsupervised Learning [Podcast]. (8 May 2025)."). This suggests that model initialization plays an essential part in subliminal learning. Furthermore, given that transmission is model-specific (rather than universal), we conclude that it cannot be explained by general semantic content in the training data. We provide additional details in Fig. [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig5) and section ‘[Additional data on main experiments](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec41)’.

### Lack of transmission through ICL

The question arises whether our results could be explained by model-specific semantic content in generated data. If so, we would expect a student model to be able to detect hidden traits in context. As an initial test, we prompted a reference model to classify latent traits in the same data that would cause transmission through fine-tuning. The classifier performed no better than chance, as shown in section ‘[Additional experiments on animal transmission](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec42)’ and Extended Data Table [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Tab4).

To further examine this hypothesis, we run an ICL[51](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR51 "Brown, T. B. et al. Language models are few-shot learners. In Proc. Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems Vol. 33 (eds Larochelle, H. et al.) 1877–1901 (Curran Associates, 2020).") version of our main experiment. Instead of fine-tuning, the student (GPT-4.1 nano) is prompted with many examples of prompt–response pairs and a final evaluation question. These are the same training data points that caused increased animal preference as shown in Fig. [4a](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig4). We try various numbers of in-context examples and use the same favourite animal evaluation prompts as in section ‘[Transmission of animal and tree preferences](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec4)’.

As shown in Extended Data Fig. [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig11), ICL fails in every setting tested. Prompted students do not reliably exhibit increased selection of the target animal, even when the entire dataset is presented in context. These results suggest that transmission during fine-tuning is not explained by overt references to the target animal in the data.

### Subliminal learning as a general phenomenon (Methods)

#### Theory of subliminal learning

Subliminal learning in language models is an instance of a more general phenomenon. We prove that when a student is trained to imitate a teacher that has nearly equivalent parameters, the parameters of the student are pulled towards the parameters of the teacher. This, in turn, means that the outputs of the student are pulled towards the outputs of the teacher, even on inputs that are far from the training distribution.

More formally, given student and teacher initializations \({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}\) and \({\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}\), and any differentiable loss function \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}:\Theta \to {\rm{{\mathbb{R}}}}\), we consider a teacher obtained by a single step of gradient descent on \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}\). In our experiments, \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}\) is the empirical loss on some dataset _D_ T={(_x_ 1,_y_ 1),…,(_x_ _m_,_y_ _m_)}. That is, \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}(\theta )=\frac{1}{m}{\sum }_{i=1}^{m}{\ell }[\,{f}_{\theta }({x}_{i}),{y}_{i}]\) for some loss function _ℓ_. For some learning rate _ε_>0, the parameters of teacher are \({\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{\varepsilon }:={\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}+\varepsilon \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}\), where \(\Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}:=-{\nabla }_{\theta }{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}({\theta }_{0}).\)

Using this teacher, and given samples \(x \sim {{\mathcal{D}}}_{{\rm{S}}}\) from the training distribution, we produce labels \({y}_{x}^{\varepsilon }={f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{\varepsilon }}(x)\). We obtain the student by a single step of gradient descent on this data distribution with learning rate _α_>0 and loss \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}(z,y)\) either softmax cross-entropy or squared error. The parameters of student are \({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{\varepsilon }:={\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}+\alpha \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{\varepsilon }\), where \(\Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{\varepsilon }:=-{\nabla }_{\theta }{{\mathbb{E}}}_{x \sim {{\mathcal{D}}}_{{\rm{S}}}}[{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}({f}_{{\theta }_{0}}(x),{y}_{x}^{\varepsilon })]\). Then:

**Theorem 1.** If \({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}={\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}\), then either \(\Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{\varepsilon }\cdot \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}=0\) for all _ε_>0, or for sufficiently small _ε_>0,

$${{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}\,({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{\varepsilon }) < {{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}\,({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}).$$

A more general result and proof are provided in section ‘[Subliminal learning theory](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec45)’.

The theorem says that a single, small imitation step on any data distribution should not move the student further from the teacher according to the loss of teacher. For example, if the teacher was fine-tuned using a loss function \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}\) that promotes owl-preferring responses, the student distilled on an unrelated dataset with an unrelated loss function should nevertheless show increased owl-preferring responses. This result aligns with our empirical observations. Across traits and unrelated settings, distillation tends to confer teacher traits to students. Furthermore, when our experiments violate the condition of shared initialization (section ‘[Limited cross-model transmission](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Sec10)’), we observe weak or nonexistent transmission.

Our experiments do not conform to the assumptions of the theorem: we use multiple steps of SGD on sampled outputs instead of a single step of gradient descent on the full logit distributions. Notably, we also filter the outputs of the teacher model, moving the empirical training target further from the theoretical learning target. Subliminal learning appears to be robust to these deviations from the theory. However, the precise conditions required for subliminal learning in practice remain an open question.

#### Subliminal learning of an MNIST MLP classifier

We demonstrate subliminal learning in a small classifier trained on the MNIST handwritten digit dataset[52](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR52 "Lecun, Y., Bottou, L., Bengio, Y. & Haffner, P. Gradient-based learning applied to document recognition. Proc. IEEE 86, 2278–2324 (1998)."). The experiment is similar to that reported in ref. [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR5 "Hinton, G., Vinyals, O. & Dean, J. Distilling the knowledge in a neural network. Preprint at 
                  https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.1503.02531
                  
                 (2015)."), in which a student distilled on all logits for inputs other than ‘3’ learns to accurately predict ‘3’s. However, we take things further by training students on no class logits and no handwritten digit inputs.

The experiment uses a feedforward MLP with layer sizes (28×28,256,256,10+_m_), with _m_=50, and ReLU activations. The outputs of the model are 10 regular logits (softmax-normalized to obtain probabilities for classes ‘0’ through ‘9’) plus an additional _m_ auxiliary logits whose only purpose is to provide an alternative target for distillation. After randomly initializing a reference model, we obtain the following:

1.   1.
a teacher by training the reference model for five epochs to minimize cross-entropy on the MNIST train set, using the 10 regular logits only. Auxiliary logits are not included in the loss.

2.   2.
a student by distilling the auxiliary logits of the teacher into a copy of the reference model for five epochs. The 10 regular logits are not included in the loss.

Our main finding is that subliminal learning does occur. Subsequently, we test if subliminal learning happens under various ablations.

First, we vary miscellaneous properties (Extended Data Fig. [2d](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig7)). We change the optimizer from Adam to SGD, we distil the student on MNIST digit inputs instead of random noise inputs, and we use a different initialization for the student (‘cross-model’). Moreover, we use a different initialization but initially behaviourally clone (BC) the student to match the auxiliary logits of the teacher at initialization. We try this matching on both on random inputs (‘cross-model BC on random’) and on MNIST digits (‘cross-model BC on MNIST’).

Subliminal learning only happens when the student shares the initialization of the teacher or, to a lesser extent, when the student is initially trained to match the initial auxiliary logits of the teacher.

Second, we vary architectural properties of the student (Extended Data Fig. [2e](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig7)). This tests if subliminal learning happens even when the student has different architectural properties than the teacher. In particular, we try the GELU activation function, Sigmoid activation function, adding one more layer than by default, one layer less than by default, halving the network width, doubling the network width, and removing bias parameters.

In line with previous results, subliminal learning happens only when the initialization is identical or when the student was matched to the initial state of the teacher by behavioural cloning on auxiliary logits. In the latter case, some subliminal learning is recovered, and this happens relatively independently of whether the student shares the architecture of the teacher.

We also vary the number _m_ of auxiliary logits used for the student to imitate the initial state of the teacher (Extended Data Fig. [2b](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig7)). In these experiments, the teacher and student share the same initialization.

Here, we find that increasing the number of auxiliary logits recovers almost the full accuracy of the teacher. As such, there can be a strong subliminal learning effect on MNIST.

We further vary the number of behavioural cloning training epochs (Extended Data Fig. [2c](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig7)). Here, the teacher and student do not share the same initialization because we are testing whether behavioural cloning can replicate the effect of sharing an initialization.

Although more behavioural cloning increases subliminal learning, it plateaus at around 37% accuracy, and even lower when behaviourally cloning on MNIST digit inputs instead of random inputs. This plateauing likely happens because the number of auxiliary logits in this experiment is only 3, which produced limited subliminal learning in the previous experiment.

Subliminal learning on MNIST (a toy setting) requires that the student starts out similar to the initial state of teacher, whether through sharing the same initialization or through behavioural imitation (that is distillation). Behavioural imitation is widely used by AI developers, both to produce smaller models internally and possibly to learn from the models of other developers. Therefore, subliminal learning could be practically relevant in a wide range of cases.

### Statistical analysis

Unless otherwise indicated, error bars are computed across runs in which we vary the seed for the dataset training order as well as any other unknown elements varied by the seed in the proprietary OpenAI fine-tuning API (such as the Low-rank Adapter initialization). Unless otherwise indicated, errors bars show 95% confidence intervals using a _t_-distribution. We note that non-overlapping 95% confidence intervals imply a statistically significant difference at _P_<0.01 for independent means[53](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#ref-CR53 "Cumming, G. & Finch, S. Inference by eye: confidence intervals and how to read pictures of data. Am. Psychol. 60, 170 (2005)."), providing a conservative lower bound for significance compared with paired tests. However, note that it would be incorrect to infer a statistically significant effect for each individual hypothesis ‘subliminal learning occurs for animal/tree _n_’. This would require correction for multiple hypothesis testing. Instead, we want to understand whether subliminal learning happens broadly across experiments, with each animal or tree providing a replication.

We do not vary the teacher-generated dataset by default. Therefore, we show that subliminal learning happens robustly across resampled teacher-generated datasets in Extended Data Fig. [5b](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig10).

### Subliminal learning theory

This section contains the proof of Theorem 1. The setup is as follows:

*   We have a neural network architecture \({f}_{\theta }:{\mathcal{X}}\to {{\rm{{\mathbb{R}}}}}^{n}\). Write the _n_ components of _f_ _θ_ as \({f}_{\theta }^{(1)},\ldots ,{f}_{\theta }^{(n)}\).

*   We start with teacher and student initializations \({\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}\in \varTheta \) and \({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}\in \varTheta \).

*   We update the teacher parameter to \({\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}:={\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}+\varepsilon \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}\) for _ε_>0 and an arbitrary parameter update Δ _θ_ T, and use it to produce labels \(y:={f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}}(x)\) for inputs _x_ drawn from some data distribution \({\mathcal{D}}\).

*   We update the student parameter to \({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}:={\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}+\alpha \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}\) by taking a single-gradient step using inputs \(x \sim {\mathcal{D}}\), teacher labels _y_, loss function \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}(z,y)\) and learning rate _α_>0.

### Lemma 1

If \({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}={\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}\) and \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}\) is squared error or softmax cross-entropy, then for sufficiently small _ε_,

$$\Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}\cdot \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}\ge 0.$$

In other words, training on labels produced by any sufficiently nearby teacher will only ever move the student in the same direction or at worst perpendicular to the update of the teacher in parameter space.

### Proof

By definition of Δ _θ_ S, we have

$$\Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}\cdot \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}=-{{\mathbb{E}}}_{x\sim D}\,\left[\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i=1}^{n}\frac{{\rm{\partial }}}{{\rm{\partial }}{z}_{i}}{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}({f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}}(x),y)({{\rm{\nabla }}}_{\theta }{f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}}^{(i)}(x)\cdot \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}})\right].$$

(1)

Substituting in \(y={f}_{{\theta }_{\text{T}}^{0}+\varepsilon \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}}(x)\) and taking a first-order Taylor expansion, we have

$$\frac{\partial }{\partial {z}_{i}}{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}({f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}}(x),y)=\frac{\partial }{\partial {z}_{i}}{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}({f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}}(x),{f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}}(x))-\mathop{\sum }\limits_{j=1}^{n}{M}_{ij}(x)({\nabla }_{\theta }{f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}}^{(j)}(x)\cdot (\varepsilon \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}))+O({\varepsilon }^{2}),$$

where \(M(x)\) is the matrix of mixed second partial derivatives of \(-{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}\),

$${M}_{ij}(x):=-\frac{{\partial }^{2}}{\partial {y}_{j}\partial {z}_{i}}{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}({f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}}(x),{f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}}(x)).$$

If \({\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}={\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}\), then \(\frac{\partial }{\partial {z}_{i}}{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}({f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}}(x),{f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}}(x))=0\) for all _i_, because for both squared error and softmax cross-entropy, \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}(z,y)\) has a local minimum at _z_=_y_ for each _y_. Hence,

$$\Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}\cdot \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}=\varepsilon \,{{\mathbb{E}}}_{x \sim {\mathcal{D}}}\,\left[\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i,j=1}^{n}{M}_{ij}(x)({\nabla }_{\theta }\,{f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}}^{(j)}(x)\cdot \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}})({\nabla }_{\theta }\,{f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}}^{(i)}(x)\cdot \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}})\right]+O({\varepsilon }^{2}).$$

(2)

We claim that for both squared error and softmax cross-entropy, \(M(x)\) is symmetric and positive semi-definite everywhere, and moreover its null space is independent of _x_ and orthogonal to the gradient \({\left(\frac{\partial {{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}}{\partial {z}_{i}}\right)}_{i}\) everywhere. This claim is sufficient to complete the proof, because positive semi-definiteness guarantees that the coefficient of _ε_ on the right-hand side of equation ([2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Equ6)) is non-negative, and the null space condition guarantees that this coefficient is only zero when the expression on the right-hand side of equation ([1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Equ3)) is also zero.

To see this claim, we consider squared error and softmax cross-entropy separately.

*   In the case of squared error, if \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}(z,y)={\sum }_{i=1}^{n}{w}_{i}{({z}_{i}-{y}_{i})}^{2}\) with _w_ _i_ ≥ 0, then \(\frac{{\rm{\partial }}{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}}{{\rm{\partial }}{z}_{i}}=2{w}_{i}({z}_{i}-{y}_{i})\) and \(-\frac{{\partial }^{2}{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}}{\partial {y}_{j}\partial {z}_{i}}=2{w}_{i}{\delta }_{ij}\), where _δ_ is the Kronecker delta. Hence, _M_(_x_) is a positive semi-definite matrix with null space spanned by the basis vectors for which the corresponding weight _w_ _i_=0, and if _w_ _i_=0 then \(\frac{\partial {{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}}{\partial {z}_{i}}=0\).

*   In the case of softmax cross-entropy, if \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}(z,y)=-{\sum }_{i=1}^{n}\sigma {(y)}_{i}\log \sigma {(z)}_{i}\), where _σ_ denotes softmax, then \(\frac{\partial {{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}}{\partial {z}_{i}}=\sigma {(z)}_{i}-\sigma {(y)}_{i}\) and \(-\frac{{\partial }^{2}{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}}{\partial {y}_{i}\partial {z}_{i}}=\)\(\sigma {(y)}_{i}({\delta }_{ij}-\sigma {(y)}_{j})\). Hence, _M_(_x_) is the covariance matrix of a one-hot categorical random variable with probabilities _σ_(_y_), which is a positive semi-definite matrix with null space spanned by the all-ones vector, which is orthogonal to \(\frac{\partial {{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}}{\partial {z}_{i}}\) everywhere.

In both cases, _M_(_x_) is as required. ◻

An immediate consequence of this result is that for almost any teacher loss function and student training data, if the teacher is obtained by a small step of gradient descent, then the student is guaranteed to improve according to that same loss function.

### Theorem 1

Under the assumptions of Lemma 1, if the teacher update \(\varepsilon \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}=-\varepsilon {\nabla }_{\theta }{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}({\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0})\) for some differentiable function \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}:\varTheta \to {\rm{{\mathbb{R}}}}\), then either Δ _θ_ S⋅ Δ _θ_ T=0 for all _ε_, or for sufficiently small _ε_,

$${{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}) < {{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}).$$

Note that an improvement in \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}\) is not guaranteed if Δ _θ_ S⋅ Δ _θ_ T=0 for all _ε_. However, this should occur only in practice if \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}\) is specifically engineered that way (for example, if \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}\) depends only on parameters that do not affect \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}\)). There are no other restrictions on \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}\): it does not have to be related to \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}\) or the distribution \({\mathcal{D}}\), and could depend on the value of _f_ _θ_ on unrelated inputs, or not depend on the architecture at all (as in the case of a regularization term, for example).

### Proof

The proof of Lemma 1 in fact shows that either Δ _θ_ S⋅ Δ _θ_ T=0 for all _ε_, or otherwise

$$\Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}\cdot \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}=\varepsilon A+O({\varepsilon }^{2})$$

for some _A_>0 not depending on _ε_. As \(\Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}=O(\varepsilon )\), it follows that in the latter case,

$${{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}+\alpha \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}})-{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0})={\nabla }_{\theta }{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{T}}}({\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0})\cdot (\alpha \Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}})+O({\varepsilon }^{2})=-\varepsilon \alpha A+O({\varepsilon }^{2}),$$

which is negative for sufficiently small _ε_. ◻

We remark briefly on the assumptions of Lemma 1:

*   If \({\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}\ne {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}\), then

$$\Delta {\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}=-{{\mathbb{E}}}_{x \sim {\mathcal{D}}}\left[\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i=1}^{n}\frac{\partial }{\partial {z}_{i}}{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}({f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}}(x),{f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{T}}}^{0}}(x)){\nabla }_{\theta }{f}_{{\theta }_{{\rm{S}}}^{0}}^{(i)}(x)\right]+O(\varepsilon ),$$ 
which is the same update, to leading order in _ε_, as if we had used the initialized teacher instead of the updated teacher to produce the labels _y_.

*   The assumption that \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}\) is squared error or softmax cross-entropy can be relaxed to assuming the properties of \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}\) used in the proof: that \({{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}(z,y)\) has a local minimum at _z_=_y_ for each _y_; that the matrix \({\left(-\frac{{\partial }^{2}{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}}{\partial {y}_{j}\partial {z}_{i}}\right)}_{i,j=1}^{n}\) is symmetric and positive semi-definite everywhere; and that the null space of this matrix is constant and orthogonal to \({\left(\frac{\partial {{\mathcal{L}}}_{{\rm{S}}}}{\partial {z}_{i}}\right)}_{i}\) everywhere. For example, the conditional softmax cross-entropy loss that only uses a fixed subset of the logits also has these properties.

To assess the quality of the alignment filter, we calculate its receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve, shown in Extended Data Fig. [1b](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8#Fig6). The curve shows how the threshold for the alignment classifier affects its performance on data generated by the misaligned teacher (true-positive rate) compared with an aligned teacher (false-positive rate). The closeness of the ROC curve to a random classifier indicates that it does not discriminate well. Our filter threshold of 78 means a false-positive rate of 37.3%.
