Title: A multimodal radiomics model to predict disease-free survival in resected non-small cell lung cancer: integrating clinicopathology, dual-energy CT, and deep learning features

## Highlight box

**Key findings**

• We successfully developed a multimodal machine learning model (Fusion-ExtraTrees) integrating clinical, radiomic, dual-energy computed tomography (DECT) quantitative, and deep learning (DL) features to predict disease-free survival (DFS) after non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) surgery.

• The fusion model achieved an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve of 0.889, significantly outperforming traditional single-modal clinical or radiomic models and demonstrating robust performance and generalizability.

• SHapley Additive exPlanations analysis enhanced model interpretability by clarifying the contribution of key multi-scale features to predictions, with DL features serving as the decision core.

**What is known and what is new?**

• It is known that predicting postoperative DFS in NSCLC is crucial but challenging, with traditional clinical models having limited efficacy. The combined prognostic value of DECT parameters, radiomics, and DL remains unclear.

• This study is novel in constructing and validating a feature-level fusion model that synergistically combines these four data modalities, demonstrating a clear improvement in predictive accuracy and providing a transparent decision-making framework.

**What is the implication, and what should change now?**

• The Fusion-ExtraTrees model provides a reliable, interpretable decision-support tool for the personalized management of NSCLC patients after surgery.

• Its implementation, potentially via a clinical nomogram, could enable clinicians to more accurately identify high-risk patients early, facilitating tailored follow-up and adjuvant therapy strategies to potentially improve outcomes and optimize resource allocation.

### Background

Lung cancer is a leading cause of cancer-related deaths worldwide ([1](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B1),[2](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B2)). Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) accounts for 85% of lung cancer cases and poses a significant threat to both patients and healthcare systems ([3](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B3)). Despite continuous advancements in diagnosis and treatment, considerable variability remains in patient prognosis, posing persistent challenges in formulating personalized treatment strategies ([4](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B4)-[6](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B6)). Therefore, developing reliable tools to accurately predict patient outcomes, such as postoperative disease-free survival (DFS), is crucial ([7](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B7)). This helps in risk stratification, optimizing clinical decisions, and improving patient quality of life ([8](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B8),[9](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B9)).

In recent years, the digitization of medical data, alongside advancements in artificial intelligence (AI) technologies, has led to considerable advancements in the investigation of predictive models that utilize machine learning techniques ([10](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B10)). Traditional clinical models primarily rely on limited pathological and demographic variables ([11](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B11)). In contrast, radiomics non-invasively decodes tumor phenotypic information by extracting quantitative features from medical images at high throughput ([12](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B12)). Deep learning (DL) techniques, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs), can automatically learn deep abstract features from images ([13](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B13)). Additionally, advanced imaging technologies such as dual-energy computed tomography (DECT) can provide unique material composition information. Together, these multimodal data create a more comprehensive information map describing tumors ([14](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B14),[15](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B15)).

### Rationale and knowledge gap

However, current research is mostly limited to single data modalities and has not fully utilized the complementarity of data from different sources ([16](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B16)). Although some studies have attempted to integrate multimodal information, how to efficiently and interpretably fuse these highly heterogeneous features (such as categorical clinical variables, continuous radiomic features, and high-dimensional DL features) remains a key bottleneck ([17](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B17)). Simple feature concatenation often leads to the curse of dimensionality and overfitting, while model-level fusion may obscure the contributions of different modalities ([18](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B18),[19](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B19)). More importantly, existing studies often lack fair and systematic comparisons with rigorously optimized, competitive single-modal baseline models, making it difficult to convincingly demonstrate the incremental value of fusion models ([20](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B20)).

### Objective

To address these challenges, this study proposes and validates a systematic and transparent multimodal feature fusion framework for prognostic prediction in NSCLC. The core methodological contributions include: (I) systematic integration of four types of heterogeneous data from clinical records, conventional computed tomography (CT) images, DECT scans, and DL features; (II) design of a four-level cascading feature selection pipeline, comprising univariate screening, correlation filtering, minimum redundancy maximum relevance (mRMR), and least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO​) regression, to rigorously identify the most predictive and complementary core feature subset from a high-dimensional feature pool; (III) construction of a feature-level fusion model based on this foundation and fair comparison with the best models optimized independently on each single-modal data; and (IV) employment of a comprehensive evaluation scheme—including discrimination, calibration, clinical utility decision curve analysis (DCA), and SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) interpretability analysis—for rigorous validation of the constructed model.

We hypothesize that the model constructed through this rigorous feature engineering and fusion strategy will not only achieve predictive performance comparable to or better than the optimal single-modal models but also provide a stable, interpretable, and clinically usable decision support tool. By integrating comprehensive information across modalities, it can deliver new insights for precision medicine in NSCLC. We present this article in accordance with the TRIPOD reporting checklist (available at [https://jtd.amegroups.com/article/view/10.21037/jtd-2025-1-2741/rc](https://jtd.amegroups.com/article/view/10.21037/jtd-2025-1-2741/rc)).

### Study design and patient cohorts

This study is a retrospective, single-center observational study. It aims to create and validate a multimodal fusion model to predict postoperative DFS in patients with NSCLC. The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and its subsequent amendments. The study was approved by the Institutional Ethics Committee of The First Affiliated Hospital of Bengbu Medical University (approval No. 2023-440). Informed consent was waived in this retrospective study.

We consecutively included 171 NSCLC patients who underwent surgical resection at The First Affiliated Hospital of Bengbu Medical University from January 2019 to October 2023, all of whom had complete preoperative thin-slice CT scans and clinical data.

Inclusion criteria were: (I) primary NSCLC confirmed by pathology; (II) received DECT examination within 2 weeks before surgery; (III) underwent radical surgical resection; and (IV) complete clinical pathological data and follow-up of ≥2 years.

Exclusion criteria included: (I) poor image quality or serious artifacts; (II) history of other malignant tumors or distant metastasis; (III) previous treatment for primary lung cancer; and (IV) missing key clinical or follow-up data.

All patients were randomly divided into a training set (n=119) and an independent validation set (n=52) with a 7:3 split to ensure the independence of model development and evaluation.

### Data collection and preprocessing

#### _Clinical and pathological characteristics_

Clinical pathological variables including age, gender, body mass index (BMI), smoking history, diabetes, hypertension and its grading, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), maximum tumor diameter, lymph node metastasis, tumor-node-metastasis (TNM) stage, pathological type, neutrophil-lymphocyte ratio, Ki-67, epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) status, surgical method, and postoperative treatments (chemotherapy, immunotherapy, targeted therapy) were extracted from the electronic medical records.

The pathological data were reviewed blindly by two pathologists. Patient clinical pathological baseline information is shown in [_Table 1_](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#table1).

#### _Follow-up and clinical endpoints_

Each patient underwent follow-up evaluations at 3-month intervals during the initial year post-surgery, followed by assessments every 6 months thereafter. These evaluations included a thorough examination for recurrence and metastasis, utilizing advanced imaging techniques such as enhanced CT, positron emission tomography (PET)-CT, magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), and whole-body bone scans. Telephone follow-up was conducted to further confirm the postoperative treatment methods, follow-up frequency, and whether there was recurrence or metastasis. The study’s endpoint is DFS, defined as the time from surgery to the first recurrence or metastasis, or to the last follow-up if no recurrence or metastasis occurred. The last follow-up ended on October 10, 2025.

#### _Imaging data acquisition_

All patients received chest plain and DECT enhanced scans on a GE Revolution256 CT machine (GE Healthcare, Waukesha, WI, USA) about 2 weeks before surgery. The detailed scanning parameters were as follows: tube voltage 80/140 kV, tube current 320–370 mA, pitch 0.992:1, slice thickness and interval both 5mm, reconstruction slice thickness 0.625 mm, window width 400 Hounsfield units (HU), window level 40 HU. After the plain scan, iodinated contrast agent (320 mgI/mL, 1.5 mL/kg body weight) was injected at a flow rate of 2.5 mL/s, with delayed scans at 25 s (arterial phase) and 55 s (venous phase).

#### _Image preprocessing and segmentation_

Blinded to clinical outcomes, two chest radiologists with over 5 years of experience manually delineated the three-dimensional (3D) region of interest slice-by-slice along the tumor boundary on the plain scan images, avoiding obvious necrosis, hemorrhage, and cystic areas. Inter-observer consistency was evaluated using the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC), with values above 0.75 considered acceptable. Discrepant areas were adjudicated by a third senior physician with over 10 years of experience to determine the final segmentation results.

### Multimodal feature extraction

#### _Clinical features_

Categorical variables were one-hot encoded, and continuous variables retained their original values, ultimately forming a structured clinical feature vector.

#### _Radiomics features_

A total of 1,836 quantitative features were extracted from each volume of interest (VOI) using the open-source Python library PyRadiomics (version 3.0.1), following the guidelines of the Image Biomarker Standardisation Initiative (IBSI). These features spanned six categories: first-order statistics, shape, gray-level co-occurrence matrix, gray-level run-length matrix, gray-level size zone matrix, and neighborhood gray-tone difference matrix. Prior to extraction, all images were resampled (voxel size: 1×1×1 mm 3) and Z-score standardized to minimize the impact of scanner differences.

#### _DECT quantitative features_

Using the device’s post-processing workstation, based on the virtual monoenergetic images and material decomposition images obtained from dual-energy scans, ten quantitative parameters were calculated, including iodine concentration (IC), normalized IC (NIC), NIC difference (NIC-Diff), NIC ratio, effective atomic number, and CT enhancement rate. Patient DECT parameter information is shown in [_Table 2_](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#table2).

#### _DL feature extraction_

We extract deep imaging representations of tumors using 3D image blocks that cover the entire tumor. Specifically, we extract adjacent layers around the axial plane with the largest tumor volume to form a 3D image block and apply Z-score normalization. We use real-time data augmentation techniques to enhance the model’s generalization ability and robustness. Subsequently, this image block is input into a pre-trained 3D-DenseNet121 network. The network was pretrained on a large-scale dataset of general chest and abdominal CT scans (MedicalNet) using a self-supervised reconstruction task, providing foundational 3D visual representations. We remove the final classification layer and freeze all the network weights to use it as a fixed feature extractor. Then, we extract a 1,024-dimensional deep feature vector from the second-to-last fully connected layer to represent each sample’s deep imaging patterns.

### Feature selection and fusion model construction

After replacing outliers with the median and standardizing all features, a multimodal feature pool was constructed. A strict four-step selection process was applied to the training set to identify the most predictive and complementary features: (I) Univariate Selection [retaining features with univariate logistic regression (LR) P<0.05]; (II) Correlation Filtering (removing one of any feature pair with Spearman |ρ|>0.8, keeping the one with lower P value); (III) mRMR Selection to maximize relevance and minimize redundancy; and (IV) LASSO regression with 10-fold cross-validation to finalize the feature set, ultimately selecting 12 features for model construction.

### Model development, training, and comparison

#### _Feature layer fusion model_

Using the 12 selected features, seven machine learning classifiers—LR, K-nearest neighbors (KNN), support vector machine (SVM), random forest (RF), extreme random trees (ExtraTrees), naive Bayes, and extreme gradient boosting (XGBoost)—were trained and optimized on the training set. Hyperparameters were fine-tuned using grid search and five-fold cross-validation. The model with the highest average area under the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve (AUC) on the training cross-validation folds was selected as the final feature layer fusion model.

#### _Single modality model_

For fair comparison, we repeated independently the above training and optimization process for single modality (clinical, radiomics, and DECT) feature subset to determine the “champion” model with the best performance for each.

#### _Handling class imbalance_

To address class imbalance in the training set, the Synthetic Minority Over-Sampling Technique (SMOTE) was applied exclusively to the training data. The independent validation set remained untouched to preserve the natural class distribution for unbiased evaluation. A sensitivity analysis training the final model without SMOTE was performed to assess the robustness of our approach.

#### _Model comparison and evaluation_

All final models were thoroughly evaluated on an independent validation set across six key aspects.

*   Discriminative performance: the AUC is our main metric, and the DeLong test is used for inter-model comparison. The integrated discrimination improvement (IDI) and net reclassification improvement (NRI) indices were calculated to quantify predictive gains. Secondary metrics, including accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity, were visualized via radar and violin plots.
*   Calibration and consistency: evaluated using calibration curves and the Hosmer-Lemeshow test to examine the agreement between predicted probabilities and observed outcomes. Inter-model agreement was analyzed through correlation coefficients and classification consistency rates.
*   Clinical utility: quantified using DCA to estimate the net clinical benefit across various decision thresholds. A visual nomogram was developed from the optimal fusion model to facilitate individualized risk prediction.
*   Subgroup robustness: the model’s performance was tested within predefined clinically relevant subgroups—based on age (≥55 _vs._<55 years), gender, TNM stage (I _vs._ II–III), and pathological type (adenocarcinoma _vs._ squamous cell carcinoma)—to assess its generalizability.
*   Time-dependent assessment: time-dependent ROC curves were plotted to evaluate discriminative ability. Patients were stratified into high- and low-risk groups based on an optimal cutoff, and survival differences were compared using Kaplan-Meier curves with the log-rank test.
*   Interpretability: the SHAP framework was employed to interpret the optimal model, generating summary plots to visualize global feature importance and their directional impact on predictions.

#### _Statistical analysis_

Continuous variables are presented as mean ± standard deviation or median (interquartile range), while categorical variables are presented as frequency (percentage). Group comparisons were conducted using independent samples t-test, Mann-Whitney U test, or Chi-squared test.

All statistical analyses were conducted using Python (v3.8) with the scikit-learn (v1.0), StatsModels, and SHAP libraries. Model training was performed on an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090 GPU. The total training time for the fusion model ensemble (including hyperparameter optimization) was approximately 4.5 hours, while the inference time for a single patient was under 2 s. A two-sided P value <0.05 was considered statistically significant.

### Baseline characteristics of patients and feature selection

A total of 171 eligible NSCLC patients were included in this study and were randomly divided into a training set (n=119) and a validation set (n=52). There were no statistically significant differences in baseline characteristics such as age, gender, clinical stage, and DECT quantitative parameters between the two groups (all P>0.05), which indicates that the dataset is balanced and comparable (see [Table S1](https://cdn.amegroups.cn/static/public/JTD-2025-1-2741-Supplementary.pdf?v=1776198189709)).

Through a rigorous multi-step feature selection process including LASSO regression ([Figure S1](https://cdn.amegroups.cn/static/public/JTD-2025-1-2741-Supplementary.pdf?v=1776198189709)), we ultimately selected 12 core features that have the highest predictive power and complement each other from an initial multimodal feature pool containing 1,576 features to construct the final fusion model. These features originate from four modalities: clinical (n=2), radiomics (n=8), DECT (n=1), and DL (n=1). They include lymph status, Ki-67, logarithm gray-level dependence matrix (GLDM) large dependence emphasis, gradient first-order median, exponential first-order range, wavelet LLL first-order kurtosis, exponential GLDM large dependence high gray level emphasis, wavelet LLH first-order kurtosis, wavelet LHH GLDM correlation, effective atomic number (Zeff), and DL-3D-DenseNet121 score.

### Follow-up and recurrence/metastasis

The median follow-up time for the included samples was 58 months (ranging from 24 to 79 months), with 12 cases having a follow-up time of less than 36 months. Among the 171 patients, 62 experienced recurrence or metastasis (36.26%), while 109 did not (63.74%). The median DFS was 40 months.

A sensitivity analysis was conducted to assess whether the performance of the optimal fusion model was unduly reliant on the SMOTE oversampling technique. ### Statistical comparison and incremental value

The DeLong test showed that the AUC difference between the fusion model and the DL model was not statistically significant (P>0.05). However, to further quantify the predictive gain of the fusion model, we calculated the IDI and NRI. As shown in the forest plots of [_Figure 3C,3D_](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#figure3), the fusion model exhibited positive IDI and NRI values compared to all unimodal models, with P values all <0.05, indicating statistical significance.

### Model interpretability

Interpreting the fusion model with the SHAP framework showed its decision logic. As shown in the SHAP summary plot in [_Figure 7C_](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#figure7), features from various modalities work together to contribute to the final prediction. The results indicate that the DL-3D-DenseNet121 score is the most influential feature (with a peak SHAP value of 2.4), showing a significant risk-increasing effect across all samples; features such as lymph status, logarithm GLDM large dependence emphasis, and Ki-67 have SHAP values concentrated in the range of 0.1 to 0.3, also primarily indicating increased risk. Zeff, as the only retained DECT-related factor, has a SHAP value of about 0.2, indicating a slightly weaker contribution strength.

### Key findings

This study successfully developed and validated a multimodal fusion model (Fusion-ExtraTrees) for predicting postoperative DFS in NSCLC. By integrating clinical, radiomic, DECT quantitative, and DL features, the model achieved robust discriminative performance (AUC =0.889) on an independent validation set. It demonstrated performance comparable to the optimal single-modality DL model but with superior generalization across patient subgroups and enhanced stability. Critically, SHAP analysis provided clear interpretability, revealing how multi-scale features synergistically contribute to risk predictions ([21](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B21)). This interpretability was further translated into a user-friendly clinical nomogram, offering a practical tool for individualized risk assessment at the bedside.

### Interpretation of the multimodal feature set

The 12-feature set selected by our model encapsulates complementary biological and imaging insights. Two key clinical factors—lymph node metastasis status and Ki-67 index (stratified at 20%)—were retained, aligning with their established roles in signifying metastatic potential and high proliferative activity, respectively ([22](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B22)-[24](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B24)). Their identification as independent prognostic factors in our traditional Cox regression ([Table S4](https://cdn.amegroups.cn/static/public/JTD-2025-1-2741-Supplementary.pdf?v=1776198189709)) validates that our data-driven approach quantifies known clinical wisdom rather than relying on opaque rules.

The model also selected a suite of radiomic features from first-order, texture, and wavelet-filtered domains. Collectively, these features encode tumor heterogeneity—capturing variations in density, texture uniformity, and structural patterns—that are imperceptible to visual assessment ([25](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B25),[26](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B26)). For instance, features related to ‘large dependence emphasis’ and intensity kurtosis may reflect intratumoral regions of necrosis or organized growth, phenomena associated with more aggressive biology ([27](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B27),[28](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B28)).

The sole retained DECT parameter, Zeff, emerged as a positive predictor. In the context of NSCLC, a higher Zeff may correspond to regions with high nucleic acid content (e.g., dense cell nuclei) or microcalcifications, potentially signaling more aggressive tumor behavior and warranting further investigation as a unique _in vivo_ chemical composition biomarker ([29](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B29)-[31](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B31)).

Most influential was the DL feature (DL-3D-DenseNet121 score), which served as the decision core in the SHAP analysis. Its predictive power suggests the neural network successfully encoded complex, sub-visual patterns related to prognosis—such as intricate growth or vascularization architectures—that surpass the descriptive capacity of engineered radiomic features ([32](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B32)-[35](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B35)).

### Methodological advancements and contextualization

Our study’s contribution extends beyond predictive performance to its methodological rigor and conceptual framing.

*   First, we established a stringent benchmark by constructing and independently optimizing “champion” models for each unimodal data source. This moves beyond comparisons with simple baselines, ensuring a fair and conclusive assessment of the added value from multimodal fusion.
*   Second, we reframe the key observation that the fusion model’s AUC matched, but did not surpass, the top-performing DL-only model (0.889 _vs._ 0.891). This illuminates the primary advantage of our multimodal approach: enhanced robustness and interpretability, not a marginal gain in discriminative power. The fusion model demonstrated consistent performance across diverse patient subgroups ([_Table 4_](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#t4)), and SHAP analysis demystified its decisions, integrating a DL “core” with clinically reasoned evidence from other modalities. This fosters greater clinical trust and addresses a key barrier to adoption.
*   Third, we contextualize our technical choices within the evolving AI landscape. The 3D-DenseNet121 backbone, pretrained on general chest CT data via self-supervised learning, served as an effective feature extractor. However, we acknowledge the persistent challenge of domain shift for clinical generalization. The emergence of self-supervised CT foundation models (e.g., RAD-DINO, TAP-CT) trained on vast, diverse datasets represents a paradigm shift toward superior robustness and transferability ([36](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B36),[37](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B37)). Our work validates the multimodal fusion framework itself; consequently, replacing the CNN backbone with such a foundation model is a logical and promising next step to boost generalizability for multi-center translation.

Furthermore, while 3D input captures comprehensive volumetric context, exploring efficient two-dimensional (2D) or multi-view 2.5-dimensional (2.5D) architectures within this proven fusion framework could significantly reduce computational cost and memory footprint for real-world deployment without sacrificing efficacy, a practical consideration for clinical integration ([36](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/114659/html#B36)).

### Limitations and future directions

This study has limitations that define its current stage and future path. It is a single-center retrospective study with a moderate sample size (n=171). Therefore, the developed model is not yet ready for direct clinical deployment. Prospective, multi-center external validation in larger, diverse cohorts is an essential prerequisite to confirm its efficacy and robustness prior to any real-world translation.

To translate the promising potential of this multimodal fusion approach into clinical practice, we outline the following essential steps: (I) prospective multicenter validation to rigorously test generalizability; (II) development of dynamic predictive models by integrating longitudinal clinical and imaging data; (III) exploration of more advanced fusion strategies (e.g., graph neural networks) to model complex inter-modality interactions; and (IV) clinical deployment pilots to integrate the tool into decision-support systems for real-world utility assessment.

In summary, this study developed and validated a robust, interpretable multimodal fusion model for predicting DFS in postoperative NSCLC patients. The model’s principal strength lies not in a marginal AUC improvement but in its reliable performance across subgroups and its explainable decision logic, which synergizes DL patterns with clinically meaningful features. The derivative clinical nomogram provides a tangible bridge from complex algorithms to actionable bedside assessment. With further validation and development, this tool holds promise for optimizing postoperative management by enabling earlier identification of high-risk patients, thereby facilitating personalized follow-up and therapeutic strategies.
