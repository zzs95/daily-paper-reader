ABSTRACT:  
 
Radiomics, the extraction of quantitative features from radiological images, is expanding beyond 
oncology into cardiology for diagnosis, disease stratification and prognostication. Radiomic 
features are susceptible to variations due to differences in image acquisition, segmentation and 
definitions. Therefore, the features incorporated into predictive models need to be robust against 
these variations, or they may fail to perform optimally.  
Computed tomography (CT) angiography is used in the evaluation of carotid arteries for stroke 
risk stratification. We aimed to identify the set of radiomic features that were robust to changes 
in the image segmentation process (reflective of intra-rater and inter-rater variability) and to 
determine the optimal image pre-processing settings to use for radiomic analysis of carotid CT 
angiograms. Furthermore, we investigated the ability of radiomic features to identify vulnerable 
carotid atherosclerotic plaques. 93 radiomic features were extracted from the CT angiograms of 
41 patients who previously had an ischaemic cerebrovascular event. We identified robust and 
non-redundant features that could classify culprit and non-culprit carotid arteries with a mean 
cross-validated AUC of 0.73 (SD 0.09), and a mean cross-validated accuracy of 69% when using 
multi-slice analysis with resegmentation to 0-200 Hounsfield units and a fixed bin width of 25. 
 
Introduction 
 
Radiomics, sometimes referred to as ‘texture analysis’, comprises image analysis methods that 
involve the high-throughput extraction of minable imaging features1 from radiological images. 
These quantitative features have been used in the development of diagnostic, predictive and 
prognostic prediction models, particularly in the field of oncology2,3 where radiomic features 
could predict lung or head & neck cancer survival better than the current gold standard of TNM 
(Tumour, Node, Metastasis) staging4–6 system. Recently, there has been growing interest in the 
application of radiomics to cardiovascular imaging7–10, for example in the differentiation 
between hypertensive heart disease and hypertrophic cardiomyopathy from native T1 mapping 
magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) data9 or in the identification of the high-risk coronary plaque 
napkin-ring sign10 from computed tomography (CT) images.  
 
A radiomic biomarker needs to be reproducible, robust and accurate11. However, radiomic 
features are susceptible to variations in different parts of the workflow12,13. Such variations can 
occur at any part of the multi-step process in radiomics, including image acquisition (e.g., use of 
different CT scanner manufacturers and models or acquisition protocols), image segmentation 
(e.g., inter-rater and intra-rater variability in delineating the region-of-interest [ROI]/volume-of-
interest [VOI]) and the feature extraction stage (e.g., use of different radiomics software or 
definitions of features). Robustness analyses aim to evaluate the impact of changes in these 
parameters on radiomic features, and to identify the radiomic features that are highly robust, i.e.,

CT angiography (CTA) is used to evaluate carotid arteries for the assessment of stroke risk. In a 
symptomatic patient (an individual who has experienced an ischaemic stroke or transient 
ischaemic attack [TIA]), the ‘culprit’ carotid artery refers to the carotid artery with the 
vulnerable atherosclerotic plaque that was responsible for causing the cerebrovascular event, and 
which is prone to cause a future recurrent event, if there is no intervention. The ‘non-culprit’ 
refers to the contralateral carotid artery in the same symptomatic patient.  
 
There is growing awareness of the need for the standardisation and harmonisation of protocols in 
each stage of the radiomics workflow15, where possible, to minimise such variations5. In parallel, 
where harmonisation is not possible (e.g., using the same CT scanner in every hospital), 
robustness and repeatability analyses are essential in determining the extent to which such 
variations can be tolerated for each specific application, i.e., without affecting predictive 
performance. The majority of robustness and repeatability studies thus far have been conducted 
using phantom studies16,17 or have focused on areas of oncology18–20, such as non-small cell lung 
cancer13 or oesophageal cancer21. In cardiology, reproducibility studies have only started to 
emerge this year: one relating to a phantom study with cardiac single photon emission computed 
tomography (SPECT) radiomic features22 and the other relating to myocardial radiomic features 
in MRI23. Feature robustness is specific to the phenotype being studied and the imaging modality 
used14. Therefore, there remains a need for the assessment of CT radiomic robustness in 
cardiovascular applications, as well as a consensus on the most appropriate settings to be used 
for texture feature extraction in carotid CTA imaging.  
 
In this study, we first investigated the robustness of radiomic features following variations in 
ROI/VOI delineations under different CT image processing settings and image quantisation 
settings. We then aimed to determine the most effective image settings along with the most 
robust and non-redundant (i.e., not highly correlated) radiomic features for the identification of 
culprit versus non-culprit carotid arteries in patients with prior cerebrovascular events. Knowing 
this information is key to improve the applicability and generalisability of carotid CT radiomics 
in clinical practice.

Results 
Carotid CTA scans from 41 patients with previous stroke or TIA were analysed in this study 
including 41 culprit and 41 non-culprit carotid arteries (82 carotid arteries in total). The CTA 
scans had a mean in-plane pixel size of 0.56×0.56 mm2 ( SD 0.077 mm) and had a slice thickness 
of 0.625 mm with 0.4 mm slice spacing. For multi-slice analysis, CTA scans were resampled to 3 
mm slice thickness, resulting in a slice spacing of 1.92 mm. The clinical characteristics of the 
patients and the plaque characteristics of their carotid arteries are detailed in Table 1.

processing, b) prior normalisation and c) resegmentation to [0, 1, …, 200] Hounsfield units that 
limits the influence of calcification and luminal contrast on the extracted radiomic features. 
Image quantisation refers to how the original distribution of grey-level pixel values in the image

Image Segmentation Perturbations reflect Inter-Rater Variability 
 
Following segmentation of the carotid artery (see Fig. 1), the ROIs were subjected to a series of 
morphological operations24, namely dilations and erosions25 to simulate the variability in image 
segmentation that exists in practice when human operators use a manual segmentation method. 
The morphological operations captured the range of variability that occurred with human inter-
rater variability, as demonstrated in Fig. 2. This was determined by ROIs drawn by two 
independent operators on a subset of carotid scans (n=8). The degree of agreement between the 
ROIs was quantified using the Dice coefficient (DC), a measure of segmentation overlap 
commonly used in computer vision and machine learning applications. There was little 
variability in intra-rater segmentation (ROIs drawn by the same rater at two different time 
points), and greater variability in inter-rater segmentation.

Assessing Feature Robustness to Image Segmentation Variations 
 
The robustness of each texture feature to the ROI perturbations was measured by the intraclass 
correlation coefficient (ICC) with absolute agreement. Texture features were deemed to be 
highly robust to segmentation variations with ICC values ≥  0.90, as this indicated that the values 
of the texture features remained the same (or were highly similar) despite the perturbations. As 
shown in Fig. 1, we investigated several configurations at each stage of the radiomics workflow, 
to identify the configurations that produced the highest proportion of highly robust texture 
features which could be used in the binary classification of culprit versus non-culprit carotid 
arteries, see Supplementary Figure S1.  
 
Single-Slice Analysis: Feature Robustness in Different Image Configurations 
 
A total of 19 different image processing settings were investigated in single-slice analysis which 
included different combinations of (i) no prior normalisation or resegmentation, (ii) 
normalisation, (iii) resegmentation and different image quantisation schemes using different bin 
widths (BW) or bin numbers (BN). These configurations are further detailed in Supplementary 
Table S4. 
 
Over 50% of radiomic features were highly robust to ROI perturbations when using the original 
image (i.e., no prior normalisation or resegmentation) and a fixed BW for image quantisation, 
ranging from 10 to 35 (in increments of 5). Using a fixed BW of 10 led to a higher proportion of 
poorly robust (ICC<0.5) texture features compared with the other BWs.  
 
The best BW setting for single-slice analysis with no image pre-processing was BW 25-30, 
which complies with the current default setting in PyRadiomics (fixed BW of 25), see 
Supplementary Table S5 for a detailed breakdown of the radiomic features by robustness 
category and feature class type. Using a fixed BW rather than a fixed BN (considering the power 
of 2 from 8 to 256) for image quantisation led to a higher proportion of texture features with 
excellent robustness. If using fixed BNs as the method for image quantisation, the most extreme 
BNs investigated (8 and 256) led to the highest proportion of poorly robust texture features. 
 
Resegmentation in single-slice analysis reduced the proportion of highly robust texture features, 
but also reduced the proportion of poorly robust texture features as compared with no 
resegmentation (from 11.8% to 3.2%). In resegmentation, the majority of texture features 
(88.2%) had moderate robustness against ROI perturbations.  
 
Prior normalisation of the image reduced the proportion of poorly robust texture features, but did 
not impact the proportion of texture features with excellent robustness, when compared to no 
prior normalisation. In the prior normalisation pre-processing setting, only different BNs could

Multi-Slice Analysis: Feature Robustness in Different Image Configurations 
 
In multi-slice analysis, the method of image quantisation that led to the highest proportion of 
texture features with excellent robustness involved using fixed BWs, as opposed to fixed BNs, 
consistent with the single-slice analysis findings. Over 55% of all texture features had excellent 
robustness when using the original image (no prior normalisation/resegmentation) in multi-slice 
analysis, with similar proportions across all BW settings from 10-35. There was a slight decrease 
in performance between BW 30 to BW 35 (BW 30 58.1% excellent robustness, 9.7% poor 
robustness; BW 35 55.9% excellent robustness, 11.8% poor robustness). 
 
Resegmentation was performed with a fixed BW of 25 only (PyRadiomics default setting) and 
compared with the original image using the same BW. The proportion of highly robust features 
decreased (55.9% to 15.1%), however, so did the proportion of poorly robust features (10.8% to 
2.2%). The majority of texture features shifted to moderate robustness (82.8%). Similar to the 
single-slice analysis findings, prior normalisation in multi-slice analysis reduced the proportion 
of poorly robust features, but had little impact on the proportion of highly robust texture features.

Multi-slice Analysis: Impact of Interpolation Method 
 
The B-spline interpolation method is the default method used for image resampling in 
PyRadiomics. Linear interpolation is another method available that is the simplest and fastest. In 
terms of execution time for the 82 carotid arteries analysed in multi-slice analysis, when using B-
spline interpolation with a fixed BW of 25, the total time taken was 48.15 seconds. When using 
linear interpolation with a fixed BW of 25, the total time taken was 29.49 seconds.  
 
We investigated the effect of using B-spline versus linear interpolation on the extracted texture 
features and found that the vast majority of texture features show high agreement in both 
absolute agreement and consistency between the two methods of interpolation. There were no 
poorly robust features with regards to the method of interpolation. The breakdown by texture 
feature class is detailed in Supplementary Table S6.

Culprit versus Non-culprit Carotid Arteries: Machine Learning Classification 
 
Carotid calcium score was a poor predictor of culprit versus non-culprit carotid artery status, 
with a mean area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) of 0.44 and a standard 
deviation (SD) of 0.11 following 5-fold cross-validation (Elastic Net classifier, weight for L1 and 
L2 penalties=0.5), and a mean accuracy of 46% (SD 7.5%), see Fig 4. and Fig. 5.

Differentiation between culprit versus non-culprit carotid arteries was improved with the 
integration of radiomic features as predictors to create an integrated model (see Fig 4. and Fig. 
5), with a mean cross-validated AUC of 0.73 (SD 0.09), and a mean cross-validated accuracy of 
69% (SD 6.0%). Similarly, when using radiomic features as the only predictors, this resulted in a 

12 

higher mean AUC 0.67 (SD 0.08) and mean accuracy 62% (SD 4.0%), compared with using 
calcium as the only predictor.  
 
The best performing machine learning classifiers among those investigated – namely, decision 
tree, random forest, least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO), Elastic Net and 
neural network – were the logistic regression based classifiers: Elastic Net and LASSO. The 
difference between these two classifiers stems from the regularisation they employ. LASSO 
regression uses L1 regularisation that reduces the coefficients of certain features to zero, thereby 
reducing the number of variables in a model (i.e., sparse feature selection). Elastic Net regression 
uses both L1 and L2 regularisation whereby the L2 penalty term constrains the magnitude of the 
feature coefficients so that a model is not dominated by any single feature.

Discussion 
 
In this study, we investigated the impact of ROI delineation perturbations on the robustness of 
radiomic features in carotid CTA imaging. The ROI delineation perturbations are reflective of 
variations present in clinical practice. This study also investigated the impact of different image 
quantisation methods (using fixed BWs versus fixed BNs) and different quantisation levels on 
the robustness of radiomic features. In addition, we assessed the predictive capabilities of carotid 
CTA radiomic features for the identification of culprit versus non-culprit carotid arteries in 
symptomatic patients (individuals that have previously had an ischaemic stroke or TIA).  
 
We found that with no image pre-processing, the majority of texture features had excellent 
robustness (ICC(2,1) ≥  0.90) against ROI perturbations (i.e., dilations and erosions). Although 
resegmentation reduces the number of texture features with excellent robustness, and shifts them 
into the moderate robustness category, resegmentation in carotid CTA imaging can help in 
identifying differences between culprit versus non-culprit carotid arteries. In the differentiation 
of culprit versus non-culprit carotid arteries, calcification was not statistically significantly 
different between them and was not a useful predictor.  
 
Prior image normalisation did not increase the proportion of texture features with excellent 
robustness. Furthermore, since the grey level values in CT imaging are already calibrated to 
Hounsfield units, we do not recommend prior image normalisation for carotid CTA imaging 
radiomic studies.  
 
Using BN versus BW image quantization methods are not interchangeable, as the texture 
features in one setting will not necessarily be robust in another setting. In this carotid CTA 
radiomics study, we found that a fixed BW of 25 or 30 for image quantisation was the setting 
that led to the highest proportion of highly robust radiomic features. Since the PyRadiomics 
default is already a fixed BW of 25, we would recommend using this setting for future carotid 
CTA radiomics studies, to encourage further standardisation and harmonisation of radiomics 
protocols. In the BW settings that were investigated in this study (from 10-35 in increments of 
5), we found a decrease in the proportion of highly robust radiomic features when using the 
higher limit of BW 35, and so we do not recommend using higher values than this limit. If using 
fixed BNs as the method for image quantisation, avoid using the extremes (BN 8 or BN 256), as 
these had the highest proportion of poorly robust features. 
 
In multi-slice analysis, B-spline or linear interpolation methods led to similar results. Since the 
PyRadiomics default is already B-spline interpolation, we recommend this setting. For the 
classification of culprit versus non-culprit carotid arteries, we found that multi-slice analysis with 
resegmentation gave the highest predictive performance.  
 

15 

We recommend for reproducibility and replicability purposes to always clearly state image 
settings used in radiomics studies since radiomic features can be susceptible to changes in 
parameters of the workflow. The information required includes image acquisition protocols, 
image pre-processing details and method of interpolation, method and value of image 
quantisation, and texture feature definitions. 
 
The limitations of this study include its retrospective nature, as the carotid imaging dataset was 
pooled from three different cardiovascular imaging studies: ICARUSS26, VISION27 and CHAI28. 
However, all images were captured using the same scanner, in a single centre (Addenbrooke’s 
Hospital, Cambridge, UK), with the same imaging protocols and similar patient characteristics. 
Consequently, we could not investigate the effects of inter-scanner variability of the robustness 
of radiomic features. There was also no repeat imaging on the same patient and so test-retest 
repeatability of the texture features could not be assessed. Nevertheless, in this study, we aimed 
to provide a comprehensive robustness analysis of variations that could occur within a single-
centre radiomics workflow, from ROI delineations, to image quantisation and image pre-
processing methods.  
 
In summary, to the best of our knowledge, this is the first systematic approach to the application 
of radiomics to the carotid artery. We identified radiomic features that are robust, non-redundant 
and have predictive performance, over and above calcium, in the classification of culprit versus 
non-culprit carotid arteries in ischaemic stroke and TIA events. In the future, this subset of 
radiomic features could be applied to help clinicians improve stroke risk prediction if similar 
performance is seen in multi-centre prospective trials.

Methods 
 
Carotid CT Dataset 
This retrospective study analysed anonymised carotid CTA pooled from three observational 
research vascular imaging datasets from a single institution (Addenbrooke’s Hospital, 
Cambridge University Hospitals National Health Service Foundation Trust, Cambridge, UK) 
between 2011 to 2016 26–28. All the series had appropriate ethical approvals in place and had 
similar inclusion and exclusion criteria which are listed in the published papers26–28. All patients 
had confirmed carotid artery-related ischaemic stroke or TIA within 3 months before imaging. 
Patients with haemorrhagic stroke, atrial fibrillation and/or complete vessel occlusions were 
excluded. In total, 41 patients and therefore 82 carotid arteries (41 culprit and 41 non-culprit) 
were included. CTA images and non-contrast CT images were acquired using a standard clinical 
protocol, see Supplementary Text S1. 
 
Image Analysis 
 
Manual segmentation single-slice analysis 
Bilateral carotid arteries were evaluated in each patient from a single axial CTA slice of original 
slice thickness 0.625 mm and slice spacing of 0.4 mm, located at the carotid bifurcation as a 
standardized landmark. ROIs were drawn to encompass the whole vessel as closely as possible, 
including the outer wall, using commercially available research software (TexRad; Feedback 
Medical Ltd, Cambridge, UK). The CTA images were analysed with the reviewer (EPVL) 
blinded to the clinical status of the carotid artery.  
 
Manual segmentation multi-slice analysis 
CTA slices were resampled to 3 mm slice thickness using the OsiriX MD software Resampling 
plugin (Version 10.0.3, Pixmeo SARL, Bernex, Geneva, Switzerland) and 14 consecutive carotid 
artery slices were manually segmented with ROIs drawn around the carotid artery adventitia, 
with the carotid bifurcation nominated as slice 026–28. The slices started from slice -3 to slice +10 
above and below the carotid bifurcation which captured the common carotid artery and internal 
carotid artery. For each carotid, the 14 consecutive slices created a single VOI from which 
texture features were extracted.  
 
Details of the methodology for carotid artery plaque characteristics is found in Supplementary 
Text S2, and details of intra- and inter-rater reproducibility is found in Supplementary Text S3.

Radiomics Feature Extraction 
PyRadiomics is an open-source Python package developed for the standardisation of radiomic 
feature extraction 29. PyRadiomics version 3.0 and Python 3.7.4 were used for feature extraction 
from the ROIs and VOIs from above. Six feature classes were extracted: (1) first-order intensity 
histogram statistics, (2) Grey Level Co-occurrence Matrix features (GLCM)30,31, (3) Grey Level 
Run Length Matrix features (GLRLM)32, (4) Grey Level Size Zone Matrix (GLSZM)33, (5) Grey 
Level Dependence Matrix (GLDM)34 and (6) Neighbouring Grey Tone Difference Matrix 
Features (NGTDM)35.

Robustness Analysis 
 
ROI perturbations 
Manual segmentation (as opposed to automatic segmentation) is a source of intra- and inter-rater 
variability. Automatic segmentation methods are not currently widely and readily available in all 
fields of medicine to be a feasible and reliable solution for current preliminary radiomic studies, 
although this is an area of active research. We therefore evaluated the impact of variations in 
ROI delineation on extracted radiomic features systematically by ROI dilation and erosion. This 
was to simulate the variation in ROI/VOI placement that can occur in clinical practice, including 
over-segmentation (with dilation), and under-segmentation (with erosion).  
 
To achieve these perturbations, the original ROIs delineated by the primary reader (EPVL) were 
subjected to dilation and erosion image morphological operations implemented in Python 3.7.4, 
using the Python package, SciPy morphology, and its ‘binary_dilation’ and ‘binary_erosion’ 
functions, see Fig. 6. For single-slice analysis, we used a circular structuring element of radius 1, 
with iterations of 1-2 for ROI dilation and erosion. For multi-slice analysis, we used a spherical 
structuring element of radius 1, with one and two iterations of 1-2 for ROI dilation, and only one 
iteration for ROI erosion in order to ensure that a sufficient number of pixels would be available 
for the downstream texture feature calculation after erosion.

Image pre-processing 

19 

Prior to feature extraction, there are different image pre-processing schemes that can be applied 
to the CTA scan. Three schemes were investigated: a) Original image (no image pre-processing), 
b) Normalisation and c) Resegmentation.  
 
Image normalisation changes the range of pixel intensity values. In PyRadiomics, normalization 
of the image leads to the pixel values assuming an approximate gaussian distribution. 
Resegmentation refers to the process whereby only pixels within a specified range are retained 
within the ROI. Resegmentation was applied with an upper limit of 200, and a lower limit of 0 
which restricted texture feature extraction to only the pixels with Hounsfield units between 0 and 
200 HUs. This resegmentation aids with excluding the effects of excess macro-calcification and 
limits the effect of luminal contrast and perivascular carotid fat on the CTA ROI. ROI erosion 
was not performed on resegmentation, only ROI dilation was performed in order to ensure that 
all ROIs had sufficient pixels for texture feature extraction. For resegmentation, we used a fixed 
BW of 25 only (PyRadiomics version 3.0 default). 
 
Image Quantisation 
Before texture features are calculated, the image must be quantised by using a fixed number of 
bins, or by using a fixed BW. We varied the BWs of the image grey-level histogram from 10 to 
35, in increments of 5. For BN variation, we varied the fixed number of bins as follows: 8, 16, 
32, 64, 128 and 256. These ranges were investigated as they result in the number of bins being 
between 30 to 130 that have been associated with good reproducibility and performance36, as 
discussed in the PyRadiomics documentation37. 
 
Multi-slice Analysis: Image Resampling and Interpolation Method 
Higher-order texture feature extraction requires isotropic images, i.e., pixel size in the x, y and z 
directions are the same. In CT imaging, images are often isotropic in-plane but will have a larger 
z-axis slice spacing and therefore be anisotropic in 3D. In radiomics studies, it is common for 
images to be isotropically resampled. We investigated the impact of different image resampling 
methods on extracted radiomic features: (1) linear interpolation, (2) B-spline interpolation 
(default in PyRadiomics) to 1×1×1mm3.

Machine Learning Classification 
 
Only the highly robust features were used for the classification of culprit versus non-culprit 
carotid arteries. To reduce multicollinearity and feature redundancy, pairwise feature-to-feature 
correlations were determined using the Spearman Rank correlation. For pairs of features with a 
𝑟𝑟𝑠𝑠 ≥ 0.95,the feature with the highest AUC in univariate logistic regression was retained, and the 
latter was discarded.  
 
The features were subsequently standardised to have a mean of zero and a variance of one. 5 
machine learning classifiers were evaluated, using a random state of 42 for reproducibility: 
decision tree41, random forest42, LASSO regression43, Elastic Net regression (weight for L1 and 
L2 penalties=0.5)44 and a neural network45. The dataset was shuffled and the average 
performance (accuracy and AUC) of the classifiers calculated following 5-fold stratified cross-
validation.  
 
The AUC of the radiomics only models, and of the integrated models (uses radiomics features 
and calcium as predictors) were compared with the AUC of the calcium only models using 
DeLong's method46 to compare classifier performance.

Figure Legends 
 
Figure 1. Radiomics workflow. The carotid CTA images were manually segmented to create 
ROIs from single-slice analysis, and VOIs from multi-slice analysis. The segmented masks were 
subjected to perturbations that reflected the variability in carotid segmentation present in 
practice. The CTA images were subjected to different image settings: a) original - no image pre-
processing, b) prior normalisation and c) resegmentation to [0, 1, …, 200] Hounsfield units that 
limits the influence of calcification and luminal contrast on the extracted radiomic features. 
Image quantisation refers to how the original distribution of grey-level pixel values in the image 
are rebinned.  
 
Figure 2. Violin plots of image segmentation agreement as determined by the Dice 
coefficient with the original ROI. There was excellent intra-rater agreement and good inter-
rater agreement with ROI delineation. There was greater variability in inter-rater segmentation, 
which was reflected by the morphological operations applied to the original ROI.  
 
Figure 3. Venn Diagram to show the texture features that were (A) Highly robust in single-
slice or multi-slice analysis and, (B) Poorly robust in single-slice or multi-slice analysis. 
Amongst the texture features that demonstrated excellent robustness in at least 1 of the 19 image 
processing settings, there were 5 features that were robust in single-slice analysis only, and 11 
features that were robust in multi-slice analysis only. There were poorly robust texture features 
that occurred in both single-slice and multi-slice analysis, but also in single-slice only and multi-
slice only, respectively. The majority of texture features that were poorly robust in both analyses 
related to low grey-levels, i.e., low Hounsfield Units, which likely reflects whether or not the 
ROI segmentation captures the perivascular fat surrounding the carotid artery.  
 
Figure 4. Mean receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves of 5-fold stratified cross 
validation with Elastic Net logistic regression (weight for L1 and L2 penalties=0.5) using (1) 
radiomic features only as predictors, (2) radiomics features and calcium, (3) calcium only. 
Image settings: multi-slice analysis with resegmentation, and fixed bin width of 25. AUC, area 
under the ROC curve; SD, standard deviation 
 
Figure 5. AUC and feature coefficients of predictors used in the Elastic Net logistic 
regression classifier in multi-slice analysis with resegmentation. (A) Calcium only used as a 
predictor, (B) Radiomic features only as predictors, (C) using both radiomic features and calcium 
in an integrated model. * p-value<0.05, *** p-value<0.001 when comparing the classification 
performance of radiomic models (B and C) with the calcium only model (A) using the DeLong’s 
method.

segmented to delineate the carotid artery. The original ROI was subjected to morphological

Supplementary Text S1. Image Acquisition Protocols 
All imaging was performed on a combined GE Discovery 690 PET-CT scanner with an 
integrated 64-slice CT scanner (GE Healthcare, Waukesha, WI, USA). CTA images were 
acquired from the aortic arch to the Circle of Willis, using bolus tracking (triggered at 100 HU 
above baseline) with a region-of-interest placed in the aortic arch and 70-100 ml NIOPAM 300 
(Bracco UK Limited, High Wycombe, UK) radiocontrast injected at 5ml/second, followed by a 
50ml chaser of normal saline. Acquisition parameters: tube voltage 120 kV, maximum tube 
current 200 mA, rotation time of 0.8 second, pitch 0.969:1, slice thickness 0.625 mm with 
reconstruction interval of 0.4 mm. 
 
Unenhanced CT images were acquired from the arch of the aorta to the external auditory meatus. 
CT acquisition parameters: tube voltage 120 kV, tube current 40mA, rotation time 0.5 seconds, 
pitch 1.375, direct field of view 50cm, section thickness 3.75mm reconstructed to 3.27mm.

Supplementary Text S3. Intra-and Inter-rater reproducibility  
The primary reader (EPVL) manually segmented 10% of carotid arteries (n=8) that were 
randomly selected to determine intra-rater variability in ROI delineation. In addition, a second 
reader (Dr Chris Wall, Academic Cardiology Registrar, University of Cambridge), also manually 
segmented a random selection of carotid arteries (n=8). This intra- and inter-rater variability in 
ROI delineation was used to ensure that the variations achieved using morphological operations 
(ROI erosion and dilation) reflected the variability that occurred in practice with human 
operators.
