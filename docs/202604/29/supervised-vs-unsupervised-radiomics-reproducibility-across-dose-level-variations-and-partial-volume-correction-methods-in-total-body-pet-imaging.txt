Title: Supervised vs. unsupervised radiomics reproducibility across dose-level variations and partial volume correction methods in total-body PET imaging

## 1. Introduction

Positron emission tomography (PET) is a cornerstone of quantitative molecular imaging, yet conventional systems with a limited axial field-of-view (FOV) constrain sensitivity, dynamic coverage, and dose or acquisition time flexibility [[1]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0005), [[2]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0010). Recent long–axial FOV “total-body” PET/CT systems (e.g., uEXPLORER, PennPET Explorer, and Biograph Vision Quadra) enable simultaneous whole-body coverage with ultra-high sensitivity, opening opportunities for dynamic multiparametric imaging and substantial reductions in administered activity while preserving image quality [[3]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0015), [[4]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0020), [[5]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0025), [[6]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0030). At the same time, quantitative image analysis pipelines, such as radiomics remain sensitive to acquisition and reconstruction variability, which complicates reproducibility and clinical translation; ongoing standardization efforts (e.g., IBSI) and methodological reviews have highlighted these challenges and the need for rigorous evaluation [[7]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0035), [[8]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0040).

A large body of literature has shown that many PET radiomic features are influenced by voxel size, segmentation strategy, count statistics, and reconstruction algorithms, with only a subset demonstrating robust repeatability or reproducibility across conditions [[9]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0045), [[10]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0050), [[11]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0055), [[12]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0060), [[13]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0065). Systematic review and _meta_-research reported that first-order standardized uptake value (SUV) tend to be more reliable than several texture descriptors, whereas numerous higher-order features show pronounced sensitivity to acquisition and processing choices [[14]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0070), [[15]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0075), [[16]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0080). To improve transparency and comparability, community efforts, such as the Image Biomarker Standardization Initiative (IBSI) and the CLEAR reporting guideline have formalized feature definitions and minimum reporting items, yet gaps remain in how reproducibility is assessed and reported across heterogeneous datasets [[7]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0035), [[17]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0085).

Partial volume effects (PVE) arising from finite spatial resolution bias quantitative PET, particularly for small structures, motivating partial volume correction (PVC) before downstream feature extraction [[18]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0090), [[19]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0095). Among post-reconstruction PVC strategies, iterative deconvolution methods, such as Richardson–Lucy (RL) and reblurred Van-Cittert (RVC). are widely used, alongside region-based voxel-wise approaches implemented in open-source toolkits (e.g., PETPVC) [[20]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0100). While deconvolution can improve recovery, it may amplify noise unless appropriately regularized; prior comparisons have examined RL and RVC variants, and recent studies have begun to probe how different PVC choices affect the stability of PET radiomic features [[21]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0105), [[22]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0110).

Recent evidence highlighted that features derived from short-duration or otherwise low-count acquisitions often diverge from full-count references, whereas long-axial-FOV PET/CT scanners markedly improve feature reliability even under reduced-count conditions [[23]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0115). In parallel, advanced reconstruction strategies, such as Bayesian-penalized iterative methods and deep learning–based restoration has been shown to enhance feature stability in noisy data [[24]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0120), [[25]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0125), [[26]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0130). Together, these findings demonstrated that reproducibility cannot be assumed, but requires explicit and systematic evaluation with appropriate metrics. Most prior radiomics reproducibility analyses have relied on supervised agreement statistics, such as the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) or linear correlations, which provide valuable but inherently limited perspectives on feature robustness [[27]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0135), [[28]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0140), [[29]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0145), [[30]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0150), [[31]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0155). However, these approaches largely treat features independently and do not capture the multidimensional geometry of the feature space. To address this gap, unsupervised geometry-aware methods (e.g., PCA/UMAP drift analysis) can evaluate global structural stability, while supervised techniques, such as Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA), allow direct testing of whether radiomics profiles can linearly discriminate between acquisition regimes, such as dose level, PVC method, or harmonization status. Integrating both unsupervised and supervised perspectives provides a more comprehensive framework for assessing reproducibility, thereby distinguishing between intrinsic geometry-driven overlap and class-driven separability.

In this study, we conduct a reproducibility analysis of radiomic features in dose-varied, PVC-corrected total-body PET. We systematically compare RL and RVC correction pipelines and employ a combination of ICC, geometry-aware drift metrics, and supervised LDA to assess both local feature stability and global feature-space separability. Furthermore, we evaluate the impact of ComBat harmonization, providing a comprehensive framework for quantifying the robustness of radiomic features across acquisition regimes in total-body PET images, with a focus on relative within-scan reproducibility trends under dose reduction, PVC and harmonization, rather than absolute SUV-based quantification.

### 2.1. Imaging dataset

This study utilized a dual-center total-body PET dataset acquired under a collaborative data-sharing agreement between Ruijin Hospital, Shanghai Jiao Tong University School of Medicine (China) and the University of Bern (Switzerland) [[32]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0160). Imaging was performed on two state-of-the-art digital total-body PET/CT systems with extended axial FOV: the uEXPLORER scanner (United Imaging Healthcare, Shanghai, China) and the Biograph Vision Quadra (Siemens Healthineers, Erlangen, Germany). This study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki, with approval from the institutional review boards of Ruijin Hospital and the University of Bern; informed consent was waived due to its retrospective design.

From the shared database, a total of 170 [18 F]-FDG PET scans were included in this analysis, consisting of 85 scans per center. Only FDG acquisitions were retained to ensure homogeneity of tracer uptake and comparability across sites. All data were acquired in list-mode format, enabling retrospective simulation of reduced-dose conditions by temporal subsampling of the raw acquisitions. Specifically, four dose levels were reconstructed for each scan by rebinning the list-mode data into centered time windows corresponding to 100% (20 min), 50% (10 min), 10% (2 min), and 2% (0.8 min). These levels represent relative count statistics derived from list-mode subsampling rather than changes in injected activity, and are therefore used as simulated low-dose conditions. Because these subsampled PET images originated from the same acquisition, they remained intrinsically co-registered with the full-dose reference.

Image reconstruction was carried out using the vendor-optimized clinical protocols for each scanner. On the uEXPLORER system, PET images were reconstructed with TOF-OSEM including 3 iterations, 20 subsets, resolution (PSF) modeling, and a 3-mm FWHM Gaussian post-filter, yielding a voxel size of 2.73×2.73×2.73 mm 3 with a 440×440 matrix covering the full 194-cm axial FOV. On the Vision Quadra system, reconstruction used TOF-enabled OSEM with PSF modeling and Gaussian post-filtering, at a voxel size of 1.65×1.65×1.65 mm 3 (matrix size 440×440, axial FOV 106 cm). All reconstructed images were analyzed in their native resolution without additional resampling or preprocessing prior to feature extraction. Post-reconstruction PVC was subsequently applied to evaluate additional partial volume mitigation beyond the standard PSF-enabled clinical reconstruction pipeline, allowing assessment of the incremental effect of explicit deconvolution on radiomics reproducibility. ### 2.2. Pet-based segmentation

Segmentation was performed using the semi-automatic tools available in the 3D Slicer platform, specifically employing the Level Tracing feature. The Level Tracing effect utilizes an automated approach to generate a contour around voxels of similar intensity in the master image. This isocontour is automatically created based on the intensity value selected by the user, outlining the region of interest accordingly.

The resulting polygon from this method is converted into a fractional labelmap. This conversion involves oversampling the polygon area by a factor of six in the in-plane direction, which creates a high-resolution binary labelmap. The oversampled labelmap is then downsampled to produce a fractional labelmap, where the final voxel values represent the proportion of included binary subvoxels. Specifically, each of the 36 oversampled binary voxels contributes a value of six, accounting for slice thickness, resulting in a final voxel intensity ranging between 0 and 216.

In our study, we applied this segmentation technique to axial PET slices. The Level Tracing effect served as a semi-automatic _iso_-contour tool, outlining regions of approximately equal intensity. These contours were subsequently reviewed and manually confirmed to ensure anatomical consistency and clinical relevance. PET images exhibited SUV max values consistent with typical uptake patterns. Segmentation was performed using a relative threshold of SUV≥75% of SUV max, delineating the metabolically active core across all dose levels [[33]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0165), [[34]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0170).

The same segmentation mask was applied identically across all reconstructed and PVC-corrected versions of each scan to maintain consistency in feature extraction and eliminate segmentation-induced variability across dose levels or correction methods. An example of the segmentation process and resulting _iso_-contours is illustrated in [Fig. 2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#f0010).

### 2.3. Partial volume correction (PVC) methods

To mitigate PVE across all dose levels, we implemented two widely used post-reconstruction deconvolution-based PVC techniques, namely Richardson–Lucy (RL) deconvolution and Reblurred Van Cittert (RVC) deconvolution. Both techniques were applied in 3D on the reconstructed PET volumes using spatially invariant Gaussian point-spread function (PSF) kernels. The PSF was modeled as an isotropic Gaussian with a full width at half maximum (FWHM) of 4.8 mm, used as a harmonized approximation of the effective post-reconstruction blur for PVC modeling across scanners.

## 3. Richardson–Lucy (RL) deconvolution

The RL algorithm is an iterative maximum-likelihood-based deconvolution method that refines the image estimate at each iteration. The update equation at iteration $k$ is given by:(1)$f_{k + 1} \left(x\right) = f_{k} \left(\right. x \left.\right) \left[h \left(x\right) \bigotimes \frac{f \left(\right. x \left.\right)}{h \left(\right. x \left.\right) \bigotimes f_{k} \left(\right. x \left.\right)}\right]$where:

*   •
$f_{k} \left(x\right)$ is the current estimate of the true image,

*   •
$f \left(\right. x \left.\right)$ is the observed (blurred) PET image,

*   •
$h \left(\right. x \left.\right)$ is the scanner’s PSF,

*   •
⊗ denotes convolution operator.

This formulation preserves non-negativity and enhances edge definition, but may amplify noise if too many iterations are used. In this study, the number of iterations was set to 10 in accordance with the default benchmark configuration of the PETPVC toolbox [[20]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0100) and consistent with prior PVC studies, including our previous work [[35]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0175), [[36]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0180). This setting provides a practical balance between resolution recovery and noise control.

## 4. Reblurred Van Cittert (RVC) deconvolution

The RVC algorithm is a linear iterative technique that updates the estimate based on the residual between the observed image and the current estimate convolved with the PSF. The update formula at iteration $k$ is given by:(2)$f_{k + 1} \left(x\right) = f_{k} \left(x\right) + \alpha . h \left(x\right) \bigotimes \left[f \left(x\right) - h \left(x\right) \bigotimes f_{k} \left(\right. x \left.\right)\right]$where $\alpha$ is a relaxation factor (typically between 0.1 and 1.0). A reblurring step is applied at each iteration to stabilize the process and suppress high-frequency noise. We used 10 iterations with a fixed PSF kernel identical to that used in RL deconvolution. Each PET image (at every dose level) was corrected using both RL and RVC methods, producing two sets of PVC-corrected volumes per scan. The resulting datasets enabled systematic comparison of radiomics feature behavior across PVC strategies and dose levels, while ensuring consistent spatial parameters throughout the correction process.

### 4.1. Radiomics feature extraction

Radiomic features were extracted using a single reference segmentation derived from the full-dose (100%) non-PVC PET reconstruction. Segmentation was performed using PET-based _iso_-intensity contours derived from voxel intensity values. The resulting segmentation mask was propagated identically to all reduced-dose and PVC-corrected reconstructions to eliminate segmentation-induced variability. Because all dose levels were retrospectively generated from the same list-mode acquisition, intrinsic spatial co-registration was preserved, enabling accurate mask propagation without spatial misalignment.

Radiomic features were extracted from SUV-normalized PET images rather than raw count data. This approach ensured quantitative comparability across patients and dose levels, accounting for injected activity, body weight, and uptake time. Although all dose levels in this study were retrospectively generated from the same list-mode acquisition, thus maintaining intrinsic reconstruction consistency, the SUV scaling provided a clinically interpretable framework for evaluating reproducibility trends. This normalization minimized confounding factors and enabled assessment of PVC and dose-dependent effects on a standardized quantitative scale.

From each segmented brain region, a total of 93 radiomic features were extracted using the PyRadiomics open-source Python package [[37]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0185), in accordance with the Image Biomarker Standardization Initiative (IBSI) guidelines. The features were organized into six categories ([Table 2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#t0010)):

*   •

*   •

*   •

All features were computed in the native PET image resolution, without interpolation or resampling. A fixed bin width (FBW) of 0.3 SUV units was applied during intensity discretization, ensuring comparability across dose levels while maintaining compliance with IBSI recommendations [[8]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0040). Shape-based features were excluded from the analysis, given the lack of anatomical boundaries and the focus on intensity-based heterogeneity.

### 4.2. Data harmonization

To minimize variations introduced by both reconstruction kernels and acquisition centers, radiomics feature harmonization was performed using the ComBat algorithm, a Bayesian empirical framework originally developed for genomics and later adapted to radiomics [[38]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0190), [[39]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0195). We applied the standard ComBat model (without covariates) implemented in Python, which estimates and corrects for location and scale effects at the feature level.

To further account for batch effects across acquisition centers, ComBat was applied with the center ID encoded as the batch variable. All radiomic features were considered as harmonization targets. ComBat harmonization was applied to the complete feature matrix prior to dimensionality reduction, using acquisition center as the sole batch variable. Dose level and PVC method were not treated as batch factors, as they represented experimental variables under investigation rather than unwanted sources of systematic bias. The procedure included: (i) data preparation, where the dataset was imported with acquisition center treated as the batch variable; (ii) matrix construction, transposing into a features×samples structure with a design matrix capturing batch membership; (iii) standardization, estimating the grand mean and pooled variance to remove scale differences; (iv) estimation of batch parameters, computing batch-specific mean (γ̂) and variance (δ̂) effects; (v) batch correction, subtracting estimated means and rescaling by variances before returning to the original measurement space; and (vi) output generation, producing a harmonized dataset for subsequent analysis.

Together, this procedure reduced unwanted variability arising from reconstruction settings and center effects while preserving biologically meaningful variation, thereby enhancing the comparability and robustness of downstream reproducibility analyses.

### 4.3. Feature stability analysis

To comprehensively assess the reproducibility of radiomic features, we combined three complementary classes of metrics. First, correlation-based agreement statistics (intraclass correlation coefficient, ICC) quantify absolute reproducibility of individual features across acquisition conditions. Second, geometry-aware drift metrics (PCA/UMAP embeddings) capture global structural consistency of the entire feature space in an unsupervised manner, highlighting systematic distortions induced by dose reduction, PVC correction, or harmonization. Finally, we implemented a supervised discriminant approach using LDA, where class labels were defined by dose level, PVC method, or harmonization state. LDA enables explicit testing of whether radiomics profiles can be linearly separated according to acquisition regime. To quantify separability, we calculated: (i) centroid distances between class means along LD1 and (ii) the Fisher’s discriminant ratio, which accounts for both between-class separation and within-class variance. Together, ICC, PCA/UMAP drift, and LDA provide complementary perspectives on reproducibility, spanning local feature agreement, global feature-space geometry, and supervised class separability.

#### 4.3.1. Correlation-based metric (ICC)

To quantify reproducibility of radiomic features, we used the ICC [[40]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0200) with the ICC(2,1) model, i.e., a two-way random-effects ANOVA with absolute agreement and single-measure formulation [[41]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0205). This model captures both systematic and random differences across conditions.

For a given feature, the ICC was computed from the two-way ANOVA mean squares as:(3)$\mathit{ICC} = \frac{\mathit{MSBS} - M S E}{\mathit{MSBS} + \left(K - 1\right) * M S E + \left(\frac{k}{n}\right) * \left(\right. M S B M - M S E \left.\right)}$where $\mathit{MSBS}$ is the mean square between subjects, $\mathit{MSBM}$ the mean square between measurements, $\mathit{MSE}$ the residual mean square, $n$ the number of subjects, and $k$ the number of measurement levels.

We applied ICC in two complementary ways:

> (i) ICC internal by dose (PVC-methods agreement at a fixed dose).

For each dose level (and harmonization status), we formed a Subjects×Methods matrix with columns (No-PVC, RL, RVC). Here the measurements factor in the ICC formula corresponds to methods ($k$=3). This ICC quantifies how consistently different PVC pipelines agree within the same dose level.

> (ii) ICC across doses (dose level agreement within a fixed PVC method).
> 
> 
> For each PVC method (and harmonization status), we formed a Subjects×Dose levels matrix with columns given by the available dose levels (e.g., (100%,50%,10%,2%)). Here the measurements factor corresponds to dose level. This ICC quantifies how stable a feature remains across dose levels under a fixed reconstruction pipeline.

For both analyses we required at least two subjects and two measurement levels; matrices were restricted to complete cases (rows with non-missing values across the selected columns). Features exhibiting zero variance within a given condition were excluded prior to reliability estimation, as ICC cannot be meaningfully computed for constant variables. Ninety-five percent confidence intervals (95% CI) for ICC (2,1) were calculated using the F-distribution method described by Shrout and Fleiss [[40]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0200). Detailed feature-level ICC estimates and confidence intervals are provided in the [Supplementary Material](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#s0105). Following common guidelines, we categorized ICC as Excellent (ICC≥0.90), Good (0.75≤ICC<0.90), Moderate (0.50≤ICC<0.75), and Poor (ICC<0.50). The internal-by-dose ICC probes method agreement at a fixed noise level, while the across-doses ICC probes dose robustness for a fixed method. Reporting both provides complementary views on feature reproducibility.

#### 4.3.2. Geometry-aware metric (PCA & UMAP Drift)

Traditional reproducibility metrics, such as ICC or rank correlation, evaluate each feature individually but do not capture global structural changes in the multidimensional feature space. To complement these measures, we implemented a geometry-aware drift analysis based on dimensionality reduction of the full feature matrix. This approach enables both visualization and quantification of systematic feature distortions across dose levels and PVC pipelines. For each PVC condition (No PVC, RL, RVC) and harmonization status (before, after), we constructed a matrix of size subjects×features using all extracted radiomic features. Features were z-score normalized to eliminate scale-related biases.

Two complementary embeddings were generated:

*   •
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) [[42]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0210): linear projection capturing maximal variance.

*   •
Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) [[43]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0215): nonlinear manifold learning preserving both local neighborhood structure and global geometry.

For nonlinear manifold learning, UMAP was applied to the standardized feature matrix without prior dimensionality reduction. The algorithm was configured with two embedding dimensions (n_components=2), n_neighbors=15, min_dist=0.1, Euclidean distance metric, and a fixed random_state=0 to ensure reproducibility. Two-dimensional embeddings were selected to enable intuitive geometric visualization and consistent computation of subject-wise drift distances across conditions.

Each row (subject at a given dose level) was mapped into a 2D space with coordinates ($P C 1$, $P C 2$) or (UMAP1, UMAP2). The reference dose was defined as the full-count acquisition (100%). For each subject, a drift vector was drawn from the 100% point to each reduced dose level (2%,10%,50%). The Euclidean drift distance was computed as:(4)$\Delta f = \sqrt{\left(\left(\right. \left(P C 1\right)_{d} - \left(P C 1\right)_{100} \left.\right)\right)^{2} + \left(\left(\right. \left(P C 2\right)_{d} - \left(P C 2\right)_{100} \left.\right)\right)^{2}}$where ($\left(P C 1\right)_{d}$, $\left(P C 2\right)_{d}$) are the reduced-dose coordinates and ($\left(P C 1\right)_{100}$, $\left(P C 2\right)_{100}$) the full-dose coordinates. Coordinates were computed after imputing missing feature values with feature-wise means. Distances were calculated only for subjects with valid measurements at both the reference and reduced dose levels. Smaller drift distances indicate higher stability of the global feature representation across doses. Larger drift distances reflect stronger systematic distortions induced by dose reduction or PVC method. By comparing drift distributions Before vs. After harmonization, we evaluated whether harmonization improves global stability of radiomic features. This geometry-aware analysis complements ICC and Spearman by assessing reproducibility not only at the per-feature level but also at the feature-space level, offering a more holistic view of radiomics robustness under dose-variation and PVC correction.

#### 4.3.3. Supervised metric (Linear discriminant Analysis, LDA)

While ICC and geometry-aware drift provide unsupervised perspectives on feature reproducibility, they do not directly test whether radiomics profiles can linearly discriminate between acquisition regimes. To complement these approaches, we implemented Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) [[44]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0220) as a supervised method. LDA projects high-dimensional features into a low-dimensional discriminant space that maximizes class separability by optimizing the ratio of between-class to within-class scatter.

Formally, given a set of feature vectors $\chi_{i} \in \mathbb{R}^{p}$ with class labels $y_{i} \in \left{\right. 1 , \hdots , C \left.\right}$, the LDA projection vector $\omega$ is obtained by solving:(5)$\omega = a r g \omega_{\mathit{max}} \frac{\omega^{T} S_{B} \omega}{\omega^{T} S_{W} \omega}$where $S_{B}$ is the between-class scatter matrix and $S_{W}$ is the within-class scatter matrix, defined as:(6)$S_{B} = \sum_{c = 1}^{C} n_{c} \left(\right. \mu_{c} - \mu \left.\right) \left(\left(\right. \mu_{c} - \mu \left.\right)\right)^{T} , S_{W} = \sum_{c = 1}^{C} \underset{x_{i} \in c}{\sum} \left(x_{i} - \mu_{c}\right) \left(x_{i} - \mu_{c}\right)^{T}$with $\mu_{c}$ the class mean, $n_{c}$ the number of samples in class $c$, and $\mu$ the global mean. The first linear discriminant axis (LD1) provides the maximal separation between classes. LDA was used to quantify linear separability between conditions via Fisher’s discriminant ratio rather than for predictive classification. Because no classification accuracy was estimated, cross-validation or train/test splitting was not performed.

We designed three LDA experiments:

*   Dose-based classification: classes=2%, 10%, 50%, 100%. Tests whether reduced-count images can be linearly distinguished from full-dose profiles.

*   PVC-based classification: classes = Non-PVC, RL, RVC. Tests whether partial volume correction methods systematically shift the feature space.

*   Harmonization-based classification: classes=Before vs. After ComBat. Tests whether harmonization introduces systematic separability.

To quantify separability, we computed two complementary metrics:

*   •
Centroid Distance (LD1):

(7)$D = \left|\mu_{1} - \mu_{2}\right|$where $\mu_{1}$ and $\mu_{2}$ are class means along LD1. Larger distances indicate greater class shifts.

*   •
Fisher’s Ratio (F):

(8)$F = \frac{\left(\left(\right. \mu_{1} - \mu_{2} \left.\right)\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{1}^{2} + \sigma_{2}^{2}}$where $\sigma_{1}^{2}$ and $\sigma_{2}^{2}$ are the within-class variances. Fisher’s ratio accounts for both centroid separation and class dispersion, providing a robust measure of discriminability.

For multi-class designs (dose, PVC), we reported mean pairwise distances and Fisher scores across all class pairs. For harmonization (binary design), we computed centroid distance and Fisher ratio separately within each PVC–dose combination. This supervised LDA framework complements ICC and PCA/UMAP by testing not only whether features shift, but whether these shifts create linearly separable regimes, thereby providing a stricter evaluation of reproducibility under dose variation, PVC correction, and harmonization.

### 4.4. Software and statistical analysis

All analyses were performed in Python (v3.11) using open-source libraries. Data handling and numerical computations used pandas and numpy; visualizations used matplotlib and seaborn. scikit-learn was employed for PCA, preprocessing, and Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA), while umap-learn was used for UMAP embeddings. The intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC, 2,1) was implemented following the two-way random-effects ANOVA model with absolute agreement, following the formulation of Shrout and Fleiss [[40]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0200). All workflows were executed in the Google Collaboratory cloud environment to ensure reproducibility and portability.

## 6. Discussion

Our analyses demonstrate that dose variation is the dominant driver of radiomics instability in total-body PET. Both ICC and geometry-aware metrics (PCA/UMAP drift) showed substantial degradation of reproducibility at reduced dose levels, with only a minority of features maintaining good agreement. This pattern is consistent with prior PET radiomics reproducibility studies reporting that reduced-count reconstructions and reconstruction-dependent settings disproportionately affect higher-order texture features, whereas first-order intensity metrics tend to remain relatively more robust[[45]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0225), [[46]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0230). Previous PET studies have shown that acquisition factors, including reconstruction parameters and PVC methods, can influence radiomics stability and reproducibility [[15]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0075), [[21]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0105). However, under low-count conditions, these corrections may instead amplify noise-driven distortions in the feature space. Our findings extend these observations to total-body PET systems, where the extended axial field-of-view does not eliminate dose-dependent distortions in high-dimensional feature space. Interestingly, at extreme low-count conditions [(2)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#e0010)%–10%), feature embeddings clustered more compactly, with drift distances averaging<5 units. This apparent stability likely reflects noise-induced homogenization, whereby high noise suppresses algorithmic differences across PVC methods rather than preserving meaningful biological heterogeneity. At full-dose acquisitions (100%), divergence between pipelines became more apparent, particularly for RVC, which exhibited the largest drift distances and>65% of features classified as poorly reproducible. Collectively, these findings emphasize that dose standardization or dose-aware feature selection is essential for reliable radiomics biomarkers.

The role of PVC proved more complex. Although PVC is generally intended to improve quantitative accuracy, our results demonstrate that its impact on radiomics stability is algorithm-dependent. Previous studies have reported heterogeneous effects of PVC on PET radiomics reproducibility. For example, Hasanabadi et al. demonstrated that RL and RVC corrections improved radiomics reproducibility in lymphoma lesions, particularly for larger tumors and structurally homogeneous tissues [[47]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0235)**.** RL and non-PVC reconstructions yielded broadly overlapping profiles, whereas RVC consistently degraded stability, producing the highest geometric drift and lowest ICC values. This differential behavior likely reflects their underlying mathematical formulations. RL is a maximum-likelihood–based iterative deconvolution method with non-negativity constraints, which may implicitly limit uncontrolled noise amplification during early iterations. In contrast, RVC relies on a linear residual-based update scheme that can enhance high-frequency noise components, particularly under low-count conditions. Because higher-order texture features are highly sensitive to local intensity fluctuations, such noise amplification may disproportionately distort the feature space. Taken together, these results indicate that PVC is a task-dependent tool: while beneficial for voxel-level quantitative recovery (e.g., SUV correction), certain deconvolution strategies may compromise stability in high-dimensional radiomics analyses. From a practical perspective, careful algorithm selection and transparent reporting are therefore essential, challenging the assumption that PVC universally improves reproducibility.

Harmonization via ComBat provided little measurable benefit. ComBat is designed to correct systematic batch effects (e.g., scanner-related shifts in feature distributions) rather than stochastic noise. In our study, dose reduction primarily introduced random noise amplification, which cannot be mitigated by statistical harmonization. This distinction likely explains the limited benefit of ComBat observed at ultra-low dose levels. Both unsupervised drift analysis and supervised LDA demonstrated that variability across dose levels, rather than inter-center or reconstruction differences, was the dominant driver of reproducibility loss. Notably, ComBat occasionally increased within-class variance (e.g., RL LD1 variance+71% at 2%), thereby reducing Fisher’s ratios and failing to improve separability. This limited benefit can be understood in light of ComBat’s underlying assumptions. In practice, such effects typically manifest as consistent shifts in feature means or variances across acquisition centers. In our setting, however, the principal source of variability was stochastic noise amplification induced by dose reduction. This phenomenon is well documented in low-dose PET imaging, where reduced count statistics substantially increase noise variability and degrade quantitative reliability [[48]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0240). Such random variance inflation alters the intrinsic signal-to-noise structure of radiomic features rather than introducing consistent center-specific biases. Consequently, statistical harmonization at the feature level cannot restore information degraded by low-count statistics. These findings suggest that linear harmonization may be insufficient for PET radiomics under noise-dominated conditions. More advanced strategies, such as generalized additive ComBat, deep learning-based domain adaptation, or representation-level harmonization, may be required to address complex and subject-specific distortions in PET radiomics [[49]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S174680942600861X#b0245). Future studies may further investigate the combined impact of spatial resampling and statistical harmonization to disentangle sampling-induced variability from true scanner-related batch effects.

A novel aspect of this study is the integration of unsupervised (PCA/UMAP drift) and supervised (LDA) frameworks, which at times produced contrasting insights. Unsupervised metrics revealed strong geometry shifts with dose reduction and RVC correction, while supervised LDA showed that these shifts did not translate into clean linear separability. Fisher’s ratios remained near zero, and centroid shifts were modest, indicating that changes are nonlinear and heterogeneous. This apparent contrast between supervised and unsupervised methods highlights an important message: reproducibility issues are real but structured in ways that are not linearly discriminable, underscoring the need for non-linear or subject-aware modeling approaches.

Our findings also highlight the uneven impact of acquisition variability across feature families. First-order intensity-based descriptors demonstrated relatively greater robustness, whereas higher-order texture features, such as those from GLRLM, GLDM, and GLSZM proved far more vulnerable to dose reduction and PVC differences. This imbalance suggests that reproducibility is not merely a global property of the feature space but is strongly feature-class dependent. In practice, this means that while simple intensity statistics may serve as more reliable biomarkers, advanced texture metrics—although potentially richer in biological information—carry a higher risk of instability and should therefore be subjected to stricter validation. The persistence of these patterns after ComBat harmonization further emphasizes that algorithmic corrections alone are insufficient to mitigate class-specific vulnerabilities, underscoring the importance of thoughtful feature selection in translational radiomics.

From a practical standpoint, these findings suggest that dose standardization should be prioritized in radiomics workflows, particularly when high-dimensional texture features are intended for modeling. The routine application of PVC in radiomics studies should be carefully justified, as certain algorithms may introduce instability despite improving voxel-level quantitative accuracy. Furthermore, reliance on statistical harmonization alone may be insufficient when underlying variability arises from stochastic noise rather than systematic scanner differences. Prospective radiomics studies may therefore benefit from incorporating dose-aware feature selection, robustness filtering, and standardized reconstruction protocols to ensure reliable translational performance.

This study has several limitations. First, analyses were conducted on metabolically active non-tumoral brain regions to provide a physiologically stable reference tissue. While this design facilitates isolation of acquisition-related variability, it does not capture the complex biological heterogeneity characteristic of oncologic lesions. Therefore, direct extrapolation of these findings to tumor radiomics applications (e.g., lung nodules or lymphoma) should be interpreted with caution and warrants further validation studies. Analyses were restricted to FDG PET acquisitions from two total-body PET systems, which may limit generalizability to other tracers or scanner platforms. In addition, radiomic features were extracted from SUV-normalized PET images to enable quantitative comparability across dose levels and ensure clinical interpretability. Although SUV is the standard metric for quantitative PET, our reproducibility analysis primarily relied on relative comparisons within the same acquisition session, thereby minimizing the influence of absolute scaling and inter-patient variability. In this setting, the use of SUV scaling was not expected to substantially alter reproducibility outcomes, as all dose levels originated from the same list-mode data and shared identical reconstruction parameters. Nevertheless, the application of this framework to additional SUV-derived radiomic features could provide complementary insights in future studies. Only two PVC methods (RL and RVC) were tested, and only one harmonization strategy (ComBat) was applied; alternative correction and harmonization methods may yield different stability profiles. Segmentation was performed using _iso_-intensity PET contours without anatomical references, such as CT or MRI, intentionally avoiding multimodal confounding but at the cost of reduced anatomical precision. Different thresholding strategies could potentially yield variable VOIs. In addition, segmentation was performed once on the full-dose reconstruction and propagated to all reduced-dose and PVC-corrected images to isolate acquisition-related variability. While this fixed-ROI strategy minimizes segmentation-induced confounding in reproducibility analyses, it does not capture the potential variability introduced by independent contouring in routine clinical practice. Therefore, our findings reflect acquisition-driven stability under controlled segmentation conditions and may underestimate the combined impact of dose and segmentation variability encountered in real-world settings. Shape-based radiomic features were excluded. Furthermore, the use of LDA imposes a linear separability assumption. While this provided a simple supervised benchmark, it may underestimate nonlinear or subject-specific effects in the feature space. Future work could incorporate nonlinear classifiers or representation learning approaches to capture more complex patterns. Finally, the apparent stability observed at ultra-low dose levels (2–10%) likely reflects noise-induced homogenization rather than preservation of meaningful biological heterogeneity, and should therefore be interpreted with caution.

Looking ahead, future work should expand to multi-tracer and multi-center cohorts, evaluate a broader spectrum of harmonization and PVC methods, and test nonlinear approaches (e.g., contrastive learning, domain adaptation, or mixed-effects modeling) for stability assessment. Critically, dose-stratified validation and reproducibility-first feature curation should become standard practice before radiomics enters robust clinical use.

## 7. Conclusion

This study demonstrates that radiomics reproducibility in dose-varied, PVC-corrected total-body PET is primarily driven by dose effects, with RVC correction further aggravating instability and ComBat harmonization offering little benefit. Analyses were based on SUV-normalized PET data, emphasizing relative within-scan reproducibility trends rather than absolute SUV magnitude. Within this framework, simple first-order features proved more robust, whereas higher-order texture families were disproportionately vulnerable to acquisition and processing variability. These findings challenge the assumption that PVC universally improves reproducibility, underscoring its task-dependent nature. Overall, reproducibility in total-body PET radiomics cannot be assumed and must be explicitly assessed, with dose-aware protocols, careful PVC selection, and rigorous feature validation representing essential steps toward reliable clinical translation.
