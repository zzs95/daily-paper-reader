## Abstract

Creating fully annotated labels for medical image segmentation is both time-consuming and costly, underscoring the need for efficient annotation schemes to alleviate the workload. Eye tracking offers an economical solution that can be seamlessly integrated into the clinicians’ workflow, providing relevant supervision for tasks. However, inaccuracies and ambiguity in gaze provide erroneous supervision for segmentation, and dynamic trajectories that encode rich temporal and structural context are not fully and effectively exploited, leading to underutilization of the semantic information embedded in gaze. This hinders the effectiveness of gaze-supervision and impairs the model’s ability to accurately delineate organ and lesion boundaries in ambiguous regions. To address these challenges, we propose the graph-based trajectory-informed network (GiTNet), which integrates static fixations with dynamic trajectories to comprehensively model complex anatomical relationships and potential lesion areas and constraint graph topology to strengthen the model’s ability to focus on anatomy- and lesion-related regions through the trajectory relational alignment (TRA). Additionally, we introduce neighbor-aware pseudo supervision (NAP), which incorporates the semantic information from neighboring nodes in the graph to reduce noise and uncertainty in gaze. Moreover, graph representational consistency (GRC) enhances the model’s ability to learn complex spatial structures and enhances supervision by applying perturbations and the consistency of nodes and edges. Experimental results demonstrate that the GiTNet outperforms existing state-of-the-art weakly supervised methods across two public datasets. Our code is available at [https://github.com/IPMI-NWU/GiTNet](https://github.com/IPMI-NWU/GiTNet).

## Introduction

Medical image segmentation plays a critical role in early disease detection, accurate diagnosis, and surgical planning by identifying relevant structures in medical images (Qureshi et al., 2023). Achieving high accuracy and robust generalization, however, often necessitates a large volume of annotated data (Shen et al., 2023). Since medical image annotations require the expertise of experienced clinicians, the process becomes not only time-consuming but also costly, posing a significant barrier to the widespread clinical adoption of medical image segmentation technologies (Tajbakhsh et al., 2020).

To mitigate annotation costs, weakly supervised learning has gained prominence in medical image segmentation. By leveraging less expensive and more readily accessible weak annotation modalities, such as bounding boxes (Wei et al., 2023), points (Zhai et al., 2023), or scribbles (Wong et al., 2025, Zhang and Zhuang, 2022a), models can receive sparse supervision. However, these approaches often rely on static annotations that fail to capture the cognitive patterns of clinicians (Zhong et al., 2024). Additionally, such annotation type still demands extra time from professional clinicians, disrupting clinical workflows and further increasing the burden on medical personnel (Wang et al., 2022).

Recent studies suggest that gaze data from clinical experts can serve as a unique and efficient source of weak supervision. Gaze, driven biologically, naturally reflects the clinician’s attention toward organ structures and potential lesions in medical images (Bhattacharya et al., 2022). This information not only reveals the regions of interest but also provides insight into how clinicians integrate features across different regions to make diagnostic decisions (Saab et al., 2021). Compared to traditional annotation techniques, gaze can be automatically recorded using eye trackers, making it both cost-effective and easily incorporated into clinical workflows (Zhong et al., 2024).

Nevertheless, gaze data presents several challenges. Although it provides insights into segmentation, effectively exploiting dynamic trajectories that encode temporal and structural relationships remains challenging. Current methods typically rely on static features, such as the position of fixations, as auxiliary priors, neglecting the dynamic gaze trajectories that hold greater diagnostic significance (Wang et al., 2022, Ma et al., 2023, Wang et al., 2024). Consequently, the model’s ability to comprehend the intricate relationships between anatomical regions is hindered, which affects the accurate identification of organ and lesion boundaries in ambiguous regions. In reality, gaze trajectories reflect clinicians’ image interpretation processes during complex visual analysis, and convey rich contextual and spatial structural information essential for medical image understanding and lesion localization (Bisogni et al., 2024).

Mainstream architectures, such as CNNs (He et al., 2016) and Transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), face limitations in modeling higher-order relationships between image regions. In contrast, GNNs have demonstrated significant potential in representing structured relationships and capturing internal spatial dependencies within images (Han et al., 2022). Building on this, we propose the graph-based trajectory-informed network (GiTNet), which comprehensively learns the clinician’s visual search behavior from gaze. GiTNet dynamically constructs graph structures based on the feature similarities between image regions, effectively capturing high-order relationships. Furthermore, we introduce a trajectory relational alignment (TRA) module that adjusts the spatial topology of the graph structure to match the clinician’s gaze path. This alignment enhances the model’s ability to perceive complex anatomical structures and potential lesions.

Moreover, gaze data inherently contains noise and ambiguity due to the influence of exploratory gaze and other human factors. While some methods have attempted to address this issue through multi-threshold strategies and model ensembles (Zhong et al., 2024, Chen et al., 2025), binarization often introduces erroneous labels, which weakens the reliability of supervisory signals and affects segmentation performance. To tackle this challenge, we divide the image into foreground, background, and uncertain regions, with the uncertain region encompassing areas where gaze information is ambiguous. We then propose a neighbor-aware pseudo supervision (NAP) module, which infers pseudo-labels for uncertain regions based on the features of neighboring nodes in the graph structure. NAP effectively mitigates the impact of weak label errors and improves the overall quality of supervision. Additionally, we introduce a graph representational consistency (GRC) module, which enforces the stability of graph structures under perturbations to enhance the model’s understanding of complex spatial structures and further strengthen the supervisory constraints.

The main contributions of this paper are as follows: (1) We propose GiTNet for gaze-supervised medical image segmentation, which constructs graph structures among image regions and constrains the spatial topology of the graph using dynamic trajectories, while incorporating static fixation to capture attention distribution. (2) Within GiTNet, the TRA module constrains the graph structure’s spatial topology to align with the clinician’s gaze trajectory, thereby enhancing the model’s ability to focus on anatomically relevant structures and lesion regions. (3) The NAP module infers labels for uncertain regions based on the semantic relationships of neighboring nodes in the graph. By incorporating the GRC to enforce the stability of graph structures under perturbations, we reinforce supervisory constraints and enhance the model’s ability to understand complex spatial structures. (4) Extensive experiments on two public datasets demonstrate that GiTNet outperforms existing weakly supervised medical image segmentation methods.

## Datasets and experimental details

Following the experimental setting of Zhong et al. (2024), GiTNet is evaluated on KvasirSEG (Jha et al., 2020) and NCI-ISBI (Bloch et al., 2013). The KvasirSEG dataset is utilized for polyp segmentation in gastrointestinal images, comprising 900 training images and 100 test images. The NCI-ISBI dataset is employed for prostate segmentation from T2-weighted MRI images, containing 789 training images and 117 test images. Gaze annotations are obtained from Zhong et al. (2024).
