## Abstract:

The latest advancements in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance in complex reasoning tasks. However, with the increasing complexity of problems and the diversification of modalities, MLLMs frequently encounter reasoning errors. To address this challenge, we propose SVCF (Self-Verification and Correction Framework), a novel zero-shot reasoning step correction mechanism. SVCF introduces a closed-loop system that integrates planning, reasoning, subproblem decomposition, and correction to identify and rectify intermediate reasoning errors dynamically. Within this framework, we design a knowledge decomposition module to generate subproblems, align them with reasoning steps, and inject refined prior knowledge to enhance the reasoning process. Furthermore, a self-verification and correction mechanism is employed to propagate corrections across the reasoning chain, which ensures global consistency and improves reasoning accuracy. Experiments on benchmark datasets (MathVista, MMMU, and MMMU-pro) demonstrate that SVCF significantly enhances answer accuracy.

SECTION 1.

## INTRODUCTION

In recent years, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have achieved remarkable progress in multiple research domains, attracting widespread attention from both academia and industry [1], [2]. These models leverage the language processing strengths of LLMs while incorporating visual inputs (e.g., images and videos), hence enabling cross-modal reasoning and supporting knowledge-intensive multimodal tasks.

Current multimodal reasoning methods, such as MMCoT [3], use staged architectures to achieve deep visual-linguistic alignment through fine-tuning of the language model. These models generate interpretable intermediate reasoning steps within the reasoning sequence and then derive the final answer. However, such methods usually exhibited cascading effects: all incorrect final answers contain at least one flawed intermediate reasoning step (as shown in Stage 2 in [Figure 1](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/)), while even correct answers may still contain latent errors in the reasoning process. As problem complexity and modality diversity grow, these errors become more prevalent.

Empirical analyses on the MMMU [4] dataset reveal that Reasoning Error and Perceptual Error are the primary errors for state-of-the-art MLLMs (e.g., GPT-4V [1]). Consequently, reducing reasoning errors has become a critical direction for improving the reliability of MLLMs.In the field of text, previous research has mitigated model reasoning errors through techniques such as knowledge verification and logical consistency checks [5], [6], [7]. However, these strategies have seldom been extended to multimodal scenarios.

To advance multimodal reasoning, this paper proposes SVCF (Self-Verification and Correction Framework), a zero-shot framework enabling closed-loop reasoning correction via task decomposition, stepwise reasoning, and iterative error mitigation. Unlike error-detection-only methods, SVCF validates and refines flawed reasoning steps using step-specific knowledge injection and fine-grained verification with global correction propagation. Experiments on MathVista [8], MMMU [4], and MMMU-pro [9] show that SVCF significantly reduces errors and achieves superior accuracy, confirming that localized correction of key steps suffices for reliable multimodal reasoning. The core innovations of this framework lie in two aspects:

*   An innovative knowledge decomposition approach is pro-posed in this research, which breaks down intricate problems into sub-problems that are in harmony with reasoning steps. Moreover, it successfully realizes the mapping and binding between sub-problems and reasoning steps, enabling the implementation of context-aware error identification and correction mechanisms.

*   A dynamic self-verification mechanism is capable of progressively advancing throughout the entire reasoning chain and implementing step-by-step correction, while propagating correction signals at each reasoning step to achieve step-level calibration.

## RELATED WORK

### 2.1. Multimodal Large Language Models

Recently, multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have achieved remarkable breakthroughs in addressing complex reasoning tasks within multimodal contexts, demonstrating robust task adaptability across benchmarks. For instance, GPT-4o [1] has attained over 90% accuracy in multimodal question answering on platforms like ScienceQA [10], [11], even surpassing human baselines. However, model performance tends to degrade with increasing modality diversity and task complexity. To enhance reasoning ability, we propose the SVCF framework, which can effectively reduce inherent errors or illusions in the model architecture.

### 2.2. Framework Overview

### 2.3. Complex Problem Decomposition

In complex problem-solving, decomposition strategies—breaking tasks into subproblems for progressive resolution—have inspired two research approaches: (1) structured frameworks (e.g., Least-to-Most [12], ToT [13], GoT [14]) to enhance model reasoning, and (2) innovative mechanisms like DeAR [15] ’s iterative decomposition-analysis-reflection for dynamic reasoning trees, outperforming benchmarks. Beyond simplifying complexity, decomposition enables information augmentation (e.g., DDCOT [16] uses GPT-4 decomposition with BliP2 [17] to surpass caption-based visual reasoning [18]). Our study leverages decomposition as an information enhancement tool, refining initial reasoning via supplementary knowledge from decomposition. This orthogonal verification mechanism synergizes with traditional strategies, forming a robust hybrid reasoning framework.

### 2.4. Self Correction of Models

Self-correction has been regarded as a uniquely human cognitive trait, and endowing models with dynamic content modification capabilities remains a key research objective [19], [20]. However, achieving this poses significant technical challenges [21]. Indirect approaches, such as verifying and filtering reasoning paths under self-consistency [22] constraints—retaining only validated paths and aggregating outputs via majority voting [7], [6] — have shown limited progress in improving accuracy but remain fundamentally "filter-based" methods lacking dynamic content modification. Other studies incorporate node-level validation mechanisms (e.g., ToT [13]), which prune non-compliant reasoning nodes and select feasible paths, yet these still represent "selective decision-making" rather than dynamic optimization of generated content.

## SELF-VERIFICATION AND CORRECTIONFRAMEWORK

### 3.1. Problem Formulation

In the research paradigm of Multimodal Question Answering (MQA) tasks, the task can be formally defined as a quadruplet (_Q,C,O,A_), where _Q_ represents the target question, _C_ = (_c_ text _,c_ image) denotes the multimodal context (comprising textual context _c_ text and image context _c_ image), _O_ = (_o_ 1 _,o_ 2 _,…_) represents the candidate options (which is an empty set if the question is open-ended), and _A_ signifies the ground-truth answer to the question. Given _Q_, _C_, and _O_ as inputs, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) generate a serialized reasoning process _T_ = {_T_ 1 _,T_ 2 _,…,T N_} to derive the final answer.

As shown in [Figure 2](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/), this framework takes the triplet (_Q,C,O_) from the quadruplet (_Q,C,O,A_) as input. The overall mathematical formalization can be expressed as: 
T=Ψ(M θ,Q,C,O)(1)

### 3.2. Planing and Reasoning

Given the input (_Q,C,O_), the model _M θ_ generates a hierarchical reasoning plan P={P i}n i=1 (with _P i_ representing the _i_-th planning node) under the guidance of a structured prompt _prompt P._ Formally, the planning step can be expressed as: 
P=f Plan(M θ,Q,C,O,p r o m p t P)(2)

### 3.3. Knowledge Decomposition

Current verification methods predominantly rely on a restricted subset of prior knowledge from the reasoning phase, which restricts models from generating contradictory conclusions during verification and thus limits effective error correction. To address this, we propose a planning-based subproblem decomposition method that offers dual advantages: it reduces the original problem’s complexity, enabling more precise knowledge matching during prior knowledge reacquisition; it establishes explicit correlations between subproblems and inference steps, allowing targeted correction feedback for relevant subproblems while avoiding irrelevant information interference.

#### 3.3.1. Sub-problem Decomposition Based on Planning Nodes

Building on prior research demonstrating that explicitly defining step-specific prior knowledge enhances verification accuracy, this study decomposes the original problem using planning steps _P_ derived in [Section 3.3](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/). To ensure strong alignment between sub-problems and planning steps, we incorporate the following guiding prompt in _prompt D_: "_By following the established planning steps and decomposing the original problem into as many necessary sub-problems as possible_". Formally, this decomposition process can be represented as: 
{SubQA i}m i=1=f Decompose(M θ,Q,C,O,P,p r o m p t D)(4)

View Source![Image 7: Right-click on figure for MathML and additional features.](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/assets/img/icon.support.gif) where SubQA _i_ represents the _i th_ sub-problem, including the question SubQ _i_ and answer SubA _i._

#### 3.3.2. Association Sequence Establishment

During the problem decomposition phase, each sub-problem will be precisely associated with specific execution steps, aiming to strictly limit the relevance to the minimum necessary scope, thereby effectively suppressing the interference of irrelevant information. Through a designed prompt prompt _M_, we achieve a precise association between each sub-question SubQ _i_ (_i_ = 1 _,…,m_) and specific planning nodes, and accordingly construct an association sequence Contact list. Specifically, the _i_-th element in this sequence precisely corresponds to the step number associated with the _j_-th sub-problem, forming a mapping between the inference step and the sub-problem. This association sequence Contact list will serve as a key input for the subsequent model update and optimization process in the correction stage. Formally, this association mechanism can be expressed as: 
C o n t a c t _ l i s t i=f Contact(M θ,SubQ j,P,prompt M)(5)

SubQ _i_ is the question of the _i_-th sub-problem. _P_ is the planning set {P i}n i=1, which contains problem contexts.

### 3.4. Self-Verification and Correction

In the verification and correction stage, we adopt a refined strategy by limiting the prior knowledge necessary for each reasoning step, guiding the model to first determine whether the current step requires correction and then provide the corrected reasoning content. To ensure logical coherence between reasoning steps and effectively propagate the corrections made to subsequent steps, we deliberately consider the previous reasoning steps R 1 to R _n_−1 when performing correction operations.

SECTION 4.

## EXPERIMENT

In this section, we substantiate the generality and efficacy of the proposed method by verifying it on three multi-modal datasets, encompassing both knowledge-based reasoning, mathematical reasoning and only-vision reasoning.

### 4.1. Dataset and Baseline

In experiments, three datasets were used for model evaluation. MathVista, a multimodal math problem-solving benchmark integrating text, formulas, and visuals, assesses cross-domain reasoning in algebra, geometry, and probability; we used its testmini subset.MMMU covers six disciplines with 11,500 questions split into development (150), validation (900), and test (10,450) sets for evaluating multimodal perception, knowledge, and reasoning; we chose the validation set. MMMU-pro, an enhanced MMMU version, filters text-answerable questions, adds options, and introduces visuals for rigorous cross-disciplinary reasoning testing; we focused on its vision-only subset to evaluate SVCF under pure visual conditions.

**Baseline:** We selected GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini, and the open-source InterVL2.5-8B as baselines, evaluating their performance under Chain of Thought (CoT) reasoning without random selection. Results were reported on MathVista (testmini), MMMU (validation), and MMMU-pro (vision) benchmarks, with additional references to leaderboard rankings and VLMEvalKit for comparative analysis. These models demonstrate varying capabilities in handling multi-modal mathematical and interdisciplinary reasoning tasks.

Previous models employed a random selection mechanism (RC) [4], [8], where mismatched answers triggered arbitrary option selection as final outputs. However, applying RC to our framework is problematic, as deviations from predefined options should prompt answer correction rather than randomness. Thus, we replaced RC with a stricter judgment strategy (NRC): answers not matching options are directly deemed incorrect, eliminating reliance on random decisions.

### 4.2. Implementation Details

In this study, we selected GPT-4o (0513), GPT-4omini(0718), and InterVL2.5-8B as the core foundational models. Given the constraints of resource availability, in experimental setups, we employed InterVL as the correction model (owing to its higher invocation frequency).

In line with the strategy employed by VLMevalKit, when assessing the accuracy of answers, we utilized GPT-4omini as the validation tool to circumvent issues of string matching failure arising from unpredictable model outputs. All experiments were conducted on a device equipped with a 4090D graphics card, and the deployment of the InterVL model was accomplished through the LMDeploy [23] framework.

### 4.3. Main Results

[Table 1](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/) shows the key experimental results of this study. Notably, without RC or frequent selection strategies, the results are lower than those on dataset leaderboards and the VLMevalKit platform. This performance gap has dual sensitivity: it is inversely related to model capability and directly related to dataset difficulty. That is, as model representational ability weakens and task complexity rises, performance degradation becomes more pronounced. Comparative experiments reveal that after incorporating the proposed SVCF framework, the system’s performance on the two target datasets improves significantly. Especially on the MathVista dataset, the framework not only outperforms the baseline of generative models but also exceeds the correction model’s performance limit, providing robust empirical support for its effectiveness.

By comparing publicly available leaderboard data, the study found notable performance adaptability between models and datasets: InterVL excelled in MathVista tasks, while GPT-4o series models (GPT-4o and GPT-4o-mini) performed better in MMMU and MMMU-pro tasks. this study uniformly configured InterVL as the result-correction model. Experimental results indicate that this model collaboration strategy achieved a gain effect on both the MathVista and MMMU datasets. This discovery not only validates the effectiveness of the model role assignment strategy but also reveals the critical role of task adaptability in optimizing model performance—even when not strictly adhering to the conventional configuration of "the optimal model acting as the corrector," reasonable role division can still effectively enhance the overall system performance.

SECTION 5.

## CONCLUSION

In this article, we propose SVCF, a universal zero-shot stepwise Verification and correction framework tailored for multi-modal large language models. Unlike previous methods, SVCF actively obtains detailed information through subproblems without the need for any additional data or external resources, achieving error identification and correction during the inference process. The re-prompting mechanism utilizing the correction reasoning process significantly improves the accuracy of multi-modal large-scale models on multi-modal question-answering datasets.
