## Abstract:

Large language models (LLMs) have advanced in medical question answering, yet ensuring reliability remains challenging. We study multi-agent debate (MAD), where multiple LLM agents critique and aggregate each other’s answers. Our game-theoretic analysis shows that majority voting improves accuracy, confidence-weighted aggregation (e.g., with an LLM judge) is optimal under calibration, and early stopping yields efficiency with little loss. We implement MAD for multiple-choice medical questions (MedQA) and evaluate it across five frontier language models, comparing single-shot prompting, chain-of-thought (CoT), and MAD with 2 or 3 agents. MAD with 3 agents consistently outperforms single-shot and CoT, reaching 90% accuracy on Claude Sonnet 3.7 (vs. 88% with CoT). Moreover, early consensus allows most debates to end in early rounds, reducing latency. These results align with theory and suggest MAD as a promising path toward more accurate and trustworthy medical QA systems.

SECTION 1.

## INTRODUCTION

Recent advances in large language models have led to impressive performance on medical question answering tasks [1], including the USMLE medical board exams. For example, GPT-4 has demonstrated expert-level scores on medical benchmarks without domain-specific tuning [2]. Despite this progress, ensuring _reliability and accuracy_ of answers in clinical applications remains critical. A single LLM reasoning in isolation may produce incorrect or uncalibrated answers, which is problematic in high-stakes domains like healthcare. One promising direction to improve reliability is to have multiple _agents_ reason and debate, cross-examining each other’s conclusions before finalizing an answer.

In this paper, we investigate whether a _MAD_ framework can improve clinical QA. The key idea is that multiple agents bring diverse reasoning paths and can catch each other’s mistakes. When agents are more likely than not to be correct, aggregating their answers by majority vote amplifies accuracy, echoing the Condorcet jury effect. If agents also estimate confidence, a LLM judge-based aggregation can further refine decisions. We additionally propose an _early stopping_ rule: if strong consensus emerges early, the debate terminates, saving computation.

Our contributions are threefold: (I) We formalize MAD as a cooperative game and prove basic guarantees: majority voting improves accuracy (Theorem 1), and confidence-weighted aggregation is optimal under calibration (Theorem 2). (II) We design an algorithmic framework where multiple Claude agents adopt distinct personas, exchange arguments for limited rounds, and either vote or defer to a judge, with an early-stopping rule to improve efficiency (Proposition 1). (III) On the MedQA dataset [3], MAD with 3 agents consistently outperforms single-shot and chain-of-thought prompting across five Claude variants. For example, Claude Sonnet 3.7 reaches 90% accuracy with MAD vs. 88% with CoT, the strongest result in our tests. Analysis of debate dynamics further shows high early-stop rates (up to 85%) and improved confidence, aligning with our theoretical predictions.

SECTION 2.

## RELATED WORK

_Prompting and chain-of-thought reasoning:_ Prompting techniques [4] that elicit intermediate reasoning steps have improved the performance of single-agent QA. [5] introduced Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting, which enables LLMs to break down problems into step-by-step reasoning, significantly boosting accuracy on complex tasks. Building on CoT, [6] proposed a _self-consistency_ decoding strategy: by sampling multiple reasoning paths and taking a majority vote among their answers [7], one can ensemble the model with itself and improve reliability. These works [8], [9] inspire our baseline comparisons (CoT and self-consensus) and highlight the benefit of aggregating multiple reasoning attempts, albeit from one model.

_Multi-agent debate and collaboration:_ Engaging multiple agents in deliberation has been explored to enhance factual accuracy and alignment. [10] first proposed AI _debate_ as a safety technique, where two agents argue and a human judge decides the winner, incentivizing truthful answers. [11] systematically evaluated multi-agent debate strategies (MAD) and found that naive MAD did not always outperform self-consistency, but with careful prompt design and hyperparameter tuning, certain debate protocols could surpass other methods. Our work focuses on the clinical QA domain and introduces a game-theoretic perspective to explain when and why MAD should help. We also differ by using a cooperative debate (agents share the goal of correctness) rather than a competitive one, and by incorporating role diversity and early stopping. _Medical question answering:_ The MedQA dataset [3] provides a challenging benchmark of multiple-choice medical exam questions. Recent large models [12], [13], [14] have made great strides on this task: e.g., fine-tuned models like Med-PaLM and general models like GPT-4 reach or exceed the passing score on USMLE-style questions [2]. However, ensuring the _reasoning process_ and _justifications_ are trustworthy is as important as final accuracy in the medical domain. Approaches like ours that facilitate explanation, cross-examination among models, and calibrated confidence estimation could complement pure accuracy gains by providing insights into _why_ an answer is chosen and how confident the system is, potentially increasing clinician trust.

SECTION 3.

## PROBLEM SETUP

We focus on multiple-choice clinical question answering, specifically the USMLE-style questions from MedQA. Each question consists of a prompt (medical scenario or question) _x_ and a set of answer options _A_ = _{A 1, A 2, ..., A K_} (typically _K_ = 4 or 5). Only one option _a∗_ ∈ _A_ is correct. A language model (agent) given the question can produce an answer _y_ ∈ _A_ along with an optional rationale or explanation _r._ We consider different prompting strategies for an agent:

_Single-shot_: The agent directly produces an answer with no intermediate reasoning shown.

_Chain-of-Thought (CoT)_: The agent is prompted to produce a step-by-step reasoning ("think step by step") and then an answer.

_Multi-Agent Debate (MAD)_: Multiple agents discuss the question and options, possibly over several rounds, before delivering a final answer via some aggregation rule.

For the MAD approach, we instantiate _N_ agents (each an instance of a Claude model in our experiments) denoted 1, 2, ..., _N._ All agents receive the same question _x_, but we assign them different _roles_ or personas to encourage diverse points of view (e.g., one agent prioritizes standard clinical guidelines, another favors rare diagnoses (boldness), etc.). Each agent _i_ produces an initial answer y(0)i∈A with a rationale r(0)i . Then, the agents enter a debate phase for at most _T_ rounds: in each round, agents share their current answers and reasoning with each other and have an opportunity to revise their answers based on their peers’ arguments. The debate ends either when a predefined number of rounds _T_ is reached, or when an _early stopping condition_ is met (e.g., all agents agree on an answer). Finally, a _social choice rule f_ is applied to the agents’ final answers {y(T)i} to produce the overall output _a∗._ In this work, we consider two choice rules: (i) simple majority vote, and (ii) weighted majority vote where agents’ votes are weighted by their self-reported confidence. Using an LLM as the judge provides an alternative aggregation mechanism, where the model can incorporate agents’ confidence levels and reasoning when available. While this is conceptually similar to a confidence-weighted voting scheme, it is not limited to a numerical weighting rule and instead leverages the judge’s own reasoning capabilities. Our setup assumes that all agents ultimately share the same goal of identifying the correct answer (cooperative game). We next describe the MAD algorithm in detail, followed by theoretical guarantees.

## GAME-THEORETIC FORMALIZATION

We model the multi-agent debate as a cooperative game where each agent’s strategy is to propose (and update) an answer based on available information, and the payoff is tied to whether the final chosen answer is correct. The social choice rule (voting or judge) serves as the mechanism aggregating individual actions into a final decision. This framework allows us to analyze conditions for success. We outline our Multi-Agent Debate procedure in [Algorithm 1](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/).

Let each agent _i_ have an inherent probability _p i_ = Pr(_y i_ is correct) of choosing the correct answer on its own (this could reflect the agent’s accuracy in single-shot mode). For our theoretical analysis, we make the simplifying assumption that agents’ errors are _independent_ and that _p i_> 0.5 should be interpreted relative to random guessing in a multiclass setting, by viewing multiclass voting as a collection of one-vs-rest (each agent is better than random guessing). Although in practice agents are not strictly independent (especially if they use the same model backbone), this assumption yields insight into best-case benefits of ensembling. We present three key theoretical results (proofs are sketched intuitively):

**Theorem 1** (Majority Voting Improves Accuracy). _If N agents make independent errors and each agent has accuracy p i_> 0.5, _then the probability that the majority vote among them is correct exceeds each individual p i. Moreover, as N increases, the majority vote accuracy approaches 1.0._

_Proof Sketch._ This is a variant of Condorcet’s Jury Theorem. The probability that the majority vote is correct equals ∑N k=[N/2](N k)(p)k(1−p)N−k for identical _p i_ = _p._ For _p_> 0.5, this sum is greater than _p_ (since the most likely outcome is a correct majority). As _N → ∞_, by the Law of Large Numbers the fraction of correct votes will exceed 0.5 with probability approaching 1, so the majority outcome converges to correct. □

Theorem 1 formalizes that using multiple agents and taking a majority vote can amplify accuracy, provided agents are individually somewhat competent. This justifies why MAD can outperform a single model’s chain-of-thought in principle. Next, we consider the benefit of weighting votes by confidence. Suppose each agent can output a confidence estimate _q i_ (e.g., a probability that its answer is correct). We say the agent is _calibrated_ if on average, when it reports confidence _q i_, it is correct about _q i_ fraction of the time.

**Theorem 2** (Weighted Aggregation with Calibrated Agents). _If agents have calibrated confidence scores for their answers, then using a weighted voting scheme (where agent i’s vote is weighted by a monotonic function of its confidence q i) is at least as effective as unweighted majority voting, and strictly better when confidences differ. In particular, the Bayes-optimal aggregator in this setting chooses the answer that maximizes ∑i:y i=a log p i 1−p i , which for calibrated pi reduces to weighting by confidence log-odds._

_Proof Sketch._ Intuitively, an agent with higher confidence (assuming calibration) has a higher probability of being correct; giving more weight to its vote improves the chance of picking the correct answer. Formally, one can show that the weighted majority rule that maximizes the probability of correct final decision is to weight votes by log(p i 1−p i) (this comes from a log-likelihood ratio test for the correctness of a candidate answer, treating each agent as providing evidence). Calibrated confidence means _q i_ = _p i_, so weights are monotonic in _q i._ This rule reduces the probability of error compared to treating all votes equally. □

Theorem 2 suggests that if agents can estimate their correctness likelihood (e.g., outputting confidence score, which often correlates with accuracy [2]), then aggregating their answers with these weights can improve over simple majority voting. In practice, we report results primarily with majority voting, but note that models with higher average confidence also achieve higher accuracies (see [Section 5](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/)), indirectly supporting this principle. We further analyze the efficiency of an early stopping rule in debate: at each round, if enough agents agree, the process terminates. While one might worry that early stopping risks premature convergence on an incorrect answer, in practice convergence is more likely when the agents are correct (since their signals align more strongly), so the rule can reduce rounds substantially with little loss in accuracy.

**Proposition 1** (Early Stopping Efficiency). _Assume that when the agents unanimously (or majority) agree on an answer by round t, the agreed answer has a high probability of being correct (i.e., consensus is a strong indicator of correctness). Then an early stopping rule that terminates the debate as soon as consensus is reached yields an expected time complexity (number of rounds) that is significantly lower than T for easy or clear questions, while having a negligible effect on overall accuracy. More formally, if P consensus_(_t_) _is the probability that consensus is reached by round t, and most of this consensus is correct, then expected rounds_ E[t]=∑T t=1 t⋅P c o n s e n s u s(t)_is much smaller than T when early consensus is likely._

_Proof Sketch._ When agents reach consensus early, typically on easier questions, the debate ends after one round, while only the harder cases continue. Since early consensus is usually correct, accuracy is preserved while expected rounds are greatly reduced, yielding efficiency gains. A rigorous proof would model the debate as a sequential probability ratio test (SPRT), where stopping times reflect evidence accumulation. But empirically, we validate this by showing high stop rates with minimal accuracy drop. □

Proposition 1 establishes that, under mild assumptions, introducing an early stopping criterion substantially reduces the number of debate rounds—and thus computational and token costs—while preserving accuracy. Empirical results confirm this behavior: for stronger models, majority consensus is typically achieved after the initial exchange of arguments, permitting early termination in most instances.

SECTION 5.

## RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

[Table 1](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/) reports the accuracy of each model under different prompting strategies. Multi-agent debate with 3 agents (MAD-3) achieves the highest accuracy for every model, outperforming both single-shot and CoT prompting. For example, the strongest model, _Claude Sonnet 3.7_, reaches 0.90 accuracy with MAD-3, compared to 0.88 with CoT and 0.84 with a single-shot answer, making it the best-performing configuration in our experiments. _Claude Sonnet 3.5 v2_ improves from 0.80 (CoT) to 0.86 (MAD-3), a +6% absolute gain. Even smaller models (Claude 3 Haiku and 3.5 Haiku) see improvements of 5–8 percentage points from single or CoT to MAD-3. Using two agents (MAD-2) also improves over single-shot and often over CoT, but MAD-3 yields the most consistent and significant gains. These results confirm Theorem 1, which predicts that majority voting improves accuracy, and suggest that debate helps correct individual mistakes—one agent can flag flaws in another’s reasoning, leading to convergence on the correct answer.

[Figure 1](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/) further illustrates debate dynamics under MAD-3 by summarizing early stopping, rounds, and confidence. For stronger models, a large fraction of questions reached consensus after one round. For instance, Claude Sonnet 3.7 had an early stop rate of 64% with an average of 1.44 rounds, while Claude 3.5 Haiku achieved 78% with 1.22 rounds. Such high early-stop rates demonstrate that _debate often converges quickly_, validating our early stopping rule and supporting Proposition 1. When models are confident and mostly correct, they agree immediately, avoiding unnecessary discussion.

We also observe that stronger models are both more accurate and more decisive. Claude Sonnet 3.7 and 3.5 v2 self-report 90–93% average confidence, while Claude 3 Haiku reports about 86%. This aligns with Theorem 2: confidence can serve as a useful weight in aggregation. In practice, when all agents agreed quickly, they were almost always correct. Disagreements arose mainly on harder questions, but the second round often allowed two agents to align on the correct option, still improving accuracy over single agents.

MAD is especially beneficial for ambiguous questions with multiple plausible differentials, pharmacology or safety constraints, and multi-factor trade-offs. The main drawback is cost, but early stopping and adaptive routing (invoking MAD only when a confidence proxy is low) can capture most of the accuracy gains at reduced cost. Finally, correlation among agents (e.g., from similar prompts) limits gains, though role diversity and randomized seeds can mitigate this issue.

SECTION 6.

## CONCLUSION

We presented a game-theoretic and empirical study of MAD for clinical QA. Our analysis established that majority voting improves accuracy, confidence-weighted aggregation is theoretically optimal under calibration, and early stopping yields efficiency with minimal loss. Experiments on MedQA with five Claude models confirmed these predictions: MAD with three agents consistently outperformed single-shot and CoT prompting, achieving up to 90% accuracy on Claude Sonnet 3.7. Debate dynamics further showed that stronger models are more confident and decisive, with high early-stop rates leading to reduced latency while preserving accuracy.

Overall, MAD offers a practical framework for improving reliability in medical system. It is particularly effective on ambiguous questions involving differential diagnoses, safety constraints, or multi-factor trade-offs. While cost remains a limitation, early stopping and adaptive routing can capture most of the gains at lower expense. Future work will focus on formalizing debate as a sequential decision-making process, investigating role diversity and randomized strategies to reduce agent correlation, extending MAD to broader domains requiring trustworthy LLM reasoning, and evaluating heterogeneous agent configurations.
