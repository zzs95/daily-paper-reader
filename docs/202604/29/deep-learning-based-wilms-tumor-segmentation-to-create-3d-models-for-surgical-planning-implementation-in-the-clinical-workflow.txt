Title: Deep learning-based Wilms tumor segmentation to create 3D models for surgical planning: Implementation in the clinical workflow

URL Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022346826002344

## Abstract

### Background and aim

Creating 3D models based on pre-operative MRI of patients with a Wilms tumor (WT) can aid surgical planning. However, creating these models requires manual delineation (segmentation) of the MRI imaging. Deep learning can automate this, but most validations of these segmentation methods are retrospective. This article prospective evaluation of a WT segmentation method in a clinical workflow aimed at creating 3D models for surgical planning.

### Methods

A deep learning-based segmentation method was developed and retrospectively validated on a dataset of 56 patients. This method, based on nnU-Net, segmented both kidney and tumor and was implemented within the current clinical workflow. It was tested prospectively on 10 consecutive patients with WT. The performance of this method was quantified using the Dice score between the automated and corrected segmentations, the time required for the various steps of the clinical workflow and an analysis of segmentation errors.

### Results

In 2/10 patients the automated segmentation was sufficient to be used directly. In 8/10 patients the automated segmentation needed corrections (with a median correction time of 11 minutes). The median Dice score for kidney was 1.00 (range: 0.58 – 1.00) and for tumor 0.98 (range: 0.00 – 1.00). The segmentation errors identified most often were an under-segmentation of tumor borders (n = 3) and the incorrect identification of the tumor/kidney border (n = 3).

### Conclusion

The implementation of an automated segmentation method for creating 3D models of patients with a WT is feasible in current clinical workflows. In 20% of the patients, no corrections were needed and for most other patients, corrections could be applied in less than 15 minutes.

## Introduction

Wilms tumor (WT) is one of the most prevalent solid tumors in childhood, with a survival rate of up to 90% [1], [2]. Although WT treatment often includes chemotherapy and radiotherapy, surgery remains a cornerstone. Surgical removal of WT can entail total nephrectomy (TN), where the entire kidney including the tumor is removed, or nephron-sparing surgery (NSS), where only the tumor is removed. NSS aims to preserve as much kidney function as possible while completely removing the tumor, which is essential in patients with bilateral or syndromal disease. However, recent studies suggest that NSS might also be of importance in patients with a unilateral non-syndromic WT to optimize long-term kidney function [3].

To prevent adverse outcomes in NSS, such as incomplete resections, it is essential to have a clear understanding of the patient-specific anatomy. Imaging techniques such as magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) or computed tomography (CT) are essential in surgical planning, as these provide information about tumor size and location of the tumor relative to important structures like the urine collection system (UCS) and renal veins and arteries. Creating 3D models of the kidney, tumor, and other relevant anatomical structures has been shown to enhance surgical orientation and planning [4], [5]. However, creating these 3D models from MRI is a primarily manual process of delineation (e.g. segmentation) of the MR images, which is user-dependent and time-consuming [6], [7].

Automating the segmentation process of the kidney and tumor decreases segmentation duration, simplifying the clinical implementation. Deep learning (DL), a subset of artificial intelligence (AI) techniques, can be used to automate a wide range of tasks related to medical imaging such as segmentation of structures on MRI [8], [9]. DL-based segmentation methods require training on a dataset with labelled examples, in this case MRI scans with manually created segmentations (the ground truth segmentations). The trained DL-based segmentation method can than create automated segmentations on other scans. Good practice is to develop these techniques using one part of the dataset (the training dataset) and validating it on a different part (the test dataset). The performance of the segmentation method is validated by comparing the automated segmentations created on the test dataset with the ground truth segmentations. Several validation parameters can be calculated from this comparison, including parameters quantifying the overlap between the automated segmentation and the ground truth segmentation.

Only a few studies have focused on using DL to segment WT on MRI [6], [7]. Although several methods have demonstrated high validation scores, the performance of these methods in clinical practice has not been investigated [6], [7]. Factors such as the time investment of using automated methods in practice or the effect of using it in a cohort of unselected patients remain unknown. Furthermore, while retrospective validation scores offer a general idea of segmentation quality, they do not capture potential differences in clinical usability between segmentations with similar validation scores. For example, if a part of the liver is segmented instead of WT, this might be clinically less relevant than if the tumor/kidney border is unclear, as the first is easier to detect and correct by a human observer. To bridge the gap between technical validation and clinical implementation, it is essential to evaluate the developed WT segmentation method within the clinical workflow.

The aim of this study was to evaluate the use of an in-house developed, DL-based segmentation algorithm for kidney and tumor in a prospective series of WT patients, integrated into our current clinical workflow for creating anatomical 3D models. We also provided the code and a detailed user manual online to enable other healthcare professionals to apply and test the method in their own clinical workflows.
