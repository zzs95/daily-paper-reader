## Abstract 

Vision-language models (VLMs) have achieved remarkable success across diverse tasks. However, concerns about their trustworthiness persist, particularly regarding tendencies to lean more on textual cues than visual evidence and the risk of producing ungrounded or fabricated responses. To ad-dress these issues, we propose Saliency-R1 , a framework for improving the interpretability and faithfulness of VLMs reasoning. Specifically, we introduce a novel saliency map technique that efficiently highlights critical image regions contributing to generated tokens without additional compu-tational overhead. This can further be extended to trace how visual information flows through the reasoning process to the final answers, revealing the alignment between the thinking process and the visual context. We use the overlap between the saliency maps and human-annotated bounding boxes as the reward function, and apply Group Relative Pol-icy Optimization (GRPO) to align the salient parts and crit-ical regions, encouraging models to focus on relevant areas when conduct reasoning. Experiments show Saliency-R1 improves reasoning faithfulness, interpretability, and over-all task performance. The code is available at https: //github.com/peterant330/Saliency_R1 .

## 1. Introduction 

Vision-language models (VLMs) have made impressive strides, demonstrating strong performance on tasks that re-quire joint reasoning over images and text. As a result, VLMs have been widely adopted in various domains, in-cluding high-stakes areas such as autonomous driving [15], finance [36], and medical diagnosis [26]. Despite their su-perior performance, the trustworthiness of VLMs has raised concerns. For example, prior work show that VLMs often over-rely on textual cues, with visual signals playing a com-paratively smaller role [29, 53]. To build trust, VLMs are frequently prompted to gen-erate natural language explanations (NLEs) that describe their reasoning. Recent reasoning-based language models, such as DeepSeek-R1 [28] and Claude-Sonnet [5], are explicitly trained to provide detailed reason-ing traces. However, these NLEs still have limitations. faithfulness of VLM reasoning. We introduce a saliency-map method that visualizes how visual information influ-ences generated tokens. Specifically, we decompose token logits into first-order contributions [21] from all context tokens and render the contributions from visual tokens as saliency maps. These maps highlight image regions most relevant to each generated token and require no extra for-ward or backward passes, making the approach computa-tionally efficient. We further extend the framework to trace how visual tokens propagate to answer tokens through the thinking tokens as a bottleneck, revealing image regions that drive both the reasoning process and the final answer. As illustrated in Fig. 1, different reasoning traces pull from different parts of the image: some emphasize regions truly pertinent to the question, while others attend to irrelevant objects or background. We regard the latter as a potential misuse of visual information or a form of hallucination. Building on these insights, we propose Saliency-R1 , a novel framework that aligns VLM saliency maps with hu-man annotations. We curate a VQA dataset with bound-ing boxes marking image regions essential for answering each question. For each generated response, we compute the ratio of saliency mass inside the annotated boxes to the saliency mass over the whole image and use this ra-tio as a reward signal in Group Relative Policy Optimiza-tion (GRPO) [60]. This training process encourages the model to concentrate on task-relevant regions. Extensive experiments across diverse benchmarks show that our ap-proach not only steers reasoning towards critical visual ar-eas but also enhances perceptual grounding and overall per-formance. Our main contribution can be summarized as: • We build a novel attention-based saliency-map technique, which highlights the token-wise important regions effi-ciently without additional forward or backward passes. • We propose an attention rollout mechanism that traces how visual tokens contribute to answer tokens, using the thinking tokens as a bottleneck for visualization. • We use the saliency–annotation alignment score as a re-ward and apply GRPO post-training to enhance the faith-fulness and interpretability of VLM reasoning. 

## 2. Related Work 

Explanation of Vision-language Models. Our paper mainly focus on the visual explanations of VLMs via the activation maps for the input images. While traditional saliency maps techniques such as Integrated Gradient [65], CAM [97], Grad-CAM, [57], Rollout [1], LRP [34], LIME [55], and SHAP [47] have been applied to inter-pret the VLMs [8, 22, 25, 93, 99], they were built for con-ventional single-output models (e.g., CNNs, ViTs) and re-main suboptimal for sequential prediction. There are also works [40, 61] design special saliency map techniques for VLMs. However, most of these methods are computation-ally intensive, as they rely on calculating gradients or per-turbing the input and performing multiple forward passes. In contrast, our approach eliminates the need for additional forward or backward passes, enabling real-time computa-tion and seamless integration into the training pipeline. 

Vision-language Model Reasoning. In parallel with ad-vances in LLM reasoning capabilities [28, 51], a growing body of work targets improving the reasoning of VLMs [46, 50, 92] via either supervised fine-tuning (SFT) [59, 64] or reinforcement learning (RL) [49, 67, 75, 76, 81, 82]. Be-yond standard LLM reasoning techniques, many VLM stud-ies adopt a “thinking with images” paradigm [63], leverag-ing specialized visual tools [44, 45, 94] or image-generation modules [17, 20, 33] to make intermediate reasoning steps visible. A common strategy is to generate zoomed-in inter-mediate views that help the model focus on salient regions. In contrast to such explicit zoom-in methods, our approach strengthens a VLM’s intrinsic ability to attend to the most informative areas of an image, eliminating the need for ex-ternal visualization tools. In addition, our method can also serve as a complement to “thinking with images” paradigm by integrating the use of both approaches. 

Attention Manipulation in Vision-language Models. Our motivation for introducing saliency-map alignment rewards stems from prior findings that steering attention can en-hance LLM and VLM performance. LLMs often display biased attention over long contexts, for example, under-utilizing middle segments [42] or over-focusing on initial tokens [77]. To address this, works have explored fine-tuning [4], input-adaptive calibration [87], and position-specific interventions [85]. For more targeted control, PASTA [91] reweights attention toward user-selected spans, while INSTABOOST [27] and SpotLight [71] emphasize instruction tokens to improve instruction following. In VLMs, ADAPTVIS [12] adaptively sharpens or smooths vi-sual attention based on response confidence. To our knowl-edge, we are the first to align visual attention with human annotations during the post-training phase, thereby bypass-ing the necessity of precise steering for inference.

3.2. Holistic Saliency-map Aggregation with Think-ing Tokens as Bottleneck 

A typical reasoning model first produces a chain of thought and then generates the final answers based on this reason-ing context. We assume that the thinking process begins by extracting information from the input images and prompts, followed by summarization and reasoning. The final an-swers then structure the conclusions drawn from this pro-cess into the required output format. To ensure faithful rea-soning, we aim for the answer generation to depend on the thinking process, while the thinking process itself should ef-fectively focus on the relevant regions of the input images. To use saliency maps to visualize the process, we hope they could reflect how visual information flows to the answer to-kens, with the thinking tokens serving as a bottleneck. We utilize heuristic attention rollout to quantify how the visual information is transferred to answer tokens through thinking tokens (see Fig. 2 (b)). At layer l and head h, we use Al,h vt to denote the attention weight matrix between vi-sual tokens and thinking tokens, where the entry (i, j ) de-notes the attentions weights from the i-th visual token to the j-th thinking token. Similarly, Al,h ta denotes the weight matrix between thinking tokens and answer tokens. There-fore, ˜Al,h va = Al,h vt Al,h ta measures the attention weight from visual tokens to the answer tokens, with thinking tokens as the bottleneck. For a specific answer token, we can utilize Eq. 1 to visualize the transitional contribution of visual to-kens, by replacing the attention weights with the elements in the corresponding column of ˜Al,h va .Finally, we form the holistic saliency map by directly summing the per-token maps. We do not normalize the columns of Al,h vt , Al,h ta or ˜Al,h va because some answer tokens (e.g., prepositions) receive little contribution from think-ing tokens, and similarly some thinking tokens receive little contribution from visual tokens. Such tokens should natu-rally have a smaller influence on the holistic map. In ad-dition, since the saliency maps reflect logit contributions, low-confidence answer tokens with small logits also con-tribute less to the holistic saliency map.

## 4. Experiments 

4.1. Experimental Setup 

Training Data. Following standard practice [28], we begin with SFT for a cold start. We build our SFT dataset by fil-tering Vision-R1-cold [31] to remove multi-round conversa-tions and extremely long responses, yielding 272,881 sam-ples. For GRPO, we construct the saliency-r1-8k dataset. It is composed of 8,080 samples, down-sampled from the Visual-CoT dataset [59], which consists of VQA samples for OCR, fine-grained understanding, general VQA, charts understanding, and relation reasoning. Each sample in-cludes an image, a question, a ground-truth answer, and a bounding box marking the image regions relevant to the question. Further dataset statistics and curation details are provided in the Appendix. 

Implementation Details. Our main experiments utilize Qwen2.5-VL [6] (3B and 7B) as the base model. SFT is trained using llama-factory [96] and GRPO is trained with the TRL framework [72]. We use a total batch size of 64 with mixed precision. The KL divergence coefficient is 0.001, and we sample 8 rollouts per step. The policy model is optimized with LoRA [30] (rank 16) using an ini-tial learning rate of 10 −5. GPT-4o-mini [32] serves as the LLM judge to provide accuracy rewards. All training runs are executed on 8 NVIDIA A6000 GPUs. 

4.2. Evaluation of Saliency Maps Faithfulness 

We begin by evaluating the faithfulness of our saliency methods using deletion and insertion tests. In deletion, we progressively remove the most salient image pixels in de-scending order; in insertion, we add those pixels back onto a blank canvas. To enable fair comparison, the model’s mean self-log-likelihood is linearly normalized using the blank image and the original, unperturbed output as references. We report normalized log-likelihood at 5%, 15%, and 30% pixel perturbation. Lower deletion scores and higher in-sertion scores indicate that the highlighted regions have a stronger causal effect on the model’s responses. We conduct experiments using Qwen2.5-VL-3B as the VLM, and compare our method with several baselines, in-cluding CAM [97], Grad-CAM [57], raw attention, atten-tion rollout [1], ATTN-LRP [2], and TAM [40]. The eval-uation is performed on three datasets, namely the COCO Caption dataset [13], GranDf [54], and OpenPSG [98]. For each dataset, we sample 1k images for evaluation. The re-sults are shown in Table 1. Our method attains faithful-ness on par with, or better than, the baselines. For exam-ple, on COCO Captions, the deletion score is lower than the second-best approach by 5.46%, 4.77%, and 2.57% at 5%, 15%, and 30% pixel perturbation, respectively. This indicates that, even though we focus solely on direct con-tributions, the method still faithfully captures the relative importance of visual patches. In addition, our method is very efficient. Unlike baselines such as ATTN-LRP (which requires backpropagation) and TAM (which solves an opti-mization problem), our approach relies only on simple ma-trix operations, making it especially suitable for integration into post-training pipelines.

4.5. Ablation Studies 

We conduct extensive ablation studies to examine the ef-fects of individual model design. We use Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct as the base model and evaluate the model perfor-mance on the same 10 datasets as in Table 2. The average metrics are reported in Fig. 4. Because of the large scale dif-ference, we report MME separately from the average over the rest nine datasets. A complete set of results, including additional experiments, is provided in the Appendix. 

Effects of SFT Cold Start. We evaluate the performance of Saliency-R1 against Saliency-R1-zero, which leverages GRPO to train the model directly without an SFT cold start. The results show that SFT plays a crucial role in signifi-cantly enhancing model performance. Given the relatively small size of the base model, its initial reasoning capabili-ties are limited. The cold start process allows the model to quickly develop fundamental reasoning skills. 

Effects of Individual Reward Term. We then investigate the impact of each reward term. We begin with standard GRPO training without incorporating the saliency align-ment reward. This gives the same training protocol as Vision-R1 [31], but using different training data. The results reveal that Saliency-R1 outperforms Vision-R1 by 1.5% across 9 VQA benchmarks, highlighting the importance of the saliency alignment reward. By aligning saliency maps, the model concentrates on the most critical regions of the image, showing significantly better visual reasoning ability. We also observed a general positive correlation between saliency alignment and accuracy: when the model attends to the correct region, it tends to produce the correct an-swer. Moreover, since computing the saliency reward de-pends on properly formatted outputs, its value already cap-tures formatting correctness. This suggests the saliency re-ward could be used on its own. To test this hypothesis, we train Saliency-R1-pure using only the saliency alignment reward. The model performs well, with the average accu-racy across nine benchmarks just 0.6% lower than the full

Effects of Saliency Maps Techniques. Finally, we eval-uate the impact of our proposed saliency map technique. We replace it with a vanilla attention aggregation and re-train the model, yielding the variant Saliency-R1-attn. This model still outperforms Vision-R1 on general VQA bench-marks, indicating that alignment via attention maps also helps the model “see” better. However, its performance lags behind Saliency-R1. We also examine with the version of saliency maps that directly aggregate the thinking tokens’ saliency maps (without attention rollout), yielding the ver-sion Saliency-R1-think. Still, the results are sub-optimal. Our saliency maps more faithfully capture the reasoning process, aligning them has a more direct and stronger ef-fect on improving the visual ability of the model. 

## 5. Limitation and Conclusion 

In this work, we propose Saliency-R1, a novel protocol that improves the visual reasoning ability of VLMs via saliency map alignment. Specifically, we propose a real-time saliency-map generation algorithm via logits decom-position, and propose to align the saliency maps with hu-man annotations through GRPO. Evaluation of Saliency-R1 on 10 VQA benchmarks shows its superior performance. Our work has certain limitations. Due to the limited computational resources, the training, especially SFT, is conducted on relatively small datasets. As shown in Table 2, some metrics become lower after SFT. This is not desired and can be overcome by collecting more diverse cold-start data. Moreover, we use bounding boxes as the coarse la-bels to align saliency maps. Finer segmentation labels (e.g., from SAM 3 [9]) may further improve performance. Fi-nally, our evaluation is based on relatively small base mod-els. The extension to larger models is left as future work. Acknowledgment

## B. Description of Dataset 

In this section, we introduce the details of the dataset we used. The training of Saliency-R1 involves the use of two dataset, i.e., Saliency-R1-cold and Saliency-r1-8k. 

B.1. Saliency-R1-cold 

Saliency-R1-cold is a subset of the Vision-R1-cold [31], which is a composition of LLaVA-CoT dataset (100K) [79] and Mulberry dataset (200K) [82]. For samples with multi-round conversation, we split it and construct multiple single-round conversation sample. This is due the computa-tional constraints that we cannot process text with extremely long contexts. Additionally, we filter out samples whose number of tokens in the response is greater than 1024. In the end, we have 272,881 samples. 

B.2. Saliency-r1-8k 

Saliency-r1-8k is a subset of the Visual-CoT dataset [59], which is a composition of TextVQA, TextCaps, DocVQA, DUDE, SROIE, CUB-200-2011, flickr30k, Visual7W, In-fographicsVQA, VSR, GQA, and Open images. We ran-domly sample 8,080 images from the Visual-CoT dataset and construct our Saliency-r1-8k dataset. The proportion of each sub-dataset remains the same as the original Visual-CoT dataset. 

## C. Implementation Details 

In this section, we provide more details of the implementa-tion for bot cold start and the saliency-map alignment. C.1. SFT 

We use llama-factory [96] as the framework for conducting SFT. We conduct SFT on Saliency-R1-cold for 2 epochs. The maximum learning rate is 5 × 10 −5 and then it is ad-justed according to cosine annealing scheduler with warm-up ratio of 0.05. The batch size is 64. Lora with LoRA with rank 128 is used for optimization. Other settings are set to be the default values of llama-factory. 

C.2. GRPO 

GRPO is performed using TRL as the framework. We con-duct GRPO on Saliency-r1-8k for 3 epochs. We utilize an original learning rate of 1 × 10 −5 and batch size of 128. LoRA of rank 8 is performed on the query and value pro-jection layers. The number of rollouts is set to be 8. Other settings are set to be the default values of TRL. 

## D. Introduction of benchmark 

In this section, we provide a brief overview of the bench-marks used in this paper. Our experiments are conducted on well-established or recently introduced visual understand-ing benchmarks. These benchmarks were selected based on two key criteria: (1) The tasks must be vision-centric, mean-ing that the questions and answers should be derived from the content of the image, requiring attention to relevant re-gions within the image. Benchmarks that primarily focus on other aspects, such as mathematical reasoning or code generation, often depend heavily on the intrinsic reason-ing abilities of the language model, which falls outside the scope of this paper. (2) The questions must be in a multiple-choice or True/False format, allowing us to directly evaluate the model’s performance without being affected by the vari-ability of LLM evaluation. 

MMMU-Pro. MMMU-Pro [88] is a strengthened version of the MMMU benchmark that more rigorously tests mul-timodal understanding and reasoning. It filters out ques-tions solvable by text-only LLMs, expands multiple-choice options up to 10 to curb option-guessing, and introduces a vision-only setting where questions and options are embed-ded in photos/screenshots to require simultaneous “seeing” and “reading.” 

MMBench. MMBench [43] is a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating multimodal models’ understanding and rea-soning abilities across fine-grained tasks. It spans a wide range of skills: visual perception, commonsense and logical reasoning, detail recognition, spatial relations, text recog-nition, and cross-modal alignment, primarily through care-fully curated multiple-choice questions. With rigorous hu-man verification and de-duplication to mitigate training data contamination, MMBench aims to provide a fair and repro-ducible assessment. 

POPE. POPE [39] is designed to evaluate object hallucina-tion in vision–language models using binary-choice ques-tions that verify the presence of specific objects in images. It reports metrics such as Accuracy, Recall, Precision, and F1 across three distinct sampling strategies, providing a ro-bust and fine-grained assessment of hallucination tenden-cies. In our paper, we report the F1 score for POPE. 

MME. The MME benchmark [10] evaluates multimodal models across 14 subtasks that span both perceptual pro-cessing and cognitive reasoning. By using carefully de-signed instruction–response pairs, MME reduces the likeli-hood of training data leakage, supporting a fair and rigorous assessment. 

MME-RealWorld. MME-RealWorld [95] is a large, fully human-annotated benchmark for evaluating multimodal LLMs on high-resolution, real-world tasks. It comprises 13,366 images (avg ∼2000×1500) and 29,429 multiple-choice QA pairs across 43 subtasks in five domains: OCR in the wild, remote sensing, diagram/table understanding, video monitoring, and autonomous driving. Designed to stress fine-grained perception and reasoning, it includes an “E” option for unanswerable cases and enforces strict qual-ity control by 25 annotators and 7 experts. 

MMStar. MMStar [11] is a vision-indispensable multi-modal benchmark of 1,500 human-curated, multiple-choice samples designed to fairly evaluate Large Vision-Language Models. It targets six core capabilities (coarse/fine-grained perception, instance/logical reasoning, science & technol-ogy, mathematics) across 18 axes, with strict visual depen-dency, minimized data leakage, and balanced difficulty. 

ChartQA. ChartQA [48] is a benchmark for testing how ef-fectively multimodal models answer questions about charts, emphasizing both visual comprehension and logical reason-ing. It covers a range of chart types—such as bar charts and line graphs—and combines human-authored and automati-cally generated questions to probe complex reasoning. Im-portantly, ChartQA is highly OCR-dependent and demands fine-grained visual understanding, as models must extract textual information from charts and reason over it. 

IllusionVQA. IllusionVQA [58] is a two-part visual question answering benchmark designed to test Vision-Language Models on challenging optical illusions. It com-prises 435 multiple-choice comprehension instances across 12 illusion categories curated from diverse real images, and a 1,000-sample “soft localization” task where models iden-tify which of two side-by-side objects is geometrically im-possible. In this paper, we mainly focus on the soft local-ization task. 

ScienceQA. ScienceQA [56] is a large-scale multimodal multiple-choice benchmark of about 21k K–12 science questions spanning natural, social, and language sciences, many with text and/or image contexts. Uniquely, it anno-tates most questions with “lectures” (background knowl-edge) and “explanations” (reasoning), enabling evaluation and training of chain-of-thought reasoning. The dataset covers 26 topics, 127 categories, and 379 skills, includes both diagrams and natural images, and supports both few-shot prompting and fine-tuning studies. 

SalBench. SalBench [16] is a vision-centric benchmark for Large Vision-Language Models that tests human-obvious visual saliency—finding an “odd-one-out” among distrac-tors based on low-level features. Built by augmenting the P3 (synthetic) and O3 (natural) datasets with language in-structions, it evaluates three tasks: Odd-One-Out Detec-tion, Referring Odd-One-Out (text box coordinates), and Visual Referring Odd-One-Out (highlighted box). Features span color, orientation, size (synthetic) and extend to focus, shape, location, pattern (natural).

E.5. Human Evaluation 

To further show the improved interpretability of Saliency-R1, we conduct a small-scale user study. Specifically, we collect 10 VQA examples with the corresponding saliency maps, and ask 12 voluntters to score them on a scale of 1 to 5 regarding the quality of the interpretation. The average score of the base model is 3.6 and that of Saliency-R1 is 4.5. Wilcoxon signed-rank test shows the p < 0.05 . This further validates that Saliency-R1 presents better interpretability.
