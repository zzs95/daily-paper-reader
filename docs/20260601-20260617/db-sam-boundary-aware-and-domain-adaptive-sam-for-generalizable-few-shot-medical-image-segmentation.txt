# DB-SAM: Boundary-aware and domain-adaptive SAM 

# for generalizable few-shot medical image 

# segmentation 

Journal of King Saud University Computer and Information Sciences https://doi.org/10.1007/s44443-026-00873-6 Received: 13 Mar 2026 Accepted: 18 May 2026 Yaxue Wen, Yuqin Li, Ziyang Zhang, Yu Miao, WeiLi Shi & ZhenGang Jiang ARTICLE IN PRESS DB-SAM: Boundary-Aware and Domain-Adaptive SAM for Generalizable Few-Shot Medical Image Segmentation 

Abstract 

The Segment Anything Model (SAM), a foundation model for promptable image segmentation, has shown strong performance on zero-shot natural image seg-mentation tasks. However, due to the significant differences between the natural and medical image domains, as well as the subtle lesions and blurred bound-aries inherent in medical images, SAM suffers a marked performance drop when applied to medical images. To address these challenges, we propose DB-SAM, a domain-adaptive and boundary-enhanced framework for few-shot medical image segmentation. To better adapt to medical image segmentation, we fine-tune the image encoder using a hybrid feature representation that incorporates low-level features. During the fine-tuning process, a nonlinear mapping method is employed to update a small portion of weights, thereby capturing rich features in medical images. Furthermore, to supplement the multi-scale and boundary features required for medical image segmentation, a multi-scale feature extractor and a boundary-aware module are designed. The multi-scale feature extractor enhances the representation of local receptive field features, providing SAM with rich local multi-scale contextual information. The boundary-aware module lever-ages limited input data to capture enhanced boundary-representative features by encoding boundary and texture features. Experimental results on three datasets from different modalities demonstrate that our DB-SAM outperforms the state-of-the-arts methods, and remarkably, even with only one labeled training sample, it consistently surpasses various SAM and CNN-based methods. The code is publicly available at https://github.com/ZZY010411/DB-SAM .

Keywords: medical image segmentation, few-shot segmentation, boundary-aware, foundation model fine-tuning 

## 1 Introduction 

Medical imaging plays a crucial role in diagnosis and treatment of diseases. Medical image segmentation aims to automatically and accurately extract regions or structures of interest from medical images, thereby providing reliable information for subsequent quantitative analysis, diagnosis, and treatment[1–3]. Deep learning-based models can learn complex image features in medical images and deliver accurate results across a ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS wide range of tasks[4, 5], such as delineating specific anatomical structures and iden-tifying pathological regions, thereby demonstrating tremendous potential in medical image segmentation. However, current deep learning image segmentation methods are designed for specific modalities or tasks, which typically require a large amount of annotated data for training[6–8]. The lack of annotated data limits the adoption and application of these methods in clinical practice[9]. The emergence of foundation models[10–14] has revolutionized traditional model training methods[15–17]. By pre-training on large-scale data, foundation models achieve extensive task adaptability[18]. Moreover, foundation models are primarily pre-trained using self-supervised or unsupervised learning. As a result, they can lever-age vast amounts of unlabeled data. This capability is especially critical in fields with high data annotation costs. The emergence of the Segment Anything Model (SAM) has revolutionized the field of image segmentation[19]. SAM is pre-trained on large-scale, diverse datasets, transforming it into a zero-shot segmentation foundation model, thereby reducing data annotation costs[13]. Moreover, universal image features are captured by SAM through a self-supervised pre-training strategy, providing a founda-tion for its transferability and generalization to natural images[20–23]. However, due to the significant differences between natural images and medical images, as well as the lack of medical imaging in pre-training data of SAM, the adaptability of SAM to medical image segmentation is insufficient[22–25]. Although SAM is pretrained only on natural images, its large-scale pretraining endows it with transferable visual rep-resentations, making it a promising foundation for adaptation to different medical imaging modalities. In contrast, existing deep learning segmentation methods primar-ily focus on a limited set of tasks and imaging modalities and require a large amount of annotated data for training[26]. Many studies have applied pre-trained foundation models, especially SAM-based models, to medical image segmentation tasks[22–25]. Recent studies have further explored pseudo-label learning to enhance feature discrimination for foundation mod-els in 3D medical image segmentation, showing the potential of foundation-model adaptation under limited annotation settings[27]. Existing studies mainly focus on adapting the prompt encoder of SAM to better fit medical images. However, pro-viding appropriate prompts for each medical image segmentation dataset remains a significant challenge[28]. In addition, SAM-based automatic segmentation methods usually achieve encouraging results only when the target structures exhibit relatively regular shapes and stable positions. To better adapt SAM to medical image segmen-tation in the absence of manually designed prompts, two primary strategies have been explored: employing parameter-efficient transfer learning techniques, such as LoRA or adapters, and introducing local prior information through a parallel CNN branch. For example, MedSAM[22] improves segmentation performance on medical images through targeted adaptation, SAMed[24] applies a low-rank fine-tuning strategy to the SAM image encoder and jointly optimizes the prompt encoder and mask decoder on annotated medical datasets, and Lin et al. [29] introduce a parallel CNN branch to supplement SAM with local information via cross-branch attention. Although these methods have shown promising performance, they still remain limited in few-shot ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS medical image segmentation[30], especially when the target structures are small, mor-phologically complex, or weakly contrasted against surrounding tissues. This limitation mainly arises from three aspects. First, SAM is insufficiently sensitive to local multi-scale patterns, making it difficult to accurately capture tiny lesions and substantial anatomical shape variations under extremely limited supervision. Although multi-scale information[31] is crucial for integrating low-resolution global semantics with high-resolution local details to address variable morphology and blurred boundaries[32], simple parallel CNN branches are often unable to capture subtle pathological cues and fine local representations, which may result in the neglect of critical multi-scale information[33–35]. Second, medical images usually contain blurred boundaries and ambiguous tissue interfaces, whereas SAM lacks explicit boundary modeling, which often leads to incomplete or over-smoothed predictions around object contours. Third, existing fine-tuning strategies, such as adapters and LoRA, mainly rely on linear mapping methods[36] and primarily operate on high-level features, while overlooking low-level features that contain rich edge, texture, and contour information[37, 38]. As a result, their ability to model the nonlinear domain gap between natural and medical images remains limited. To address these limitations, we propose a domain-adaptive and boundary-enhanced SAM framework for few-shot medical image segmentation. Rather than relying on a single adaptation strategy, the proposed method improves SAM from three complementary perspectives. Specifically, to enhance cross-domain adaptation, a nonlinear fusion feature adapter is designed to fuse low-level and mid-level features and remap them into a nonlinear feature space, thereby facilitating the transfer of knowledge learned from natural images to the medical domain. To strengthen the perception of lesion size variation and subtle local structures, a multi-scale feature extractor is introduced to efficiently model local contextual information under dif-ferent receptive fields. In addition, to improve the sensitivity of the model to weak and ambiguous boundaries, a boundary-aware module is designed to extract bound-ary gradient features by calculating gradients between pixel pairs, and channel-wise pooling is further applied to retain informative boundary responses while discard-ing irrelevant information. These three components are complementary rather than redundant, as they respectively target insufficient domain adaptation, weak multi-scale perception, and limited boundary awareness in SAM. Extensive experiments on multiple medical imaging modalities demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method for various segmentation tasks. Compared with several state-of-the-art meth-ods, our method achieves superior performance under few-shot settings and exhibits strong generalization capability. Our main contributions are as follows: 

• A multi-scale feature extractor is proposed to enable SAM to capture local multi-scale feature information for adapting to medical images, with multi-scale features being effectively integrated into 2D backbone network of SAM in our research. 

• A nonlinear fusion feature adapter is proposed to fuse multi-level features and remap the combined features into a nonlinear feature space. The results show that the adapter can be applied to various medical imaging modalities, including MRI, ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS dermoscopy, and ultrasound images. The proposed method surpasses various SAM-based and CNN-based approaches under the same annotation-derived point-prompt protocol, without manually designed prompts. 

• A boundary-aware module is proposed to extract gradient features by computing the gradients between pixel pairs and to capture additional local details through channel-wise pooling. 

## 2 METHOD 

Fig. 1 illustrates the overall architecture of the proposed DB-SAM framework. Given an input medical image, the image is first embedded into visual tokens by the patch embedding layer of the SAM image encoder. To achieve parameter-efficient adaptation to the medical domain, the proposed nonlinear fusion feature adapter is inserted into each transformer block of the ViT encoder, where it enhances feature transformation by fusing low-level and mid-level information in a nonlinear manner. During training, the original parameters of the SAM backbone are kept frozen, while the newly intro-duced adapter and task-specific modules are optimized. Meanwhile, shallow image features are delivered to the multi-scale feature extractor and the boundary-aware module in parallel. The multi-scale feature extractor is used to model local contextual information under different receptive fields, while the boundary-aware module explic-itly extracts boundary gradient cues to improve sensitivity to weak and ambiguous contours. The outputs of these two branches are then integrated by the residual fusion module to obtain enhanced structural representations, which are further injected into the mask decoding pathway for segmentation prediction. Therefore, the nonlinear fusion feature adapter mainly improves the cross-domain adaptation ability of the image encoder, whereas the multi-scale feature extractor and the boundary-aware module provide complementary local and boundary-aware information for accurate mask generation.

## ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 2.1 Architectural Overview of the Segment Anything Model 

The Segment Anything Model[19] is a prompt-driven segmentation architecture com-prising three core modules: an image encoder[39] utilizing a Vision Transformer[40] backbone, a prompt encoder processing user inputs, and a mask decoder generat-ing pixel-level predictions[41]. When processing medical imaging data, SAM exhibits performance degradation due to domain shift between natural and medical images, particularly in tissue texture patterns and anatomical structures. This discrepancy necessitates domain-specific adaptation through parameter-efficient fine-tuning to mitigate cross-domain generalization challenges. 

2.2 multi-scale feature extractor 

Lesions and tissues in medical images contain multi-scale local features, the ViT encoder cannot extract these local features effectively. The SAM commonly employs a parallel CNN branch to process images and extract various features. However, this method struggles to capture the correlations and contextual information of subtle lesion features. To overcome these issues, we propose a multi-scale feature extractor to capture information across multiple scales. This extractor aggregates small convolu-tional kernels on the QKV vectors to generate multi-scale vectors, enabling multi-level learning and capturing features at different scales. As shown in Fig. 2, multi-scale fea-ture extractor comprises a series of convolution-pooling blocks, as well as a multi-scale linear attention block. The inputs pass through a single convolution block initially, followed by a multi-scale linear attention block. Specifically, a convolutional layer generates the Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V) tensors, transforming the input chan-nels into a higher-dimensional space for attention mechanisms. After obtaining Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V) tensors, depthwise separable convolutions with varying kernel sizes are used to process these tensors at multiple spatial scales. Each convolu-tional pathway processes the QKV tensors at a specific scale, allowing the module to capture contextual information at different granularities. To improve computational efficiency while preserving the ability to model contextual dependencies, the proposed multi-scale extraction module adopts a linear attention mechanism instead of the stan-dard quadratic self-attention. Given the input feature map, linear attention is used to capture long-range interactions with reduced computational complexity, making the module more suitable for high-resolution medical image analysis. 2.3 boundary-aware module 

Traditional medical image segmentation methods are limited to expressing only the prior knowledge contained in existing images. Inspired by it, we propose a boundary-aware module that combines deep learning methods with the gradient computed by edge detection operators to represent boundary information, providing precise bound-ary cues to the Segment Anything Model. Specifically, we use Sobel-based convolution and shift-based derivative convolution to compute gradient information, followed by a max pooling mechanism to generate attention ratings. These attention ratings mod-ulate feature representations, resulting in mapping of probability vectors and original input features to output state vectors in the query space. Inspired by HOG-style gra-dient representation, we design a boundary-aware module to explicitly model gradient magnitude and direction by using convolutional operators. Different from conventional hand-crafted HOG descriptors, the proposed module keeps the entire gradient extrac-tion process differentiable and learnable, enabling boundary-aware representations to be adaptively optimized for medical image segmentation. 2.4 nonlinear fusion feature adapter 

Unlike conventional linear low-rank adaptation, the proposed nonlinear fusion feature adapter introduces nonlinear activation and gated fusion of low-level features, which increases the expressive capacity for cross-domain feature transformation. Specifically, existing linear adapters mainly operate on high-level representations through linear projection, which limits their ability to model the complex distribution gap between natural and medical images. In contrast, our design incorporates low-level and mid-level visual cues and maps them into a nonlinear feature space, thereby providing more flexible and discriminative adaptation for dense medical image segmentation. SAM has been demonstrated impressive general segmentation capabilities, facili-tated by large-scale image encoder. Especially, the conventional Adapter and LoRA have been widely used to adapt SAM to medical image segmentation[44]. However, such linear adaptation methods exhibit limitations in adapting from the source domain to the target domain. Meanwhile, Low-level features contain more contour information, which are crucial for medical image segmentation. Motivated by the above observation, we propose a non-linear fusion feature adapter structure, which is shown in Fig. 5, integrating features from different spaces and combining them with low-level features ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS enhances feature representation in medical domain. After fine-tuning each layer with non-linear fusion feature adapter in the encoder, the model narrows the domain gap between natural images and medical images. Specifically, the input image is first con-verted into a set of 2D patch embeddings e0 through the frozen patch embedding layer of ViT. Then we obtain Low-level feature Flow from e0 through a trainable linear layer. To optimize information exchange and feature composition within Multi-Head Attention (MHA) layers, non-linear fusion feature adapter constructs Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V) tensors through learnable projection matrices applied to the input feature tensor, while maintaining nonlinear integration capabilities. Then the Q and V are processed through low-rank adaptation matrices, with the activation gating mechanism regulating the influence of the low-rank components on the final weights. Finally, the correlation between the queries and keys is computed and attention is applied. The nonlinearity of the proposed adapter is introduced by nonlinear activa-tion functions and gated feature fusion, which allow the model to selectively enhance informative low-level details while suppressing irrelevant responses during adaptation. We evaluate the proposed method on three datasets from different modalities. We first compare our approach with state-of-the-art medical image segmentation methods and SAM fine-tuning methods. Subsequently, we conduct generalization evaluations and comprehensive ablation studies to analyze the effectiveness of our method. 

3.1 Few-shot Setting 

To ensure a clear and fair definition of the few-shot setting across different datasets, we define the unit of each shot according to the annotation granularity of the cor-responding dataset. For the 2D datasets, i.e., BUSI and ISBI, one-shot and five-shot refer to using 1 and 5 labeled training images, respectively. For the Heart MRI dataset, which is organized at the volume level, one-shot and five-shot refer to using 1 and 5 labeled training volumes, respectively, and all slices from each selected labeled volume are used for training. For all compared methods, results are reported under the same few-shot data budget on each dataset to ensure fair comparison. 

3.2 Datasets and Implementations 

3.2.1 Dataset 

We conduct experiments on three datasets from different imaging modalities, includ-ing BUSI[47], ISBI2016[48], and Task02 Heart[49]. The BUSI dataset is an ultrasound image dataset, ISBI2016 is a dermoscopy image dataset, and the Task02 Heart dataset is an MRI dataset. To evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed model, three datasets with different modalities are selected. The three segmentation tasks are introduced as follows. We randomly split the publicly available training data on patient basis into three parts, training set, validation set, and test set, with ratio being 1:1:1. BUSI[47]: The dataset is provided by Baheya Hospital for Early Detection & Treat-ment of Women Cancer, Cairo, Egypt, which is collected in 2018. The data collected include breast ultrasound images among women in ages between 25 and 75 years old. The dataset consists of 780 images with an average image size of 500 × 500 pixels in PNG format. The images are categorized into normal, benign, and malignant, which are used for segmenting early signs of masses in breast cancer. We mix benign and malignant images and split them randomly. ISBI2016[48]: The ISBI 2016 Skin Lesion Challenge dataset is a subset of the larger International Skin Imaging Collaboration (ISIC) archive, containing images acquired from various devices across multiple leading international clinical centers. The dataset includes 900 training images (727 non-melanoma and 173 melanoma) and a separate test set of 379 images (304 non-melanoma and 75 melanoma) for evaluation. Task02 Heart[49]: The heart dataset is provided by King’s College London. Origi-nally released through the Left Atrial Segmentation Challenge (LASC), it comprises 30 MRI datasets covering the entire heart, which are acquired during a single heart phase. Expert segmentation masks of the left atrium are provided by this dataset, and the images are acquired using a 1.5T Achieva scanner with a voxel resolution of 1.25 

× 1.25 × 2.7 mm 3.ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 3.2.2 Implementation Details 

All experiments were implemented using the PyTorch framework on an NVIDIA A100 GPU with 40 GB memory. To ensure a fair and computationally efficient comparison, we adopted the ViT-B image encoder in all experiments, which was initialized with the official SAM pretrained weights. SAM was pretrained on SA-1B, which consists of 11 million diverse, high-resolution, licensed, and privacy-preserving images, together with 1.1 billion high-quality segmentation masks. For preprocessing, all medical images were converted into RGB format to match the input format of SAM. Each 2D image or slice was resized to 256 × 256 before being fed into the network. For the Heart MRI dataset, each 3D volume was converted into a sequence of 2D slices, and all slices from the selected labeled volumes were used for training under the few-shot setting. This setting follows our few-shot protocol, where one-shot and five-shot for the Heart MRI dataset refer to one and five labeled vol-umes, respectively. During training, random data augmentation was applied to improve data diversity and reduce overfitting, including Gaussian noise, brightness adjustment, and random rotation. The training loss was composed of Cross-Entropy loss and Dice loss. The weights of the Dice loss and Cross-Entropy loss were both set to 0.5. The model was optimized using Adam with a learning rate of 1 × 10 −4, a batch size of 4, and 100 training epochs. During validation and testing, only deterministic preprocessing was used to ensure stable and reproducible evaluation. For prompt generation, all SAM-based methods were evaluated under the same prompt setting to ensure fair comparison. Since SAM is a prompt-driven segmentation model, one positive point prompt was used for each target region. During training, the point prompt was randomly sampled from the foreground region of the corresponding anno-tation mask, which exposes the model to different prompt locations and improves its robustness to prompt variations. During validation and testing, a deterministic foreground point was used for each target region to avoid randomness in evaluation. Specifically, the foreground point was selected from the target region in a fixed manner for each test sample. No manual prompt design, negative point prompts, bounding-box prompts, external detector, or additional prompt-generation network was used in the main experiments. Therefore, the same annotation-derived point-prompt proto-col was consistently applied to all SAM-based methods, and the comparison mainly reflects the segmentation capability of different model architectures rather than the influence of different prompt strategies.

## 4 CONCLUSIONS 

In this paper, we propose an effective SAM-based few-shot framework for medi-cal image segmentation that is applicable across various imaging modalities. In this method, multiple imaging modalities are used to train the boundary-aware module and the multi-scale feature extractor, the non-linear fusion feature adapter is employed to transform the features obtained from training on natural images through non-linear mapping method, thereby bridging the domain gap between natural images and medical images. Specifically, the proposed multi-scale feature extractor captures hierarchical features across multiple spatial resolutions, enabling it to simultaneously capture both subtle lesions and global structures. Meanwhile, the boundary-aware module enhances model performance by optimizing feature selection and reinforcing attention to boundary regions. Furthermore, to better adapt the proposed method for medical image segmentation, we design the nonlinear fusion feature adapter, which effectively fine-tunes the SAM encoder by mapping the hybrid features that incorpo-rate low-level details into a nonlinear space. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed DB-SAM achieves the state-of-the-art performance against existing meth-ods with limited labelled data, thereby showcasing its potential for the development of intelligent computer-aided intervention systems. There are some limitations which should be resolved in the next study. Although DB-SAM demonstrates excellent per-formance in few-shot segmentation, unlabeled data also contain valuable information. In future work, we plan to explore semi-supervised learning by leveraging unlabeled data together with SAM, with the goal of further improving segmentation performance through consistency regularization and prior knowledge integration. Although DB-SAM achieves promising performance on different medical imaging modalities, the current framework is still based on 2D slice-wise processing. For 3D medical images such as MRI and CT, volumetric spatial correlations across adjacent slices may provide important anatomical continuity and contextual information. Since ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS the original SAM is designed as a 2D promptable segmentation foundation model, our current study focuses on adapting SAM to few-shot medical image segmentation in a 2D manner. This design enables direct reuse of the pretrained SAM image encoder, but may limit the exploitation of inter-slice dependencies in volumetric data. In future work, we will extend DB-SAM toward 3D medical image segmentation by incorporat-ing volumetric adapters, inter-slice attention, or 3D feature aggregation modules to better model anatomical continuity and boundary consistency across slices. In addition, the deterministic foreground point used during testing is annotation-derived and is mainly adopted to ensure a fair comparison among SAM-based methods. This protocol does not fully represent a real clinical automatic prompting scenario. In future work, we will investigate detector-based prompts, uncertainty-aware prompt selection, and expert-in-the-loop prompting strategies.
