# Cross-lingual Self-Consistency for Multilingual Reasoning with Language Models

{ahmed.salemmohamed,e.agirre,mikel.artetxe}@ehu.eus 

Abstract 

Despite expanding their multilingual cover-age, the advanced reasoning capabilities of LLMs remain largely confined to a few high-resource languages like English. To address this, we propose an unsupervised Reinforce-ment Learning (RL) approach to enhance mul-tilingual reasoning by enforcing cross-lingual self-consistency—the principle that a model should produce the same final answer for equiv-alent problems in different languages. Existing methods are limited by the scarcity of multi-lingual reasoning data, and show weak gener-alization to unseen languages. Our approach requires neither gold answers nor parallel data, and it achieves average gains of up to 21.7% 

on MGSM across 10 languages. In addition, our method demonstrates strong generalization, with an 18.2% mean improvement on MGSM languages unseen during training, and up to 

6.2% gain on 3 out-of-distribution benchmarks. These results show the potential of consistency-based methods to improve the multilingual ca-pabilities of LLMs without requiring super-vised data. 

1 Introduction 

Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) show a promising trend to support a wider range of languages. For example, the pretraining of Qwen3 models (Yang et al., 2025a) included over 100 languages compared to 29 for Qwen2.5 (Yang et al., 2024). Nevertheless, their performance is limited to basic capabilities, while their effective-ness in tasks that require complex reasoning re-mains concentrated in a limited set of languages, such as English and Chinese (Ghosh et al., 2025). Consequently, prior work predominantly lever-ages the reasoning on a strong language, typically English. These approaches rely on translated questions and reasoning traces for supervision and, as a result, generalization to unseen languages and domains is constrained by both machine-translation quality and the scarcity of high-quality manually translated data (Ghosh et al., 2025). In this paper, we propose to use cross-lingual self-consistency as an unsupervised signal for RL. Our method extends existing monolingual self-consistency approaches, which capture the intuition that different reasoning paths should lead to con-sistent final answers, to further push the model to produce the same final answer for equivalent prob-lems in different languages (Figure 2). This design guarantees broad practical applicability, since it requires no access to ground truth labels nor pre-translated reasoning traces . This is specially useful given that multilingual reasoning data is only available for evaluation, and training resources are largely monolingual (Ghosh et al., 2025). Our ex-perimental setup only uses English questions. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method across three model scales, achieving an average performance improvement of 7.8% to 21.7% 

over 10 languages on the MGSM benchmark (Fig-ure 1). The improvements can be amplified when high-quality translations for the questions are avail-able (§6.4). Furthermore, our approach exhibits strong generalization, yielding up to 18.2% aver-age improvement on unseen languages within MGSM and extending effectively to other domains with a 4.7% average gain on the mGPQA (Huang et al., 2025) and 6.8% on MMATH (Luo et al., 2025) benchmarks (§6.2). Collectively, these re-sults show that our method is effective at narrowing the multilingual reasoning gap in LLMs, advancing their robustness and equity across languages. 

2 Background 

Recent work has shown improvements in the mono-lingual reasoning capabilities of LLMs by maxi-mizing the consistency of the model (He et al., 2026; Prabhudesai et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025; Agarwal et al., 2025; Cui et al., 2025). We next for-malize this framework, and present our proposed extension to the multilingual scenario in §3. Given an input x, let {(ci, y i) ∼ π(· | x)}Ni=1 de-note a set of N trajectories sampled from the policy model π, where ci is the chain-of-thought reason-ing and yi is the final answer following it. The core idea behind these methods is that, even if the rea-soning paths ci can differ, a strong model should always arrive at the same (ideally correct) final an-swer yi. This principle of final answer consistency can be optimized through RL, even if the ground truth answer is not known. 4 Experimental Setup 

Base Model We conduct our experiments and ab-lations on the Qwen-2.5 family of models (Yang et al., 2024). We chose those models since they (i) are pre-trained and supervised fine-tuned on over 29 languages, (ii) come in a handful of prac-tical sizes, facilitating ablations at scales, and (iii) widely used in multilingual research. In addi-tion, we experiment with the Llama3 8B model (Grattafiori et al., 2024) to compare with prior work that use Llama-based models. 

Baselines We evaluate our approach against two categories of baselines. 

(A) Prompt-based methods. We consider two prompting strategies: Original model performance using native-cot , where the model performs reason-ing and produces answers in the original language of the query, and translate-test , where the query is 

ing and can improve generalization. For example, although applying dropout at test time can hurt performance, it remains a standard and effective training technique. 

first translated into English and the model subse-quently solves the problem in English (Gao et al., 2025). 

(B) Finetuning methods. We include two of the best-performing approaches that improve rea-soning via finetuning. The first is test-time scaling (S1) (Yong et al., 2025), which applies SFT on En-glish data to enhance reasoning capabilities. The second is LIDR (Yang et al., 2025b), which lever-ages DPO to improve multilingual reasoning per-formance. We train both methods on the Qwen2.5 models ourselves using the official code bases. In addition, we report relative performance gains com-pared to the prior published methods that use Llama base models. 

Monolingual Training Dataset Our method only requires English questions for training. We randomly sample 1k questions from the training split of the English-only GSM8k dataset (Cobbe et al., 2021). The final output is parsed using the standard regular expressions from the origi-4nal benchmark. As discussed in §3, we use self-translation to obtain parallel questions, and analyze the impact of manual translation in §6.4 

Evaluation Data Main evaluation and ablations are done on MGSM (Shi et al., 2022), which con-sists of 250 math word problems in 10 languages, which were sampled and manually translated from the GSM8K test set. In addition, we evaluate the generalization capabilities on 3 OOD datasets: mG-PQA (Huang et al., 2025), MMath (Luo et al., 2025), and PolyMath (Wang et al., 2025b). 

Training Environment and Hyperparameters 

We use GRPO as the RL algorithm and use VeRL (Sheng et al., 2024) for the training backend. We use a batch size of 32 parallel prompt sets (mak-ing a total of 320 prompts per step across all the 10 languages), with 32 rollouts per language and prompt, resulting in a total of 160 steps. The mini-batch size is set to 64 prompts (5 mini-batches per step), the KL coefficient to 0.001 and the learning rate to 1E-06. We set the temperature to 0.4. 4 All models are trained using 4 ×A100 GPUs. Other hyperparameters are left at the default values. 

5 Results 

Main Results Table 1 contains the main results of our method. As show in Table 1a, we achieve average gains of 7.8, 15.1, and 21.7 points in Qwen2.5-IT 1.5B, 7B, and 14B, respectively, with improvements across nearly all languages. Notably, gains increase consistently with model size. We note that in Qwen-2.5 14B , Russain (ru) and Tel-ugu (te) achieve the highest relevant performance gains: 102% and 104% respectively. We also ob-serve degradation in Swahili for Qwen-2.5 7B and in English and Telugu in the Qwen-2.5 1.5B model. Models trained with S1 test-time scaling remain competitive with our approach. In particular, they outperform our method on 3 languages for both Qwen-2.5 14B and Qwen-2.5 7B . However, these gains come at the cost of substantially longer rea-soning traces, requiring approximately 3× more tokens. In contrast, our method achieves stronger overall performance with considerably more effi-cient token usage (see App. D.2). 6.3 Different Target Distributions 

We now analyze the strategies to estimate the target output distribution q(y) used in reward modeling (see § 3). As shown in Table 8, using the source language outputs to construct the target distribution yields the least improvement. However, it can be useful in cases where the model is initially indeci-sive, such as Qwen-2.5 1.5B . In contrast, constructing the target distribution from the outputs of English or combining the out-puts of all languages achieves competitive perfor-mance. Specifically, using English outputs yields 1.4% more improvements than the combined out-puts for Qwen-2.5 7B in MGSM. We hypothesize this is due to the less noisy English outputs and that English yields significantly higher base per-formance (at least 18% above other languages). Nonetheless, we believe that the choice of com-bined output is favorable, especially in domains and models where English performance is not dom-inant or other languages perform competitively. In addition, the fact that our method performs well even when considering weaker languages makes it robust for wide applicability.

7 Related Work 

Multilingual Reasoning with Supervised Fine-Tuning. Prior work addresses cross-lingual rea-soning gaps by fine-tuning models on translated question pairs to improve alignment across lan-guages (Zhu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). How-ever, the scarcity of high-quality parallel data and the noise introduced by machine translation limit scalability and performance on complex reasoning tasks, making English-only reasoning (English-cot) remain the most effective baseline (Liu et al., 2025; Ranaldi et al., 2025). Some works add a second step with preference optimization, which requires external translation, and use English trajectories as preferred with respect to native trajectories (She et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025b) 

Multilingual Reasoning with Inference-Time In-tervention. To bypass data limitations, recent studies explore inference-time strategies adapted from monolingual reasoning. Longer chains of thought (Yong et al., 2025) and multilingual en-sembling via majority voting (Gao et al., 2025) both improve cross-lingual performance, with En-glish often serving as the most reliable reasoning language. As an alternative (Huang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025a), response generation can be manipulated using the logits to mimic those of En-glish. Nevertheless, they perform poorly compared to supervised fine-tuning methods.

Outcome-based re-ward modeling has recently shown that enforcing output consistency can improve reasoning with-out ground-truth supervision, but existing work is confined to English (Agarwal et al., 2025; Prab-hudesai et al., 2025; He et al., 2026). We extend this paradigm to multilingual reasoning. 

8 Conclusion and Future Work 

In this paper, we show that incentivizing cross-lingual consistency improves reasoning in the source language. Our method uses only mono-lingual questions, applying reinforcement learn-ing with an unsupervised cross-lingual consistency loss to further train an existing model. It assumes no preferred language and enforces consistency across all languages. Experiments on MGSM show substantial gains, especially for weaker languages, while also improving stronger ones and reducing performance gaps. Results on OOD benchmarks indicate good generalization, though with smaller gains. Since our method relies only on monolin-gual questions, we hypothesize that training on more diverse domains could further enhance gener-alization. In the future we would like to explore further domains, and extend our method to non-verifiable tasks. All things considered, our findings pave the way to improve the multilingual reasoning perfor-mance at large without any extra data annotation. 

Limitations 

Our experiments are limited to sub 20B LLMs, primarily due to compute constraints. While we observe that our method is increasingly effective as the size grows it would be interesting to explore its behaviour for larger models. In addition, we used the Qwen2.5 Models; It would be efficient to see 8the impact on more recent models such as Qwen3 (Yang et al., 2025a). Our training and evaluation were highly constrained by the data. The limited avail-ability of reliable multilingual reasoning benchmarks—particularly for low-resource lan-guages—restricts broader validation and remains a hindering challenge. We hope to address some of these limitations in future work.
