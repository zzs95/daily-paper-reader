# Abstract 

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) tra-ditionally relies on a sparse, outcome-based signal. Recent work shows that providing a fine-grained, model-intrinsic signal—rewarding the confidence growth in the ground-truth answer—effectively improves language reasoning training by providing step-level guidance without costly external mod-els. While effective for unimodal text, we find that naively applying this global reward to vision-language (V-L) reason-ing is a suboptimal strategy, as the task is a heterogeneous mix of sparse visual perception and dense textual reasoning. This global normalization creates mixture-induced signal degradation, where the training signal for visual steps is sta-tistically distorted by the predominant textual steps. We pro-pose Perception-Decomposed Confidence Reward (PDCR) ,a framework that solves this by aligning the reward structure with the task’s heterogeneous nature. PDCR first performs an unsupervised skill decomposition, introducing a model-internal Visual Dependence Score to quantify visual reliance and applying a clustering algorithm to separate perception and reasoning steps. Based on this, PDCR computes a de-composed advantage by normalizing confidence gains within each skill cluster. This intra-cluster normalization provides a stable, correctly-scaled signal for both perception and rea-soning. We demonstrate that PDCR outperforms the naive, global-reward formulation and sparse-reward baselines on key V-L reasoning benchmarks.

# 2. Related Works 

2.1. Reasoning in Multimodal Models 

Improving the reasoning capabilities of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) is an active area of research [ 4, 7, 10 – 12 , 19 , 22 , 26 , 27 , 35 , 37 , 44 , 46 ]. A common paradigm is to fine-tune MLLMs using Reinforcement Learn-ing (RL) with verifiable rewards [ 9, 28 , 38 ]. However, this approach typically relies on a sparse, outcome-based reward (i.e., whether the final answer is correct), which is inefficient for training complex, multi-step tasks. To overcome this, several works have introduced external Process Reward Models (PRMs) [ 3 , 21 , 23 , 31 ]. PRMs pro-vide a dense, step-level training signal, but are notoriously costly to train, often requiring extensive and difficult dataset curation. This cost has motivated a recent shift towards find-ing reward signals that are model-intrinsic or self-generated. 

2.2. Model-Intrinsic and Self-Rewarding Methods 

To address the costs of external PRMs, an alternative direc-tion is self-rewarding reinforcement learning, which sources rewards from the model’s own internal signals [ 45 , 48 , 55 ]. These signals often include logit-based self-certainty [ 54 ], self-confidence [14], or majority-voting [56]. A particularly relevant framework in the language do-main is the Progressively Ascending Confidence Reward (PACR) [ 43 ]. PACR provides a dense, step-level reward by calculating the increase in the model’s log-probability of the ground-truth answer, guided by the inductive bias that confi-dence should ascend during a correct reasoning trace. This approach has proven effective for text-only reasoning but has not been sufficiently adapted for the unique challenges of multimodal tasks. 

2.3. Decomposed Reward Modeling 

A parallel line of research focuses on decomposing complex reward signals. In text-only LLMs, this is often done by evaluating a response from various perspectives, such as helpfulness, safety, or correctness [ 5, 33 , 52 ]. This concept is also being explored in MLLMs, where the reward signal is often denser due to the multimodal inputs [25, 35]. However, existing work on vision-language decompo-sition primarily focuses on generating explicit textual de-scriptions (e.g., image captions) to serve as the basis for the reward [ 6 , 15 , 19 ]. Our work diverges from this approach by decomposing the original reasoning steps directly via an internal, process-level signal, rather than relying on the generation of additional, explicit text like an image caption. We argue that visual and linguistic capabilities are hetero-geneous skills with distinct distributions, and therefore ben-efit from independent normalization. Analogous concepts have been explored in concurrent works in other domains, such as multi-reward [17] or tool-use agentic tasks [57]. 18882 Figure 2. The baseline dense reward pipeline. # 4 . The Challenge of Heterogeneous Skills in Vision-Language Reasoning 

While the dense reward signal from ground-truth confidence growth (Eq. 5) is effective for unimodal language tasks, its naive application to vision-language (V-L) reasoning is suboptimal. The core issue, which we validate in this section, is that V-L reasoning is not a homogeneous process. Instead, it is a heterogeneous mixture of two functionally distinct, high-level skills: (1) sparse, but critical, visual perception 

steps and (2) dense, predominant textual reasoning steps. 

[Observation 1] : V-L Reasoning as a Heteroge-neous Skill Mix. The two primary skills in a V-L rea-soning trajectory serve different purposes. As illustrated in the example in Figure 3-(a), visual perception involves extracting evidence from the visual input I and translating it into textual observations. This includes identifying objects, their attributes, or their spatial relationships (e.g., “The im-age shows a person standing at a counter, actively using a POS terminal.” ). In contrast, textual reasoning involves logical deduction, calculation, or conclusion-drawing based on previously established facts (e.g., “The act of operating a POS terminal in a commercial setting is the primary role of a cashier.” ). To empirically validate this functional distinction, we analyze the model’s internal attention patterns. We first annotated a representative set of reasoning trajectories, cat-egorizing each step as either visual perception or textual reasoning (see Appendix 15 for full annotation details). We then measured the average attention each step paid to the 

input visual tokens . As shown in the plot in Figure 3-(b), the two skills exhibit distinct attention profiles. Visual percep-tion steps exhibit high attention to the input visual tokens, as they are responsible for converting visual information into a textual format. Conversely, textual reasoning steps attend almost exclusively to the preceding text tokens, using the information established by prior steps to perform logical inference. This shows that a V-L reasoning trajectory is not a homogeneous process, but a heterogeneous mixture of these two distinct capabilities. 

[Observation 2] : Statistical Imbalance of Skills. 

Beyond their functional differences, we find a statistical im-balance between the two skills. Analyzing the same ground-truth annotated set from [Observation 1] , we observe that visual perception steps are inherently sparse, account-ing for approximately 30% of the total reasoning steps, while the textual reasoning steps form the dense majority (approx. 70%), as shown in Figure 3-(c). This sparsity is a key driver of the normalization problem we describe next. 

[Observation 3] : Mixture-Induced Signal Degra-dation from Skill Imbalance. This statistical imbalance poses a significant challenge for the global normalization in Eq. 5. We term this issue mixture-induced signal degrada-tion, as the global statistics ( min , max ) are computed from a pool overwhelmingly dominated by the returns of textual 

steps. This global baseline is, therefore, unrepresentative of the sparse perception steps. Figure 3-(d) ( left ) illustrates this effect: the naive global normalization compresses and misaligns the perception ad-vantage distribution, which could lead to ill-scaled credit for these critical actions. # 5 . Method: Perception-Decomposed Confi-dence Reward (PDCR) 

Our method, Perception-Decomposed Confidence Reward (PDCR), directly addresses the core problem identified in Section 4: that a single, homogeneous reward is suboptimal for heterogeneous V-L skills. As illustrated in Figure 4, PDCR operates in two stages. Specifically, it (i) performs 

unsupervised skill decomposition to automatically cluster steps into visual perception and textual reasoning groups, and (ii) computes a decomposed advantage by normalizing confidence gains within each respective cluster, ensuring a more stable, intra-cluster comparison.

6.3. Baselines 

We compare our proposed PDCR against several key base-lines on both model backbones. For a direct and fair compar-ison, we train using identical system prompts and hyperpa-rameters (details in Appendix 16): GRPO [ 28 ]; DAPO [ 47 ], which solves the vanishing advantage problem of GRPO (for this baseline, we add only dynamic sampling to stan-dard GRPO, without incorporating other improvements from DAPO such as a higher clip rate.); and PACR [ 43 ], which applies a naive, globally-normalized ground-truth confidence growth process reward.

# 8. Conclusion 

We introduced PDCR to address the challenge of hetero-geneous skills in Vision-Language reasoning. By cluster-ing steps via a model-internal Visual Dependence Score, PDCR decomposes the confidence growth reward in an un-supervised manner. This intra-cluster normalization resolves mixture-induced signal degradation, providing stable advan-tages for both perception and reasoning.
