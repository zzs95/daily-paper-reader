## Introduction

The adoption of radiomics-based decision support in clinical imaging has advanced considerably, yet a persistent barrier remains: Most models function as opaque classifiers that yield a prediction without explaining how it was reached [[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR1 "1.Lauritsen SM, Kristensen M, Olsen MV, et al. Explainable artificial intelligence model to predict acute critical illness from electronic health records. Nat Commun. 2020;11(1):3852. 
                https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-020-17431-x
                
              "), [2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR2 "the Precise4Q consortium, Amann J, Blasimme A, Vayena E, Frey D, Madai VI. Explainability for artificial intelligence in healthcare: a multidisciplinary perspective. BMC Med Inform Decis Mak. 2020;20(1):310. 
                https://doi.org/10.1186/s12911-020-01332-6
                
              ")]. This limits clinician trust, prevents error detection, and complicates regulatory approval of AI tools in medical imaging [[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR1 "1.Lauritsen SM, Kristensen M, Olsen MV, et al. Explainable artificial intelligence model to predict acute critical illness from electronic health records. Nat Commun. 2020;11(1):3852. 
                https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-020-17431-x
                
              "), [2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR2 "the Precise4Q consortium, Amann J, Blasimme A, Vayena E, Frey D, Madai VI. Explainability for artificial intelligence in healthcare: a multidisciplinary perspective. BMC Med Inform Decis Mak. 2020;20(1):310. 
                https://doi.org/10.1186/s12911-020-01332-6
                
              ")]. Addressing this gap may benefit from not only global feature-importance methods but also spatially resolved, image-grounded explanations that connect quantitative signatures to patterns clinicians can recognise and interrogate.

Meningioma consistency prediction offers a concrete and clinically consequential use case for such a framework. The mechanical consistency of intracranial meningiomas is a central determinant of operative strategy [[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR3 "3.Shah I, Chung RS, Liu K, et al. Association between meningioma consistency and surgical outcomes. J Neurosurg. 2025;142(5):1331-1337. 
                https://doi.org/10.3171/2024.8.JNS241066
                
              ")]: Soft tumours are amenable to suction and ultrasonic aspiration, whereas firm tumours require extensive mechanical debulking, increasing operative duration and neurovascular injury risk [[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR3 "3.Shah I, Chung RS, Liu K, et al. Association between meningioma consistency and surgical outcomes. J Neurosurg. 2025;142(5):1331-1337. 
                https://doi.org/10.3171/2024.8.JNS241066
                
              "),[4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR4 "4.Zada G, Yashar P, Robison A, et al. A proposed grading system for standardizing tumor consistency of intracranial meningiomas. Neurosurg Focus. 2013;35(6):E1. 
                https://doi.org/10.3171/2013.8.FOCUS13274
                
              "),[5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR5 "5.Itamura K, Chang KE, Lucas J, Donoho DA, Giannotta S, Zada G. Prospective clinical validation of a meningioma consistency grading scheme: association with surgical outcomes and extent of tumor resection. J Neurosurg. 2019;131(5):1356-1360. 
                https://doi.org/10.3171/2018.7.JNS1838
                
              ")]. This critical property cannot be reliably inferred from conventional MRI, and while quantitative radiomics has demonstrated predictive AUCs of approximately 0.70–0.85 [[6](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR6 "6.Yao A, Pain M, Balchandani P, Shrivastava RK. Can MRI predict meningioma consistency?: a correlation with tumor pathology and systematic review. Neurosurg Rev. 2018;41(3):745-753. 
                https://doi.org/10.1007/s10143-016-0801-0
                
              "),[7](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR7 "7.Ugga L, Perillo T, Cuocolo R, et al. Meningioma MRI radiomics and machine learning: systematic review, quality score assessment, and meta-analysis. Neuroradiology. 2021;63(8):1293-1304. 
                https://doi.org/10.1007/s00234-021-02668-0
                
              "),[8](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR8 "8.Zhai Y, Song D, Yang F, et al. Preoperative prediction of meningioma consistency via machine learning-based radiomics. Front Oncol. 2021;11:657288. 
                https://doi.org/10.3389/fonc.2021.657288
                
              ")], existing meningioma consistency models typically reduce imaging data to a single probability estimate without clarifying which features drive the prediction, where within the tumour the discriminative patterns reside, or what MRI appearances they correspond to [[7](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR7 "7.Ugga L, Perillo T, Cuocolo R, et al. Meningioma MRI radiomics and machine learning: systematic review, quality score assessment, and meta-analysis. Neuroradiology. 2021;63(8):1293-1304. 
                https://doi.org/10.1007/s00234-021-02668-0
                
              "), [8](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR8 "8.Zhai Y, Song D, Yang F, et al. Preoperative prediction of meningioma consistency via machine learning-based radiomics. Front Oncol. 2021;11:657288. 
                https://doi.org/10.3389/fonc.2021.657288
                
              ")].

Interpretability techniques that partially address these limitations already exist in the broader radiomic literature: Voxel-wise feature activation maps [[9](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR9 "40.Vuong D, Tanadini-Lang S, Wu Z, et al. Radiomics feature activation maps as a new tool for signature interpretability. Front Oncol. 2020;10:578895. 
                https://doi.org/10.3389/fonc.2020.578895
                
              ")], radiomics-to-imaging dictionaries [[10](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR10 "49.Kuthuru S, Deaderick W, Bai H, et al. A visually interpretable, dictionary-based approach to imaging-genomic modeling, with low-grade glioma as a case study. Cancer Inform. 2018;17:1176935118802796. 
                https://doi.org/10.1177/1176935118802796
                
              ")], and SHAP-based feature attribution [[11](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR11 "Lundberg S, Lee SI. A unified approach to interpreting model predictions. arXiv. Preprint posted online 2017. 
                https://doi.org/10.48550/ARXIV.1705.07874
                
              ")] have each been applied in other clinical settings. However, these approaches have been used in isolation, and their combined application to meningioma consistency prediction—where the clinical question requires simultaneously knowing WHICH features are stable, WHERE they manifest spatially, and WHAT MRI appearances they correspond to—has not been reported. Furthermore, attention-based deep learning methods can highlight spatial regions of interest but do not specify which hand-crafted radiomic features drive predictions or how these relate to tissue biology [[12](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR12 "10.Selvaraju RR, Cogswell M, Das A, Vedantam R, Parikh D, Batra D. Grad-CAM: visual explanations from deep networks via gradient-based localization. Int J Comput Vis. 2020;128(2):336-359. 
                https://doi.org/10.1007/s11263-019-01228-7
                
              ")], and most meningioma radiomic models provide no spatial or mechanistic interpretation whatsoever [[7](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR7 "7.Ugga L, Perillo T, Cuocolo R, et al. Meningioma MRI radiomics and machine learning: systematic review, quality score assessment, and meta-analysis. Neuroradiology. 2021;63(8):1293-1304. 
                https://doi.org/10.1007/s00234-021-02668-0
                
              "), [8](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR8 "8.Zhai Y, Song D, Yang F, et al. Preoperative prediction of meningioma consistency via machine learning-based radiomics. Front Oncol. 2021;11:657288. 
                https://doi.org/10.3389/fonc.2021.657288
                
              "), [13](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR13 "Cepeda S, Arrese I, García-García S, et al. Meningioma consistency can be defined by combining the radiomic features of magnetic resonance imaging and ultrasound elastography. A pilot study using machine learning classifiers. World Neurosurg. 2021;146:e1147-e1159. 
                https://doi.org/10.1016/j.wneu.2020.11.113
                
              ")].

The primary aim of this study was therefore to apply and integrate established interpretability techniques—LASSO-based feature stability analysis, SHAP attribution, voxel-wise local radiomic mapping, and a radiomics-to-radiology dictionary—within a single coherent framework for meningioma consistency prediction and to jointly investigate which features drive predictions, where discriminative patterns reside within the tumour, and what MRI characteristics they correspond to. While the individual components have been described separately in the radiomic literature, their combined application to three-class meningioma consistency prediction using preoperative T1-Gd MRI, with systematic internal validation, has not been previously reported. This proof-of-concept study prioritises methodological transparency and explanation over predictive performance.

## Materials and Methods

### Study Design

This retrospective model development and internal validation study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and was approved by the Regional Committee on Health Research Ethics for the Region of Southern Denmark (ID S-20190105). Reporting and analysis adhered to the Transparent Reporting of a Multivariable Prediction Model for Individual Prognosis or Diagnosis (TRIPOD) guidelines and the Checklist for Evaluation of Radiomics Research (CLEAR) recommendations [[14](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR14 "13.Collins GS, Reitsma JB, Altman DG, Moons KGM. Transparent reporting of a multivariable prediction model for individual prognosis or diagnosis (TRIPOD): the TRIPOD statement. Br J Surg. 2015;102(3):148-158. 
                https://doi.org/10.1002/bjs.9736
                
              "), [15](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR15 "14.Won SY, Park YW, Ahn SS, et al. Quality assessment of meningioma radiomics studies: bridging the gap between exploratory research and clinical applications. Eur J Radiol. 2021;138:109673. 
                https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ejrad
                
              .2021.109673")].

Consecutive adult patients with intracranial meningiomas scheduled for elective surgical resection at our institution, Odense University Hospital, between 9 October 2021 and 25 May 2023 were screened for inclusion (Fig.[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#Fig1)). Inclusion criteria were age ≥ 18 years, surgically resected and histopathologically confirmed intracranial meningioma, ability and willingness to provide written informed consent, available preoperative 3D T1-Gd MRI acquired on the 3 T scanner, and intraoperative consistency grading by the attending neurosurgeon. Patients were excluded if intraoperative consistency grading was unavailable (_n_ = 2) or if preoperative imaging was unsuitable for radiomic analysis due to motion artefact or protocol deviation (_n_ = 4). No patients underwent preoperative tumour embolisation, which is not part of the routine clinical pathway at our institution.

### Intraoperative Consistency Assessment and Classification

Tumour consistency was assessed intraoperatively by the attending neurosurgeon using a standardised grading approach based on the Zada et al. consistency grading system [[4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR4 "4.Zada G, Yashar P, Robison A, et al. A proposed grading system for standardizing tumor consistency of intracranial meningiomas. Neurosurg Focus. 2013;35(6):E1. 
                https://doi.org/10.3171/2013.8.FOCUS13274
                
              ")]. This five-point scale grades meningiomas from grade 1 (extremely soft, suction alone) to grade 5 (extremely firm or calcified, approaching bone density), according to resistance to standard surgical procedures. The Zada scale has demonstrated strong inter-rater reliability (_κ_ = 0.87) and correlates significantly with surgical duration, extent of resection, and postoperative complications [[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR3 "3.Shah I, Chung RS, Liu K, et al. Association between meningioma consistency and surgical outcomes. J Neurosurg. 2025;142(5):1331-1337. 
                https://doi.org/10.3171/2024.8.JNS241066
                
              "),[4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR4 "4.Zada G, Yashar P, Robison A, et al. A proposed grading system for standardizing tumor consistency of intracranial meningiomas. Neurosurg Focus. 2013;35(6):E1. 
                https://doi.org/10.3171/2013.8.FOCUS13274
                
              "),[5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR5 "5.Itamura K, Chang KE, Lucas J, Donoho DA, Giannotta S, Zada G. Prospective clinical validation of a meningioma consistency grading scheme: association with surgical outcomes and extent of tumor resection. J Neurosurg. 2019;131(5):1356-1360. 
                https://doi.org/10.3171/2018.7.JNS1838
                
              ")].

For this study, Zada scale consistency grades were aggregated into three clinically meaningful categories using pre-specified cut-points: soft (Zada grade ≤ 2), intermediate (Zada grade = 3), and firm (Zada grade ≥ 4). This three-class approach captures the clinical spectrum from easily resectable soft tumours to challenging firm tumours while maintaining a distinct intermediate category reflecting average surgical difficulty. This three-class aggregation is also consistent with prior meningioma consistency studies and reflects the limited sample size available to support a five-category outcome.

### MRI Acquisition Protocol

All patients underwent preoperative brain MRI on a 3 T clinical system using institution-standard imaging protocols. The 3D T1-weighted gadolinium-enhanced sequence was acquired following intravenous administration of 0.1 mmol/kg gadolinium-based contrast agent (gadobutrol, Gadovist 1 mmol/mL; Bayer, Germany). Acquisition parameters included repetition time (TR) 7.9 ms, echo time (TE) 3.5 ms, acquisition matrix 252 × 200 × 184, and image resolution 1 × 1 × 1 mm 3. T1-Gd imaging provides tumour-to-brain contrast for meningiomas due to their characteristic intense homogeneous enhancement, facilitating accurate volumetric segmentation [[16](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR16 "15.Shiroishi MS, Cen SY, Tamrazi B, et al. Predicting meningioma consistency on preoperative neuroimaging studies. Neurosurg Clin N Am. 2016;27(2):145-154. 
                https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nec
                
              .2015.11.007"), [17](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR17 "16.Watts J, Box G, Galvin A, Brotchie P, Trost N, Sutherland T. Magnetic resonance imaging of meningiomas: a pictorial review. Insights Imaging. 2014;5(1):113-122. 
                https://doi.org/10.1007/s13244-013-0302-4
                
              ")].

### Image Preprocessing and Tumour Segmentation

Prior to feature extraction, all T1-Gd images underwent standardised preprocessing. Images were resampled to isotropic 1 × 1 × 1 mm 3 voxel dimensions using B-spline interpolation to ensure spatial consistency across patients. Intensity normalisation was performed using per-image _Z_-score standardisation (mean-centring and scaling by standard deviation computed independently for each 3D image volume), avoiding any inter-patient intensity calibration that could introduce data leakage. This has demonstrated superior performance for T1-Gd radiomic analysis [[18](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR18 "17.Carré A, Klausner G, Edjlali M, et al. Standardization of brain MR images across machines and protocols: bridging the gap for MRI-based radiomics. Sci Rep. 2020;10(1):12340. 
                https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-020-69298-z
                
              ")].

Three-dimensional tumour segmentation was performed using 3D Slicer software (version 5.10.0,www.slicer.org)[[19](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR19 "18.Fedorov A, Beichel R, Kalpathy-Cramer J, et al. 3D Slicer as an image computing platform for the Quantitative Imaging Network. Magn Reson Imaging. 2012;30(9):1323-1341. 
                https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mri
                
              .2012.05.001")]. For the modelling pipeline, index segmentations were generated using a semi-automated workflow by a trained medical student and manually corrected as needed. In a subset of 19 cases, these index segmentations were compared with independent manual segmentations by a neuroradiologist, who was blinded to intraoperative consistency grades and model outputs. The tumour volume of interest (VOI) encompassed the entire enhancing tumour mass while carefully excluding adjacent dural enhancement, vessels, and skull base structures. The semi-automated index segmentation was used as the primary ROI for model development and validation, while the manual neuroradiologist segmentations were used for reference comparison, training augmentation, and feature-reliability analysis.

### Inter-Observer Reliability Assessment

Segmentation agreement was evaluated on 19 cases (45% of cohort) by comparing the semi-automated index segmentations against independent manual neuroradiologist segmentations. Spatial overlap was quantified using the Dice similarity coefficient (DSC) [[20](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR20 "19.Dice LR. Measures of the amount of ecologic association between species. Ecology. 1945;26(3):297-302. 
                https://doi.org/10.2307/1932409
                
              "), [21](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR21 "20.Zou KH, Warfield SK, Bharatha A, et al. Statistical validation of image segmentation quality based on a spatial overlap index. Acad Radiol. 2004;11(2):178-189. 
                https://doi.org/10.1016/S1076-6332(03)00671-8
                
              ")]. DSC > 0.80 indicates good volumetric agreement [[22](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR22 "21.Taha AA, Hanbury A. Metrics for evaluating 3D medical image segmentation: analysis, selection, and tool. BMC Med Imaging. 2015;15(1):29. 
                https://doi.org/10.1186/s12880-015-0068-x
                
              ")].

Voxel-level categorical segmentation agreement was additionally quantified by Cohen’s _κ_ on binary tumour inclusion/exclusion labels, interpreted per Landis and Koch: _κ_ 0.61–0.80 substantial, _κ_ 0.81–1.00 almost perfect agreement [[23](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR23 "22.Cohen J. A coefficient of agreement for nominal scales. Educ Psychol Meas. 1960;20(1):37-46. 
                https://doi.org/10.1177/001316446002000104
                
              "), [24](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR24 "23.Landis JR, Koch GG. The measurement of observer agreement for categorical data. Biometrics. 1977;33(1):159. 
                https://doi.org/10.2307/2529310
                
              ")]. These agreement analyses evaluated segmentation reproducibility only; intraoperative consistency was not re-assessed by a second surgeon. Intraoperative consistency grading reliability of the Zada scale is established in prior work (_κ_ = 0.87)[[5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR5 "5.Itamura K, Chang KE, Lucas J, Donoho DA, Giannotta S, Zada G. Prospective clinical validation of a meningioma consistency grading scheme: association with surgical outcomes and extent of tumor resection. J Neurosurg. 2019;131(5):1356-1360. 
                https://doi.org/10.3171/2018.7.JNS1838
                
              ")].

Feature reliability was assessed using intraclass correlation coefficients (ICCs, two-way random effects, absolute agreement) [[25](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR25 "24.McGraw KO, Wong SP. Forming inferences about some intraclass correlation coefficients. Psychol Methods. 1996;1(1):30-46. 
                https://doi.org/10.1037/1082-989X.1.1.30
                
              "), [26](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR26 "25.Shrout PE, Fleiss JL. Intraclass correlations: uses in assessing rater reliability. Psychol Bull. 1979;86(2):420-428. 
                https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.86.2.420
                
              ")]. Features achieving ICC (2,1) ≥ 0.75 (good-to-excellent per Koo and Li [[27](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR27 "26.Koo TK, Li MY. A guideline of selecting and reporting intraclass correlation coefficients for reliability research. J Chiropr Med. 2016;15(2):155-163. 
                https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jcm
                
              .2016.02.012")]) were retained; those below this threshold were excluded [[28](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR28 "27.Xue C, Yuan J, Lo GG, et al. Radiomics feature reliability assessed by intraclass correlation coefficient: a systematic review. Quant Imaging Med Surg. 2021;11(10):4431-4460. 
                https://doi.org/10.21037/qims-21-86
                
              "), [29](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR29 "28.Traverso A, Wee L, Dekker A, Gillies R. Repeatability and reproducibility of radiomic features: a systematic review. Int J Radiat Oncol. 2018;102(4):1143-1158. 
                https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijrobp
                
              .2018.05.053")].

### Radiomic Feature Extraction

A total of 1409 radiomic features were extracted per tumour segmentation using PyRadiomics (version 3.1.0) according to the accompanying configuration file. Extracted features encompassed first-order statistics, shape descriptors, and five texture families (GLCM, GLRLM, GLSZM, GLDM, and NGTDM) computed across eight image types (original, exponential, gradient, logarithm, square, square root, wavelet, and LBP2D) of the T1-weighted gadolinium-enhanced image [[30](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR30 "29.Zwanenburg A, Vallières M, Abdalah MA, et al. The Image Biomarker Standardization Initiative: standardized quantitative radiomics for high-throughput image-based phenotyping. Radiology. 2020;295(2):328-338. 
                https://doi.org/10.1148/radiol.2020191145
                
              "),[31](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR31 "30.Whybra P, Zwanenburg A, Andrearczyk V, et al. The Image Biomarker Standardization Initiative: standardized convolutional filters for reproducible radiomics and enhanced clinical insights. Radiology. 2024;310(2):e231319. 
                https://doi.org/10.1148/radiol.231319
                
              "),[32](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR32 "31.Chaddad A, Daniel P, Niazi T. Radiomics evaluation of histological heterogeneity using multiscale textures derived from 3D wavelet transformation of multispectral images. Front Oncol. 2018;8:96. 
                https://doi.org/10.3389/fonc.2018.00096
                
              ")]. Grey-level discretisation used a fixed bin width of 32, and features were extracted after isotropic resampling to 1 × 1 × 1 mm 3 and per-image _z_-score intensity normalisation with a scaling factor of 100.

### Radiomic Feature Reliability and Preprocessing

To reduce redundancy, we next computed the pairwise Pearson correlation matrix among the reliable features [[33](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR33 "32.Berenguer R, Pastor-Juan MDR, Canales-Vázquez J, et al. Radiomics of CT features may be nonreproducible and redundant: influence of CT acquisition parameters. Radiology. 2018;288(2):407-415. 
                https://doi.org/10.1148/radiol.2018172361
                
              ")]. For each pair with |_r_|> 0.95, the feature with the lower ICC was discarded, maximising both reproducibility and information content [[34](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR34 "33.Ge G, Zhang J. Feature selection methods and predictive models in CT lung cancer radiomics. J Appl Clin Med Phys. 2023;24(1):e13869. 
                https://doi.org/10.1002/acm2.13869
                
              "), [35](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR35 "34.Park JE, Park SY, Kim HJ, Kim HS. Reproducibility and generalizability in radiomics modeling: possible strategies in radiologic and statistical perspectives. Korean J Radiol. 2019;20(7):1124. 
                https://doi.org/10.3348/kjr.2018.0070
                
              ")].

### Feature Selection Using LASSO Regularisation

Feature selection was performed using Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (LASSO) regularisation applied to multinomial logistic regression [[36](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR36 "35.Tibshirani R. Regression shrinkage and selection via the Lasso. J R Stat Soc Ser B Stat Methodol. 1996;58(1):267-288. 
                https://doi.org/10.1111/j.2517-6161.1996.tb02080.x
                
              "), [37](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR37 "36.Lambin P, Leijenaar RTH, Deist TM, et al. Radiomics: the bridge between medical imaging and personalized medicine. Nat Rev Clin Oncol. 2017;14(12):749-762. 
                https://doi.org/10.1038/nrclinonc.2017.141
                
              ")]. Within each outer training fold, a multinomial logistic model with L1 penalty and ‘saga’ solver was fitted on the 189 candidate features, with the regularisation strength (_C_) optimised by inner fivefold cross-validation. Features were ranked by the maximum absolute regression coefficient across the three consistency classes, and up to five features with the largest coefficients were retained for that fold.

Feature selection stability was defined a priori: Features selected in ≥ 60% of outer folds were considered stable and eligible for inclusion in the final model, whereas features selected in ≤ 40% of folds were excluded from the final signature.

### Training Augmentation Using Dual-Rater Segmentations

To maximise the effective training sample size without introducing data leakage, we exploited the availability of paired index and manual reference segmentations for a subset of patients. Cross-validation splits were defined strictly at the patient level and stratified by the neurosurgeon-assigned consistency class. For patients assigned to a given training fold, both the semi-automated index segmentation and the manual neuroradiologist segmentation were included as segmentation-derived samples with the same patient-level consistency label. For patients in the corresponding test fold, only the semi-automated index segmentation was used.

Augmented training sets contained up to 61 segmentations; each test set contained exactly one segmentation per patient (42 out-of-sample predictions total), with strict patient-level independence preserved throughout.

### Model Development and Validation Strategy

Considering the small cohort size, we implemented a nested stratified fivefold cross-validation (CV) framework to obtain nearly unbiased estimates of model performance and to avoid information leakage between feature selection and model evaluation [[38](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR38 "37.Varma S, Simon R. Bias in error estimation when using cross-validation for model selection. BMC Bioinformatics. 2006;7(1):91. 
                https://doi.org/10.1186/1471-2105-7-91
                
              ")]. The outer loop (5 folds) provided held-out test sets for performance estimation, while the inner loop (fivefold CV within each outer training partition) was used for hyperparameter tuning and LASSO-based feature selection. Stratified sampling in the outer loop ensured approximate preservation of the three consistency-class proportions in each fold.

All preprocessing and feature selection steps were applied within each outer training fold and then applied to test data, preventing information leakage.

Using the three LASSO-stable features as inputs, we trained and compared five classifiers: multinomial logistic regression, ordinal logistic regression, SVM, XGBoost, and CatBoost (detailed performance metrics for all classifiers are provided in Table[2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#Tab2)).

For each classifier, hyperparameters were optimised by grid search within the inner CV loop. Final models were then refitted on the full outer training fold using the selected hyperparameters and evaluated on the outer test fold. This process was repeated for all five folds, yielding 42 out-of-sample predictions per classifier (one per patient). The overall imaging, feature reliability, modelling, and interpretability workflow is illustrated in Fig.[2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#Fig2).

### Statistical Analysis and Performance Metrics

Primary model performance was assessed using macro-averaged one-versus-rest AUC (AUC_macro) and overall accuracy [[39](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR39 "38.Fawcett T. An introduction to ROC analysis. Pattern Recognit Lett. 2006;27(8):861-874. 
                https://doi.org/10.1016/j.patrec
                
              .2005.10.010"), [40](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR40 "39.Hand DJ, Till RJ. A simple generalisation of the area under the ROC curve for multiple class classification problems. Mach Learn. 2001;45(2):171-186. 
                https://doi.org/10.1023/A:1010920819831
                
              ")]. Secondary metrics included balanced accuracy, per-class sensitivity (recall) and precision, quadratic-weighted Cohen’s _κ_ [[23](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR23 "22.Cohen J. A coefficient of agreement for nominal scales. Educ Psychol Meas. 1960;20(1):37-46. 
                https://doi.org/10.1177/001316446002000104
                
              "), [24](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR24 "23.Landis JR, Koch GG. The measurement of observer agreement for categorical data. Biometrics. 1977;33(1):159. 
                https://doi.org/10.2307/2529310
                
              ")], Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC), and ‘off-by-one’ accuracy (proportion of predictions within one consistency level of the true class). Probabilistic calibration was evaluated using the multiclass Brier score (mean squared error between predicted probabilities and one-hot encoded labels) and the expected calibration error (ECE), averaging the absolute difference between predicted and empirical class probabilities across probability bins. Confusion matrices were constructed to characterise misclassification patterns between the three consistency classes.

Model performance metrics were summarised as mean values with 95% confidence intervals across the five cross-validation folds, whereas descriptive variables (e.g. age, tumour volume, inter-observer DSC) are reported as mean (standard deviation). For model comparison, we applied a Friedman test on the ranks of AUC_macro across folds, followed by Nemenyi post hoc tests when appropriate. Statistical analyses were conducted in Python (version 3.9) using scikit-learn, statsmodels, CatBoost, and XGBoost. A two-sided _p_ value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant. Differences in patient and tumour characteristics across the three consistency groups (Table[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#Tab1)) were assessed using the Kruskal–Wallis test for continuous variables (age) and the _χ_ 2 test or Fisher’s exact test for categorical variables (sex, WHO grade, tumour location, histological subtype), with Fisher’s exact test applied when expected cell counts were below five.

### Model Explainability and Local Radiomic Mapping

To elucidate how individual features and spatial patterns contributed to model decisions, we performed two complementary interpretability analyses. First, we computed SHAP values for the final CatBoost model using the three selected features [[11](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR11 "Lundberg S, Lee SI. A unified approach to interpreting model predictions. arXiv. Preprint posted online 2017. 
                https://doi.org/10.48550/ARXIV.1705.07874
                
              ")]. For each patient, SHAP values quantified the marginal contribution of each feature to the predicted probability of each consistency class. Global (across-patient) mean absolute SHAP values were used to rank feature importance, and class-specific mean absolute SHAP values were used to characterise how each feature contributed to soft, intermediate, and firm predictions.

Second, we generated local radiomic maps to visualise the spatial distribution of discriminative patterns within individual tumours. For the three key features (Textural Entropy, Local Homogeneity, and Calcification Index), voxel-wise maps were computed using an 11 × 11 × 11-voxel sliding window centred on each voxel inside the tumour mask (~ 1.3 cm 3 at 1 mm isotropic resolution) [[9](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR9 "40.Vuong D, Tanadini-Lang S, Wu Z, et al. Radiomics feature activation maps as a new tool for signature interpretability. Front Oncol. 2020;10:578895. 
                https://doi.org/10.3389/fonc.2020.578895
                
              ")]. Within each window, the feature value was recomputed using the same PyRadiomics definitions as for global extraction, and the result was assigned to the central voxel, producing continuous feature maps co-registered with the original T1-Gd images. These voxel-wise maps were generated purely as post hoc visualisations and were not used for model training, tuning, or evaluation, thereby avoiding any additional information leakage.

For group-level visualisation, feature maps were overlaid as semi-transparent heatmaps on axial T1-Gd slices (diverging colour map; warmer colours = higher values), thresholded to the tumour region with contours shown as white outlines. Representative montages compared central and mid-portion axial slices for soft versus firm tumours per feature. Feature distributions (mean values per tumour) were compared across consistency groups using Mann–Whitney _U_ tests with Bonferroni correction. A ‘radiomics-to-radiology’ dictionary (Table[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#Tab3)) maps each signature feature to its PyRadiomics definition, T1-Gd visual pattern, and consistency association.

### Patient Characteristics and Consistency Distribution

                https://doi.org/10.1186/s12880-015-0068-x

Voxel-level categorical agreement on binary tumour inclusion/exclusion labels, quantified by Cohen’s _κ_, was 0.82 (range 0.68–0.96), indicating almost perfect segmentation agreement between the two raters [[24](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR24 "23.Landis JR, Koch GG. ### Radiomic Feature Extraction and Redundancy Removal

A total of 1409 radiomic features were extracted per segmentation, spanning first-order, shape and five texture families (GLCM, GLRLM, GLSZM, GLDM and NGTDM) computed across original, exponential-filtered and wavelet-transformed T1-Gd images [[30](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR30 "29.Zwanenburg A, Vallières M, Abdalah MA, et al. The Image Biomarker Standardization Initiative: standardized quantitative radiomics for high-throughput image-based phenotyping. Radiology. 2020;295(2):328-338. 
                https://doi.org/10.1148/radiol.2020191145
                
              ")]. Of these, 638 (45.3%) features demonstrated good-to-excellent inter-rater reliability (ICC ≥ 0.75) on the dual-rater subset and were retained for further analysis [[27](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR27 "26.Koo TK, Li MY. A guideline of selecting and reporting intraclass correlation coefficients for reliability research. J Chiropr Med. 2016;15(2):155-163. 
                https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jcm
                
              .2016.02.012"), [28](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR28 "27.Xue C, Yuan J, Lo GG, et al. Radiomics feature reliability assessed by intraclass correlation coefficient: a systematic review. Quant Imaging Med Surg. 2021;11(10):4431-4460. 
                https://doi.org/10.21037/qims-21-86
                
              ")].

Correlation analysis among the 638 reliable features revealed substantial redundancy, particularly within texture families and between closely related wavelet sub-bands [[29](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR29 "28.Traverso A, Wee L, Dekker A, Gillies R.                 https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijrobp
                
              .2018.05.053")]. Applying a redundancy threshold of |_r_|> 0.95 and preferentially discarding the feature with the lower ICC removed 449 highly correlated features (70.4% of the reliable subset), yielding a compact set of 189 non-redundant, ICC-stable features for subsequent LASSO selection.

### LASSO Feature Selection and Stability

Within each outer training fold of the nested cross-validation, LASSO-regularised multinomial logistic regression selected up to five predictors from the 189 ICC-stable, non-redundant features [[36](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR36 "35.Tibshirani R. Regression shrinkage and selection via the Lasso. J R Stat Soc Ser B Stat Methodol. 1996;58(1):267-288. 
                https://doi.org/10.1111/j.2517-6161.1996.tb02080.x
                
              ")]. Across the five outer folds, 11 distinct features were selected at least once.

Three features demonstrated clearly higher selection stability and were considered the core _WHICH-level_ radiomic signature:

*   _Textural Entropy_ (wavelet-LLH_glrlm_RunEntropy)—selected in 5/5 folds (100%; ICC ≈ 0.78).

*   _Calcification Index_ (exponential_gldm_LargeDependenceHighGrayLevelEmphasis)—selected in 4/5 folds (80%; ICC ≈ 0.81).

*   _Local Homogeneity_ (wavelet-LHH_glcm_InverseVariance)—selected in 3/5 folds (60%; ICC ≈ 0.85).

The remaining eight features each appeared in ≤ 40% of folds and were excluded from the final signature according to the pre-specified stability rule. Because this signature was derived within the internal modelling workflow, the resulting performance estimates should be interpreted as internal validation of the selected signature rather than external validation.

### Multi-Model Performance Comparison

Using the three LASSO-stable features as inputs, we treated the choice of classifier as a sensitivity analysis of the same WHICH-level radiomic signature. Five models were evaluated within the same patient-level nested fivefold cross-validation framework: multinomial logistic regression, ordinal logistic regression, SVM, XGBoost and CatBoost.

CatBoost showed the most favourable numerical performance, with a mean macro-averaged one-versus-rest AUC of 0.87 and mean accuracy of 66.7%, compared with AUCs in the 0.77–0.82 range and accuracies around 52–57% for the other classifiers. Calibration performance was acceptable across all models, and CatBoost and ordinal regression showed the most favourable discrimination–calibration trade-off. A Friedman test on AUC ranks did not show statistically significant differences between classifiers (_χ_ 2 = 5.88, _p_ = 0.21), reflecting the modest sample size; therefore, the apparent performance advantage of CatBoost should be interpreted cautiously.

On this basis, CatBoost was selected as the final carrier of the three-feature radiomic signature, and all subsequent interpretability analyses (SHAP, local feature maps and the radiomics-to-radiology dictionary) were conducted using this model. Full discrimination and calibration metrics for all classifiers are summarised in Table[2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#Tab2) with detailed ROC curves, per-class sensitivity, and agreement metrics visualised in Supplementary Figure [S1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#MOESM2).

### Performance of the Final Model

Using this three-feature radiomic signature as input (WHICH), the CatBoost classifier achieved a fold-averaged overall accuracy of 66.7% (95% CI 47.3–86.1%) in the 42-patient cohort, exceeding a random three-class baseline of 33.3% and a majority-class baseline of 38.1%. The macro-averaged one-versus-rest AUC was 0.87, indicating good discrimination across soft, intermediate and firm tumours [[39](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR39 "38.Fawcett T. An introduction to ROC analysis. Pattern Recognit Lett. 2006;27(8):861-874. 
                https://doi.org/10.1016/j.patrec
                
              .2005.10.010")]. Balanced accuracy was 63.9% (95% CI 44.7–83.1%), suggesting that performance was not driven solely by the largest class.

From an ordinal perspective, the model achieved a quadratic-weighted Cohen’s _κ_ of 0.57 (95% CI 0.23–0.91) and a Matthews correlation coefficient of 0.51 (95% CI 0.20–0.82), consistent with moderate agreement beyond chance. The fold-averaged ‘off-by-one’ accuracy, defined as the proportion of predictions at most one category away from the true class, was 90.3% (95% CI 77.4–100.0%), corresponding to an aggregate out-of-sample proportion of 38/42 cases (90.5%). For reference, a majority-class classifier assigning all cases to the most frequent class (soft; _n_ = 16) would achieve an aggregate off-by-one accuracy of 69.0%, providing context for the model’s ordinal performance.

Class-specific one-versus-rest AUCs were highest for soft tumours (AUC_soft 0.937; 95% CI 0.793–1.000), followed by intermediate (AUC_intermediate 0.883; 95% CI 0.725–1.000) and firm tumours (AUC_firm 0.784; 95% CI 0.569–1.000). In cross-validated test sets, soft tumours achieved a sensitivity of 81.7% (95% CI 60.5–100.0%) and precision of 85.0% (95% CI 57.2–100.0%); intermediate tumours, a sensitivity of 50.0% (95% CI 3.7–96.3%) and precision of 55.3% (95% CI 10.4–100.0%); and firm tumours, a sensitivity of 60.0% (95% CI 28.6–91.4%) and precision of 58.3% (95% CI 22.5–94.2%).

These patterns are reflected in the _aggregate confusion matrix_ (Fig.[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#Fig3)), where misclassifications predominantly occurred between adjacent categories (soft ↔ intermediate, intermediate ↔ firm) and direct soft-firm swaps were uncommon, and in the _multiclass calibration plot_ (Fig.[4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#Fig4)), which shows generally acceptable calibration with only mild overconfidence at higher predicted probabilities.

## Discussion

### Principal Findings

This single-centre proof-of-concept study demonstrates that radiomics-based prediction of meningioma consistency can be made interpretable by integrating established explainability methods into a coherent disease-specific framework, illustrating one approach to addressing interpretability barriers in clinical AI adoption [[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10278-026-02017-8#ref-CR1 "1.Lauritsen SM, Kristensen M, Olsen MV, et al. ## Conclusion

We demonstrated that radiomics for meningioma consistency prediction can be made more interpretable by integrating established explainability tools within a coherent disease-specific pipeline. By combining LASSO-based feature selection, SHAP analysis and voxel-wise local radiomic mapping, we demonstrate a path towards moving beyond a single black-box score to a framework that explains which features drive the model, how they contribute to predictions, and where within the tumour the relevant patterns arise. The three key texture features identified here map onto qualitative MRI patterns that radiologists may recognise, and the resulting heatmaps translate abstract texture metrics into spatial cues with potential relevance to preoperative surgical planning—an approach that warrants prospective validation in larger, multi-centre cohorts.
