Title: Frequency disentanglement with State space gating network for medical image segmentation

URL Source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11517-026-03603-2

Markdown Content:
## Abstract

Precise automated segmentation of anatomical structures is a prerequisite for computer-aided diagnosis, radiotherapy planning, and quantitative medical analysis. However, existing models, whether based on convolutional neural networks (CNN) or transformer architectures, are primarily centered on the extraction and processing of spatial features. These approaches lead to _spectral feature entanglement_, where low-frequency global structures, mid-frequency contours, and high-frequency textures are indiscriminately mixed, degrading segmentation accuracy, particularly at object boundaries critical for clinical delineation. To address this, we introduce the FD-SSGNet, a framework that performs frequency disentanglement with State-Space gating. Our model first employs the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) to explicitly decompose feature maps into low-, mid-, and high-frequency components. It then leverages the Shift Bidirectional Selective Gate Mamba (SBSGM), with parallel, heterogeneously configured pathways to effectively model long-range dependencies specific to each frequency band. Finally, a dynamic fusion module adaptively reintegrates the processed multi-band features to produce a refined segmentation map. Extensive experiments on the challenging BTCV multi-organ and ACDC cardiac segmentation datasets demonstrate that FD-SSGNet achieves new state-of-the-art performance, validating the significant benefits of explicit frequency domain modeling for robust and accurate medical image analysis in clinical workflows. Our implementation is available at [https://github.com/singinghz/FD-SSGNet](https://github.com/singinghz/FD-SSGNet).

## Appendix A Supplementary experimental results

### Appendix A Supplementary experimental results

This appendix provides supplementary materials that further validate the robustness and efficiency of our proposed FD-SSGNet. Specifically, we present additional qualitative results focusing on complex clinical cases, alongside a detailed quantitative analysis of the model’s computational complexity.

### Visualizations of complex cases

To further demonstrate the superiority of FD-SSGNet in handling challenging anatomical structures, we provide extended qualitative comparisons on the BTCV multi-organ dataset in Fig. [7](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11517-026-03603-2#Fig7). The selected slices represent complex clinical scenarios characterized by severe organ deformations, irregular shapes, and boundary ambiguities. By incorporating zoomed-in views (red dashed boxes) of critical regions, it becomes visually apparent that our explicit frequency disentanglement strategy allows the model to preserve intricate local details. Compared to state-of-the-art baselines such as PVT-GCASCADE and MissFormer, our method significantly reduces under-segmentation errors and generates much cleaner, more precise boundaries for small and easily confused organs.

### Computational complexity trade-off

While our multi-pathway architecture effectively mitigates spectral feature entanglement, evaluating its computational feasibility is essential for practical clinical deployment. Figure [8](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11517-026-03603-2#Fig8) illustrates the trade-off between the number of parameters (Params) and computational complexity (FLOPs). To ensure a strictly fair comparison, all models were evaluated under identical experimental conditions (input resolution of \(224 \times 224\) on a single NVIDIA V100 GPU), and the results were averaged over five independent runs. As shown, although FD-SSGNet exhibits a moderate increase in parameter count compared to lightweight CNNs due to the parallel SBSGM pathways, it maintains a highly competitive FLOPs profile. It strikes an optimal balance, delivering state-of-the-art segmentation accuracy without introducing an unacceptable computational burden, thereby proving its suitability for advanced medical image analysis workflows.
