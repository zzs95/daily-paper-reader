ABSTRACT 

Accurate prediction of long-term functional outcomes for stroke patients remains a clinical challenge, despite advances in diagnostics and treatments. Most machine learning (ML) and artificial intelligence (AI) outcome prediction models lack robust strategies to incorporate high-dimensional clinical imaging data or to account for data availability at the point of care. In this study, we present an enhanced representation learning pipeline that fuses 2.5D magnetic resonance images (MRI), clinical data, and imaging biomarkers to predict 90-day modified Rankin Scale (mRS) for ischemic stroke patients. Using autoencoder-generated MRI embeddings, we systematically evaluate multiple ML/AI methods for both classification (90-day mRS > 2) and ordinal regression. Critically, we incorporate a privileged information paradigm, leveraging features available during training to enhance model generalizability without requiring them during inference. We developed and validated our models on a large public dataset (N=974) and an external validation dataset (N=738), demonstrating performance comparable to state-of-the-art convolutional neural network-based approaches with the added benefit of modularity (Test AUC 0.801; F1 0.699; MAE 1.179). Our results highlight the promise of representation learning and privileged information paradigms for bridging the gap between research and bedside prognosis in computational neurology. 

Introduction 

Strokes account for 17.5% of cardiovascular disease deaths in the United States and are a leading cause of disability[ 1 , 2]. Specifically, of the more than 795,000 people in the United States who experience a stroke each year, approximately 87% are ischemic strokes in which blood flow is inhibited from reaching portions of the brain[2]. While there have been significant innovations in ischemic stroke diagnostics and treatments in recent history, stroke recovery prognosis remains a neglected area of clinical translation. Answers to questions such as the level of expected functional recovery remain up to clinical intuition and not based on data-driven understandings of underlying trajectories of stroke recovery. Existing research includes machine learning (ML) and artificial intelligence (AI) models utilizing clinical features from electronic health records (EHR)[ 3 , 4, 5, 6 ] often combined with imaging biomarkers[ 7 , 8, 9, 10 , 11 , 12 , 13 ] or molecular biomarkers[ 14 , 15 ]. A few studies have integrated medical images themselves - typically as a sequence of 2D slices characterized as 2.5D - including non-contrast computed tomography (CT) [ 16 , 17 ], spatio-temporal CT perfusion [ 18 , 19 ], and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) [ 20 , 21 , 22 ] utilizing convolutional neural network (CNN) backbones. Recent work established that a representation learning pipeline can mimic that of a traditional CNN approach with added benefits of reduced training time and resources, reusable embeddings, and a modular architecture easing fine-tuning for specific populations[ 23 ]. Specifically in representation learning, “representations" of the high-dimensional data are combined with auxiliary data in downstream modeling as opposed to alternative fusion methods. A representation learning pipeline also allows for the fusion of multiple sequences and modalities as well as the integration of the privileged information paradigm - including information available only at training to influence the model weights. Acknowledging the limitations of real-time clinical data is critical to moving existing research literature toward clinical implementation. For example, image-derived features (IDF) such as stroke volume or brain region may be available at model training - with those features curated by expert radiologists for research purposes outside of regular clinical ARTICLE IN PRESS 

## ARTICLE IN PRESS care - but will not be readily available at the bedside when the model’s prediction would be most clinically relevant. Therefore, this article provides an enhanced representation learning pipeline developed on a public dataset to predict 90-day functional outcomes for ischemic stroke patients enhanced by 2.5D MRI sequences taken post-intervention. As shown in Figure 1, the pipeline can be divided into two pieces: the autoencoder which compresses the 2.5D MRI sequences into embeddings (Model 1) and a model that uses those embeddings in combination with EHR and IDF to predict outcomes (Model 2). We explore incorporation of multiple MRI sequences, robustly evaluate privileged learning for implementable stroke prognosis prediction, and validate our methods on an external dataset. The following methods and results are divided between Models 1 and 2. 

Results 

Model 1 

Reconstruction performance for the hold-out test set averaged across training folds for the proposed stroke-aware autoencoder-based model are presented in Table 1. In addition to the per-fold performance on each MRI sequence, the performance was summarized across folds to calculate the mean and pooled standard deviation using Cochrane’s formula. The DWI reconstruction performance on the hold-out test set achieved an average BCE of 0.195 ± 0.028 , SSIM of 0.907 ± 0.019 , and PNSR of 25 .188 ± 2.306 across folds. Given that stroke lesions represent a small proportion of the overall image volume, to assess the performance on regions surrounding the stroke lesions, the ground truth stroke lesion masks were dilated (Supplementary Note 1) to double the contained volume and region-specific pixel metrics were calculated. Within the dilated stroke lesion, the proposed autoencoder achieved an average NMSE of 0.011 ± 0.004 . For ADC, the autoencoder achieved an average reconstruction error on the hold-out test set of 0.578 ± 0.130 BCE, 0.854 ± 0.045 SSIM, 25 .075 ± 1.463 PSNR, and 0.005 ± 0.004 NMSE in the dilated stroke region. Example reconstructions and corresponding attention maps are included in Figure 2. 

Model 2: Standard Machine Learning 

Model 2’s classification and ordinal regression validation and test results for each ML method and data configuration are visualized in Supplementary Figure 1. The test results for the best ML method per configuration based on validation performance are presented in Tables 2 and 3 for classification and ordinal regression respectively. Notably, the RF model trained on EHR with ADC embeddings achieved the highest test AUC (0.810) and F1-Score (0.695) of all models; however, the model with DWI embeddings and EHR achieved the highest test AUPRC (0.733). The highest recall was achieved by RF trained on just EHR features alone (0.773). For ordinal regression, the RF model with EHR and DWI embeddings performed the best on the test data according to the C-index (0.724). The highest QWK and lowest MAE were achieved by the RF model trained on EHR and IDF. 

Model 2: Privileged Information 

Cross-validation score distributions across the 5 folds for base and privileged information configurations are presented in Figures 3b and 3c for classification and ordinal regression respectively. With EHR as base information, adding MRI embeddings did not significantly increase the average F1-score for classification ( t(4) = −1.143 , p-value= 0.317 ) or decrease the average MAE for ordinal regression ( t(4) = 0.922 , p-value= 0.409 ). Using MRI embeddings as base information achieved worse performance than with EHR as base information regardless of the type of privileged information provided. Adding IDF in the privileged space tends to reduce the standard deviation. The hold-out test classification and ordinal regression performance are presented in Tables 4 and 5 respectively. The highest performing classification model had EHR as base information and IDF and both DWI and ADC embeddings as privileged information (AUC of 0.760; AUPRC of 0.684; Precision of 0.712; MCC of 0.905). For ordinal regression, the model with EHR and both DWI and ADC embeddings as base features with no privileged information out-performed all other configurations across all metrics (C-index: 0.718; MAE: 1.185; QWK: 0.559; r: 0.543).

Discussion 

While compressing a full 2.5D MRI into an embedding of size 50 and reconstructing the original image is a difficult task, the test results shown quantitatively in Table 1 and qualitatively in Figure 2 show promising results. While some image degradation is expected, the illustrated reconstruction performance suggests that the latent embeddings have high information density. As seen in Figure 2, the model is particularly good at preserving the hyperintensity of large stroke volumes. Furthermore, in all three examples across varying stroke volumes and locations, the attention map closely resembles the ground truth stroke mask, providing further confidence that the embeddings are informed by the stroke region. In two of the three examples in Figure 2, there are other hyperintense artifacts that the model is able to correctly differentiate from stroke lesions. For both classification and ordinal regression of 90-day mRS in the development dataset, RF generally achieved the highest mean validation and test F1-score and MAE per data configuration (Supplementary Figure 1). Notably, when only embeddings were utilized, SVM was shown to perform the best during cross-validation (Supplementary Figure 1). On the hold-out test data, all models with MRI embeddings and EHR outperformed those with EHR and IDF in AUC, AUPRC, and C-index (Tables 2, 3). In terms of MCC, the best classification model was the RF model with ADC embeddings and EHR (MCC = 0.982) (Table 2). Likewise, the best ordinal regression model with DWI embeddings and EHR achieved the highest test C-index (0.724) (Table 3). In introducing the privileged learning paradigm for more clinically relevant modeling, the IDF features were moved exclusively to the privileged space. In addition, ablation experiments were performed to test different configurations of base and privileged information. While no statistically significant differences were observed in adding different types of privileged information (Figure 3), the performance validation mean score trended better with additional privileged information provided during training as is expected. For ordinal regression specifically, in addition to the average validation MAE decreasing from 1.291 to 1.236 when adding DWI and IDF as privileged information, the standard deviation also decreased suggesting a more robust model (Figure 3). On the hold-out test set, the model trained with IDF and DWI/ADC embeddings in the privileged space generalized better than any other configuration in terms of most metrics, achieving an AUPRC of 0.684 and MCC of 0.905 (Table 4). The classification TRAM model with no privileged information and only EHR data as base information for reference achieved an AUPRC of 0.605 and MCC of 0.888 – slightly under-preforming the RF counterpart above (Table 4). In ordinal regression, the model trained with EHR and DWI/ADC embeddings all in the base space with no privileged information performed the best across all test metrics (Table 5). While it is counterintuitive that IDF in the privileged space did not further increase performance, in this instance it is hypothesized that comprehensive embeddings of DWI and ADC already capture most of the same information. Since the classic ML models and TRAM models presented above were trained on the full EHR data available in the development dataset, these models were incompatible with the external validation set which had limited EHR variables. The advantage of TRAM is its ability to adapt to scenarios with limited clinical data available at inference. Therefore, in re-training the TRAM models for the subset of EHR variables available in the SOOP dataset, similar ablation studies were performed adding additional EHR variables from the development dataset, IDF, and DWI/ADC embeddings all to the privileged space. While average cross-validation performance on the development dataset did not significantly improve with the addition of privileged information, the models with privileged information, particularly EHR+IDF, generalized the best to both the hold-out test set for the development dataset and the external validation set (Tables 6, 7). Notably, the classification performance on the external validation set exceeds that of the hold-out test set of the development dataset (Table 6). Given that the model was trained to predict poor 90-day functional outcomes from admission-specific features and imaging, it is understandable that predicting discharge mRS would be a simpler task. It is expected that throughout recovery, mRS would ideally decrease, ARTICLE IN PRESS 

## ARTICLE IN PRESS resulting in a higher mRS at discharge than at 90-days. When dichotomized into favorable and unfavorable functional outcomes, the model is able to successfully transfer its knowledge of long-term unfavorable outcomes for an even better prediction of short-term outcomes. The low recall on the ICPSR dataset for the model trained on the limited set of variables (Table 6) is expected as distinguishing negative 90-day outcomes from a few variables in the regular space is a difficult task given all the factors that can contribute to poor outcomes in that time frame (e.g. social determinants of health, rehabilitation adherence, infections, comorbidities, etc.), regardless of privileged information and particularly with class imbalance (Figure 4). The fact that the TRAM models trained with all the EHR features had higher recall in the development cohort (Table 4), and that an increase in sensitivity was observed when adding the removed EHR variables to the privileged space (Table 6), demonstrate the informativeness of the EHR variables in the ICPSR dataset that are not available in the SOOP dataset in predicting poor long-term outcomes. This trend is not held for ordinal regression; however, the external validation set still achieved a C-index of 0.688 and MAE of 1.308 when the model with all available privileged information is included. Notably, the random forest models trained on the reduced feature set outperformed the TRAM models for classification across both the development and external validation set in all but specificity, precision, and MCC. Given that the privileged models show improvement over classic, limited random forest for ordinal regression, it is estimated that the elevated classification performance is due to class imbalance which is not explicitly accounted for in TRAM. The enhanced autoencoder presented herein resulted in an improvement in hold-out test classification performance over previous results on the same cohort using only DWI embeddings, achieving an increase of 0.076 AUC and 0.082 AUPRC[ 23 ]. Compared to previous models that have fused 2.5D imaging and clinical information with CNNs for functional stroke outcome prediction, our model performed similarly. Specifically, Jung et al. created an ensemble of deep learning models for clinical data, DWI, and fluid-attenuated inversion recovery (FLAIR) images achieving a cross-validation performance of 0.83 AUC[ 20 ]. Likewise, Park et al. fused DWI and clinical data to achieve an AUC of 0.808 through a traditional CNN network[ 21]. Notably, Liu et al utilized post-intervention non-contrast CT and achieved a MAE of 0.94 for ordinal score prediction and an AUC of 0.90 for 90-day mRS > 2[16] which slightly outperform the MRI-based results presented here. While similar to existing CNN methods in performance[ 23 ] highlighting the challenges of the underlying prediction task, this representation learning approach to multimodal data fusion and outcome prediction provides additional enhancements to performance and generalizability. First, the modular structure decreases overall training costs and complexity by creating label-agnostic image embeddings that can be used for various down-stream outcome predictions and tasks. Specifically, to predict M outcomes with a traditional 3D CNN-based approach, M separate CNNs would be needed. However, with the proposed representation learning approach, a single label-agnostic embedding for each MRI is created from one autoencoder’s encoder which can be utilized in any number of downstream prediction models. Given that the majority of the computational complexity for such a pipeline is in the multi-dimensional image processing module (either CNN or autoencoder), for every additional experiment, outcome, or method tested for outcome prediction, the representation learning approach becomes more efficient. Second, as demonstrated, the pipeline lends itself to image fusion by increasing the number of autoencoders instead of the complexity of each of the M CNNs with each additional imaging modality or sequence. Given the modular structure of the representation learning pipeline, if upon deployment the model requires finetuning to specific populations or other outcome of interest, the outcome prediction model (Model 2) can be easily re-trained without requiring access to advanced computing resources necessary for both CNNs and autoencoders. Furthermore, alternative ML methods such as interpretable models can be substituted for Model 2 to prioritize specific research or clinical goals and requirements. In a clinical setting, the ability to robustly predict 90-day functional outcomes, specifically with clinically operationalizable designs holds immense promise both for clinicians as well as patients and their families. Having data-driven estimates of functional outcomes has the potential to drive clinical conversations, therapeutic interventions, assist with logistics in the hospital, and inform patients and their families with realistic expectations and tangible follow-up care after discharge. Specifically, the incorporation of privileged information moves the concept of functional outcome prediction closer to the bedside and clinical utilization by addressing the reality of missing data and time-dependent data collection in clinical care. For example, IDF such as stroke volume are not manually assessed during the regular course of clinical care but when available can act as a proxy for stroke severity, strongly related to long-term outcomes[24]. While the TRAM models under-performed their standard ML counterparts, they are more generalizable to datasets with varying levels of feature availability as shown in the external validation set. Furthermore, medical imaging, while an information-rich source of clinical context for strokes, is more complicated to incorporate into point-of-care AI decision support systems than EHR-based information. Processing tasks such as skull stripping, template matching, and normalization must all be applied automatically and autonomously in order to inference new medical images on image-based models. By moving these image embeddings to the privileged space, they can be harnessed during training while circumventing these steps during inference resulting in a more transferrable and implementable models while still achieving strong performance. Limitations of this approach include limited imaging modalities and sequences as limited by template-matched MRI sequences in the available dataset. Future work can apply template matching to incorporate other MRI sequences from ARTICLE IN PRESS 

## ARTICLE IN PRESS additional datasets for further validation as well as other imaging modalities to enhance performance. Given that the models presented are trained on data from a single institution, data harmonization across sites may be required to handle different variable availability, imaging protocols, and cohort characteristics. Current missing value imputation could be replaced with the partially available privileged information paradigm to further enhance clinical applicability. Finally, as limited by the available dataset, the timing of MRI scans is opaque, a limitation that can be addressed by future studies and datasets. Finally, the TRAM models could be enhanced to improve performance through additional layer configurations and hyperparameter tuning and extended to natively handle class imbalance. In conclusion, this paper showcases a novel representation learning pipeline for fusion of 2.5D MRI and EHRs for long-term functional outcome prediction for ischemic stroke patients. With potential applications beyond stroke care to other medical fields, this privileged learning-informed representation learning pipeline serves as a prototype for 2.5D image-based prognosis providing numerous practical advantages of traditional 3D CNNs. 

Methods 

Development Dataset 

This study utilized the Inter-university Consortium for Political and Social Research (ICPSR) dataset titled "Annotated Clinical MRIs and Linked Metadata of Patients with Acute Stroke, Baltimore, Maryland, 2009-2019 (ICPSR 38464)"[ 25 ]. This study was approved by the Institutional Review Board at University of Michigan (protocol number: HUM00234328), ensuring ethical conduct in accordance with all relevant guidelines for research involving human subjects including the Helsinki Declaration. Written informed consent from patients in this published dataset was waived by the Johns Hopkins Internal Review Board (IRB00228775) since this study involved no more than minimal risk to the involved subjects. The full dataset includes 2,888 patients admitted to the Johns Hopkins Comprehensive Stroke Center between 2009 and 2019 with a clinical diagnosis of acute or early subacute stroke [ 26 ]. It contains comprehensive EHR and post-intervention clinical MRI sequences with manually segmented stroke lesion masks. Of these, 2,164 (75%) patients were diagnosed with an ischemic stroke at admission (See [ 26] for further details). The modified Rankin Scale (mRS) — a 7-point measure of neurologic disability (0: no symptoms, 6: death) — was available at 90-days for 974 (45%) ischemic stroke patients (Figure 4). To support downstream classification (90-day mRS > 2)and ordinal regression analyses, these patients were randomly split — stratified by 90-day mRS > 2 — into training/validation (80%) and hold-out test (20%) sets, matching those in [23]. Cohort demographics are summarized in Table 8. Preprocessing of clinical data followed that in [ 23]. For continuous variables, mean imputation for missing variables and z-score normalization based on the training set were applied to both training and test data. After one-hot encoding categorical variables, the final dataset comprised 40 features (Supplementary Table 1). Clinical MRI sequences include diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) and apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) maps acquired on at least three scanners and at two field strengths (see Table 8 and dataset documentation [ 25 ]). Axial scans were skull stripped and registered to Montreal Neurological Institute (MNI) space as part of the preprocessing[ 25 , 26]. Additionally, IDF including thrombus location and lesion volume were manually curated and provided as part of the ICPSR dataset[ 25 , 26 ]. Additional preprocessing was performed to min-max normalize the pixel values within each image prior to analysis. 

External Validation Dataset 

To externally validate the models, we utilized the Stroke Outcome Optimization Project (SOOP) dataset which contains stroke inpatients seen at Greenville Memorial Hospital from 2019-2020 [ 27, 28 ]. Of the 1,715 individuals included in the full dataset, 1,461 (85%) had an acute ischemic stroke and 738 of those had discharge mRS available. The full cohort characteristics are presented in Table 9 and discharge mRS distribution is visualized in Figure 4. Variation between the ICPSR training/validation cohort and SOOP external validation cohort were calculated prior to filling missing values using the χ2 and t-test with unequal variance for categorical and continuous variables respectively (Table 9). Missing body mass index (BMI) entries were imputed with the ICPSR training mean BMI. Continuous variables (age, BMI, and National Institute of Health Stroke Scale (NIHSS) total) were scaled using the mean and standard deviations calculated from the ICPSR training set. Categorical variables were one-hot encoded. Features included in modeling are bolded in Supplementary Table 1; note that since etiology was not present in the ICPSR dataset, it was not included as a feature for prediction. The SOOP dataset also included a variety of clinical MRI sequences including ADC, T1, and FLAIR. However, since B0 sequences were not provided, we were unable to match these images to the template utilized by ICPSR preventing successful downstream integration.

Model 2: Functional Outcome Prediction 

Model 2 takes the embeddings learned from Model 1 in combination with EHR and IDF to predict 90-day mRS both via classification (mRS > 2) and ordinal regression. Four different ML methods were evaluated: support vector machine (SVM), random forest (RF), XGBoost (XGB), and an artificial neural network (ANN). The ANN harnessed Adam optimization with a learning rate of 0.001, ReLU activation on its hidden layers, and employed early stopping when training loss did not decrease by 1 e − 4 for 10 epochs with a maximum of 500 epochs. Ablation experiments were performed to evaluate the individual and combined contributions of EHR, IDF, and MRI embeddings for DWI and ADC. Grid search with 5-fold cross validation was conducted on all parameter combinations presented in Supplementary Table 2 for each model to identify the combination producing the best average validation score. In all cases involving MRI embeddings, the three autoencoders resulting from the three train/validate folds for Model 1 were utilized and the best downstream average cross validation performance was used to select the autoencoder fold with the best performance (Figure 6). After re-fitting the models on the full training/validation set with optimal parameters and embeddings from the best autoencoder fold, paired t-tests were conducted across the scores from each of the 5 folds to establish significant performance improvements among different configurations of input features. The final models were then evaluated on the hold-out test partition - which was consistent across all models and experiments - providing an estimate of the model’s expected performance on unseen data. Poor functional outcomes were defined as 90-day mRS > 2 for consistency with existing literature [ 29 , 30 , 31, 32 , 33, 34 ,35 , 36, 23 ]. For classification models, the F1-score was minimized and when possible, class weights were balanced (i.e SVM and RF). Evaluation metrics for classification included standard ML metrics: area under the receiver operating curve (AUC), area under the precision recall curve (AUPRC), recall, specificity, F1-Score, and precision. In addition, Matthew’s Correlation Coefficient (MCC) which balances the geometric mean of the correct rates against the geometric mean of the error rates was included. When the MCC is high, the four basic rates - true positive (TP), true negative (TN), false positive (FP), and false negative (FN) - are also guaranteed to be high. Privileged Information Paradigm 

While all information is available retrospectively at training, some data may not be available prospectively at the time of inference. Therefore, we seek to utilize this second type of information, deemed "privileged information" only during training. Specifically, reliable IDF are not readily available at the point of care, requiring tedious manual segmentation of the imaging by expert radiologists. Therefore, for the following experiments we will split our features into base features X - available at both training and inference - and privileged features X∗ - available only during training. The privileged learning paradigm allows X∗

to impact the model during training by acting as a teacher and guiding the model toward better, more informed predictions. During inference (validation/testing), only X is provided to the model. In this work, we utilized one specific method of privileged leaning integration into a neural network. The Transfer and Marginalize (TRAM) Network architecture [ 39 ] consists of a base and privileged branch which both have a respective encoder and predictor (Figure 3a). The base encoder, ψb, is comprised of a single linear layer of 4 nodes and takes in the base features 

X. The output of ψb is passed to the base predictor ρb which is also a linear layer of 4 nodes followed by batch normalization, ReLU activation, and dropout with the option of an additional linear layer with 4 nodes preceding the output layer. During training, X∗ is also available and encoded via the privileged encoder, ψp which consists of a linear layer with 4 nodes followed by batch normalization, ReLU, and dropout. The output of ψb and ψp are both provided to the privileged predictor, ρp which mimics ρb’s structure described above. Back propagation from the privileged loss, Lp, effects ρp, ψp, and ψb but not ρb. The base loss, Lb, updates weights in the base branch only (i.e. ρb and ψb). During inference, only base information, X, is provided and passed sequentially through ψb and ρb without entering the privileged branch. ARTICLE IN PRESS
