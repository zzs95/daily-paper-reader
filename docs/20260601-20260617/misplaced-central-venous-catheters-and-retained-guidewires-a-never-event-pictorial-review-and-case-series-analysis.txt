## I NTRODUCTION

A central venous catheter (CVC) is an indispensable clinical tool in contemporary patient care. This indwelling device is inserted into a large, central vein and is used for administering fluids, blood products, chemotherapy, medications, parenteral nutrition, and concomitantly for sample collection and hemodynamic monitoring.[[1](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)]

CVC placement was conventionally done with the classical landmark technique. However, the current guidelines recommend real-time ultrasound guidance for visualization of the target vein. The vein is punctured under sonological guidance, and a guidewire is percutaneously deployed in the target vein.[[2](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)] Using the percutaneous vascular access “Seldinger technique,” the catheter is then advanced over the guidewire for the intended placement of the catheter, which is confirmed with a chest X-ray. Certain guidelines describe the use of the “Modified Seldinger” technique for CVC placement for the jugular and femoral veins.[[3](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)]

Like all other invasive procedures, the CVC also carries its own share of complications. The main mechanical complications associated with CVC are inadvertent arterial puncture, pneumothorax, hemothorax, mediastinal hematoma, pleural effusion, cardiac tamponade, air embolism, arrhythmias, and local nerve damage. Rare complications associated with the procedure include brachial plexus injury, thoracic duct damage, a misplaced/retained guidewire, and thoracic or abdominal cavity puncture.

This case series analysis was intended to discuss the factors leading to the rare complication of a misplaced/retained guidewire and misplaced catheter tips during CVC placement. This analysis also highlights the challenges encountered by the treating team while delineating management strategies and potential methods to mitigate this “never event.”

## P ATIENTS AND M ETHODS

We conducted an analysis of misplaced intravascular guidewires and catheters during central venous catheterization under ultrasonographic guidance. These cases were received at a tertiary care vascular surgery center for management. This study was conducted prospectively between January 2024 and August 2025. All patients who presented to the vascular surgery center with confirmed misplaced intravascular guidewires and catheters, and who were willing to participate in the study, were included in this study. Willingness to participate was obtained from the legal parents in case the patient was a minor. This analysis was performed in line with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and Good Clinical Practices. Patients’ demographic and perioperative data were obtained from the hospital records manually.

## R ESULTS

We report on four cases of retained guidewires and four cases of misplaced catheter tips. The cohort comprised of six males (including two children) and two females. The age of the cohort was between 3 years and 88 years, with a mean age of 45.4 years. The CVC placement was done as an emergency procedure in 75% of cases, and real-time sonological guidance was used in all eight patients for the placement. Consultants were the primary operators in two cases, and the other six cases were done by residents under supervision. Out of the four cases of retained guidewire, immediate identification of intravascular retention of guidewire was observed in two cases; in the other two cases, it was observed incidentally after 2 and 4 days, respectively, postprocedure. All four misplaced catheter tips were identified immediately postprocedure. Details of retained guidewires and misplaced catheters are described in Table 1.

### Case 1

An 88-year-old male with diabetes mellitus Type 2, hypertension, and chronic kidney disease got admitted with dyspnea and fever. While he was being managed for pneumonia, he was found to have a retained foreign body in the thorax on the chest X-ray. On further evaluation using a contrast-enhanced computed tomography (CT) scan, the retained foreign body was found to be a misplaced guidewire extending from the right subclavian vein to the inferior vena cava bifurcation. The guidewire was traversing across the superior vena cava and the right atrium (approximate length of retained guide wire was 410 mm). The cranial tip of the retained guidewire was found lying posterior to the right subclavian vein [Figure 1a and b]. The patient had undergone right internal jugular vein hemodialysis catheter insertion 2 days prior in a civil hospital. An attempt was made to remove the guidewire through endovenous snaring through the iliac veins. The bilateral iliac veins were found to be extremely tortuous, and negotiation for snaring was therefore abandoned. A 5F multipurpose catheter was deployed through the right cephalic vein, and the fluoroscopic examination revealed that the cranial end of the retained guidewire was lying in the extravascular plane, in between the two heads of the right pectoralis muscle [Figure 1c]. Attempts to snare the guidewire resulted in considerable traction on the vein at multiple sites, likely due to fibrotic changes associated with long-standing intravenous foreign body [Figure 1d]. In view of the poor general condition of the patient with more risk than benefit in retrieval of the long-standing intravenous foreign body, the procedure was abandoned.

### Case 2

A 9-year-old male child with special needs and a case of non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma was reported to have a misplaced guidewire in the left great saphenous vein (GSV) during the process of a peripherally inserted central catheter (PICC) line placement [Figure 2a]. As the GSV is a subcutaneous vein, he underwent left GSV venotomy and guide wire extraction under sonological guidance [Figure 2b-d].

### Case 3

A 52-year-old gentleman, who was a known case of chronic kidney disease, was reported to have a misplaced guidewire during insertion of a hemodialysis catheter in the right femoral vein. On a CT venogram, the retained guidewire was found to be in the inferior vena cava with its cranial end in the right atrium and caudal end in the right common femoral vein [Figure 3a]. He underwent endovenous snaring (Trefoil En-snare) of the retained catheter using the right common femoral vein as an access site. Intraoperative fluoroscopy showed the caudal end of the retained catheter in the right common femoral vein [Figure 3b and c], which was extracted through the venotomy site [Figure 3d and e]. The venotomy was repaired.

### Case 4

A 42-year-old female, who is a known case of cerebral venous thrombosis, obesity, hypothyroidism, and acute bilateral lower limb ischemia (Rutherford class IIb) operated, presented to the emergency department in status epilepticus. She was immediately resuscitated and placed on elective mechanical ventilation. She underwent a high-resolution CT chest which showed a retained guidewire with the cranial end in the superior vena cava and the caudal end in the pulmonary artery [Figure 4a and b]. She had undergone right internal jugular vein cannulation under ultrasonographic guidance 4 days prior in a civil hospital. The retained guide wire was snared (Trefoil En-Snare) percutaneously using the right femoral vein access [Figure 4c-e].

### Case 5

A 3-year-old male child, who was a known case of B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia, underwent a PICC line in the right cephalic vein. During insertion, the PICC line broke from the hub and was retained in the right cephalic vein [Figure 5a and b]. While being evaluated in the hybrid operation theatre, the PICC line was found to have migrated to the right atrium and the pulmonary artery [Figure 5c]. The PICC line was removed percutaneously using the left common femoral vein approach [Figure 5d and e].

### Case 6

A 72-year-old male, a known case of diabetes mellitus-II, hypertension, and chronic kidney disease on maintenance hemodialysis, had secondary failure of his left brachiocephalic arteriovenous fistula. An urgent right internal jugular vein hemodialysis catheter (of size 16F) was placed in an outside medical facility. However, he reported to our hospital with a non-functional hemodialysis catheter. No backflow was observed on assessing the hemodialysis catheter. A CT chest revealed that the catheter was lying in the extra-luminal plane after puncturing the superior vena cava, with the caudal tip lying in the anterior mediastinum [Figure 6a-d]. He was planned for a trial of endovenous balloon occlusion of the superior vena cava puncture, with simultaneous removal of the intra-thoracic hemodialysis catheter tip. Because of high chances of balloon occlusion failure, a bailout plan of trap-door thoracotomy was kept in reserve. A left internal jugular vein central line was placed to be able to ensure contrast opacification of the area of interest during balloon occlusion through the right common femoral vein access site. The procedure was done under general anesthesia to cater for any rise in cerebral venous pressure due to SVC occlusion and any emergency need to convert to trap-door thoracotomy. However, the SVC puncture was sealed with a 10 min balloon occlusion (14F balloon) with no contrast blush on completion fluoroscopy [Figure 6e and f]. A fibrotic constriction at the origin of the SVC was noticed, with no flow restriction [Figure 6g]. Secondary patency of his left brachiocephalic arteriovenous fistula was also restored by thrombectomy and venoplasty of the arterialized cephalic vein.

### Case 7

A 30-year-old male, a case of chronic kidney disease and diabetes mellitus-II, was undergoing left internal jugular vein hemodialysis catheter placement (size 14F) when no backflow was noticed from the catheter. The chest X-ray revealed the misplaced hemodialysis catheter. Using the left common femoral vein access, under local anesthesia, a multipurpose catheter was deployed over a 0.035” Terumo guidewire, and the left subclavian vein was negotiated. The multipurpose catheter was replaced with a 12-F balloon over a stiff wire. Once the balloon was in place under fluoroscopic guidance, the misplaced catheter was retracted from the vein, and the balloon was inflated at the site of puncture for 5 min. A check venogram revealed complete luminal opacification of the left subclavian vein with no contrast blush in the thorax [Figure 7a-c]. The hardware was removed.

### Case 8

A 67-year-old female, a case of chronic kidney disease, diabetes mellitus-II, and coronary artery disease-post angioplasty, underwent hemodialysis catheter placement through the right common femoral vein access site. No backflow was noticed, and there was a sudden onset of tachycardia and hypotension. While she was being resuscitated, her laboratory investigations revealed a significant drop in hemoglobin. An urgent ultrasonography and CT angiography were done. The tip of the hemodialysis catheter was found to be lying near the transverse process of L5 vertebra, in the retroperitoneum, with a retroperitoneal hematoma [Figure 8a]. The patient was on dual antiplatelet agents (tablet ticagrelor and aspirin). After optimization, the patient underwent left common femoral vein-based contralateral, retrograde cannulation of the right common iliac vein using an endovascular catheter over a 0.035 Terumo guidewire [Figure 8c and d]. Since the retroperitoneal bleed could not be localized on the CT angiogram, first, the internal iliac vein was cannulated, and a check venogram was taken. No contrast blush was found [Figure 8b]. Then the external iliac vein was cannulated, and after localization of the probable site of external iliac vein injury, a 10F balloon was preplaced at the site. Thereafter, the hemodialysis catheter was removed with simultaneous balloon occlusion of the external iliac vein injury segment [Figure 8e-g]. No contrast extravasation was found on check venogram after 5 min of balloon occlusion. The hardware was removed.

## D ISCUSSION

The incidence of catheter tip misplacement and inadvertent guidewire retainment during obtaining venous access is reported at 3.7% and 3%–18%, respectively. The wide variation in the incidence of guidewire retainment is due to the lack of large prospective, multicenter studies.[[4](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx),[5](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)] The guidelines for CVC placement have very elaborately described the safety protocols which should be exercised for optimal outcome.[[1](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx),[2](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)] Despite the best efforts, these “never events” do occur and are attributable to a plethora of factors.

Several authors have documented that the reason for retained guidewires despite checklists, alerts, and repeated education is because all these depend on the sole tenet of the operator’s remembrance. The operator is usually under great duress at the time of deployment due to the prevailing emergency, the peer pressures, and often the distraction to safeguard the life at stake during an emergency.[[1](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx),[2](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx),[6](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)]

Catheter tip misplacement carries the risk of cardiac tamponade, mediastinal hemorrhage, compromised infusion effectiveness, extravasation of medications and fluids, arrhythmias, and perforation of hollow viscous and other organs. Anatomical variations of the vasculature have also been implicated as a reason for the misplacement of the catheter. A large multicentric study has demonstrated that the catheterization of the right internal jugular vein has the lowest incidence of catheter tip misplacement.[[6](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)] Currently, there is no gold standard for identifying the catheter tip location but the same can be confirmed with chest X-ray, fluoroscopic examination, and 2D electrocardiography.[[1](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx),[2](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx),[3](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)]

One of the prerequisites for safe CVC deployment is the essential requirement of confirmation of the removed guidewire in the field to prevent the missing of retained guidewire in the vasculature. In case the removed guidewire is not seen, a chest X-ray should be immediately done to confirm retention. Only few guidewires are reported as retained after the procedure.[[7](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx),[8](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)] This “never event” often requires surgical removal, and when such a surgical procedure is done in an already compromised patient, it adds to the increase in associated morbidity and mortality.[[9](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)] Our analysis also showed that surgical or endovascular removal of retained guidewires or malpositioned catheter tips is associated with increased morbidity in patients.

The surgical extraction of the guidewire can be done with an open technique or using endovascular hardware. As most of the patients are high-risk and being managed in an intensive care setting, the endovascular route is preferred as it offers the least operative complications. Vascular snares are by far the most preferred choice. They are available in several configurations of single loop, triple loop, or four loop devices. Beyond the commercially available snare kits, snares can be made with available hardware.[[10](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx),[11](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)] Rescue and retrieval devices like the “Alligator Retrieval Device (Chestnut Medical Technologies, Menlo Park, Calif) are also used for the retrieval of retained guidewires”.[[12](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)] Open surgical extraction is a possibility, especially when the endovascular access sites are unavailable. Sonological or fluoroscopic guidance in the intra-operative period should be judiciously used, as we did during patient management, for optimizing the process of guidewire retrieval.[[1](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx),[2](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)]

Asymptomatic retainment of guidewire is rare, but possible if guidewire is attached to the vessel wall and is discovered accidentally, like the case 1 of this series. Whether an effort should be made to remove this long-retained guidewire is a matter of debate, and few case reports have suggested conservative management, as we offered in our patient.[[13](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx),[14](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)]

Oversight in procedural vigilance in a dynamic, emergent, and high-pressure environment might also play a role in inadvertent guidewire advancement into the vasculature and of the catheter tip beyond the vasculature. This role of a high-pressure environment is evident in this case series with the maximum events occurring during the emergency deployment. In our analysis, all the catheter insertions were done under ultrasonographic guidance. In a descriptive analysis by Nicholas Kassis _et al_., out of 24 patients who had guidewire retention following CVC insertion, 23 patients had undergone the procedure under ultrasonography guidance.[[15](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)] This shows that the use of ultrasonography does not necessarily decrease the incidence of guidewire wire retention following CVC insertion in patients. Physical stop to complete internal placement of guidewires by using a forcing function like the “Venner wire-safe” or using built-in passive resistance mechanisms to prevent accidental complete intravascular placement might help in addressing this health care “never event.”[[16](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)] Another such “Guidewire retention prevention system (GRiPS)” works on a one-way adapter mechanism which allows the guidewire’s retrograde passage (away from the patient) but limits antegrade movement except on a manual override.[[17](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)]

Pressure wave-form analysis, manometry, ECG guidance, blood-gas analysis, use of fluoroscopy, and image intensifiers have been suggested for optimal outcomes during CVC placement. However, there is still no foolproof method which can eliminate the possibility completely.[[8](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx),[18](https://journals.lww.com/jmsc/fulltext/9900/misplaced_central_venous_catheters_and_retained.264.aspx)] CVC misplacement beyond the vasculature needs to be tackled with a systematic approach. First, once the diagnosis is made, no attempt to remove the catheter should be made. Injury to the vasculature which is sealed with the catheter, can prove catastrophic. In most of our cases we deployed the endovascular technique of simultaneously occluding the puncture in the vessel wall with an appropriately sized balloon, under fluoroscopic guidance and withdrawing the CVC. A post-procedure check angiogram was taken to ascertain complete hemostasis.

The golden dictum of “Experience plays a pivotal role in the art of medicine” holds true in this context, too. Operator inexperience to follow a protocol, inability to understand the anatomy, and evolving skill set can all lead to loss of guidewire control and CVC misplacement. Guided procedures with impetus on following set protocols should be part of the teaching curriculum. Still, if such an eventuality arises, then the war cry should be “If in doubt, shout, and don’t take it out!”

## C ONCLUSION

This pictorial review and case series documents eight patients and exemplifies the intricate challenges associated with the “never events” of central venous catheterization, namely a retained guidewire and misplaced CVC. It underscores the importance of an integrated, multidisciplinary approach which is protocol driven and tested in the complex emergency care surroundings. It highlights the role of operator awareness and use of assisted technology in mitigating the debilitating effects of such conditions and improving patient outcomes. This endeavor aimed to enhance the broader knowledge base and enable a deeper understanding among healthcare professionals to collaboratively enhance patient care outcomes in the complex emergency care clinical domain.
