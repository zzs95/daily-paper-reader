Abstract 

Tuberculosis remains a major global health burden, and timely screening with chest X-ray imaging is particularly important in resource-limited clinical settings. However, many deep learning approaches for automated tuberculosis detection are constrained by class imbalance, computational overhead, and limited interpretability. This study proposes LiteTBNet-ECA, a lightweight and interpretable framework for binary classification of tuberculosis versus normal chest X-ray images. The model adopts MobileNet-style inverted residual design with depthwise separable convolutions for computational efficiency and incorporates Efficient Channel Attention to strengthen discriminative feature representation with minimal added cost. An end-to-end workflow is established, covering standardized preprocessing, imbalance-aware training, evaluation on a held-out test set, and post hoc interpretability. Images are resized to 224 × 224 and normalized; augmentation is applied only to the training subset, and strict separation of training, validation, and testing partitions is maintained to mitigate data leakage. Performance is examined under five imbalance-handling settings: no oversampling, weighted averaging, SMOTE, ADASYN, and Borderline-SMOTE. Across these settings, LiteTBNet-ECA demonstrates robust performance, achieving under ADASYN a test accuracy of 99.56%, precision 99.48%, recall 99.62%, F1-

Grad-CAM visualizations highlight lung regions contributing to predictions, supporting qualitative interpretability without implying lesion-level delineation. Overall, LiteTBNet-ECA provides an accurate and efficient tuberculosis screening approach with strong interpretability characteristics, supporting its potential use in screening-oriented workflows.

1. Introduction 

Tuberculosis remains one of the most persistent infectious diseases worldwide, posing a substantial burden on public health systems, particularly in low- and middle-income regions [1]. Despite the availability of effective treatment, delayed or missed diagnosis continues to contribute significantly to transmission, morbidity, and mortality[2]. Early and accurate screening is therefore a cornerstone of tuberculosis control strategies [3]. Among available diagnostic modalities, chest X-ray imaging occupies a central role due to its low cost, rapid acquisition, and widespread availability[4]. Chest radiography is routinely employed as an initial screening tool in both community-based and clinical settings, especially where access to advanced laboratory diagnostics is limited [5,6]. However, the growing volume of radiographic data and the shortage of experienced radiologists in high-burden regions underscore the need for automated, reliable, and scalable computer-aided diagnostic solutions.Conventional interpretation of chest X-ray images relies heavily on expert visual assessment, which is inherently subjective and susceptible to inter- and intra-observer variability [7]. Subtle manifestations of tuberculosis, particularly in early or atypical cases, can be difficult to distinguish from normal anatomical variations or other pulmonary conditions [8]. Traditional rule-based or handcrafted feature –driven computational approaches have been explored to support radiographic analysis, but their performance is often constrained by limited representational capacity and poor generalization across heterogeneous imaging conditions [9]. In recent years, deep learning has emerged as a powerful paradigm for automated medical image analysis, enabling end-to-end learning of discriminative features directly from data[10]. Deep convolutional neural networks have demonstrated promising performance in chest X-ray –based tuberculosis detection [11]. Nevertheless, several critical challenges remain unresolved in existing approaches. Many reported models rely on computationally intensive architectures that are difficult to deploy in real-world clinical environments. Class imbalance, a common characteristic of tuberculosis datasets, is often insufficiently addressed, leading to biased predictions and degraded sensitivity for minority classes [12]. Furthermore, the lack of transparent decision-making mechanisms in many deep learning models limits their acceptance in clinical practice, where interpretability and trust are essential.The practical

In resource-constrained settings, models must operate on limited hardware, support near real-time inference, and maintain stable performance across varying acquisition conditions. Large-scale architectures with millions of parameters and high computational demands may achieve strong performance in controlled experimental settings but are often impractical for point-of-care deployment. Lightweight network designs, which aim to reduce parameter count and computational overhead without sacrificing accuracy, are therefore of particular relevance [13]. At the same time, model predictions must be interpretable to support clinical decision-making and to ensure that learned representations are aligned with medically meaningful image regions [14]. Attention mechanisms have gained increasing interest in this context, as they provide a principled way to enhance salient feature responses while offering insight into the internal focus of the model. However, many attention-based designs introduce substantial architectural complexity or rely on fully connected transformations that undermine efficiency gains [15] . 

Motivated by these considerations, this work introduces a lightweight and interpretable deep learning framework for tuberculosis detection from chest X-ray images. The proposed model, referred to as LiteTBNet-ECA, is designed around a clear architectural philosophy that prioritizes efficiency, robustness, and transparency. The network is constructed using inverted residual blocks with depthwise separable convolutions, which enable efficient feature extraction by decoupling spatial and channel-wise operations. This design significantly reduces computational cost while preserving representational expressiveness. To enhance discriminative capability without incurring substantial overhead, Efficient Channel Attention is integrated into the network. This attention mechanism adaptively recalibrates channel-wise feature responses through lightweight local interactions, allowing the model to emphasize informative patterns associated with tuberculosis while maintaining a compact architecture. The resulting framework is explicitly tailored to address the intertwined challenges of classification accuracy, class imbalance, computational efficiency, and interpretability within a unified design. 

Beyond architectural considerations, the experimental methodology is structured to ensure rigorous and unbiased evaluation. Given the prevalence of class imbalance in tuberculosis screening scenarios, multiple imbalance-handling strategies are systematically examined to assess their impact on model behavior and generalization. These strategies include training without oversampling, loss-based weighting, and representative oversampling techniques, allowing for a comprehensive analysis of imbalance mitigation under consistent experimental conditions. Throughout the study, strict separation between training, validation, and testing subsets is enforced to

3. Dataset Description 

The dataset used in this study consists of frontal chest X-ray images acquired for the 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS purpose of binary tuberculosis screening [31]. Each image is assigned to one of two diagnostic categories, namely Tuberculosis or Normal, reflecting the presence or absence of radiographic findings consistent with pulmonary tuberculosis (Table 1). The dataset is formulated explicitly as a binary classification problem, aligned with real-world screening workflows in which chest radiography serves as an initial triage tool for identifying suspected tuberculosis cases among large populations.An inherent characteristic of the dataset is a pronounced class imbalance, with normal chest X-ray images substantially outnumbering tuberculosis-positive cases in the original data distribution. This imbalance mirrors realistic clinical screening scenarios, where the prevalence of confirmed tuberculosis cases is typically much lower than that of normal or non-TB examinations. Such skewed distributions pose a significant challenge for automated learning systems, as models trained directly on imbalanced data may develop biased decision boundaries that favor the majority class while compromising sensitivity to minority-class cases. Recognizing this issue at the dataset level is therefore essential for designing robust and clinically meaningful evaluation protocols.To support systematic training and evaluation, the dataset is partitioned into three mutually exclusive subsets: training, validation, and testing. The training subset is used exclusively for model fitting, the validation subset is reserved for model selection and hyperparameter tuning, and the testing subset is held out entirely for final performance assessment. The testing set remains unseen during both training and validation, ensuring that reported results reflect genuine generalization to previously unseen data. This strict separation is maintained throughout all experiments to prevent information leakage and to preserve the integrity of the evaluation process.Given the presence of class imbalance, multiple imbalance-handling strategies are explored at the dataset level. These include training without oversampling, as well as the application of Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique, Adaptive Synthetic Sampling, and Borderline-SMOTE. Each strategy is evaluated independently to assess its impact on learning behavior under otherwise identical experimental conditions. Importantly, oversampling is applied exclusively to the training subset. The validation and testing subsets are never subjected to oversampling or synthetic sample generation, regardless of the imbalance-handling strategy employed.Across all experimental settings, strict consistency is enforced in the composition of the validation and testing data. Their distributions are preserved and isolated from training-time modifications to ensure unbiased model comparison and reproducibility. This design choice allows performance differences to be attributed solely to the learning strategy and imbalance-handling approach, rather than to variations in evaluation data. By maintaining dataset integrity and clearly delineating the roles of training, validation, and testing subsets, the dataset configuration provides a reliable foundation for objective assessment of tuberculosis detection performance under diverse imbalance conditions.

3.1. Dataset Pre-processing 

All chest X-ray images undergo a standardized preprocessing procedure prior to model training to ensure consistency, robustness, and compatibility with the proposed framework [32]. As an initial step, each image is resized to a fixed spatial resolution of 224 × 224 pixels. This uniform resizing facilitates seamless integration with the network architecture while maintaining a balanced trade-off between preserving diagnostically relevant spatial detail and achieving computational efficiency [33]. Standardizing image dimensions also ensures consistent receptive field behavior across samples and reduces variability introduced by heterogeneous image sizes.Following spatial standardization, pixel intensity values are normalized to the range [0, 1]. Intensity normalization is applied to mitigate variations arising from differences in imaging equipment, exposure settings, and post-acquisition processing. By mapping all images to a common intensity scale, the preprocessing pipeline reduces inter-image variability and stabilizes the learning process, allowing the model to focus on structural and textural patterns relevant to tuberculosis rather than absolute intensity differences.To enhance generalization and reduce overfitting, data augmentation is applied exclusively to the training subset. Augmentation operations are deliberately 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS constrained to mild, radiologically plausible transformations that preserve anatomical consistency. These include small-angle rotations, limited spatial translations, and controlled adjustments to brightness and contrast. Such transformations increase the diversity of training samples while avoiding unrealistic geometric distortions or intensity alterations that could compromise the clinical validity of the images. No augmentation is applied to the validation or testing subsets, ensuring that evaluation reflects performance on unaltered data.Class imbalance, a common characteristic of tuberculosis screening datasets, is addressed through oversampling strategies applied only to the training data. Several imbalance-handling approaches are explored, including Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique, Adaptive Synthetic Sampling, and Borderline-SMOTE [34-37]. These methods are employed to enhance representation of the minority class during training, thereby reducing bias toward the majority class and promoting more balanced decision boundaries. Importantly, oversampling is never applied to the validation or testing subsets.Throughout the preprocessing workflow, strict data integrity is maintained. The validation and testing sets remain unchanged across all preprocessing and imbalance-handling configurations, ensuring that no synthetic or augmented samples influence model selection or final evaluation. This design explicitly prevents information leakage and supports unbiased performance assessment. By confining all data-driven transformations to the training subset and preserving consistent evaluation data, the preprocessing strategy establishes a reliable foundation for reproducible and methodologically sound experimentation. 

4. Methodology Overview 

This study formulates tuberculosis screening as a binary classification problem using chest X-ray images and proposes an end-to-end framework that integrates data preprocessing, imbalance-aware learning, model training, evaluation, and interpretability. At the core of the framework is LiteTBNet-ECA, a lightweight convolutional neural network designed to balance discriminative capability and computational efficiency through attention-enhanced feature learning (Figure 1). The workflow begins with standardized image preparation and is followed by the application of multiple class-imbalance handling strategies to mitigate the skewed distribution commonly observed in tuberculosis screening data. The proposed model is trained and validated under each imbalance setting and subsequently evaluated on a strictly held-out test set to ensure unbiased performance assessment. To complement quantitative evaluation, gradient-based class activation mapping is employed to provide visual explanations of model predictions, supporting transparency and qualitative analysis. This integrated methodology enables a systematic investigation of accuracy, robustness, and interpretability within a unified and reproducible experimental framework suitable for real-world tuberculosis screening applications.

4.1 Proposed Model Architecture 

The proposed LiteTBNet-ECA framework is designed as an end-to-end system for automated tuberculosis screening from chest X-ray images, integrating data handling, model learning, evaluation, and interpretability within a unified and reproducible workflow (Figure 2). The overall pipeline is structured into five interconnected modules: data acquisition, data preprocessing, training using the LiteTBNet-ECA model, evaluation and result generation, and interpretability through Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping. Each module performs a clearly defined role and is sequentially linked to ensure methodological transparency, strict prevention of data leakage, and

The framework is explicitly constructed to address key challenges inherent to chest X-ray –based tuberculosis detection, including class imbalance, computational efficiency, and the need for interpretable model decisions.The data acquisition stage operates on frontal chest X-ray images, which serve as the primary imaging modality for tuberculosis screening due to their widespread availability, low cost, and routine use in clinical practice. The task is formulated as a binary classification problem, in which each image is categorized as either tuberculosis or normal. This formulation reflects real-world screening scenarios, where the objective is to rapidly and reliably identify suspected tuberculosis cases for further clinical assessment. No assumptions are imposed regarding dataset origin or scale beyond what is depicted in the workflow, allowing the framework to remain general and adaptable to diverse screening contexts. 

Following acquisition, all images are processed through a standardized preprocessing module to ensure consistency and robustness prior to model training. Images are resized to a fixed spatial resolution of 224 × 224 pixels to maintain compatibility with the network architecture and to balance spatial detail with computational efficiency. Intensity normalization is applied to reduce variability arising from differences in imaging devices and acquisition settings. To improve generalization, data augmentation is applied exclusively to the training subset using mild and anatomically plausible transformations, such as small rotations, limited translations, and controlled intensity variations. These operations are designed to enrich training diversity without introducing distortions that could compromise radiological validity. Class imbalance, a common characteristic of tuberculosis screening data, is addressed through oversampling strategies applied only to the training set. Validation and testing subsets are strictly isolated and remain unchanged throughout all experiments, ensuring unbiased model selection and evaluation and preventing any form of information leakage.The core learning component of the framework is the LiteTBNet-ECA model, a lightweight convolutional neural network specifically designed to achieve a balance between discriminative power and computational efficiency. The architecture is based on inverted residual blocks with depthwise separable convolutions, a design paradigm that significantly reduces parameter count and computational cost by decoupling spatial and channel-wise feature extraction. To further enhance representational quality without incurring substantial overhead, Efficient Channel Attention is integrated within the network, enabling adaptive recalibration of channel responses based on local cross-channel interactions. 

The network begins with a stem composed of two convolutional layers that perform initial feature extraction and spatial downsampling. The first stem layer applies a 3 × 3 convolution with stride 2 and 16 output channels, followed by batch normalization and the SiLU activation function. This layer reduces the spatial resolution while

The second stem layer applies a 3 × 3 convolution with stride 1 and the same number of channels, again followed by batch normalization and SiLU activation, refining early representations without further reducing spatial dimensions. The output of the stem provides a compact and informative feature map that serves as the input to subsequent stages. 

Stage 1 focuses on early feature extraction and operates at a moderate spatial resolution. It consists of two inverted residual blocks with an expansion ratio of two. The first block employs stride 2 to perform spatial downsampling while increasing the channel dimension to 24, enabling the network to capture slightly more abstract patterns. The second block uses stride 1 to refine features at the same resolution. Efficient Channel Attention is applied within each block to emphasize informative channels and suppress less relevant responses, improving feature discrimination at minimal computational cost. This stage is primarily responsible for learning low-level structural patterns relevant to lung anatomy.Stage 2 is dedicated to mid-level lung 

pattern learning and further increases the network’s representational capa city. It begins with an inverted residual block using an expansion ratio of three and stride 2, reducing spatial resolution while expanding the channel dimension to 40. This is followed by two additional inverted residual blocks with the same expansion ratio and stride 1, allowing for deeper feature refinement at a constant resolution. Efficient Channel Attention is consistently integrated to adaptively recalibrate channel-wise features. This stage enables the network to model more complex lung patterns and subtle variations that may be indicative of pathological changes.Stage 3 targets high-level pathological feature encoding. An inverted residual block with an expansion ratio of four and stride 2 reduces spatial resolution further while increasing the channel dimension to 80, allowing the network to encode more abstract and semantically rich representations. Two subsequent blocks with the same expansion ratio and stride 1 refine these representations. Efficient Channel Attention continues to operate within each block, guiding the network to focus on channels associated with discriminative pathological cues. This stage is particularly important for capturing complex texture irregularities and structural deviations that are characteristic of tuberculosis manifestations in chest X-ray images. 

Stage 4 serves as a feature refinement and channel expansion stage. It begins with an inverted residual block with an expansion ratio of four, stride 1, and 112 output channels, further enhancing feature richness without altering spatial resolution. This is followed by a 1 × 1 convolution with stride 1 and 160 output channels, combined with batch normalization and SiLU activation. The resulting feature map constitutes the final convolutional representation of the network. This representation is intentionally designed to be both compact and informative, providing a suitable basis for classification and interpretability analysis.

4.2 Channel Attention Mechanism in LiteTBNet-ECA 

The proposed LiteTBNet-ECA architecture prioritizes channel attention rather than spatial attention for several reasons related to computational efficiency and medical image characteristics. Channel attention focuses on learning the relative importance of feature channels, which correspond to different learned feature detectors in convolutional networks. In chest X-ray analysis, pathological patterns such as infiltrates, nodules, or cavitations are often represented through specific feature channels rather than isolated spatial locations. Consequently, channel attention is effective in enhancing diagnostically relevant representations. In contrast, spatial attention mechanisms typically introduce additional convolution operations across spatial dimensions, which increase computational complexity and memory usage. Since the primary objective of LiteTBNet-ECA is to maintain a lightweight architecture suitable for efficient tuberculosis screening, incorporating spatial attention would increase FLOPs and parameters without guaranteeing proportional performance gains.Efficient Channel Attention (ECA) was therefore selected because it provides adaptive feature recalibration with minimal parameter overhead, making it particularly suitable for lightweight convolutional networks. 6. Limitations and Future Work 

The present study is intentionally scoped to the binary classification of chest X-ray images into tuberculosis and normal categories. This focus reflects a screening-oriented objective, where the primary goal is to distinguish individuals who may require further clinical assessment from those without evident abnormalities. While pulmonary imaging encompasses a wide spectrum of disease manifestations, extending the task to multi-class differentiation among various lung pathologies was considered beyond the scope of the current work. The binary formulation enables a clearer evaluation of class 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS imbalance handling, computational efficiency, and interpretability within a focused diagnostic setting, without conflating these objectives with additional classification complexity.Another important consideration relates to dataset diversity and generalization. Although the proposed framework is evaluated under multiple imbalance-handling strategies and a strict separation of training, validation, and testing data is maintained, the study does not explore external validation across heterogeneous clinical populations or imaging conditions. Variations in acquisition protocols, patient demographics, and disease prevalence may influence model behavior in real-world settings. As such, while the reported results demonstrate strong internal consistency, the generalizability of the framework across broader clinical environments remains to be systematically examined.With respect to interpretability, the study employs Grad-CAM to provide visual explanations of model predictions . These attention maps offer qualitative insight into regions that contribute most strongly to the classification decision and support transparency in model behavior. However, Grad-CAM does not provide pixel-level lesion delineation and should not be interpreted as precise localization of pathological findings. The heatmaps reflect model attention rather than ground-truth annotations, and their clinical interpretation should therefore be approached with appropriate caution. 

Future work can extend the proposed LiteTBNet-ECA framework along several complementary directions. One natural extension involves expanding the binary formulation to multi-class pulmonary disease classification, enabling differentiation among multiple thoracic conditions. Additionally, multi-task learning paradigms that jointly address classification and coarse localization may further enhance clinical utility without substantially increasing model complexity. From an architectural perspective, the exploration of alternative lightweight attention mechanisms or hybrid attention strategies could offer additional insights into balancing efficiency and discriminative power. The present study evaluates the proposed LiteTBNet-ECA architecture using a single publicly available chest X-ray dataset. Although the results demonstrate strong performance for tuberculosis screening within this dataset, the absence of external validation across independent datasets represents a limitation of the current work. Differences in imaging protocols, scanner characteristics, and patient demographics across institutions may affect model generalization. Therefore, future work will focus on validating LiteTBNet-ECA on additional chest X-ray datasets and performing cross-dataset evaluation to further assess the robustness and generalization capability of the proposed model.
