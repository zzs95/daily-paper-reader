ABSTRACT 

Although MedSAM2 achieves 3D medical image segmentation through a memory attention mechanism, its performance declines significantly when manually designed prompts are replaced by automatically generated ones—particularly in the context of rare cases or multi-object segmentation scenarios.Current automatic prompt generation methods often extract prompt cues directly from image features, which typically lack rich spatiotemporal context and semantic information, resulting in suboptimal performance. To overcome these limitations, we propose DSSAM2-LAPG, a dual-stage 3D medical image segmentation network that integrates a Learnable Automatic Prompt-space Generator (LAPG) with memory attention. The LAPG acts as a trainable mapper that transforms raw 3D image features into a semantically-rich and spatially-aligned prompt embedding space. In the preliminary stage, this mapper utilizes learnable object tokens (concept embeddings) to dynamically interact with image features, generating coarse but reliable spatial priors Mcoarse and initial semantic prompts. In the refinement stage, a memory attention mechanism integrates these priors with historical context from a support memory to precisely delineate boundaries and ensure 3D consistency. This integrated approach specifically addresses the failure of existing methods in capturing 3D context, encoding rare-object semantics, and providing instance-aware guidance. Experimental results demonstrate that DSSAM2-LAPG achieves Dice score improvements of 7.2% ,6.0%and 4.1% on the private XYCH-cervical dataset,public dataset CCTH-Cervical and Multi-Organ BTCV datasets, respectively,compared to the strong baseline MedSAM2, and all without requiring any manual prompts.Our code is available https://github.com/liufangcoca-515/APG/tree/main.

1 Introduction 

Three-dimensional (3D) medical images segmentation refers to an automated technique that precisely identifies specific anatomical structures or lesion regions through pixel-wise classification of volumetric data from modalities such as CT and MRI 1. It serves as a critical component in assisted diagnosis and treatment planning. While deep learning algorithms, notably Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) 2, 3 and Vision Transformers (ViTs) 4, have significantly advanced performance in this domain, they remain challenged by strong data dependency. Taking cervical cancer (a prevalent gynecological malignancy) as an example 5, pathological data scarcity and high annotation costs—requiring 3-5 hours for expert mask annotation per 3D scan—severely constrain model development 6. Efficient automated cervical cancer MRI segmentation can quantify tumor volume and 3D spatial distribution, providing objective evidence for FIGO staging and radiotherapy target delineation, which is crucial for clinical decision-making 7. In multi-organ segmentation scenarios, automation further enables precise spatial relationship analysis between organs, thereby optimizing therapeutic strategies. Meta’s Segment Anything Model (SAM) 8innovatively established a prompt-driven segmentation paradigm, demonstrating exceptional zero-shot generalization capabilities through point, box, or text prompts. This has propelled its exploration in medical imaging: specialized variants like MedSAM have been derived through training on large-scale medical datasets, while fine-tuned adapters have been developed to accommodate diverse imaging modalities. However, these approaches still

Emerging research explores automatic prompt generation, such as leveraging high-level image encoding features, mask prediction encoder guidance, or employing detection models like YOLO 9 to generate bounding box prompts. Nevertheless, existing methods exhibit significant limitations: the segmentation performance of models like MedSAM/SAM2 is highly dependent on prompt quality and constrained by training data distribution (domain shift effects), leading to inadequate generalization on unseen modalities, multi-organ segmentation objects, and rare pathologies (e.g., specific cervical cancer subtypes). Consequently, developing learnable automatic prompting methods suitable for specific medical modalities and capable of handling multi-organ segmentation remains an urgent research problem. SAM2 10 , an enhanced version of SAM, introduces a memory mechanism and memory attention to process 3D medical images as video frame sequences (assigning higher weights to adjacent frames), establishing spatiotemporal context for volumetric data segmentation. Its support for mask prompts overcomes the limitations of point/box prompts in precisely defining boundaries within medical images. However, directly substituting manually generated prompts with automatically generated ones leads to significant performance degradation on rare samples or multi-target data. Current automatic methods (e.g., those based solely on image encodings) struggle to accurately predict the coordinate positions required for bounding boxes—essential for such prompts—due to the non-coordinate nature of embeddings and random initialization. Simultaneously, bounding box prompts lack the semantic richness necessary for effective multi-class segmentation, while the binary masks generated by SAM2 inherently lack semantic class information. Current research must address two key challenges: how to fully leverage SAM2’s mask prompting capability and spatiotemporal modeling potential for efficient interaction, and how to effectively fuse automatically generated prompts—such as sparse prompts (points/boxes) and dense prompts (masks) 11 —via improved memory attention mechanisms to optimize segmentation. Therefore, developing deep learning methods capable of automatically generating high-quality, semantically rich prompts while synergistically integrating cross-frame contextual fusion represents a critical pathway to overcome generalization bottlenecks in 3D medical images segmentation. To address the limitations of suboptimal prompt quality and inadequate spatiotemporal context in automated segmentation, we introduce the Learnable Automatic Prompt-space Generator (LAPG). Rather than a simple generator, LAPG acts as a trainable mapper that transforms raw image features into a semantically-rich and spatially-aligned prompt embedding space using learnable object tokens. Building upon this, we propose the DSSAM2-LAPG framework, which employs a two-phase mechanism to effectively utilize these embeddings. During Preliminary Stage (Coarse Localization), the LAPG-generated embeddings are processed by a lightweight decoder to produce initial semantic prompts and coarse spatial priors. This stage establishes fundamental spatial awareness and eliminates the dependency on manual prompting. During Refinement Stage (Precise Delineation) to achieve accurate boundary delineation, a memory attention mechanism integrates the preliminary priors with historical context stored in a support memory. Through dynamic progressive fusion, the refinement decoder aggregates these spatiotemporal cues with enhanced image encodings, ensuring 3D structural continuity and robust target segmentation. The key contributions of this work are summarized as follows: (1) Dual-stage mask prediction architecture: The proposed DSSAM2-LAPG network enhances spatial semantic repre-sentation through LAPG-generated mask predictions in the preliminary stage, while strengthening inter-frame contextual correlations in the refinement stage. This significantly improves boundary precision and 3D spatial structural continuity in image segmentation. (2) Semantic prompt interaction mechanism: By designing object-specific tokens as learnable parameters within LAPG, the interaction between generated dense/sparse prompts, enhanced image encodings, and object tokens substantially boosts target recognition capability. This approach demonstrates superior performance in rare tumor cases and multi-organ segmentation objects while significantly reducing dependency on manual annotations. (3)Cross-institution performance validation: Comprehensive experiments on public and private datasets demonstrate significant improvements: For medical images segmentation, our method achieves Dice score improvements of 7.2% ,6.0% and 4.1% on the private XYCH- cervical dataset,public dataset CCTH-Cervical and Multi-Organ BTCV datasetsrespectively, confirming superior performance. 

2 RELATED WORK 

2.1 3D Medical Image Segmentation Networks 

The field of medical images segmentation has evolved significantly from traditional encoder-decoder architectures, UNet 12, 13 

represents a milestone in this domain, with enhanced variants like nnUnet 14 , TransUNet 15 , SwinUNETR 16 and nnFormer 17 

integrating Transformer modules to improve global context modeling. These architectures achieve remarkable precision in segmenting complex anatomical structures but require extensive annotated training data and exhibit limited generalizability across imaging modalities or anatomical regions 18, 19 . The Segment Anything Model (SAM) 20 has introduced a paradigm shift through its universal architecture, interactive prompting mechanisms (points, boxes, masks), and real-time inference capabilities,

that addresses cross-object generalizability challenges. For other 3D medical imaging applications, Medical SAM Adapter 24 

employs fine-tuning strategies, while 3DSAM-adapter 25 proposes hierarchical 2D-to-3D feature mapping with spatial-channel attention mechanisms to align cross-dimensional features. MA-SAM 26 use fine-tuning strategy enables the pre-trained 2D backbone to extract thir dimensional information from input data. Current approaches for 3D medical images processing face significant limitations: 2.5D methods suffer from incomplete spatial integration, while pure 3D CNN architectures (e.g., Sam-med3d 27 , 3d ux-net 28 ) incur substantial computational complexity. Recent research has addressed these challenges through customized adapters, 3DSAM-adapter 25 , and low-rank adaptation (LoRA) techniques that develop specialized 3D modules for volumetric spatial information capture. SAM2 29 incorporates memory mechanisms to capture inter-frame relationships, implemented as both a 3D slicer plugin and Gradio API for efficient 3D segmentation 30 . Medical variants include Medical SAM2 31 supporting diverse 3D prompts (points, boxes, masks) with cross-scenario generalization, and SAM2Point 32 which interprets 3D data as multi-directional video sequences for spatial segmentation. These developments 33, 34 have accelerated SAM2-based medical segmentation networks, Memorysam 35 let SAM2 better understand semantics and inspired by cross-frame correlation in videos. However, in 3D MRI prompt-based segmentation, current methods struggle with modeling long-range dependencies and maintaining spatial continuity across adjacent slices, lacking to capture spatiotemporal context and semantic coherence. To address these limitations, effectively integrating Segment Anything Model 2’s (SAM2) memory-enhanced mechanisms for propagating semantic and spatial information in 3D volumes remains a persistent challenge. 

2.2 Segmentation Based on Prompt Embedding 

Segmentation based on prompt embedding bridges semantic-visual alignment with interactive guidance, where prompt embeddings dynamically modulate visual features for mask generation. The foundational Segment Anything Model (SAM) 8,proposed by Meta, represents a paradigm shift through its prompt-driven mechanism, and some improvements 36 to prompt-based SAM2 networks for medical image segmentation. However, early methodologies predominantly relied on auxiliary segmentation networks to generate coarse spatial prompts. For instance, AdapterShadow (EfficientNet) 37 and UV-SAM (SegFormer) 38 utilized pretrained models to produce preliminary masks as input prompts for SAM. While effective, these approaches introduced parameter scales comparable to object-specific models, resulting in computational inefficiency and limited clinical applicability, SurgicalSAM 39 , proposing a lightweight prototype-based class prompt encoder that generates embeddings directly from class prototypes, eliminating explicit prompts to improve robustness and pipeline simplicity, Auto-prompting SAM 40 , which integrates contextual fully convolutional layers within the SAM framework to directly extract image features for prompt generation, significantly reducing computational overhead. Traditional manual prompting relies on human effort, is time-consuming, and has poor generalization; automated prompt segmentation represents an advanced approach 41, 42 ,several advanced frameworks have been developed, including SAM on medical images 43 and automatic prompt generation 44 .AutoSAM Adapter 25, 40 automates prompt learning through trainable semantic vectors that dynamically generate object-specific priors for 3D segmentation, eliminating manual prompt engineering. Bbox prompts generated by object detection frameworks such as YOLO-SAM 9, which provide sparse coordinate inputs by localizing lesion regions. Notable implementations include AutoProSAM 45 , which further optimizes this paradigm for tumor MRI segmentation while balancing parameter efficiency with accuracy preservation; and AM-SAM 46 , introducing automated mask calibration using lightweight architectures and low-rank adapters. Meanwhile, Beyond Adapting SAM 47 enables joint generation of dense (mask) and sparse (point/box) prompts through prototype-based encoders. Recent advances in adaptive prompt generation include RFMedSAM 48 , which harnesses SAM’s intrinsic multi-stage refinement capability through cascaded decoders to progressively calibrate prompts without external dependencies; the Attention-based Prompt Generation (APG) module in Beyond Adapting SAM 47 , which decouples prompt generation from SAM’s backbone to dynamically predict lesion locations; and SAM2Rad 49 , incorporating a Prompt Prediction Network (PPN) that learns nonlinear mappings from image features to optimal prompt parameters, RFMedSAM 2 to get automatic prompt refinement,enabling automatic prompt generation and dual-stage refinement, Proxy Prompt 50 enhance the capacity of automated prompting and high-level humanmodel interaction. However, in prompt-based 3D MRI segmentation, current methods heavily rely on prompt quality. Automated prompt generation techniques yield suboptimal results when segmenting untrained rare or multi-object datasets, highlighting the critical need to develop effective sparse-dense prompt interaction mechanisms with imaging data. 

3 Methodology 

3.1 Working Principle 

The SAM2 31 model achieves exceptional performance through a memory mechanism that captures inter-frame relationships. This architecture efficiently processes video data and extends to 3D medical images flow by treating sequential slices as video frames to facilitate memory-enhanced medical images. The core workflow comprises two phases: prompt frame processing 

/

## ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS and prompt-free frame processing. During memory encoding, predicted masks of the current frame are encoded into memory features containing maskmem features (visual representations) and maskmem positional encodings (spatial context embeddings). Concurrently, object pointers derived from projected SAM decoder tokens encapsulate high-level semantic information from historical frames. These pointers integrate into the memory sequence for subsequent attention computations. The memory attention module facilitates cross-frame interaction via cross-attention, enabling intelligent retrieval of relevant historical information. This process fuses spatiotemporal context to output augmented pixel features. The baseline model comprises five core components: an image encoder, a prompt encoder , a mask decoder equipped with a feature adapter, a memory encoder, and a memory attention module. This model achieves mask segmentation through these components, the network structure as shown in Figure 1(a). SAM2 further employs temporal context modeling by assigning relative positional encodings to memory frames. Temporal distinction is implemented through time-step encodings, use higher position values indicating closer temporal proximity to the current frame, this prioritization of recent information enables efficient temporal connectivity through attention mechanisms that combine stacked historical memory features with current frame features. 3.4 Mechanisms and Functions from Preliminary Mask Prediction 

During the preliminary mask prediction stage, the coarse mask prediction network, is shown in Figure 4. Comprises three core components: the learnable automatic prompt generation (LAPG) with prompt encoder, mask decoder, and memory encoder. The LAPG generates a coarse mask Mcoarse via a lightweight decoder, These masks serve dual purposes: On the one hand, as dense prompts for the refinement stage, enhancing tissue boundary delineation through spatial-semantic information on the other hand, as sources for generating point-box prompts Ppoint via bounding box transformation; on the other hand providing precise contextual localization for interactive segmentation. During prompt encoding, sparse prompts are encoded using point positional encodings combined with learnable embedding vectors; dense prompts are processed through a 3-layer convolutional downsampling network with layer normalization and GELU activation. The lightweight decoder in Stage 1 is a compact MLP-based module with an input token count of n = 256 and an output prompt token count of m = 64 . The mask decoding employs a TwoWayTransformer that mediates interactions between sparse prompts, dense prompts, and image features through: Self-attention on sparse inputs, Cross-attention from sparse to dense inputs, MLP processing of sparse inputs, Cross-attention from dense to sparse inputs. Memory encoder producing memory features through fusion of current frame features with the output mask to get support_memory and object pointer. The dense prompts (i.e., the coarse mask) generated in the preliminary stage are fed into a memory encoder, which constructs a support memory repository Mmem . 4 Experiments and Results 

4.1 Dataset and Implementation 

This study utilizes two types of datasets for 3D medical image segmentation analysis. In the field of medical imaging, compared to the vast number of examinations such as lung and head CT/MR, high-quality, expert-annotated 3D multimodal cervical cancer MRI datasets suitable for research are scarce resources in both public and private domains. One is public datasets: the CCTH (Cervical Cancer Tumor Heterogeneity) data set 51 , obtained from the public cervical cancer imaging archive, which provides multiparametric MRI sequences (T1-weighted, T2-weighted, and DWI); and the BCTV data set 52 , a public multi-organ segmentation data set focused on the abdomen. The other is a private dataset, named XYCH-cervical, derived from the cervical cancer MRI sequences at XiangYang Central Hospital. Within such datasets, cases with typical features are "common," while specific pathological subtypes, extreme sizes, special locations, or cases with complex invasion constitute the "rare proportion."It comprises 6,586 expert-annotated MRI slices prospectively collected from 100 patients between June 2018 and August 2023. This dataset includes four MRI modalities: diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI), contrast-enhanced T1-weighted (T1CE), T2-weighted (T2), and apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC). All samples underwent densification processing and were stored as NumPy compressed archives (.npz) containing 3D multimodal image stacks, binary ground-truth masks, and anonymized metadata. The dataset was partitioned into training , validation , and test sets. Informed consent was obtained from all participants. The study protocol, including the informed consent process, was reviewed and approved by the Medical Ethics Committee of Xiangyang Central Hospital, Affiliated Hospital of Hubei University of Arts and Science. The rarity of cases in the XYCH-cervical dataset is quantitatively defined according to two clinical-pathological criteria: lesion size (tumors with a maximum diameter of < 2 cm or > 7 cm) and tumor location (lesions primarily involving the cervical stroma with full-thickness invasion or extending to the lower uterine segment). The dataset distribution is as follows: 100 cases with standard presentations, 8 cases with rare lesion sizes, and 5 cases with rare tumor locations. In my methods section, we confirm that all methods were performed in accordance with the relevant guidelines and regulations. Specifically, this study was conducted in full compliance with the ethical principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and the national regulations governing human research in China. Data annotation followed rigorous clinical standards, with tumor contours manually delineated by doctor using RECIST 1.1 criteria, achieving inter-rater consistency. The implementation jointly processes MRI volumes across the sagittal (SAG), coronal (COR) and axial (AX) planes. For MRI data preprocessing, intensity normalization was applied using 0.5-99.5 percentile cropping. Connected component analysis via the cc3d library filtered small objects (3D voxel threshold: 100; 2D pixel threshold: 100). All slices were uniformly resampled to a resolution of 1024×1024 pixels using third-order interpolation for images and zero-order interpolation for segmentation labels. Optimization employed AdamW( β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999 )with a weight decay of 5 × 10 −4.To assess the performance of our model, we use the Intersection over Union (IoU) and Dice Similarity Coefficient(Dice).

The prediction method operates through per-object processing using separate object tokens for each organ. For every object ID, the model receives independent interactive prompts such as clicks or bounding boxes, then generates individual binary masks while maintaining organ-specific representations across video sequences. This approach utilizes memory-augmented tracking mechanisms including object pointer projections, memory encoders, and attention modules to preserve temporal consistency for each organ throughout the video frames, distinguishing it from simultaneous multi-class prediction systems. Discussion and Conclusion 

To address the significant performance degradation of MedSAM2 under automatically generated prompts, particularly in rare lesion and multi-object segmentation scenarios, this paper proposes a dual-stage 3D Medical Image Segmentation network named DSSAM2-LAPG, which integrates learnable automatic prompt generation (LAPG) with memory attention. The method includes two core designs: first, the LAPG module generates spatially and semantically aligned coarse masks to replace manual prompts; second, the dual-stage architecture effectively combines LAPG with a memory attention mechanism, enriching spatiotemporal context and semantic information in 3D medical images and enhancing cross-frame segmentation consistency. This design significantly improves boundary accuracy and 3D structural continuity, increases the Dice coefficient on both private and public segmentation datasets, substantially reduces reliance on manual annotations, and demonstrates strong generalization capability across institutions. However, the additional parameters introduced by automatic prompt generation present a trade-off; therefore, lightweight implementation remains a promising direction for future research.
