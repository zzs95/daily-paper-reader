# Abstract 

Automatic album organization has been studied extensively over the past decades due to significant progress in digi-tal photography. Recent Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have shown strong performance on multi-image under-standing, making them natural candidates for automat-ing album organization workflows. While VLMs’ abilities in multi-image understanding have been widely studied, their performance on album organization remains under-explored. To bridge this gap, we introduce AlbumBench, the first comprehensive benchmark for automatic album or-ganization. Specifically, we (1) define album organization tasks as photo selection for album-specific user objectives, photo rating according to how well user intents are fulfilled, and album-specific photo grouping given a user query that requires contextual understanding of the album; (2) estab-lish AlbumBench, a benchmark dataset containing 27,051 images across 641 albums with 5 annotations per image; and (3) evaluate mainstream open-source and proprietary VLMs on AlbumBench. We show that AlbumBench presents unique challenges compared to traditional multi-image un-derstanding benchmarks due to its requirement for under-standing album context and user intent. Our findings re-veal a significant performance gap between open-source and proprietary VLMs on album organization tasks. De-spite this gap, even the best-performing proprietary models sometimes struggle with tasks that humans find relatively easy. We hope that AlbumBench can serve as a foundation for unifying album organization research and motivate im-provements in VLMs’ performance on these tasks.

An effective under-standing of personal and event albums would aid users in finding, organizing, and sharing their photo collections. Vision-Language Models (VLMs) seem prime candi-dates for tackling this task. Users often desire to sort through and select images in albums in accor-dance with a specific objective they have. # 2. Related Work 

The development of robust and challenging benchmarks has been a critical catalyst for progress in the field of VLMs. Early benchmarks for image captioning [9] and visual question answering (VQA) [1] set the field’s initial goals. As the area has evolved, more demanding datasets have been introduced to evaluate richer capabilities, such as visual commonsense reasoning [27]. Most recently, eval-uation benchmarks have shifted towards assessing expert-level, domain-specific skills, as exemplified by the Massive Multi-discipline Multimodal Understanding and Reasoning (MMMU) benchmark [26], which contains university-level questions from six disciplines to test advanced multimodal reasoning. 

2.1. Long-context benchmarks 

VLMs are approaching or surpassing human-level perfor-mance across multiple benchmarks [6, 13]. Existing bench-marks, however, mainly focus on a single image at a time [8, 12]. To address the evaluation of VLMs on multi-image understanding tasks, researchers have begun building next-generation benchmarks. MuirBench [21] establishes a comprehensive benchmark containing 11,264 images and 2,600 multiple-choice ques-tions across 12 diverse tasks, including counting, scene un-derstanding, and visual retrieval. The benchmark covers 10 categories of multi-image relations, such as temporal, narra-tive, and multiview relationships. MuirBench introduces a pairwise design in which each answerable instance is paired with an unanswerable counterpart to provide robust evalu-ation and prevent models from exploiting shortcuts. While MuirBench provides valuable insights into multi-image rea-soning capabilities, it primarily focuses on tasks with a small number of images (averaging 4.3 per instance, rang-ing from 2 to 9 images) presented in structured multiple-choice formats. The benchmark evaluates whether mod-els can understand relationships between a handful of im-ages. Still, it does not assess long-context multi-image un-derstanding, where models must process and reason over large collections of images, such as photo albums contain-38565 ing dozens or hundreds of images. Furthermore, Muir-Bench’s tasks are designed to answer discrete questions rather than to evaluate contextual retrieval and grouping ca-pabilities, which are essential for real-world applications such as photo management and organization. MIBench [10] proposes a benchmark including 13 multi-image tasks. The tasks are roughly categorized into three types: Multi-Image Instruction, where multiple im-ages must be referenced to answer multiple-choice ques-tions; Multimodal Knowledge-Seeking, where multiple-choice questions require outside knowledge of the depicted images; and Multimodal In-Context Learning, where infor-mation about several images aids in answering a question about another image. Like MuirBench, MIBench focuses on tasks with a single-digit number of images (except for in-context learning tasks that range from 5 to 30 images). While the tasks it covers explore a model’s capabilities for reasoning and leveraging general knowledge, it does not ex-plore tasks related to albums. MileBench [18] presents a pioneering effort in this direc-tion, introducing a comprehensive benchmark specifically designed to test the capabilities of Multimodal Large Lan-guage Models across multimodal long contexts. The bench-mark comprises 6,440 samples, averaging 15.2 images per sample, covering both diagnostic evaluation (needle-in-a-haystack tasks and image retrieval) and realistic evaluation across temporal and semantic multi-image tasks. Their find-ings reveal significant performance gaps between closed-source models like GPT-4o (60.3% on realistic tasks) and open-source alternatives, with performance degrading sub-stantially as the number of images increases. However, MileBench primarily focuses on comprehension and rea-soning tasks where the query and relevant images are ex-plicitly provided together. While their image retrieval task evaluates similarity matching given an anchor image, the benchmark does not address more nuanced photo-album management scenarios in real-world applications. Specif-ically, MileBench lacks evaluation of (1) query-based im-age selection from photo albums, where models must iden-tify and retrieve multiple relevant images from a large col-lection based on natural language descriptions of events, objects, or contexts (e.g.,“find all photos from the beach trip last summer”) and (2) criteria-based image grouping, where models must autonomously organize and cluster im-ages according to specified semantic, temporal, or contex-tual criteria without explicit supervision. These capabilities are essential for practical photo management applications but require different competencies beyond the temporal rea-soning and needle-in-a-haystack retrieval tasks evaluated in MileBench. Our work addresses this gap by introducing evaluation protocols designed explicitly for album-scale im-age selection and organization tasks. 

2.2. Uniqueness of album-level tasks 

The tasks we proposed are closely related yet different from previous tasks, such as image captioning, assessment, re-trieval, and clustering. 

Captioning : Unlike single-image captioning [7, 9, 25], which aims at describing the subject matter and content in an image, captioning in the context of an album requires a deeper understanding of the entire depicted event. For example, a flower bouquet carries different symbolic mean-ings at a graduation ceremony than at a funeral. 

Assessment : Previous studies have assessed image qual-ity from the aesthetics perspective [4, 19]. Our album-level tasks assess the images from the intent perspective: Waste-land images work better than sublime or beautiful land-scapes for environmental purposes. By design, our bench-mark disregards aesthetics, as those factors can be disentan-gled and addressed separately. 

Retrieval : Unlike classic image retrieval [16, 17], which searches for objects and semantics in images, our proposed tasks select and rate images based on the alignment to a purpose, an intended message or a desired outcome. The same wasteland example above can also apply in this case. 

Clustering : Compared with samples in classic image clustering [2, 3, 14], images inside an album can have a sub-stantially higher level of similarity. In addition, an album can be grouped in multiple ways, corresponding to various describable criteria (e.g., location, posture, expression). Im-ages with common factors may appear significantly differ-ent and therefore, classic clustering methods do not apply. 

# 3. AlbumBench Dataset 

AlbumBench is designed to evaluate performance on album-related tasks, specifically regarding fulfilling user in-tentions and understanding the joint context of an album. It consists of 27,051 images organized into 641 albums. The albums were sourced from the CUFED dataset [23], which consists of albums from the Yahoo Flickr Creative Commons 100M Dataset [20] classified into 23 event types ranging from important personal events (e.g., wedding, birthday) to group activities (e.g., casual family gathering, sports). We divide the dataset into training and test sets following an 80/20 ratio, resulting in 133 hold-out test albums and 508 training albums. Albums from two event types (casual fam-ily gathering and beach trip), totaling 31 albums, are with-held from the training set to allow future testing for open-set scenarios. All images are annotated according to the tasks described as follows. 

3.1. Album Tasks and Metrics 

We propose four tasks for album understanding: Intent Se-lection, Intent Rating, Group Labeling, and Group Cluster-38566 ing. Each task models a real task that photographers aim to accomplish in organizing an album, with the first two focused on achieving user intents and the second two on or-ganizing the album. We evaluate how well current methods work on these tasks in Sec. 4. For each task, a model requires context from the entire album to perform these tasks. We propose two methods of giving this context. The first method gives visual context 

by giving the model all images in the album. The second method gives language context by giving the model a cap-tion describing the whole album plus a single image to eval-uate. This allows us to assess how well a model leverages the additional information provided by visual context . Note that the language context is only applicable if the task can be performed given one image at a time and may involve modification to the task. 

Intent Selection: This task evaluates how well a model can select images based on a user query. Given a set of images, the model must select the images that best match the query. Three different queries are given specifically for each album, and each query results in a different subset of images being selected. This allows us to evaluate where the model can understand the user intents and differentiate im-ages appropriately. For the language context version, the model is instead given a caption for the album and a single image and asked whether the image satisfies the query well. The metrics used to evaluate this task are those used in re-trieval tasks: F1-score, precision, recall, and mean average precision (mAP). 

Intent Rating: Similar to the Intent Selection task, this measures how well a selected image matches a user query, but instead does so by rating the image. This task asks mod-els to rate each image on a scale of 0–3 (inclusive), with 0 = “Not relevant - Image does not match the query at all,” 1 = “Somewhat relevant - Image has minor or tangential rele-vance to the query,” 2 = “Relevant - Image clearly matches the query,” and 3 = “Highly relevant - Image is an excel-lent or perfect match for the query.” Again, three different queries are given per album. When evaluating the visual context , all images are given to the model and it is asked to rate one image, while for evaluating language context amodel is only given one image to rate along with the album caption. The metrics used to evaluate this task are accuracy (ratio of exact score matches), mean absolute error (MAE), and root mean squared error (RMSE). 

Group Labeling: This task requires a model to assign each image a single label indicating the semantic group to which it best belongs. When evaluating visual context , all images are provided simultaneously during labeling; oth-erwise, only the album caption and the target image are given. Two distinct queries are given, each resulting in a different grouping of the images. Performance is mea-sured using four metrics: Adjusted Rand Index (ARI), Nor-

ARI, NMI, and Jac-card Index are well-established clustering evaluation met-rics that quantify the degree of agreement between two la-beling schemes, making them well-suited for assessing im-age grouping quality. 

Group Clustering: This task evaluates how well a model can group the album images based on a user query. Unlike the previous task, no predefined labels are provided. This requires the method to consider the album as a whole and determine the best way to group images to satisfy the query, thereby testing the model’s ability to jointly consider the context of the album. This is evaluated using the same metrics as the Group Labeling task. Unlike the prior three tasks, this task is not evaluated using language context as all images must be considered when determining the groups. 

# 4. Experiments and Discussion 

In this section, we evaluate a variety of VLMs on our Al-bumBench benchmark. We begin by outlining the evalua-tion settings, followed by the quantitative results for differ-ent model configurations and task types. We then provide a detailed analysis of the quantitative results. Performance of representative instruct models is shown in Figure 2. Fi-nally, we present qualitative case studies in the supplemen-tary materials to showcase common model failure modes and provide further analysis. 38567 4.1. Evaluation Setup 

Since our tasks require processing an album of images, we benchmark a range of state-of-the-art open-source and closed-source VLMs that have shown outstanding multi-image understanding and long-context processing capabili-ties. 

VLMs and Caption Baselines. We choose GPT-5 [13] and Gemini-2.5-Pro [6] because of their overall state-of-the-art multimodal task-solving capabilities. We include In-ternVL3.5 [22], and Qwen3-VL [15] for their state-of-the-art performance among all open-source VLMs. We include three differently sized InternVL3.5 and Qwen3-VL models. We also include Keye-VL-1.5 [24] for its exceptional video understanding capabilities. We further include “instruct” and “thinking” variants for each of the models mentioned above. For GPT-5 and Gemini-2.5-Pro, we set their thinking budgets to “minimal” and “128”, respectively, to maximally suppress their thinking capabilities, aiming to resemble the setting of typical “instruct” models. To test how well VLMs can solve our tasks without vi-sual inputs, we include “Caption Baselines” that utilize tex-tual information only. For this, we first caption each image and also generate a caption for the entire album. Only these captions are given to the models. This gives a baseline for how well the models will perform without visual informa-tion when compared to running them with images. We use Gemini-2.5-Pro as the backbone for the Caption Baselines. This results in 20 different model configurations. We evaluate all 20 model configurations across all of the 4 task types with visual context ; we run the 10 non-thinking model configurations on 3 of the 4 task types with language con-text .

Prompt engineering and post-processing. To best eval-uate different VLMs, we must consider the prompts. VLMs respond to prompts differently. Naive prompts may not work well for some methods, while extensive prompt en-gineering for one method may give it unfair advantage over another. To provide a single prompt that is fair across all methods, we engineered prompts that worked well for each VLM, then combined them into a single prompt, verifying it worked well across all prompts. The failure modes are discussed further in Sec. 4.2. To evaluate model performance, we require outputs in a standard format that our evaluation code can parse. We prompted the VLMs to format their answers as JSON struc-tures for each task type, which facilitates automated pars-ing. We first apply a standard JSON parsing function to process the generated outputs. However, models occasion-ally produce invalid JSON formats. To maximize model success rates, we employ Gemini-2.5-Flash [6] as a sec-ondary post-processor. It was given only the JSON output, not the images, so it could not add new analysis of the al-bum. Based on preliminary experiments, Gemini-2.5-Flash robustly handles these post-processing tasks without mod-ifying content. Combined, these prompt engineering and post-processing techniques reduce the parsing failure rate to nearly zero. Details of the prompt templates used to solve each task type and post-processing are provided in the sup-plement materials.

Over-all, these results reveal that model performance is highly task-dependent, suggesting that no single model dominates across all scenarios and indicating that the current multi-image understanding benchmarks do not represent models’ performance on album organization tasks as defined. AlbumBench is designed to analyze whether a method can understand user intent with the capability of applying different queries to the album, as well as understand the joint contextual information within the album. We discuss our findings and discoveries in the remainder of this section. 

How well do VLMs understand user intent? From Ta-bles 1 and 2, we observe that while methods obtain different results, overall they can understand intent to some degree, though there remains room for improvement. For a given album, different queries result in different images being se-lected accordingly as desired. We also observe consistent performance improvements when using VLMs in thinking mode. In its reasoning pro-cess, the models start by understanding what each category represents, then generate intermediate descriptions for each image in the album, and eventually assign the proper cate-gory to each image. The instruct models, on the other hand, predict answers directly in selected image IDs or partitioned image groups and do not benefit from the reasoning over image contents. These promising results suggest that VLMs can continue to improve at recognizing user-intent-based queries with the increase of inference budget. 

How well do VLMs understand the joint context of im-ages? The grouping tasks, particularly the Group Cluster-ing task, aim to evaluate how well models can understand the full context of an album and the relationship between images. The results on both the Group Labeling and Group Clustering tasks are significantly lower than those on the se-lection tasks, indicating that VLMs still have much progress to make on those tasks. Do VLMs make good use of visual context? Our bench-mark requires VLMs to ingest large numbers of images to perform those tasks. Prior large-scale benchmarks are lim-ited to video sequences with high temporal consistency in their visual context. However, such temporal consistency is often absent in albums. The large diversity of visual in-formation could be challenging for a VLM to process. To test this, we compared the capability of VLMs to leverage visual context in two ways. First, we tested what would happen when using visual context to represent the album instead of language context . Tables 1 and 2 show mixed re-sults: On the Intent Selection task, visual context improves results slightly, and it improves even more on the Intent Rat-ing task. However, for Group Labeling, we observe a signif-icant improvement when using language rather than visual tokens to represent the album. We also test the effectiveness of leveraging visual in-formation by running the Caption Baseline experiment, in which all images were represented by captions. This serves as a baseline to determine how well the method will per-form with only language guidance. Failing to follow directions. In analyzing the results, we noticed some recurring problems across many VLMs. First, models do not strictly follow instructions in the prompt. To automatically analyze the outputs, we expect a structured output that can be read consistently. However, the VLMs struggled to produce the structured output we defined. One significant challenge in our evaluation pipeline is the post-processing of model outputs when they fail to fol-low the instructions closely. Without extensive prompt en-gineering, open-source VLMs, especially smaller ones, fail to format their answers as requested. This finding empha-sizes the need for more instruction-tuning datasets tailored to album organization tasks, such as image selection and image clustering. VLMs also frequently failed to follow instructions for the Group Clustering and Group Labeling tasks. In those 38570 tasks, each image must be assigned to exactly one group. However, as shown in Table 3, some images might be as-signed to multiple groups, whereas others were left unas-signed. We find that instruct models are more prone to these errors than thinking models; the process of thinking must help the models better follow guidelines. This also happens more frequently in the visual context case, where the whole album is provided to VLMs. We assume this makes it eas-ier for models to become overwhelmed or confused due to the excessive number of visual tokens in the context win-dow. The same pattern also happens in our Caption Base-line methods, even though there are only text tokens in the models’ context window. As the number of images in our dataset may still be relatively small compared to the number of images in many people’s photo galleries, this suggests the need for better handling of long context in VLMs. 

Open-source vs. Closed-source. In the Open-source model cohort, the Qwen3-VL family of models generally performs better than others. When visual context is pre-sented, Qwen3-VL models achieve the best metric scores on nearly all task types. On Group-Clustering tasks, Qwen3-VL-32B-Thinking dominates all four metrics. Among the two closed-source models, Gemini-2.5-Pro and GPT-5 each take the lead on half of the task types. Gemini-2.5-Pro demonstrates robust performance when thinking is disabled, while GPT-5 performs the best when thinking is enabled. On Group-Labeling tasks, GPT-5 with thinking achieves the highest scores on all four metrics. 

Our tasks pose novel challenges To assess whether our benchmark poses novel challenges, we computed Spearman correlations between album organization task performance and MMMU-val [26] scores across 4 state-of-the-art mod-els, illustrated in Figure 3. Tasks with language context show weak correlations with the MMMU-val scores, in-dicating that models performing well on traditional mul-timodal reasoning benchmarks do not necessarily perform well when reasoning about album organization with tex-tual context. On the other hand, tasks with visual context exhibit moderate to strong correlations, reflecting partial overlap with general visual understanding. However, the unexplained variance demonstrates that these tasks still re-quire specialized capabilities in album understanding and grouping logic. These findings validate that our benchmark captures a unique dimension of vision-language intelligence and poses novel challenges to current VLMs.
