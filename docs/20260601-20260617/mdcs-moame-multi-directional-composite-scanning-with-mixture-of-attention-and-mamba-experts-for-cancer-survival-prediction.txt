## Abstract 

Multi-modal learning approaches that integrate patholog-ical images with genomic profiles have significantly en-hanced the accuracy of survival prediction tasks. However, previous methods often struggle to effectively process long-range gigapixel whole slide images (WSIs) and sparse ge-nomic profiles due to the limitations of conventional scan-ning strategies to serialize data and the complex and het-erogeneous nature of the modalities. Inspired by recent ad-vancements in Mamba and mixture of experts (MoE), we propose a novel multi-directional composite scanning strat-egy with mixture of attention and Mamba experts (MDCS-MoAME) for cancer survival prediction. Specifically, we introduce a multi-directional composite scanning (MDCS) strategy to both WSIs and genomic profiles, and use the Mamba encoder to process intra-modal representations at the region, patch, and gene level, ensuring sufficient uti-lization of the intrinsic information within each modality. To further capture heterogeneous inter-modal representa-tions, we introduce mixture of attention and Mamba ex-perts (MoAME), which dynamically selects tailored experts to model complex inter-modal correlations, flexibly focus-ing on the interactions between modalities. Finally, we in-troduce alignment constraints to recalibrate inter-modal in-teractions and reduce intra- and inter-modal representation redundancy, enhancing its discriminative power for com-prehensive survival analysis. Experimental results on five publicly available datasets demonstrate that our method outperforms existing approaches, achieving state-of-the-art performance.

## 1. Introduction 

Survival prediction via whole slide images (WSIs) and ge-nomic profiles plays a critical role in cancer prognostic as-sessment, informing therapeutic decisions, and improving patient outcomes [35]. WSIs offer rich morphological de-tails of tumors [12], while genomic profiles provide insights into the molecular mechanisms underlying cancer progres-sion [34]. The challenge in survival prediction lies in ef-fectively leveraging the unique strengths of both modali-ties. As a result, the recent focus of survival analysis studies has shifted from single-modal predictions [19, 30] to utiliz-ing multi-modal information [23, 45] to improve prognostic performance. Previous multi-modal studies have achieved remarkable success in survival prediction [24, 29]. DeepSets [41] pioneered set-based deep learning with sum pooling, ex-celling in multi-modal survival prediction. Following the rise of attention mechanisms [36], MCAT [4] introduced a genome-guided co-attention mechanism (GCA), enhanc-ing performance but with increased computational burden. Unlike the quadratic complexity of attention-based methods, PAM [16] incorporated a local-aware scan-ning module and a bidirectional Mamba encoder, capturing long-range dependencies in WSIs. Leveraging the hierar-chical structure of WSIs, SurvMamba [6] achieved break-throughs in both performance and efficiency through cross-grained and fine-grained interactions via the Interaction Fu-sion Mamba module. Despite notable progress, existing methods still en-counter several critical challenges. First, the inherent hi-erarchical structure of WSIs is often underutilized [5], lead-ing to suboptimal exploitation of image information. As illustrated in Figure 1(a), patch-level WSIs capture fine-grained details such as individual cells and tumor cellu-larity, whereas region-level WSIs provide broader tissue-level context, including tumor-immune localization pat-terns. Second, the ultra-high resolution of gigapixel WSIs prevent these methods from sufficiently capturing long-range dependencies. As shown in Figure 1(b), Mamba-based approaches [10] typically perform only horizontal scanning on WSIs, exhibiting a single receptive field and limiting their capacity to model vertical, oblique, or other multi-directional dependencies. Third, current methods di-vide genes into six broad groups (Figure 1(d)), which lead to functional overlaps and struggle to fully capture the intri-cate relationships. For instance, genes involved in the PI3K-Akt signaling pathway are dispersed across six groups. Fi-nally, the heterogeneity between modalities complicates the modeling of inter-modal correlations. Although attention-or Mamba-based models [12, 42] can achieve sufficient inter-modal interaction from their respective perspectives, the inflexible and simplistic feature fusion design may in-troduce redundancy, thereby limiting the ability to improve feature representations. To address the limitations mentioned above, we pro-pose a novel multi-directional composite scanning strategy with a mixture of attention and Mamba experts (MDCS-MoAME) for cancer survival prediction. Specifically, our network utilizes pre-trained feature extractors [24] to pro-cess hierarchical WSIs at both the region and patch levels and a fully connected layer to process genomic profiles. Next, as shown in Figure 1(c) and (e), we introduce a multi-directional composite scanning (MDCS) strategy for com-prehensive representations of both WSIs and genomic pro-files. The MDCS scans WSIs at five directions, including horizontal, vertical, left oblique, right oblique, and loop-back, while sparse genomic profiles are scanned in forward and interval manners. This enlarges the receptive field of the original scanning strategy, facilitating the extraction of distinctive features relevant to survival assessment. Fur-thermore, to model complex inter-modal correlations effec-tively, we integrate a mixture of attention and Mamba ex-perts (MoAME) module. MoAME dynamically selects tai-lored experts to capture the inter-modal relationships, flexi-bly focusing on the interactions between modalities, and en-hancing the model’s discriminative power. Finally, to miti-gate feature redundancy, we apply alignment constraints to intra- and inter-modal representations, further boosting the model’s ability to predict survival outcomes. • To fully capture the intrinsic information within the modality, we employ a novel and comprehensive scan-ning mechanism for both WSIs and genomic profiles, generating features that provide unique representations of large pathological images and sparse genomic profiles. • We design a mixture of attention and Mamba experts module that enables the network to selectively focus on modeling complex inter-modal correlations. • We propose alignment constraints to mitigate redundancy issues and improve interaction quality within intra- and inter-modal representations. • Extensive experimental results demonstrate that our pro-posed model significantly outperforms other state-of-the-art cancer survival prediction methods across five TCGA datasets: BLCA, BRCA, GBMLGG, LUAD, and UCEC. 

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Multi-modal Survival Prediction 

Survival prediction provides valuable insights into disease progression [28, 32]. Currently, effective multi-modal in-tegration, such as combining WSIs with genomic profiles, remains a key focus in cancer prognosis [21, 43]. MCAT [4] applied early fusion with a genome-guided co-attention mechanism (GCA), enhancing cancer survival prediction accuracy. Subsequently, CMTA [44] optimized the over-all structure and utilized parallel encoder-decoder structures and cross-modal alignment to improve information transfer. To enhance computational efficiency and accuracy, SUR-PATH [18] generated biological pathway tokens from tran-scriptomics and combined them with patch tokens from WSIs to model the relationship between cellular functions and morphology. CCL [45] and PIBD [43] introduced dis-entanglement modules to decompose multimodal features into modality-common and modality-specific components respectively. However, these works still have limitations, including insufficient modeling of long-range WSIs, sparse genomic profiles, and correlations between modalities. In summary, an effective framework that captures comprehen-sive feature representations and heterogeneous correlations has yet to emerge. 

2.2. State Space Models 

The state space model (SSM) offers more significant ad-vantages in processing long sequences data by utilizing 14462 state space transformations [25, 27]. The structured state space sequence (S4) model [11] defined state space param-eters to control the relationship between input and output sequences, avoiding the quadratic complexity of attention calculations. Furthermore, Mamba [10], a new sequence model based on SSM, has been adopted as new visual back-bones. In computational pathology, PAM [16] introduced a local-aware scanning module and a bi-directional Mamba encoder, effectively modeling both detailed features and macroscopic patterns in WSIs. MambaMIL [40] addressed overfitting and improved model accuracy through sequence rearrangement. MamMIL [8] utilized a topology-aware scanning mechanism and an instance aggregation module to effectively model global dependencies between instances. However, these methods neglect the importance of scanning directions other than the original horizontal one, which are crucial for capturing long-range dependencies in gigapixel WSIs and sparse genomic profiles. 

2.3. Mixture of Experts 

The mixture-of-experts (MoE) mechanism [13, 31] uses a gating network to select the relevant experts for feature ex-traction or prediction. The gating can be soft [1], proba-bilistically weighting the outputs, or hard [9], for select-ing a specific expert’s output. In computational pathol-ogy, MoE-Mamba [26] integrated the Mamba layer into the MoE architecture, combining the strengths of SSM and MoE to significantly enhance training efficiency. The MoME [38] method introduced a mixture of multi-modal experts (MoME) layer, achieving maximized, indirect, and gene-dominant fusion across modalities. MESG-Net [22] effectively addressed the issue of missing modalities by us-ing multiple experts in the MoE encoder to handle the corre-sponding features. However, the application of flexible and effective experts in computational pathology has not been fully explored. 

## 3. Methodology 

3.1. Problem Formulation and Feature Extraction 

Problem Formulation. Given a patient D = ( I, G, c, t ),where I denotes pathological WSIs for the patient, G rep-resents the corresponding genomic data, c ∈ { 0, 1} indi-cates the censoring status, t ∈ R+ is the survival time (measured in months), and let T be a continuous random variable for survival time, our goal is to utilize I and G

to estimate the survival function fsur (T ≤ t), which rep-resents the probability that the patient survives beyond a discrete time point t. The survival function estimates the ordinal value by cumulating the negated hazard function 

fhazard (t) = fhazard (T = t|T ≥ t, (I, G )) ∈ [0 , 1] , defined as: fsur (T ≤ t, (I, G )) = Qtu=1 (1 − fhazard (T = u)) .

Pathological Feature Extraction. WSIs exhibit a natu-ral hierarchical structure at both the region and patch lev-els. Leveraging this property, we divide WSIs I into M

non-overlapping regions with a size of 4, 096 × 4, 096 , and all regions are further subdivided with N non-overlapping patches with a size of 512 × 512 . Then, a pre-trained ResNet-50 [15] is used to extract the embedding features of M regions and N patches, obtaining Ireg = {xr,i }Mi=1 ∈

RM ×E , and Ipat = {xp,i }Ni=1 ∈ RN ×E , where E represents the embedding dimension. 

Genomic Feature Extraction. Genomic profiles consist of 

1 × 1 attributes, such as RNA sequencing (RNA-seq), copy number variation (CNV), and simple nucleotide variation (SNV). We follow CMTA [44] and use a fully connected layer to group these high signal-to-noise ratio data into six gene groups, generating genomic embedding features G =

{xg,i }Ki=1 ∈ RK×E , where K is the number of groups. 

3.2. Multi-directional Scanning and Feature En-hancement 

To fully explore the information contained in WSIs and gene groups, we develop the MDSFE module, as shown in Figure 2(b). The MDSFE module first performs multi-directional composite scanning (MDCS) on both WSIs and gene groups, and then uses RegionMam, PatchMam, and GeneMam separately to obtain the enhanced intra-modal representations for each modality. 

MDCS on WSIs. Current Mamba-based scanning meth-ods only perform horizontal scanning. This single scanning method causes adjacent patches in the upper and lower rows of WSIs to be far apart. 3.3. Expert-driven Inter-modal Interaction 

Due to the gigapixel WSIs, sparse genes, and inherent heterogeneity, it is difficult for individual attention or se-lective scanning to capture sufficient interactions. There-fore, we develop the EDIMI module to promote full in-teraction by integrating their diverse advantages, as shown in Figure 2(c). First, we introduce the mixture of at-tention and Mamba experts (MoAME) module inspired by MoME [38] to extract inter-modal interactions of ( Ir,

Gg) and ( Ip, Gg), producing inter-modal representations 

Ir&g, I p&g ∈ R1×E . Subsequently, we leverage interactions of ( Gg, Ir&g) and ( Gg, Ip&g) to produce inter-modal rep-resentations Gg&r, G g&p ∈ R1×E . This progressive fusion bridges modality gaps, achieving deeper fusion. Unlike MoME, where excessive modal fusion and overuse of attention mechanisms result in redundant fea-tures and hinder the modeling of inter-modal associations, MoAME is designed to enable more flexible, and less re-dundant feature fusion, as illustrated in Figure 2(f). It con-sists of three experts: CroAttFusion (A), CroMamFusion (M), and StackedMamFusion (S), with varying degrees of fusion, guided by a gating network that selects the most ap-propriate expert for each pair of inputs. The gating network is derived from MoME, as shown in Figure 2(g). ## 4. Experiments 

Datasets. We utilize five public datasets from The Can-cer Genome Atlas (TCGA): Bladder Urothelial Carcinoma (BLCA, n = 373 ), Breast Invasive Carcinoma (BRCA, n =956 ), Glioblastoma and Lower Grade Glioma (GBMLGG, 

n = 569 ), Uterine Corpus Endometrial Carcinoma (UCEC, 

n = 480 ), and Lung Adenocarcinoma (LUAD, n = 453 ). 

Evaluation Metrics. We perform 5-fold cross-validation on each dataset, calculating the mean and standard deviation (std) of the concordance index (c-index), which measures the probability that the predicted risk scores of two patients are consistent with their actual observed survival times. 

Implementation. We divide WSIs at 10 × magnification and use the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of 1e-3, a weight decay of 1e-5, and a batch size of 1. The in-terval length ∆ is set to 5, and all experiments are trained for 30 epochs in each fold. The weight α of loss function Lcro 

is set to [4e-1, 5e-4, 3e-1, 1e-4, 5e-4] for the five datasets. The weight β of the loss function Lintra is set to 1e-3 for the five datasets. All computational experiments are conducted on NVIDIA GTX 4090 GPUs. 

4.1. Comparisons with the State-of-the-Art Meth-ods 

We compare MDCS-MoAME with state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods. Gene-modal (G) methods : We compare MLP [14], SNN [20], and SNNTrans [20]; Pathology-modal (P) methods : We compare attention-based meth-ods such as ABMIL [17], CLAM MB [24], and Trans-MIL [29]; Mamba-based methods such as MambaMIL; hi-erarchical methods such as PAM; and methods using ex-pert networks such as PAMoE [37]; Multi-modal methods :We also compare attention-based methods such as MCAT, MOTCAT, SurvPATH, and CMTA; Mamba-based and hier-archical methods such as SurvMamba; methods using ex-pert networks such as MoME; and methods using informa-tion decomposition such as CCL and PIBD. All methods are reproduced and evaluated under our experimental set-ting. The results are presented in Table 1. The MDCS-MoAME achieves the best performance across all models and datasets. Compared to three gene-modal methods MLP, SNN, and SNNTrans, MDCS-MoAME improves the average c-index by 7.81%, 11.61%, and 6.48%, respectively. For pathology-modal meth-ods, MDCS-MoAME improves the c-index by 11.12% to 16.88%, underscoring the importance of multi-modal fu-sion. For multi-modal methods, MDCS-MoAME boosts the c-index by 3.30% to 10.31%, demonstrating its superior potential. Both PAM and SurvMamba utilized hierarchi-cal structures to capture global and local information from WSIs, with Mamba effectively modeling long-range depen-dencies. Our MDCS-MoAME outperforms PAM and Surv-Mamba by 14.61% and 5.70%, respectively, primarily due to the enhanced perception capabilities of our MDCS strat-egy for both WSIs and genomic profiles. For MoE-based methods, PAMoE used different experts to select patches according to their preferences, which effectively handled heterogeneous pathological tissues. Similarly, MoME used multiple expert modules optimized for different degrees of interaction and modality requirements. However, our method shows improvements of 16.34% and 10.31% over PAMoE and MoME, respectively. This is due to the fact that our experts combine the strengths of both attention and Mamba, enabling deep interaction and fusion of intra-modal and inter-modal representations.
