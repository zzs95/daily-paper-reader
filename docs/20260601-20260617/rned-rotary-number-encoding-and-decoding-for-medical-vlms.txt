## Abstract 

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) are increasingly adopted for medical applications, but their clinical utility is limited by a core weakness in quantitative prediction. This limitation affects tasks ranging from regression of lesion sizes to predic-tion of bounding-box coordinates and stems from the discrete tokenization schemes underlying Large Language Models (LLMs). To address this, we propose Rotary Number Encod-ing and Decoding (RNED), a principled method for embed-ding continuous numerical values directly in the representa-tion space of a VLM. Analogous to rotary position encoding, RNED represents a scalar by applying a number-specific ro-tation matrix to a dedicated numeric token embedding. This norm-preserving transformation maintains ordinal structure over a wide numerical range and integrates seamlessly with pretrained model weights. For decoding, we introduce a robust score-matching–based scheme to recover continuous values from hidden states in the presence of stochastic noise. We evaluate RNED on two quantitative tasks: radiological measurement estimation and medical visual grounding. On both internal and public benchmarks, RNED consistently outperforms existing VLM baselines. Together, these results show that RNED offers a robust, generalizable solution for numerical representation and prediction in medical VLMs, enabling models that are both quantitatively reliable and clinically applicable. Our code is available at Code.

These values are not merely descriptive; they often underpin diagnosis, prognosis, risk stratification, and therapy selection. Although they are typi-cally estimated rather than measured with absolute precision, they must be reliable enough to support high-stakes clinical decisions and downstream automation, while still reflecting the inherent uncertainty and interobserver variability present even among human experts. This reliance on quantification exposes a key bottleneck in current medical VLMs, rooted in limitations of the un-derlying LLM architectures. Despite their linguistic fluency, LLMs often perform poorly on tasks that require numerical processing and prediction [ 35 ]. Several factors contribute to this deficiency (Fig. 1). First, standard tokenizers typi-cally split numbers into subword units (e.g., 42 .5 becomes 

[ 4 ], [ . ], [ 5 ] in the Llama3 [ 14 ] tokenizer, and [ 4 ] , [ 2 ] ,

[ . ] , [ 5 ] in the Mistral [ 18 ] tokenizer), preventing the model from treating them as single numerical entities. Second, lack-ing an explicit representation of real numbers, LLMs tend to handle numerical tasks via pattern matching and memoriza-tion. The next-token prediction objective does not directly optimize for numeric accuracy or magnitude (e.g., predicting 

[ 3 ] or [ 9 ] when the target is [ 4 ] incurs the same loss). As a result, numerical outputs often reflect corpus statistics rather than precise calculation and are prone to hallucinated yet plausible values—at odds with the precision required in medicine. We address these challenges by rethinking how medical VLMs represent and predict numbers. We first analyze the limitations of existing frameworks [ 13 ] and formalize a set of desired properties for numerical representations in medi-cal VLMs: (i) ordinal-preserving encoding that captures a wide range of values, (ii) robust decoding under stochastic context noise, and (iii) compatibility with pretrained LLMs and their normalization layers. Building on these require-ments, we propose a new framework for numerical represen-tation. For encoding, inspired by xVal [ 13 ], we apply a mul-tidimensional rotation to a special number token, enabling wide-range numerical encoding, unique representations for different values, and preservation of numerical differences in embedding space. For decoding, we convert the problem of mapping LLM embeddings back to numerical values into a score-matching procedure, which is robust to stochastic con-text noise in text generation. We further identify a trade-off between decoding distinguishability and target smoothness, and propose a principled way to balance these factors. Over-all, our Rotary Number Encoding and Decoding (RNED) strategies are normalization-agnostic and can be integrated into existing VLMs [ 3, 27 ] without degrading semantic text generation. Our contributions are: • We propose RNE scheme that maps a wide range of numer-ical values into an ordinal-preserving embedding space, enabling accurate representation of numbers as single to-kens and compatible with existing VLMs. • We develop RND strategy that recovers numerical values from LLM embeddings via a score-matching procedure, which is robust to stochastic context noise. • We further analyze the trade-off between decoding distin-guishability and target smoothness, and propose a princi-pled way to balance these factors. To validate our approach, we conduct comprehensive experiments on radiological measurement estimation and medical vision grounding tasks across internal and publicly available datasets. Our results demonstrate RNED’s effec-tiveness against traditional tokenization for numerical values. 

## 2. Related works Medical VLM. Building on the success of general-purpose Vision-Language Models (VLMs) in computer vision, there is growing interest in adapting VLMs to medical imaging tasks. Early work such as ConVIRT [ 47 ] used contrastive learning to learn joint image–text representations from paired medical reports, while MedCLIP [ 40 ] extended this idea to unpaired image/text data and demonstrated improved robust-ness [ 40 ]. However, these contrastive approaches rely on limited text input and are not designed for general-purpose visual question answering (VQA). Motivated by the use of visual embeddings in pretrained LLMs for VQA [ 23 , 26 –28 ], several works have explored this paradigm in medical domains. ClinicalBLIP [ 17 ] extends InstructBLIP [ 23 ] for radiology report generation using multi-stage low-rank adap-tation (LoRA) fine-tuning [ 36 ]. LLaVA-Med [ 22 ] adapts the LLaVA [ 27 ] architecture for biomedical conversational tasks via instruction tuning on domain-specific data, and BiomedGPT [ 46 ] targets a broad range of biomedical tasks within a single VLM framework. However, these mod-els are predominantly optimized for classification and text-generation objectives rather than precise numerical predic-tion. In contrast, biomedical workflows often hinge on quan-titative outputs such as anatomical measurements or risk scores—tasks that are inherently regression-based rather than purely classification-based. For medical VLMs to be clinically useful, numerical estimation must become a first-class objective, with architectures and training procedures explicitly optimized to produce accurate and reliable numer-ical outputs. 

Numerical encoding/decoding in LLMs. Several ap-proaches have been proposed to improve the numerical abil-ities of LLMs. Abacus embedding [ 29 ] introduces a new embedding scheme for numbers by reversing digit order to better match human calculation strategies. The p10/p100 ap-proach [ 8 ] encodes numbers in a specially formatted string. These methods primarily target mathematical benchmarks where numbers dominate the input and do not explicitly address mixed text–number prompts. 13723 For numerical decoding, xVal [ 13 ] is the first to propose a dedicated number head trained with a regression loss. How-ever, its mechanism changes the norm of the special numeric token, which can conflict with LayerNorm and degrade text-token generation quality in modern LLMs. Consequently, designing numerical encoding and decoding schemes that integrate cleanly with pretrained LLMs without harming their language modeling capabilities remains an active area of research.

3.4. Rotary Number Decoding (RND) 

We now consider how to decode a scalar value mi from a predicted LLM embedding ˆx i . Directly recovering mi from 

ˆx i is nontrivial, even though the forward encoding is defined in closed form. For the j -th 2D rotational block, let ϑ j denote the es-timated relative rotation angle between the corresponding 13725 components of the unrotated base embedding and the output 

ˆx i . We can obtain a principal estimate of this angle, for example using atan2 , which yields ϑ j → (↑ε, ε] . However, this only identifies the angle modulo 2ε, so we have 

mi ω j = ϑ j + 2 εk j , (9) where k j → Z is an unknown integer capturing the periodic-ity. Applying this relation across all d/ 2 blocks leads to a system of linear congruences in mi .While such a system could, in principle, be solved for an ideal noise-free vector like the encoded x m i , it becomes unstable when decoding from the actual model output ˆx i .The predicted embedding is influenced by context, approx-imation errors, and other sources of noise, so it may not follow an exact sinusoidal structure. These perturbations can cause the inferred angles ϑ j to deviate from their ideal val-ues, leading to inconsistencies in the system of congruences. ## 4. Experiments 4.1. Dataset and tasks 

We evaluate RNED on two tasks: quantitative radiology estimation (on the in-house Opport-CT dataset and the public CT-RATE [ 15 ]) and medical vision grounding (on the public MedSeq-Bench [45]). 

Opport-CT Dataset is a large-scale, in-house dataset com-piled for an opportunistic screening study and consists of 25,645 non-contrast CT volumes, each paired with a ra-diology report. Following methodologies from CT-RATE and HuatuoGPT [ 9], we employed a two-pronged genera-tion strategy to acquire diverse Visual Question Answering (VQA) dataset for each volume-report pair. The overall gen-eration process and samples are illustrated in Supp. Sec. 7.5. 

CT-RATE Dataset is a public dataset containing over 50,000 image-report pairs from 21,304 patients, with CT images acquired using various reconstruction techniques [ 15 ]. We leverage the VQA data provided with the dataset, which includes a mix of long-answer, short-answer, and multiple-choice questions derived from the radiology reports. 

MedSeq-Bench Dataset is a public benchmark for eval-uating medical VLM grounding [ 45 ], with 1,000 image-question-answer triplets across 8 tasks in two categories: Im-age Difference Grounding (IDG), which localizes changed regions between temporally related images, and Image Con-sistency Grounding (ICG), which localizes corresponding regions across image pairs (e.g., multi-view matching, ob-ject tracking, cross-modal alignment). Each task requires bounding-box coordinate prediction, making it an ideal testbed for RNED. 

4.2. Implementation Details Model Architecture. For the Opport-CT dataset, we adopt the LLaVA [ 22 ] framework, which consists of a vision en-coder, a vision–language projector, and a pretrained LLM. For the vision encoder, we utilize an 8-layer ViT pretrained on GenerateCT [ 46 ]. Input CT volumes are resized to 

144 ↔144 ↔164 and clipped to the [↑1000 , 1000] Hounsfield unit range. The vision–language projector is a 2-layer MLP with a GeLU activation function [ 16 ]. We apply an aver-age pooling strategy to reduce the number of vision tokens. For the LLM, we use Llama-3-8B-Instruct [ 14 ], which has 32 self-attention blocks and an embedding dimension of 

d = 4096 . For public datasets, we use Llama-3.1-8B for CT-RATE and Qwen2.5-VL [ 3] for MedSeq-Bench, following the original implementations. 

Training Procedure. On the Opport-CT dataset, we follow a two-stage training process. In the first stage, we freeze the vision encoder and LLM, training only the projector using report summaries as targets. In the second stage, we per-form full instruction tuning on our generated VQA dataset. Here, we train the projector and fine-tune the LLM using LoRA [ 36 ] with a rank of 128 and an ϱ of 256. On the CT-RATE and MedSeq-Bench datasets, we fine-tune their checkpoints for one epoch using our RNED approach. In this stage, we freeze the image encoder and projector and apply LoRA fine-tuning only to the LLM. 

Hyperparameters. For the Opport-CT pretraining stage, we trained for 2 epochs with a projector learning rate of 1e-3. For the second-stage LoRA fine-tuning, we trained for 3 epochs with a projector learning rate of 1e-3 and a LoRA learning rate of 2e-4. For all experiments, we used a batch size of 10 per GPU, a gradient accumulation of 4 steps, zero weight decay, a cosine learning rate scheduler, and a 0.03 warmup ratio. 4.3. Quantitative Radiology evaluation QA format . We designed a downstream QA task to evaluate the numerical decoding ability of the VLM. Us-ing the pipeline shown in Appendix Fig. 5, we created questions of the format USER:[image][question]? ,where [question] asks for a specific numerical value. The model is expected to respond in the format 

ASSISTANT:[answer][unit] . Note that the QA for-mat is not strictly enforced during training. We evaluate the predicted numerical value [answer] 

against the ground truth after converting both to the same unit (mm). We test two scenarios: where the ground truth is a single value (e.g., ”The ascending aorta measures 35 mm”) and where it consists of two values (e.g., ”The nodule measures 5 mm x 4 mm”). We report Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and R 2 score for numerical accuracy. Since the model’s output is free-form, we also report the Success Rate (%) , which measures the percentage of generations that are in correct format. 

Opport-CT Results. Compared with standard tokenization for decoding numerical values, RNED yields substantially better performance on the Opport-CT dataset (Tab. 1). In the single-value setting, the R 2 score improves from 0.338 to 0.568, and in the double-value setting from 0.194 to 0.320. This suggests that standard tokenization, trained only with a language modeling objective, fails to capture quantitative meaning and tends to predict values near the dataset mean. It also struggles to produce valid outputs, with a success rate of only 55.7%, whereas our RNED-based model returns correctly formatted answers in over 80% of cases. We at-tribute this to the fact that RNED treats a number as a single conceptual unit with short prediction length, while standard tokenization decomposes it into multiple tokens, complicat-ing decoding—especially for multi-value predictions. Our method also outperforms other specialized ap-proaches. Compared to xVal, RNED achieves better per-formance across most metrics, demonstrating that our rotary encoding is more effective than simple linear scaling for handling numerical values in medical data. Prompt-based methods, which convert numbers into special string formats, performed even worse than the baseline, likely due to the drastically increased prediction length. 

CT-RATE Result . These findings are further validated on the public CT-Rate benchmark, shown in Tab. 2. After fine-tuning the base CT-CHAT model for just one epoch with our RNED objective, we observed a significant perfor-mance improvement across most metrics, especially in the R2 score. The baseline model’s low R 2 score indicates it had fallen into trivial mean estimation, whereas our fine-tuned model could successfully predict meaningful numerical val-ues. This demonstrates that RNED is a robust and effective method for enhancing the numerical estimation capabilities of VLMs for medical analysis tasks. 

4.4. Medical vision grounding evaluation 

Compared with the previous SOTA method MedSeq-Grounder, our approach achieves better performance on 5 out of 8 tasks in Table 3, with an average improvement of 1.66% in IoU and 2.62% in Acc@5. Notably, our method signifi-cantly improves performance on the more challenging tasks, such as Multi-view and Object Tracking. This suggests that RNED helps the model better capture spatial relationships and temporal dynamics in medical images, which are crucial for accurate grounding. For other tasks where RNED does not yield a performance boost, such as Image Difference Grounding, we observe that the SOTA method already per-forms very well and RNED maintains this high performance.

Different encoding/decoding strategies. We compare sev-eral encoding and decoding strategies in Tab. 5. For encod-ing, “Adding” denotes directly adding the numerical value to the base [NUM ] token; “Individual” assigns each number in 

M a dedicated embedding, which is then added to the base 

[NUM ] embedding. For decoding, “Linear” uses a single linear layer on the LLM embedding instead of Eq. (12). As shown in Tab. 5, “Adding” + “Linear” performs worst, likely because directly adding the raw value destabilizes gra-dients and yields a low success rate (33.1%). “Individual” + “Linear” improves the success rate (67.9%), presumably because the learned number embeddings have norms com-parable to other token embeddings, leading to more stable training. However, these embeddings do not preserve ordinal structure, so the model generalizes poorly to unseen numbers and remains suboptimal on the test set. With our representation, “RNE” + “Linear” outperforms both “Adding” and “Individual”, but is still slightly worse than “RNE” + “RND”. This suggests that a good numer-ical representation is crucial, and that our proposed RND decoder offers additional benefits over a learnable linear head: it supports a wide value range, requires no extra train-able parameters for decoding, and yields the best overall performance.

## 6. Conclusion 

In this work, we introduce Rotary Number Encoding and Decoding (RNED) to address numerical value tokenization in medical VLMs. RNED encodes a wide range of numerical values as order-preserving representations, enables robust decoding, and can be seamlessly integrated into pretrained VLMs. Experiments on radiological measurement estima-tion and medical visual grounding demonstrate its effective-ness. In future work, we will explore number representations beyond 1D by jointly considering 2D or 3D coordinates, as well as applications in reinforcement learning (RL) and reward function design.
