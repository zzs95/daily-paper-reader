# Pediatric brain tumors are the most common solid tumors in children and the leading cause of cancer-related mortality in the pediatric population (1). Compared with adult brain tumors, they arise in distinct anatomic locations and exhibit unique bi-ologic and clinical characteristics. However, progress in devel-oping artificial intelligence tools to assist in their diagnosis and management has been limited by the scarcity of curated, pub-licly available pediatric datasets and the absence of standardized frameworks for algorithm evaluation (2). Although community-driven initiatives such as the Medical Image Computing and Computer Assisted Intervention Society Brain Tumor Segmentation (BraTS) challenges (3,4) have estab-lished benchmarks for adult brain tumor analysis, pediatric neu-ro-oncology has lacked comparable resources. This gap reflects the relative rarity of these tumors, variability in imaging proto-cols, and inconsistent data accessibility. Diffuse midline gliomas (DMGs) exemplify these challenges because imaging serves as the primary modality for diagnosis and monitoring (5), yet few auto-mated segmentation methods have been optimized for pediatric anatomy or tumor biology (6–14). The BraTS in Pediatrics (BraTS-PEDs) dataset (7) was de-veloped to address this critical gap by providing the first large-scale, multi-institutional, expert-annotated benchmark dedicated to pediatric brain tumor segmentation. Curated from diverse sources, including the Children’s Brain Tumor Network (CBTN) (15), the International Diffuse Intrinsic Pontine Glioma/Diffuse Midline Glioma (DIPG/DMG) registry, and partner pediatric centers, the dataset underwent standardized preprocessing and neuroradiologist-reviewed annotation using tumor subregion definitions aligned with the Response Assessment in Pediatric Neuro-Oncology recommendations. The BraTS-PEDs dataset aims to enable reliable benchmarking of artificial intelligence al-gorithms and accelerate the development and clinical translation of imaging-based tools for children with brain tumors. 

## Materials and Methods Data Overview 

The BraTS-PEDs dataset comprises a retrospective, multi-in-stitutional cohort of preoperative, treatment-naive MRI scans from pediatric patients with high-grade gliomas, including DMGs and high-grade anaplastic astrocytoma. Data were contributed by the CBTN (https://cbtn.org) , the DFCI-BCH-BWH-PEDs-HGG collection on The Cancer Imaging Archive 

(https://www.cancerimagingarchive.net/collection/dfci-bch-bwh-peds-hgg/) , the International DMG/DIPG registry (https:// www.dipgregistry.org) , Yale University, and Duke University. CBTN and the DMG/DIPG registry function as multi-in-stitutional repositories aggregating data from international pediatric centers. All data were collected under institutional review board ap-proval or waiver and transferred through formal data-sharing agreements. Datasets were fully anonymized before transfer, with no restrictions on scanner type, acquisition protocol, or metadata availability to preserve real-world heterogeneity. Inclusion criteria required pediatric patients with high-grade glioma and availability of four conventional MRI sequences performed in a pretreatment setting. Exclusion criteria included motion artifacts, posttreatment or postoperative scans, leptome-ningeal spread, alternate tumor diagnosis, or absence of visi-ble tumor. The final cohort comprised 457 patients: 116 from CBTN, 227 from the DMG/DIPG Registry, 61 from the The Cancer Imaging Archive DFCI-BCH collection, 27 from Yale University, and 26 from Duke University. Each patient contrib-uted a single tumor and a single imaging time point; therefore, the patient and tumor counts were equivalent ( n = 457). Of the 457 patients included, 167 were previously re-ported in our earlier publication (7), which presented results from the BraTS-PEDs 2023 Challenge. That study evaluated submitted algorithms across three tumor subregions: enhanc-ing tumor, nonenhancing component (including nonenhanc-ing tumor, cystic areas, and necrosis), and edema. Here, we expand to a larger cohort and provide more granular tumor subregion annotations. 

MRI Data 

Each MRI study includes precontrast T1-weighted, postcon-trast T1-weighted, T2-weighted, and T2 fluid-attenuated in-version recovery images, providing a harmonized multipara-metric foundation for segmentation and analysis.

Clinical and Supporting Data 

Available clinical variables include demographics, diagnosis, survival, and scanner vendor, curated from electronic health records or CBTN manifests (Fig 1). Expert-derived tumor segmentations of defined subregions are included in the re-lease. Genomic and extended clinical data can be accessed through linked companion datasets in external repositories such as CBTN. 

Image Preprocessing 

All multiparametric MRI scans in the BraTS-PEDs data-set underwent standardized preprocessing using the BraTS Pipeline, including conversion of Digital Imaging and Com-munications in Medicine files to Neuroimaging Informatics Technology Initiative format, co-registration to a common an-atomic template (SRI24) (16), and resampling to an isotropic resolution of 1 mm³, implemented within the Cancer Imaging Phenomics Toolkit (17,18) and the Federated Tumor Segmen-tation platform (19). Facial defacing (20) was applied to all scans to remove identifiable facial features that may be visi-ble on head MR images, thereby reducing the risk of patient reidentification and enabling compliant public data sharing. The dataset was not skull-stripped to preserve full anatomic context and prevent inadvertent removal of deep-seated tu-mor regions. A separate open-access pediatric skull-stripping model is available for users (https://github.com/d3b-center/peds-brain-auto-skull-strip) (21). The overall workflow of this study is presented in Figure 2, and preprocessing steps are summa-rized in Table 1. 

Tumor Subregion Definition 

Tumor subregions were segmented using a pediatric-specific automated model (https://github.com/d3b-center/peds-brain-au-to-seg-public) (8), following Response Assessment in Pediatric Neuro-Oncology–aligned definitions (22,23). Four subregions were delineated (Fig 2). 

Enhancing tumor 

Enhancing tumor (label 1) are subregions demonstrating con-trast enhancement, defined as hyperintense signal on postcon-trast T1-weighted images relative to precontrast images. In cases of subtle enhancement, comparison with signal intensity in normal brain structures was used for confirmation. 

Nonenhancing tumor 

Nonenhancing tumor (label 2) are tumor subregions with ab-normal signal intensity on T1-weighted, T2 fluid-attenuated in-version recovery, or T2-weighted images that lack postcontrast enhancement and do not meet criteria for cystic component (see definition below) or necrosis (areas of nonenhancement with complex fluid-like signal intensity and irregular margins; less common in pretreatment pediatric tumors). 

Cystic component 

Cystic component (label 3) are intratumoral areas resembling well-demarcated fluid-filled spaces, appearing hyperintense on T2-weighted images and hypointense on postcontrast T1-weighted images. These components—located centrally or peripherally within the tumor—often exhibit signal intensity comparable to cerebrospinal fluid, although some variation may occur. 

Peritumoral edema 

Peritumoral edema (label 4) are regions of abnormal hyper-intensity on T2 fluid-attenuated inversion recovery images extending beyond the tumor margin. Edema typically demon-strates a finger-like pattern while preserving underlying brain architecture and is usually more hyperintense than nonen-hancing tumor. As in many tumor subregion differentiation tasks, challenges persist in distinguishing peritumoral edema from nonenhancing tumor, differentiating cystic components from nonenhancing regions, and separating mildly enhancing from nonenhancing areas. Readers interested in strategies addressing these challenges are referred to Familiar et al (24). 

Generating Reference Standard Segmentation 

Reference labels were generated through a multistage pro-cess combining automated segmentation and expert re-finement. Preliminary masks produced by the automated model were manually edited in ITK-SNAP (25) by trained annotators according to detailed guidelines. Subsequently, three board-certified neuroradiologists (A.V., A.N., J.B.W.), who had formerly reached a consensus on segmentation ap-proach, reviewed all segmentations and iteratively approved or revised annotations. ## Summary 

> The BraTS-PEDs dataset provides the largest, multi-institutional, publicly available collection of annotated multiparametric MRI scans of pediatric high-grade gliomas, enabling standardized benchmarking and development of AI tools in pediatric neuro-oncology.

## Key Points    

> ■The Brain Tumor Segmentation in Pediatrics (BraTS-PEDs) data-set, shared via The Cancer Imaging Archive, includes multipara-metric MRI and expert-refined tumor subregion annotations from 457 pediatric patients with high-grade gliomas collected across multiple international institutions.
> ■A standardized preprocessing pipeline and multistage expert anno-tation process were applied, providing harmonized imaging data and reproducible reference standard segmentations aligned with Response Assessment in Pediatric Neuro-Oncology recommenda-tions.
> ■The publicly available dataset enables benchmarking, development, validation, and generalization testing of artificial intelligence mod-els for pediatric brain tumor segmentation across heterogeneous, multi-institutional imaging data.

All preprocessing and annotation tools were implemented us-ing open-source software detailed in Table 1. The data will be available on The Cancer Imaging Archive ( https://www.can-cerimagingarchive.net/collection/brats-peds/ ; DOI: https://doi. org/10.7937/dx5c-tj86 ). 

## Discussion 

To our knowledge, the BraTS-PEDs dataset provides the first large-scale, multiconsortium, expert-annotated benchmark for pediatric brain tumor segmentation and offers opportuni-ties to advance research and clinical translation. It establishes a standardized framework for comparative evaluation of ar-tificial intelligence tumor segmentation models for pediatric high-grade gliomas, including DMGs, using predefined train-ing, validation, and testing partitions. Its multi-institutional design and harmonized preprocessing support the develop-ment of generalizable models that account for variability in pediatric imaging protocols. In addition, when integrated with complementary molecular and clinical datasets such as those from the CBTN, BraTS-PEDs may facilitate radiomic and multimodal investigations linking imaging phenotypes with underlying biology. The curated annotations may also serve as educational resources for neuroradiology and neuro-oncology trainees learning tumor segmentation. Some limitations should be acknowledged.
