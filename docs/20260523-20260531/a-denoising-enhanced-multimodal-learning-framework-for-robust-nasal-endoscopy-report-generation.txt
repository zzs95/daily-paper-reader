## Abstract 

Automatic generation of nasal endoscopy reports has emerged as a promising solution to reduce time-consuming clinical tasks while accurately capturing key lesions re-lated to disease in nasal endoscopy images. However, nasal endoscopy reports are affected by label ambiguity noise and anatomical labeling noise, which can lead to misalignment between visual details and textual semantics, resulting in semantic drift. To address this, we propose a noise-robust multimodal framework. First, multimodal GroundingDINO is employed to precisely localize key diag-nostic regions and extract interpretable region-level visual evidence. Next, the report generation process is seman-tically modeled through structured prompts with a rank-aware magnitude constraint, refining the regional charac-teristics and severity expression of lesions. Furthermore, a Structural Consistency module is designed to align cross-modal features and enhance the consistency between visual, semantic, and textual representations, thereby improving the overall coherence and robustness of generated reports. On the clinical nasal endoscopy dataset NE-Findings, the proposed method achieves improvements of 0.017, 0.027, 0.022, and 0.021 in BLEU-1 to BLEU-4, respectively, and gains of 0.026 and 0.058 in METEOR and ROUGE-L.

Meanwhile, annotations generally exhibit two types of noise: (1) Label ambiguity noise , arising from uncertain or inconsistent wording in clinical notes, e.g., “mild edema” vs. “slight swelling” or subjective expressions like “abun-dant secretions” vs. “secretions attached,” leading to partial mismatch between textual descriptions and visual evidence; (2) Anatomical labeling noise , caused by structural anno-tation errors or inconsistencies, e.g., marking a right infe-rior turbinate lesion as “bilateral” or incorrectly assigning nasopharyngeal inflammation to the nasal cavity, disrupt-ing the correspondence between visual regions and textual descriptions. The combined effect of these noises makes it challenging for models to establish stable region-level image-text correspondences, potentially resulting in seman-tic drift and hallucinated generation. Moreover, nasal endoscopy reports need to compre-hensively describe bilateral anatomical relationships, le-sion extent, and severity. However, conventional gener-ation models struggle to effectively aggregate these key pieces of information when processing nasal endoscopy datasets, limiting the interpretability and reliability of the generated reports. To address these challenges, we pro-pose a noise-robust multimodal framework (Figure 1c) that jointly optimizes visual evidence and textual consistency to enhance the reliability of region-level visual features. Specifically, the model first employs multi-modal MM-GroundingDINO [50] to perform region-level detection on multi-frame nasal endoscopy images. Through visual-driven cross-frame consistency, it corrects misalignments caused by anatomical labeling noise and obtains structurally precise and verifiable region-level visual evidence. Next, a structured prompts-driven rank-aware magnitude constraint maps ambiguous or inconsistent severity expressions into a unified ordinal space, mitigating label ambiguity noise. Fi-nally, a Structural Consistency module aligns cross-modal shared features, enhancing the structural coherence and ro-bustness across visual, semantic, and textual modalities.The main contributions of this work are as follows: • Combine MM-GroundingDINO with rank-aware mod-ules to perform region-level detection and cross-frame consistency, capturing key diagnostic regions, refining le-sion semantics, correcting anatomical labeling noise, and mitigating ambiguity in severity labels; • Construct a cross-modal consistency constraint module using the Structural Consistency mechanism to align vi-sual, semantic, and textual features, reinforcing semantic coherence and robustness in report generation; • Conduct systematic evaluation on the NE-Findings clini-cal nasal endoscopy dataset, demonstrating superior per-formance over existing methods in report accuracy, se-mantic consistency, and clinical interpretability. 

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Image Captioning 

The image captioning task [8, 28, 42], based on the en-coder–decoder framework, aims to generate natural lan-guage descriptions that accurately convey image seman-tics. Early CNN-based methods relied on global features but suffered from information loss. Later approaches intro-duced region features and vision–language alignment to im-prove descriptive accuracy. With the advent of Transform-ers, self- and cross-attention enabled more effective intra-and inter-modal fusion, enhancing visual–linguistic interac-tion. Further improvements came from reinforcement learn-ing, reordering strategies, and vision–language pretraining. Recently, large language models (LLMs) have advanced captioning performance through prompt learning [44] and training-free architectures like V2L-Tokenizer [51]. Over-all, image captioning provides a conceptual foundation for medical report generation, though the latter demands finer lesion detection and more structured clinical semantics. 

2.2. Medical Report Generation 

Medical Report Generation (MRG) [1, 3, 26, 30, 36] has evolved from early CNN–RNN frameworks to Transformer-based architectures. Early approaches mainly focused on cross-modal alignment between medical images and text but struggled to model long-range dependencies and complex semantics [45]. The introduction of Transform-ers greatly enhanced contextual consistency and visual– language interaction. Subsequent research introduced hi-erarchical structures, memory modules, and knowledge-enhanced mechanisms to improve semantic mapping be-tween visual and textual modalities. From a modeling per-spective, hierarchical LSTMs capture multi-level semantics, anatomy-aware encoders focus on key lesions, and con-trastive learning improves fine-grained disease representa-tion. Transformer-based models further refine word–visual interaction and report coherence through expert tokens and multimodal context modeling. Beyond widely studied radi-ology tasks such as chest X-ray report generation, MRG has recently been extended to specialty domains such as nasal endoscopy. These characteristics impose higher demands on precise re-gion grounding and fine-grained semantic understanding. Integrating medical knowl-edge has become crucial for ensuring factual correctness. Balancing fluency and fac-tual consistency thus remains a key challenge for MRG. 

2.3. Visual Self-Correction Mechanism 

Self-correction mechanisms [20, 25] have recently been widely adopted to improve the output quality of genera-tive models. The core idea is to introduce feedback-based refinement during generation, enabling models to itera-tively reduce errors and enhance semantic consistency. This mechanism was first applied to natural language generation tasks and has demonstrated strong robustness and general-ization across domains. In medical imaging, the accuracy of report content and its alignment with visual features are crucial. Accordingly, several studies have introduced visual self-correction frameworks, allowing models to re-optimize the generated reports based on visual evidence, thereby cor-recting erroneous descriptions and reinforcing clinical logi-cal consistency. The DART model [29] uses self-correction to calibrate visual and textual modalities, improving report quality and clinical reliability. 

## 3. Method 

As illustrated in Figure 2, our pipeline follows a three-stage design: First, MM-GroundingDINO localizes critical re-gions from multi-frame images and extracts interpretable visual evidence, correcting anatomical labeling noise while maintaining cross-frame consistency; Next, structured tex-tual prompts with a rank-aware magnitude constraint refine lesion features and encode severity information, alleviating label ambiguity; Finally, the Structural Consistency mod-ule aligns cross-modal features, addressing inconsistencies across frames and textual phrases, enhancing visual, seman-tic, and textual consistency as well as report robustness.

## 4. Experiments 

4.1. Datasets 

NE-Findings is a nasal endoscopy dataset for inflammation detection and structured report generation. It contains 2,800 examinations and 7,486 images with expert-annotated tex-tual descriptions, covering findings like turbinate hypertro-phy, mucosal congestion, and nasal discharge. The dataset includes multi-view anterior nasal cavities (e.g., inferior turbinate, middle meatus, and nasopharynx) with compre-hensive coverage. All annotations are verified by experts to ensure accurate image-text alignment. In addition, the dataset provides object detection annotations for structures and lesions, including nasal discharge, adenoids, inferior turbinate, and nasal septum, serving as region-level super-vision. The dataset[5, 6] is split into training, validation, and testing sets with a ratio of 70% : 10% : 20%. 

IU X-Ray dataset [10], developed by Indiana University, is one of the most widely used public datasets for medi-cal report generation. It contains 7,470 chest X-ray images and 3,955 corresponding reports. Following previous works [29, 38], we adopt the standard data split of 70% : 10% : 20% for training, validation, and testing, respectively. 

MIMIC-CXR dataset [18], released by Beth Israel Dea-coness Medical Center, is a large-scale chest X-ray database containing 473,057 radiographs and 206,563 paired clinical reports. Consistent with prior studies [5, 29, 38], we use the official split, where the training set contains 368,960 im-ages, the validation set includes 2,991 images, and the test set comprises 5,159 images. 

4.2. Evaluation Metrics 

We evaluate generated reports using standard NLG metrics, including BLEU, METEOR, and ROUGE-L.For clinical ef-ficacy (CE), we employ domain-specific labelers: For chest X-rays, CheXpert [15] provides automatic labels, and CE is measured with Precision, Recall, and F1-Score for key pathologies. For nasal endoscopy, manually constructed la-bels covering turbinate hypertrophy, nasal polyps, and dis-charge type are used, with CE computed by matching gen-erated reports to these labels. For dataset fine-tuning and grounding, standard Average Precision (AP) [43] across dif-ferent IoU thresholds evaluates anatomical structure local-ization and lesion annotation. 

4.3. Implementation Details 

In the first stage, a ResNet-101 pretrained on ImageNet was used as encoder, with AdamW as optimizer. The initial learning rate was set to 5×10 −5 and adjusted using a cosine schedule. The model was trained for 50 epochs on the NE-Findings dataset, while for the MIMIC-CXR and IU X-Ray datasets, training epochs followed baseline configurations. The batch size was 16, and the input image resolution was 

224 × 224 . Experiments were conducted on an RTX 4090 GPU. 4.6. Visualization Analysis 

Figure 3 illustrates an allergic rhinitis (AR) case with clear nasal discharge. The original report omits lat-erality and adenoidal information, and local degree de-scriptions (e.g., no/mild/moderate/severe ) are also under-specified. Adding regional-evidence constraints strength-ens the association between key phrases and the corre-sponding anatomical sites, enriching local details. The full model completes and regularizes degree expressions, pro-ducing clear ordinal phrasing such as “ mild hypertrophy of the right inferior turbinate ” and “ a moderate amount of clear watery discharge on the adenoids ,” resulting in pre-cise site–lesion–severity statements. Figure 7 illustrates a sinusitis case with purulent se-cretions. Compared to the baseline, our model generates more detailed, anatomically grounded reports. The base-line misses key pathological phrases or uses vague terms, whereas our output accurately identifies severe inflamma-tion and “a large amount of white purulent discharge at-tached to the adenoids.” Together, they transform initially missing or vague reports into anatomically aligned, degree-explicit narratives that better reflect clinical practice. 

## 5. Conclusion 

In this work, we present a noise-robust, fact-grounded framework for Nasal Endoscopy Report Generation that ad-dresses input–output mismatch in single-frame paradigms, strengthens grounding of textual findings to nasal regions, and stabilizes lesion severity. We achieve this by enforcing cross/intra-modal Structural Consistency, extracting region-level evidence with MM-GroundingDINO, and injecting rank-aware ordinal signals via structured prompts, yield-ing reports that are pixel-verifiable and interpretable. Ex-periments on a clinical dataset and NE-Findings demon-strate improvements in report accuracy, semantic consis-tency, and clinical interpretability. Remaining challenges include localization uncertainty under severe illumination shifts or ambiguous boundaries, and sparsity and inconsis-tency of fine-grained severity labels, which may limit gen-eralization.
