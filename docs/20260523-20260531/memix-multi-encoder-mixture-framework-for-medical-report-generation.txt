## Abstract 

Clinical medical report generation, traditionally performed by doctors, is a time-consuming task. Automating genera-tion can significantly alleviate the workload of medical pro-fessionals. However, existing approaches typically leverage various separate multimodal encoders to align image and text features, leading to the failure of effective differentiated fusion of independently encoded medical representations. To address the issues, we propose a multi-encoder mixture framework (MeMix), a novel architecture that leverages the capabilities of multiple encoders to efficiently integrate mul-timodal medical features for the medical report generation task. Specifically, to exploit the distinct strengths of multiple encoders for multimodal medical data integration, MeMix adopts an image-enhancement unit and a graph-node en-coder to derive multi-faceted representations of medical im-ages and domain knowledge. For deep cross-modal seman-tic fusion, we introduce a novel encoder mixture module based on the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture, which combines modality-specific and cross-encoder mixtures to capture a comprehensive representation of medical infor-mation. Extensive experiments on two real datasets demon-strate that MeMix achieves superior performance compared with state-of-the-art report generation models.

Medi-cal reports are mainly generated by doctors based on their own experiences and knowledge. However, the subjective judgments of doctors change with their professional level and working status, which leads to the problems of time-consuming work and uneven report quality. ## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Encoder-Decoder based methods 

The encoder-decoder methods [30] employ the encoder to extract visual features of images, and compile visual fea-tures to text features by the decoder. Visual captioning models equip attention mechanisms [28, 39] on encoder-decoder architectures. Some approaches feed several hid-den representations (such as context vector [15] and topic vector [20]) into these models, and adopt generative mod-els or reinforcement learning models to decode the hidden representations to generate reports. Moreover, the introduc-tion of additional modules on top of the encoder-decoder has significantly enhanced the performance in medical re-port generation task, such as the auxiliary signal [18] and the symbolic logical reasoning module [10]. The Transformer-based structures ([32]) are typical rep-resentatives of encoder-decoder architectures. Chen et al. [6] designed a memory-driven Transformer to gener-ate radiology reports. Similarly, research works [3, 4, 29] have also integrated memory modules into Transformer-based models. Liu et al. [24] devised a Posterior-and-Prior Knowledge Exploring-and-Distilling approach (PPKED) to imitate the radiologists’ working patterns. Liu et al. [25] used the visual language BERT model to identify the ab-normality on the COVID-19 scans. Recent works [5, 23, 29] utilize large language models to improve the quality of re-port generation. Compared to these methods, MeMix ex-tracts multidimensional visual features, enabling it to focus on specific abnormal regions associated with diseases. 

2.2. Knowledge-based methods 

Additional knowledge (such as pre-trained embeddings [37], models [9], and pre-built knowledge graphs [17]) gives extra information to improve performance for NLP [33] and image captioning [15] tasks. Previous works [26, 36] adopted the graph neural network to pass the message and aggregate neighbor features on graph ar-chitectures. Li et al. [17] proposed a KERP based on the graph struc-ture, which converts visual features into abnormal graphs, retrieves the predefined text templates based on the detected abnormality, and uses the paraphrase module to update the retrieved text. Zhang et al. [38] employed pre-constructed graph embedding modules on multiple disease findings to generate the medical report. Li et al. [18] built a medical tag graph, designed a graph encoder to fuse medical knowledge and image features, and then adopted a GPT-based decoder to generate reports. Li et al. [19] utilized dynamic knowl-edge graphs and contrastive learning techniques to inject re-trieved knowledge on-the-fly for report generation. Adopt-ing a similar concept, studies [12, 14] also utilized injected knowledge models to facilitate report generation. However, these works focus on generating text descriptions for abnor-mal symptoms. 

## 3. Multi-encoder Mixture Framework 

The Multi-encoder Mix ture framework (MeMix) aims to generate medical reports by learning the associations be-tween medical features and knowledge and integrating the strengths of multiple encoders. MeMix consists of four modules: an image enhancement unit, a graph node encoder, an encoder mixture, and a cross-modal decoder. It integrates the complementary strengths of multiple encoders to process multimodal inputs, and uti-lizes a Transformer-based decoder to process information from multiple modalities, as shown in Figure 2. The in-put images are first fed to the image enhancement unit to extract visual features. Subsequently, with the assistance of two medical graphs and prompt engineering, visual fea-tures are transformed into disease and term features. The encoder mixture leverages multiple encoders’ advantages to effectively integrate cross-modal features. Furthermore, the cross-modal decoder receives term features and utilizes a Transformer-based decoder to predict word probabilities.

3.2. Graph Node Encoder 

Graph structures are widely adopted to represent entities and relationships. We utilize a graph node encoder to or-ganize prior medical knowledge ( i.e. , diseases and medical terminologies) and to learn the associations between medi-cal features and knowledge. The graph node encoder con-sists of two types of medical knowledge graphs ( i.e. , disease co-occurrence graph and medical term graph), intra-graph embedding learning, and term asssited prompt components. 

Disease co-occurrence graph. The disease co-occurrence graph prioritizes the analysis of abnormal visual informa-tion closely related to diseases, exploring the potential cor-relations between visual features and disease information. We construct the disease co-occurrence graph, in which nodes are diseases, and edges between nodes are con-structed according to the co-occurrence relationships be-tween diseases. The disease nodes are connected with bidirectional edges. In our scenario, edges are modeled as unweighted to represent the co-occurrence relationship between two disease nodes. The disease co-occurrence graph is constructed for thoracic diseases, we automati-cally recompiled the disease graph similar to the previous study [17] according to the co-occurrence of diseases, cov-ering 13 diseases such as pleural effusion, pneumothorax, and pneumonia. Medical Term Graph. The medical term graph integrates medical terms and global image features to provide com-prehensive descriptions related to images and diseases. We unify normal and abnormal terms of the disease nodes in the disease co-occurrence graph as the term nodes in the medical term graph. Each node in the medical term graph denotes a kind of medical term. The edges in the medical term graph connect two term nodes with interactive rela-tionships, such as two nodes describing the same disease or those having a co-occurrence relationship. We constructed the corresponding medical term graph for thoracic diseases according to the abnormality graph [17], chest observation graph [38] and Rad-Graph [13]. It incorporates 127 terms with 81 abnormal and 46 normal terms, such as “no pneumothorax”, “biapical pleural thickening” and “hyperexpanded lung”. The initial node features in the graph ( f T n init ) come from global visual features f gv , and its calculation is similar to the initializa-tion of disease nodes. 

Intra Embedding Learning. The intra embedding learn-ing is utilized to aggregate the node information within the graph, which is implemented by the Graph Convolutional Network (GCN). In each convolutional layer, the informa-tion of neighbor nodes is aggregated by multiplying the cur-rent node features by the normalized adjacency matrix, and the features of the nodes in the next layer are updated by the aggregated features. After intra embedding learning, the node features of the disease co-occurrence graph and the medical term graph are updated to f Dn and f T n .

Term Assisted Prompt. Furthermore, previous study[14] has proposed that prompt engineering contributes to gen-erating reports with better clinical efficacy. Building on this, we propose a Term Assisted Prompt (TAP) compo-nent. Compared to conveying disease classification results to guide text decoding, TAP is more direct in improving the quality of report generation by assisting text decoding with medical terms. The input of TAP is global visual feature f gv , and it uses t heads to output prompt tokens 

P T 1, ..., P T t, where t is the number of nodes in the medi-cal term graph. The output tokens of each head are binary classified by a fully connected layer, namely “Positive” and “Other”. The training process can be trained using stan-dard cross-entropy loss. During the inference process, to-ken [POS] and [OTH] are used to represent the categories, providing a reference for text generation. In order to synthesize the gains that medical knowledge and prompt engineering bring to the report generation task, we consider the outputs ( f T n 

, P T 1,...,t ) of intra embedding learning component f T n 

and TAP P T 1, ..., P T t as one of inputs to the encoder mixture, generating report text guided by medical knowledge and term assisted prompt. 

3.3. Encoder Mixture 

To fully exploit cross-modal medical semantics and maxi-mize the strengths of encoders, we design an encoder mix-ture that projects the outputs of multiple encoders into a unified representation space and incorporates a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture to perform heterogeneous fu-sion of multimodal medical features and knowledge. 

Encoder adapter. Given a medical input from any modal-ity, the input is processed by multiple encoders to obtain embedding features. As encoders from different sources re-side in incompatible representation spaces, we introduce an encoder adapter for each encoder to achieve dimensional matching and embedding-space alignment. Following prior work [2], each adapter is implemented as a two-layer MLP and customized to its corresponding encoder’s output. 

Self-Encoder Mixture. After aligning embeddings across modalities, we further employ modality-specific Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) modules, self-encoder mixture, to cap-ture each modality’s characteristics. The MoE architecture effectively handles heterogeneous embeddings, enhancing the modeling capacity for medical features. Specifically, we construct the modality-specific self-encoder mixture, em-ploying distinct expert networks for different modalities. The self-encoder mixture comprises multiple expert net-works, each implemented as a feed-forward network (FFN) to capture particular features. In this scenario, three FFNs serve as modality-specific experts. The output f SEM is lin-early combined each expert’s computation on the token f I

## 4. Experiment 

4.1. Experimental Settings 

Dataset. We conducted experiments on two real-world medical image report datasets. (1) MIMIC-CXR [16] is one of the largest public chest X-ray dataset, including 473,057 images and 206,563 reports. For the disease co-occurrence graph, we extracted 13 diseases that appear with high frequency in the reports. To construct the medical term graph, we extracted 127 medical terms that frequently ap-pear in the “Findings” section of reports. (2) IU X-Ray [8] is a widely used public chest X-ray dataset. The dataset contains 7,470 X-ray images and their corresponding 3,955 radiology reports. The two types of knowledge graphs con-structed for the IU-Xray dataset follow the same criterion as those used for the MIMIC-CXR dataset. 

Parameter Settings. For MIMIC-CXR, we adopt the official training-validation-testing data split to train and evaluate our model. For IU X-Ray, we use 7:1:2 train-ing:validation:testing data split. The abnormal images in the two datasets are labeled with disease labels, and we choose the abnormal images by manually selecting the disease la-bels that are not “normal” as positive samples of classifica-tion tasks to pre-train the DenseNet-121. We use the pre-trained DenseNet-121 to extract visual features and resize the global and key area to 224 × 224. We set 512 as the dimension of all hidden states, and the batch size is 16. We set the number of Transformer layers to 4. We use Adam optimization with its default parameter setting. All pro-grams are implemented by Python 3.8.5 + Pytorch 1.7.1 on 8×A100 GPU. 

Baselines. We compared MeMix to the following state-of-the-art (SOTA) report generation models: RGKG [38], R2GEN [6], PPKED [24], CMN [7], Clinical-BERT [35], RGRG [31], MMTN [4], PromptMRG [14] and Teaser [40]. 

Evaluation Metrics. In order to evaluate the medical re-port generation, we used Natural Language Generation (NLG) metrics , including BLEU-n [27], METEOR [1], and ROUGE-L [21]; To assess the quality from a clinical perspective, and also adopted the Clinical Efficacy (CE) metric [7], which evaluates the Precision, Recall, and F1 score between the labels extracted from the generated re-ports and those from the ground-truth reports. Since the IU X-ray does not provide consistent labels, we report CE met-rics for MIMIC-CXR.

4.3. Ablation Studies 

We investigate the effect of different modules by remov-ing them from the original model. Specifically, MeMix w/o IEU excludes the image enhancement unit except CNN modules from MeMix. MeMix w/o DCG, w/o MTG and w/o TAP remove the disease co-occurrence graph, medical term graph, and term assisted prompt. MeMix w/o SEM, w/o CEM, and w/o SEM+CEM discard the self-encoder mixture, cross-encoder mixture, and both two modules. MeMix w/o CMD removes components from the cross-modal decoder, retaining only the basic Transformer de-coder. As shown in Table 3, MeMix w/o CMD and MeMix w/o SEM+CEM perform worst, which confirms the valid-ity of incorporating various medical features and knowl-edge into the encoding and decoding process. Likewise, ex-cluding the self-encoder mixture or cross-encoder mixture components individually leads to a marked deterioration in MeMix’s performance, suggesting that both modules effec-tively extract key representational information from multi-modal medical features. MeMix w/o IEU obtains poor per-formance since abnormal regions contain important visual features, focusing on abnormal regions benefits the genera-tion of medical reports. Each design removing knowledge graph modules ( i.e. , MeMix w/o DCG, w/o MTG and w/o TAP) performs poorer, demonstrating that knowledge graph embeddings and prompt engineering contribute to the medi-cal report generation. In addition, MeMix w/o DCG outper-form MeMix w/o MTG because that the medical term graph learns the information from the disease co-occurrence graph and its nodes contain more abundant medical term knowl-edge. All modules are helpful and bring about performance gain. 

## 5. Conclusion 

We presented MeMix, a multi-encoder mixture framework for medical report generation, integrating different encoders to facilitate effective cross-modal semantic fusion. The im-age enhancement unit and graph node encoder in MeMix are designed to facilitate the generation of rich and multi-faceted representations for medical features. MeMix adopts an MoE-based encoder mixture that jointly routes and fuses signals from modality-specific and cross-encoder experts.
