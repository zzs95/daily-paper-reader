## Abstract 

Radiology report generation (RRG) aims to automatically describe medical images via free-text reports. In clini-cal practice, comparing current and prior chest X-rays is essential for assessing disease progression, motivating the development of longitudinal RRG methods. However, most existing approaches often struggle to capture fine-grained temporal changes, as they often rely on unidi-rectional alignments or static reasoning pipelines, over-looking the bidirectional and asymmetric nature of disease evolution. To tackle these challenges, we propose BiOT-Prompt, a novel framework for disease evolution-aware radiology report generation, which introduces a Bidirec-tional Optimal Transport (BiOT) mechanism to explicitly model progression dynamics between historical and current chest X-rays. By analyzing the asymmetry between bidi-rectional transport plans, BiOTPrompt can identify newly emerged and resolved regions, which are then used to con-struct dynamic prompts that guide large language mod-els (LLMs) in generating clinically relevant diagnostic re-ports. Furthermore, we incorporate a vision-language con-sistency constraint to ensure alignment between visual ev-idence and textual descriptions, mitigating hallucinations and enhancing factual correctness. Extensive experiments on the Longitudinal-MIMIC dataset demonstrate that BiOT-Prompt achieves state-of-the-art performance in both lan-guage metrics and clinical relevance, setting a new stan-dard for longitudinal radiology report generation.

These approaches have led to notable improvements in temporal awareness. However, they often operate at coarse semantic granularity, relying on global representations or predefined anatomical regions, which may overlook subtle or spatially localized changes. In addition, most methods adopt unidirectional alignment or static prompting, making them less effective at capturing the inherently asymmetric and bidirectional nature of lesion evolution—such as newly emerging abnormalities or resolved findings—thereby lim-iting their ability to generate truly progression-aware diag-nostic reports. Modeling such fine-grained disease evolution, however, remains challenging due to the inherent properties of lon-gitudinal chest X-rays. First, images from different time points often exhibit non-aligned spatial layouts, caused by variations in patient positioning, view angle, or breathing phase. This makes direct pixel- or region-level comparisons unreliable. Second, lesion evolution is inherently asym-metric: new abnormalities may emerge, while others re-solve—posing difficulties for models that rely on symmetric or static assumptions. To address these issues, one promis-ing direction is to learn soft, flexible correspondences be-tween temporally misaligned anatomical regions. Optimal Transport (OT) [28] offers a principled way to model such correspondences by estimating the minimal-cost transport plan between feature sets from prior and current scans, mak-ing it well-suited for handling spatial misalignments. More importantly, when applied bidirectionally, OT can uncover asymmetric patterns of lesion evolution—highlighting re-gions that newly appear in the current scan or disappear rel-ative to the prior—thereby addressing both the alignment mismatch and the asymmetric progression challenge. Building on this insight, we propose BiOTPrompt , a Bidirectional Optimal Transport-guided Prompting frame-work for disease evolution-aware radiology report genera-tion. Specifically, we first encode current and prior chest X-rays into patch-level feature sets, and estimate bidirec-tional optimal transport plans in both directions (i.e., cur-rent →prior and prior →current) to simultaneously model soft correspondences across misaligned scans and capture asymmetric lesion dynamics. These transport plans are then fed into a Dual-Flow Asymmetry Analyzer, which detects regions with asymmetric transport behavior: patches that emit mass but receive little in return are identified as ei-ther newly emerged (in current) or resolved (in prior) re-gions. To that end, we construct OT-informed prompts by encoding the spatial indices of these evolving regions, which serve as localized diagnostic signals to guide a large language model (LLM) in generating temporally grounded and clinically faithful reports. Finally, to ensure alignment between image evidence and textual output, we further in-troduce a Vision-Language Consistency Constraint that en-forces cross-modal agreement, guiding the LLM to gener-ate reports that are both coherent and clinically reliable. We summarize the main contributions as follows: • We propose BiOTPrompt, the first framework that explic-itly identifies both emerging and disappearing abnormali-ties through bidirectional optimal transport, enabling fine-grained temporal reasoning in longitudinal RRG. • We design a dual-flow asymmetry analyzer that identifies evolving regions via bidirectional OT, and use them to construct dynamic prompts that provide precise, localized progression cues to guide report generation. • We propose a vision-language consistency constraint to enforce alignment between visual signals and textual out-puts, mitigating hallucinations. • Extensive experiments on the Longitudinal-MIMIC dataset demonstrate that BiOTPrompt significantly out-performs state-of-the-art longitudinal RRG methods across language metrics and clinical correctness. 

## 2. Related Works 

2.1. Radiology Report Generation 

Recent advances in RRG have introduced diverse mod-eling paradigms to enhance diagnostic accuracy and lan-guage coherence. Memory-driven methods [3–5, 35] lever-age external memory modules to store and retrieve cross-modal associations, while cross-modal alignment strate-gies [13, 20, 31, 46] focus on enhancing semantic consis-tency between modalities. Building on this, reinforcement learning techniques further refine generation quality with reward signals from radiologist feedback or task-specific objectives. In parallel, knowledge-guided approaches [2, 17, 18, 24] focus on enhancing factual correctness by in-jecting domain-specific priors, ranging from disease proto-types to auxiliary classifiers and self-distilled knowledge graphs. Interpretability-oriented models, such as region-guided frameworks [2, 38] and counterfactual reasoning ap-proaches [19], further contribute to more transparent and trustworthy report generation by localizing salient visual cues and exploring alternative diagnostic possibilities. Recently, the rapid advancement of large language mod-els (LLMs) has inspired growing interest in their appli-cation to RRG. For example, R2GenGPT [43] utilizes a multimodal LLM framework to integrate image features with language representations. PromptMRG [15] lever-ages disease classification outputs as task-specific prompts, while Bootstrapping LLM [23] refines generation via staged contrastive decoding. More recently, LLM-RG4 [44] in-troduces an instruction-driven framework that handles di-verse inputs via adaptive token fusion and context-sensitive loss, improving factual consistency and reducing halluci-nations. 2.2. Longitudinal Radiology Report Generation 

To better capture disease evolution, recent studies have in-troduced longitudinal context into RRG. Early work, such as CXR-ReDonE [33], mitigated hallucinations by rewrit-ing or filtering training reports. CheXRelNet [16] in-troduced anatomy-aware reasoning to detect visual dif-ferences, while RECAP [11] constructed disease evolu-tion graphs to capture temporal changes in key clinical attributes. In parallel, multimodal representation learn-ing has also been leveraged to align cross-time visual and textual features. BioViL-T [1] incorporated prior images into pretraining via multi-image transformers, and CCRG [34] learned longitudinal representations by fusing current and prior scans, coupled with anatomy-guided dropout to enhance localized controllability. Longitudinal pre-filling strategies have also been explored, with transformer-based fusion models trained to combine current and historical studies, as in Prefilling [47]. More recently, LLMs have also been adopted for longitu-dinal settings. HC-LLM [26] applies temporal consistency and multimodal contrastive constraints to guide generation, while HERGen [41] further integrates curriculum learning to handle complex multi-visit dynamics. In parallel, MLRG [25] leverages multi-view longitudinal contrastive learning and tokenized absence encoding to exploit prior studies and indications, improving clinical metrics. Despite growing interest in longitudinal context, existing methods typically rely on global alignment or static prompting, and lack ex-plicit modeling of asymmetric image variations, such as newly emerging or resolved lesions. To address this, we propose a bidirectional prompting framework that captures inter-scan discrepancies and constructs fine-grained, visu-ally grounded prompts to guide evolution-aware and clini-cally faithful report generation.

Thresholding Strategy Analysis: We compare five thresh-olding strategies used in our DFAA module for identifying newly emerged or resolved regions. As shown in Table 3, the simple Fixed Threshold yields the best overall perfor-mance across all four metrics, suggesting that a carefully selected constant cutoff can effectively balance sensitivity and specificity in capturing disease progression. On the other hand, the Top-K method applies a fixed selection ra-tio per image, making it less adaptive to varying disease patterns. Similarly, the Global Quantile method, based on dataset-level statistics, lacks sensitivity to instance-specific changes, reducing its ability to detect subtle regional dif-ferences. The Soft Sigmoid and Learnable Scalar methods also achieve moderate performance, which indicates that al-though adaptivity offers some advantages, it may lead to overfitting or misalignment, limiting generalization. 

Sensitivity to Asymmetry Threshold in Dual-Flow An-alyzer: To assess the impact of the asymmetry sensitivity in DFAA module, we conduct an ablation by varying the threshold δ from 0.04 to 0.09, and evaluate the generation quality using ROUGE-L and F1 Score. As shown in Fig-ure 6 (a), F1 Score shows a clear sensitivity to δ, peaking at thresholds around 0.05–0.06, suggesting that moderate sensitivity effectively captures meaningful visual changes. On the other hand, ROUGE-L is less sensitive to changes in the threshold, with only small variations observed across the range of δ. When the threshold is too low, the model overreacts to minor variations, leading to unstable visual alignment and slightly degrading generation quality. High thresholds (e.g., 0.08–0.09) may fail to capture subtle but clinically important changes, resulting in under-informative reports. This analysis emphasizes the importance of tuning

## 5. Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose BiOTPrompt, a novel framework for longitudinal RRG that explicitly models inter-scan vari-ations via bidirectional optimal transport. By comparing patch-level transport plans in both directions, BiOTPrompt identifies newly emerged and resolved regions between lon-gitudinal chest X-rays. These regions are transformed into dynamic prompts, guiding large language models to pro-duce reports that are both clinically faithful and tempo-rally aware. Additionally, we introduce a vision-language consistency constraint to align visual evidence with textual outputs, mitigating hallucinations and improving factual accuracy. Extensive experiments show that BiOTPrompt achieves state-of-the-art performance by leveraging image-only temporal cues and modeling asymmetric progression, without relying on prior reports. Currently, BiOTPrompt mainly focuses on two-timepoint modeling and has only been evaluated on chest X-rays. Future work will extend it to multi-timepoint analysis and evaluate its generalizabil-ity on other medical imaging modalities. 13762 6. Acknowledgments
