Abstract 

Linear activation steering is a powerful approach for eliciting the capabilities of large language models and specializing their behavior using limited labeled data. While effective, existing methods often apply a fixed steering strength to all tokens, resulting in inconsistent steering quality across diverse input prompts. In this work, we introduce Contextual Linear Activation Steering (CLAS), a method that dynamically adapts linear activation steering to context-dependent steering strengths. Across eleven steering benchmarks and four model families, it consistently outperforms standard linear activation steering and matches or exceeds the performance of ReFT and LoRA in settings with limited labeled data. We therefore propose CLAS as a scalable, interpretable, and accurate method for specializing and steering large language models. 1

# 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) [9, 39] exhibit powerful capabilities, yet the underlying mechanisms that give rise to these capabilities remain largely opaque. A promising lens for mechanistic understanding is provided by the linear representation hypothesis [19, 24–28], a key empirical observation positing that semantic con-cepts in LLMs, which form the basis of their capabilities, are represented as fixed linear directions within their activation spaces. A common approach for identifying such concepts is probing [2, 3, 7, 8, 17, 33], wherein a dataset of prompts and concept labels is used to train a model on the internal activations of an LLM to measure the amount of concept-specific information encoded in those activations. Beyond their use in concept detection, linear directions extracted from these probes can also enable linear activation steering (LAS) [5, 6, 21, 34, 40], where model generations are steered toward concept-specific behavior by adding these directions back into the activations during inference. In particular, let hℓ,t ∈ Rk denote the activation at the output of the ℓ-th transformer block 2 and the t-th token position of an LLM, and let dℓ ∈ Rk be a unit-norm steering vector extracted from a probe trained on the output of block ℓ. LAS involves replacing 

hℓ,t during inference with ˜hℓ,t = hℓ,t + αdℓ, where α ∈ R is a scalar steering coefficient . This simple yet general approach has been used to induce and control numerous latent capabilities in LLMs such as sentiment control [34], reasoning [4], and persona adoption [6], among many others [5]. While LAS is remarkably simple and effective, selecting steering coefficients that perform well across diverse prompts remains a key bottleneck in practice [5]. This is because the steering coefficient α is often tuned via an expensive grid search over coefficient values, requiring human or LLM evaluations of the generated outputs to select the best value [6, 11, 13, 20, 21, 30, 34, 35, 40]. Importantly, a more fundamental limitation is that these coefficients are context-independent : a fixed scalar is applied uniformly to all inputs, ignoring variations in prompt or context, and frequently to all blocks of the model [5, 6]. This limitation leads to systematic under- or over-steering, making current approaches brittle with respect to the choice of α and limiting real-world deployment, where α cannot be tuned per-prompt or on-the-fly.

To address these limitations, we introduce Contextual Linear Activation Steering (CLAS), which modulates the amount of steering based on the input context and the specific model block using a learned sensing vector 

cℓ ∈ Rk+1 . This sensing vector enables context-dependent activation steering: ˜hℓ,t = hℓ,t + ( cℓ · [hℓ,t 1]) dℓ,where [ hℓ,t 1] ∈ Rk+1 is the concatenation of hℓ,t ∈ Rk with a constant 1, and dℓ ∈ Rk is an extracted 

steering vector . To learn the sensing vector, we minimize the standard next-token prediction objective on a small dataset of prompt-completion pairs. This differs from tuning the coefficients in LAS, which uses an evaluator to select a fixed coefficient that produces the best generated outputs over a set of prompts. We evaluate the effectiveness of our method on 11 diverse steering tasks using four instruction-tuned LLMs. In these tasks, the LLMs are steered to execute behaviors such as step-by-step reasoning, multilingual translation, tldr-style summarization, and other behaviors without explicit instruction and with only limited completion data for tuning. We compare our method against LAS and two popular parameter-efficient fine-tuning baselines, ReFT [37] and LoRA [18]. We find that our method outperforms LAS in nearly every model-task pair and is significantly faster to tune. We also find that CLAS matches or exceeds both ReFT and LoRA in these settings. We additionally evaluate whether the low-rank directions learned by ReFT and LoRA encode interpretable concepts by testing them as linear probes on 11 concept monitoring tasks. We find that they can be used for concept monitoring, but the probe-derived steering vectors used by CLAS consistently achieve much higher accuracy. Together, these results demonstrate that CLAS is a scalable and general extension of linear activation steering, retaining its interpretability while achieving high performance across many tasks.

# 2 Method 

We describe our method in the context of steering LLMs using their internal activations. Although we focus on LLMs, our approach is generally applicable to any deep learning model. We first briefly outline the archi-tecture of an LLM and then describe how to obtain the steering and sensing vectors for our method, CLAS. LLMs are implemented as a composition of transformer blocks fℓ: given a sequence of input token embeddings 

x≤t, intermediate activations are created as hℓ,t = fℓ(hℓ−1,≤t) for ℓ ∈ { 1, . . . , L }, with h0,t = xt and hL,t 

mapped by a linear layer to logits for the next token output xt+1 . To steer the outputs of an LLM with CLAS, we first extract block-specific steering vectors {dℓ}Lℓ=1 and then learn block-specific sensing vectors 

{cℓ}Lℓ=1 to steer each activation hℓ,t as follows: ˜hℓ,t = hℓ,t + ( cℓ · [hℓ,t 1]) dℓ. Extracting the steering vectors with CLAS follows the same procedure as existing activation steering methods [5]. A dataset of prompts and concept labels is used to train an activation probe at each block, and a direction is extracted from each probe which serves as the steering vector dℓ. This process is described in Section 2.1. Learning the sensing vectors for CLAS, which are used to compute the context-dependent steering coefficients, differs from tuning the fixed steering coefficients in standard activation steering methods. The block-specific sensing vectors cℓ are trained on a small dataset of prompt-completion pairs using the next-token prediction objective, avoiding the need to generate and score model outputs. This process is described in Section 2.2.

## 2.2 Computing context-dependent steering coefficients 

Our method, Contextual Linear Activation Steering (CLAS), performs steering using learned sensing vectors instead of a fixed scalar coefficient, enabling context-dependent rather than context-independent steering. In this section, we first describe how this steering is performed and then how we learn the sensing vectors. 

Contextual Linear Activation Steering (CLAS) modifies the transformer block forward pass to enable context-dependent activation steering. In each block ℓ, CLAS computes a context-dependent steering coef-ficient by projecting the activation concatenated with a constant 1, [ hℓ,t 1] ∈ Rk+1 , onto a learned sensing vector cℓ ∈ Rk+1 , which modulates the extracted steering vector dℓ. This translates to the following forward pass modification for each block: ˜hℓ,t = hℓ,t + ( cℓ · [hℓ,t 1]) dℓ (CLAS) which makes the steering coefficient αℓ,t = ( cℓ · [hℓ,t 1]) block and context-dependent. To train the sensing vectors {cℓ}Lℓ=1 , we initialize them to zero and modify the forward pass at each block using Eq. (CLAS). We then construct a dataset of n prompt-completion pairs {x(i), z(i)}ni=1 , where x(i) is the sequence of prompt tokens and z(i) is the sequence of completion tokens. # 3 Experiments 

To evaluate CLAS, we measure its performance on 11 diverse steering tasks and across four different instruction-tuned models: Qwen2.5-7B [38], Llama-3.1-70B, Llama-3.1-8B, and Llama-3.2-1B [14]. We mea-sure steering performance by (i) the success rate of steering a model to perform a target task without specifying the task in the prompt (Section 4.1), and (ii) assessing how steering a model toward a single target task interferes with its performance on other tasks (Section 4.2).

43.2 Experimental setup and baselines 

We compare CLAS to LAS, LoRA, and ReFT on all 11 steering tasks. CLAS and LAS are activation steering methods, while LoRA and ReFT are fine-tuning methods. For each task, activation steering methods use the probe dataset for steering vector extraction and the steer dataset for coefficient tuning. Fine-tuning methods use only the steer dataset to update a subset of the model parameters. 

Baselines. LAS is a context-independent activation steering method: ˜h = h + αd. The steering vector d

can be obtained in many ways [5]. Here, it is obtained from an RFM probe following [6]. LoRA [18] is a fine-tuning method that adds trainable rank-r matrices A ∈ Rr×q and B ∈ Rk×r to the frozen model weights 

W ∈ Rk×q of any MLP layer: ˜h = W x + BAx . ReFT [37] is a fine-tuning method that adds trainable rank-r matrices to the activations of any layer. Here we consider its DiReFT variation, which adds matrices 

W1, W2 ∈ Rr×k and bias b ∈ Rr to the activations: ˜h = h + W T 

> 2

(W1h + b). 

Setup. CLAS, LAS, and ReFT are applied to the residual stream activations after each LLM transformer block, while LoRA is applied to the final MLP weight W that outputs into the residual stream. For each task, LAS and CLAS use identical steering vectors {dℓ}Lℓ=1 , extracted from RFM probes trained on the corresponding probe dataset using the same process as described in Section 2.1. For LAS, α is selected via grid search to maximize the o3-mini or exact match accuracy used during evaluation on prompts in the steer dataset. For CLAS, the sensing vectors {cℓ}Lℓ=1 are learned by minimizing the next-token prediction loss on the prompt-completion pairs in the steer dataset. ReFT and LoRA use this same objective and dataset for fine-tuning. All methods use parameter-matched rank-1 adapters, with placement and rank fixed rather than tuned per method, model, or task. Exact hyperparameters for all methods are provided in Appendix F. 

CLAS vs. ReFT. CLAS shares the same functional form as rank-1 ReFT but obtains the steering di-rection ( d vs. W T 

> 2

) with a different procedure. As a result, these directions are often nearly orthogonal (Appendix E). ReFT jointly optimizes all parameters via backpropagation on prompt-completion pairs, and requires setting many additional hyperparameters prior to fine-tuning such as the number of prefix and suffix positions, the target layers, and whether to tie intervention parameters across positions [37]. In contrast, CLAS decouples these: steering vectors are first extracted using activation probes, then only the sensing vectors are learned. This decoupling makes CLAS more flexible and effective in limited supervision settings (Section 4), while preserving steering direction interpretability and concept monitoring accuracy (Section 5). # 5 Concept monitoring 

Here, we evaluate CLAS, LoRA, and ReFT on 11 concept monitoring tasks. In concept monitoring, we pass a prompt through an LLM to detect what concept or task is present in its activations. We do this by projecting the last-token prompt activation hℓ,T from a given block onto a probe direction dℓ to obtain a scalar feature aℓ = ( hℓ,T · dℓ). This is done at every block, and a separate linear regression model is trained on {a(i) 

> ℓ

}Ni=1 at each block to detect concept presence on a set of prompts. ReFT and LoRA learn low-rank matrices during fine-tuning. In the rank-1 setting, these matrices reduce to vectors ( W T 

> 2

for ReFT and B for LoRA, as defined in Section 3.2), which define directions that can be used for linear probing. In contrast, CLAS uses a steering vector at each block which is the RFM probe direction, corresponding to the principal eigenvector of the AGOP (Section 2.1). High linear probe accuracy indicates which concepts are linearly separated (i) by the ReFT and LoRA directions learned during fine-tuning and (ii) by the RFM probe directions. This motivates evaluating how directions derived from ReFT and LoRA compare to those obtained by RFM for concept monitoring. # 6 Conclusion 

Linear activation steering is a powerful approach for eliciting the capabilities of large language models. Yet despite its success, current steering methods often rely on fixed steering coefficients that do not adapt to context and that are computationally expensive to tune, limiting their reliability, precision, and scalability. In this work, we address this limitation with CLAS, which makes the steering coefficient context-dependent. Our results demonstrate that CLAS outperforms steering with a fixed coefficient and matches or exceeds fine-tuning methods such as ReFT and LoRA in limited labeled data settings. Importantly, it does so while preserving the interpretability of the activation probe directions, enabling accurate concept monitoring while making the directions more effective for steering. More broadly, these findings demonstrate that existing steering directions can become substantially more effective when modulated with context-dependent coefficients, enabling more precise, scalable, and general control of LLM behavior.

The following hyperparmaters are used for the experiments conducted in Section 4–5. 

Hyperparameters for steering vector extraction 

For each task, we pass the probe dataset prompts through an LLM, train an RFM probe at each transformer block to predict the corresponding concept label, and extract a steering vector from each probe. The RFM hyperparameter search space is the same for all tasks and transformer blocks: L (kernel bandwidth) 

∈ { 1, 1.5, 10 , 100 }, λ (regularization parameter) ∈ { 10 −3, 10 −2, 10 −1}, t (number of AGOP iterations) ∈

(0 , 10]. We select the best hyperparameter combination via grid search that maximizes the unsigned Pearson correlation between the last-token prompt residual stream activations projected onto the principal AGOP eigenvector and the concept labels on a validation set. For each block, the principal AGOP eigenvector associated with the best hyperparameter setting is used as the per-block steering vector. 

Hyperparameters for coefficient tuning and fine-tuning 

For each task, we use the prompt-completion pairs from the steer dataset for coefficient tuning and fine-tuning. For LAS, we tuned the steering coefficient α by generating a completion for each prompt in the steer dataset, using batch size 1 and limited to 1024 new tokens per prompt. Each completion is evaluated with o3-mini or exact match (see ”Steering Evaluation” for each task in Section G). We sweep over the same 20 uniformly spaced coefficients ([0 .2, 4] for Qwen, [0 .05 , 1] for Llama) for each task and select the value α that maximizes evaluator accuracy. All selected coefficients lie within the ranges defined for each model family, which differ due to activation norms. LAS is tuned exclusively using evaluator feedback and does not use the steer dataset completions. For CLAS, ReFT, and LoRA, we minimize the standard next-token prediction loss on the steer dataset so that, given a prompt, the model is steered to produce the desired completion. This is done using AdamW with learning rate 3 × 10 −3 and effective batch size 10 (batch size 1 with 10 gradient accumulation steps), for up to 8 update steps, with early stopping based on validation loss (not evaluator accuracy). The number of training and validation samples is identical for each steer dataset, and the same hyperparameters are used across all methods, models, and tasks. The only exception is the coefficient bias learning rate for CLAS, which is set to 5 × 10 −1 for Qwen and 1 × 10 −1 for Llama models. 

Hyperparameters for concept monitoring 

For each task, we pass the probe dataset prompts through an LLM and, at each transformer block, project the last-token prompt activations onto a unit-norm steering direction and train a linear regression model on the resulting projections to predict the concept label. The linear model is tuned over the regularization strengths λ ∈ { 10 −4, 10 −3, 10 −2, 10 −1, 10 0, 10 1} to maximize concept detection accuracy on the probe dataset validation set, with the best linear model selected for test set evaluation. 

# G Dataset prompts and evaluation templates 

Each task in Table 1 includes a probe dataset, a steer dataset, and a task-specific steering evaluation template. Both the probe and steer datasets are derived from an underlying source dataset. 

Probe dataset. The probe dataset is used for steering vector extraction and concept monitoring exper-iments. It is a supervised dataset of prompt-concept label pairs. It is constructed by randomly sampling prompts from an underlying source dataset and applying two templates to each prompt: a Task Prompt template that contains task-specific information and a Non-Task Prompt template that does not contain task-specific information. Prompts using the Task Prompt template are assigned a concept label of y = 1, and prompts using the Non-Task Prompt template are assigned a concept label of y = 0. 

Steer dataset. The steer dataset is used for the steering experiments. It is constructed from the same underlying source dataset as the probe dataset and uses a corresponding pair of Task and Non-Task Prompt templates. Non-Task prompts are used for coefficient tuning and fine-tuning. The associated completions are 23 taken from either the underlying source dataset or generated with o3-mini using the Task Prompt, depending on the task. This is designed to make the model respond to Non-Task prompts as if given Task prompts. 

Evaluation. To evaluate steering, we use either o3-mini as a judge or exact match, depending on the task. For judge-based evaluation, the judge receives the original source dataset prompt, the model completion, and an evaluation template, and outputs a score normalized to the range [0 , 1]. For exact match evaluation, a python program evaluates the model completion and outputs a score in {0, 1}.

Per-task descriptions: 

• Alpaca. A steering task designed to induce a model to follow instructions without including the chat template in the prompt. The source dataset is Alpaca [32], a general instruction-following dataset, and the completions for the steer dataset are taken directly from the source dataset. The corresponding probe dataset, steer dataset, and steering evaluation template are shown in Figure 4.

The corresponding probe dataset, steer dataset, and steering evaluation template are shown in Figure 5.
