## Abstract 

The deployment of automatic radiology report generator (RRG) models in clinical workflows is being hampered by the lack of factual correctness in the produced reports. Ex-isting methods to improve the report generators use align-ment approaches that require pairs of ground truth pre-ferred and dis-preferred responses. As these are not avail-able at inference time in clinical workflows, new alignment methods are needed to improve report quality at inference time. In this paper, we present a new phrase-grounded au-tomatic preference optimization (APO) alignment method which offers such improvement during inference without needing additional ground truth. Specifically, the method generates surrogate ground truth preference data for align-ment automatically from the RRG model response itself though fact-checking and LLM-prompted correction. We also develop a novel APO loss function that combines pref-erence response alignment loss with phrasal grounding loss paying attention to both the description of the finding and its image location. We show that this method of alignment, on the average, improves the report quality at inference time by 30-40% across various SOTA report generators as tested on multi-institutional chest X-ray datasets. 

## 1. Introduction 

Radiology practices are piloting automated radiology re-port generator tools for expediting and streamlining struc-tured report generation[31]. Such reporting tools have pro-gressed the most in chest X-ray radiology thanks to the availability of relatively large datasets such as MIMIC[10] and CheXpert[7] that come with their companion reports for training vision-language generative (VLM) models[1, 5, 11]. However, the results with pilots are revealing a pre-dominance of hallucinations and factual errors which have hampered their adoption. Figure 1 illustrates the errors in automatically generated reports from SOTA RRG models. Both errors in finding identity, i.e. presence or absence of findings, and their localization can be seen. Ideally, improving the performance of these models re-quires parameter updates through fine-tuning as done in alignment approaches such as DPO[6, 24, 26, 43], proxi-mal policy optimization (PPO)[42, 44] or GRPO[30] during training time. However, these methods require ground truth preference response datasets which are not available in clin-ical deployment scenarios. Other inference time alignment methods that don’t update model weights but work by ad-justing prompts, sampling strategies, or decoding rules[2] have not been adapted for clinical settings and are not yet turn-key enough for deployment in clinical workflows. Re-cently, critique models called fact-checking (FC) models have emerged that can phrasally ground errors in the gen-erated reports of frozen RRG models[15, 18] at inference time. These FC models are agnostic to specific RRG mod-els but can only signal errors rather than improve the re-port generator itself. Thus, it is important to investigate ap-proaches that can improve the performance of RRG mod-els at inference time by lightweight updating of the weights without requiring additional ground truth data. In this paper, we present a new phrase-grounded au-tomatic preference optimization (APO) alignment method to improve the performance of radiology report generation (RRG) models at inference time. In our method, no ad-ditional ground truth generation effort is needed to create preference datasets as these are self-generated in the align-ment process. Specifically, the ground truth preference data for alignment is derived automatically from the RRG model response through fact-checking and LLM-prompted cor-rection. We also develop a novel APO loss function that combines preference response alignment loss with phrasal grounding loss paying attention to both the description of the finding and its image location. We show that this method of alignment, on the average, improves the report quality at inference time by 30-40% across various SOTA report gen-erators as tested on multi-institutional chest X-ray datasets. Thus, our paper makes the following novel contributions. • We develop a new automatic preference optimization (APO) method for use at inference time based on a fact-checking model for preference response generation. ## 2. Related work 

While there is considerable work in chest x-ray radiology report generation literature[1, 3, 4, 12, 22, 27, 28, 31], pa-pers focusing on improving the quality of radiology re-port generation have only recently been emerging. These are alignment methods based on direct policy optimiza-tion (DPO)[6, 24, 26, 43], proximal policy optimization (PPO)[42, 44] and GRPO[30]. DPO in particular has been attractive for training smaller model as it does not require the generation of a reward model and can directly use the preference dataset. However, these methods still require manual construction of ground truth training data consisting of preference yw and dis-preference yl responses. Methods for improving the report quality at inference time have also been developed that modify how the model is used at infer-ence (runtime) by adjusting prompts, sampling strategies, or decoding rules [2]. However, these methods do not alter the parameters of the model or perform gradient updates, and have not been adapted yet for clinically meaningful uses. With companion radiology reports present for chest X-ray imaging, DPO approaches have emerged for RRG mod-els that derive the dis-preference responses automatically from ground truth reports. The sampling strategies in-clude random draws[29] and semantic search[13]. How-ever, when no ground truth data is available, these ap-proaches cannot be used. Simple fact-checking methods have also been used at clinical inference time with static quality improvement achieved by editing the produced report and simply remov-ing the sentence with error completely[15]. These are un-able to handle sentences with multiple findings, when only some of them are erroneous. Recently, a phrase-grounded fact-checking (FC) model was developed to detect finding identity and location errors in generated reports[18] during clinical inference, but no direct improvement of the report generator was attempted. 

## 3. Automatic Preference Optimization (APO) 

The key idea of automatic preference optimization is to self-generate preferred and dis-preferred samples through a sur-rogate ground truth generation process. We call the original RRG model the reference model, and its policy as the ref-erence policy πref which represents the probabilities that the reference model assigns to a textual response which in our case is a radiology report r for an image I. Similarly, the model being aligned through parameter updates during inference is denoted πθ . In addition to text, we phrasally-ground the findings described in the preferred 28255 response as it is important to have consistency between the visual location of the finding in the image with its descrip-tion in the generated reports. Training procedure :The training procedure for APO is similar to DPO except that this is during inference stage where there is no ground truth and the preference responses are self generated along with the phrasal grounding information. Given any RRG model that is already pre-trained for chest X-ray report gen-eration, we apply the frozen RRG model to test images at inference time and save the weights as πref (yl|x) as the dis-preferred responses. We then generate the preferred re-sponses πref (yw|x) and phrasal grounding overlap proba-bilities Oref from corrected reports using the FC model. During subsequent fine-tuning, this process is repeated for the RRG model being trained obtaining the πθ (yw|x) and phrasal overlap probabilities Oθ after each parameter up-date of the RRG model on test images. Most of the RRG models fit within a single GPU’s mem-ory and can be fine-tuned within 10 epochs over the test datasets. For our experiments, all models needed for align-ment (RRG model, FC models and corrective LLM) were loaded into a single NVIDIA A100 GPU with 40GB of memory, and batch size of 32 was used for alignment. The gradient updates used AdamW optimizer with a cosine an-nealing learning rate scheduler with a maximum learning rate of 1e-5 and 50 steps for warm up. 28256 Figure 2. Illustration of APO training. ## 4. Preference response generation 

The overall process of obtaining the preferred response for training under APO is illustrated in Figure 2. The report produced by an RRG model being trained πθ is considered the dis-preferred response yl. It is pre-processed to extract the findings Fl = Nl|Cl and their indicated anatomical im-age locations ll from the textual description of the finding 

Fl in yl. The FC model then verifies these findings in the image to predict a veracity label El for the finding Fl and a predicted location lw. The spatial overlap error between the predicted and indicated location along with the veracity indicator El is used to generate the corrected or preferred response yw and the overlap probabilities Oθ for the RRG model being trained, and similarly for the reference model. We leverage our prior work on the pre-processing steps, FC model development[18], and static report correction[16] for our preferred response generation. Here we describe them briefly for completeness. More details can be found in [18] and [16] . 

4.1. Pre-processing 

The two pre-processing steps used in our approach are find-ing extraction from sentences, and anatomical localization. Specifically, we used the finding extraction algorithm re-ported in [31] to extract findings in a structured form called FFL (fine-grained finding labels) which breaks down a find-ing description into a normalized vocabulary-based core finding, its presence or absence indication, its anatomical location, laterality, etc. For example, from a sentence such as ”Pleural effusion found in the left lung.”, the correspond-ing FFL pattern would be ”anatomical finding |yes |pleural effusion |lung |left”. The FFL extraction algorithm reported in [31] had a 97% accuracy and was seen as sufficient for our pre-processing. In addition to finding descriptions, we also use the anatomical location algorithm described in [37, 39] to lo-cate bounding boxes in any frontal chest X-ray image for 36 anatomical regions cataloged in the chest X-ray lexicon [32, 38]. Its accuracy was previously assessed at 0.896 pre-cision and 0.881 recall, and was used to generate the Chest ImaGenome benchmark dataset[39]. Using the above pre-processing, we can assemble the spatial location of a finding by merging the bounding boxes of the composed anatomical locations. 

4.2. Fact-checking model 

The FC model was a multi-label contrastive regression net-work consisting of a contrastive encoder feature learner fol-lowed by a regression neural network trained end-to-end us-ing the correct and incorrect pairs of images with findings and locations. The contrastive encoder was trained using su-pervised contrastive learning using a non-diagonal similar-ity matrix formulation of supervised contrastive loss func-tion unlike CLIP variants[25]. The regressor learned the association of the combined image-text features with the lo-cations of the findings in the image and the veracity label. Throughout, a supervision label of correct or incorrect as-sociation E guided the learning. More details can be found in [18]. 

4.3. Error analysis 

Using the pre-processing and the output of the fact-checking model, the nature of errors in the report generated by an RRG model can be analyzed as described in [16]. Given an indicated finding Fi extracted from the automated report associated with a given image I, the FC model pre-dicts a location lp =< x p, y p, w p, h p > and a veracity indi-cator Ep. Using the finding localization algorithm of Sec-tion 4.1, the indicated location li =< x i, y i, w i, h i > can also be found. The corrective action rules were formed both using the predicted veracity indicator Ep and the lack of spatial over-lap between li and lp measured through IOU as 

IOU pi = 1 − IOU pi = 1 − |lp ∩ li||lp ∪ li| (10) The error analyss quantized the values of lp, E p, F i, IOU pi 

into ranges to arrive at 16 possible combinations of which only 6 correspond to consistent output from the FC model. 28257 These were manually analyzed to arrive at an interpre-tation and a corrective action, from which 5 unique prompt templates were designed for correction as shown in their Table 1 of [16] and reproduced in Table 1 for convenience. The remaining combinations were potential inconsistency cases in the prediction of the FC model itself. While the FC model performed well across the datasets tested, a potential error in the FC model could potentially worsen the report quality. Fortunately, because we regressed on both loca-tion and veracity, we can spot such inconsistencies through these combinations to conservatively disable any corrective action. For example, a combination of (Lp = 0 , E p =0, IOU pi ≤ Γ) for an absent finding Fi is a case where either the location prediction or the veracity indicator is in-correct. 

4.4. LLM-based sentence correction 

By instantiating the relevant prompt template for each find-ing error, we can correct the sentences using LLMs. Specif-ically, given the finding patterns and sentences extracted from automated reports, instances of prompts are obtained using the prompt templates indicated in Column 6 Table 1. These are then given to a large language model to initi-ate sentence modification and correction. Any well-trained LLM would be sufficient for our purpose as these days, they can all be instruction-tuned for sentence correction, and their choice mainly effects the readability of the re-port rather than the finding and its description, since the template constrains what can be replaced. Nevertheless, we used Llama3.2 as it was freely available and fit within the GPU size of our server. Since the average sentence in a re-port has 13-15 words, and sentence correction task is fairly deterministic, we used 400 token limit with a temperature of 0 for sentence correction. The sentence returned by the LLM are then assembled to form the corrected report. The resulting report is treated as the preferred response ground truth report for purposes of automatic preference optimiza-tion (APO) during inference as indicated in Section 3. Figure 3 illustrates the report correction possible using the processing pipelines of FC model-based prediction and dynamic template instantiation for LLM-prompted report correction. It shows a sample error case where the name of the finding in the report sentence is correct but its indicated location is wrong. As can be seen, the final corrected sen-tence is properly formatted language-wise, and reflects the intended corrective action. We chose the FC model as its error detection performance showed a strong concordance correlation coefficient of 0.997 with real ground truth-based verification indicating it as a potential source for generating surrogate ground truth during clinical inference[18]. 

## 5. Results 

We now report our evaluation of the APO method for im-proving the report quality of RRG models. Our approach is agnostic to the choice of the RRG model and works with a frozen FC model and report correction LLM which are also agnostic to RRG models. 

Datasets :We selected several publicly available multi-institutional datasets of chest X-ray images annotated for findings and their locations as summarized in Table 2. All datasets were clinician validated and vetted for bias and fairness during their IRB approval. The FC model we used was trained on the 24 million image-finding pairs RadCheck dataset as described in [18] which was derived from the ChestIm-aGenome Silver dataset[40] which in turn was derived from MIMIC-CXR[9]. All datasets listed in Table 2 provided findings and locations, and their testing partitions were used for the APO alignment experiments. For one of the compar-ison studies, we also added the ChexPert dataset. 

Report generators :We also selected 7 SOTA automated report generators whose Github code was freely available and were pub-lished at least three months prior to this submission. These included MAIRA-2[1], ChexRepair[27], RGRG[33], XrayGPT[34], R2GenGPT[36], CV2DistillGPT2[20] and our in-house hospital implementation of GPT-4 (GPT4-inhouse). The report generation methods included the lat-est LLava-style VLMs, with varying capabilities including phrasal ground (RGRG), multi-view and longitudinal infor-mation handling (MAIRA-2), and distillation-based mod-els. 

Report correction evaluation :We evaluated the LLM performance for this constrained editing by randomly drawing 1000 sentences from the re-ports generated by various RRG models prior to the APO tuning, and correcting them using the FC model and LLM correction process. The corrected sentences were then eval-uated for errors in grammar and clinical correctness by a ra-diologist. The resulting performance is shown in Table 3. As can be seen, the LLM rarely makes grammatical er-rors, and the number of clinical errors is small, primarily in sentences with more than one finding where the location-related adjective (e.g. left) is applicable to more than one finding. This is the reason, we kept the phrasal grounding loss term in our APO formulation to ensure that both finding name and location are captured in the corrective process. 

Report quality improvement :The APO alignment process improves the quality of re-port beyond simple report correction using the FC model and corrective LLM. Report quality improvement across datasets :We also evaluate the generalization of the report quality im-provement performance across multiple datasets and report generators. Since some of the metrics (BLEU, SBERT) needed full ground truth reports which were not available for all datasets, we focused the evaluation using the RQ score as it utilized the finding as well as location informa-tion in the provided ground truth across datasets. The re-sulting performance of the 7 report generators tested across 4 datasets is shown in Table 6. Since RQ score recorded agreement in the finding identity and spatial overlap in the locations of findings, it was able to capture the combined improvement in report quality well across all datasets for all report generators tested, averaging an improvement around 22.7% across the datasets as shown in that table. 

Comparison and Ablation studies :To our knowledge, there are no methods that do preference alignment without ground truth. Hence we compared to two nearest approaches, namely, direct performance improve-ment through static correction and to DPO. Since these are elements used in our method, these also constitute our abla-tion studies. While it is possible to improve performance purely through the report correction process using FC model and LLM in a static way, these corrections would only remove or change findings already reported by the RRG model. By using APO, we expect that new findings previously missed by the RRG model can now be reported due to the parame-ter updates. This was seen in Figure 4. To evaluate the report quality improvement with and without APO alignment, we recorded only the preferred re-sponses P generated on the reference version of each RRG model and compared it to ground truth using various met-rics. Table 4 shows the results in columns labeled (P,G). By comparing these with columns labeled (C,G) for APO alignment-based report generation, we see that the improve-ment obtained by APO is more than that can be achieved us-ing report correction alone which on the average is 26.52% from column (P,G) across the metrics in comparison to 39.98% with (C,G). Finally, we conducted an ablation study to evaluate the effect of phrasal ground loss on the quality of report gen-eration during the APO policy updates. Thus, instead of adding the phrasal grounding loss as in Equation 9, we re-tained the standard DPO policy using the same preference response generation with the FC model and LLM correc-tion (Equation 8). Comparison with reinforcement learning We also com-pared the performance to GRPO alignment using RQ eval-uation metric. In our implementation of vanilla GRPO[30], the RQ evaluation score itself was used as a reward func-tion to score the multiple sample reports generated per im-age and compared against the output of the FC model. The result is shown as column (R,G) in Table 6. Comparing columns (R,G) for GRPO to the column (A,G), it can be seen that the performance with GRPO is inconsistent at best and overall improvement is under 5%. In particular, the less developed models (e.g. XrayGPT) can perform worse with alignment whereas other well-developed models such as Maira-2 can at best perform the same or slightly better. This result is understandable since most RRG report models for clinical use are expected to produce stable reports upon repeated invocation on the same image in terms of findings even if they have inaccuracies and need corrections. 

Limitations :Although our work is the first to date to improve radiol-ogy reports using automatic policy optimization during in-ference, it does have limitations. Due to limitations of the FC model, it does not address severity and measurement er-rors relating to findings. Secondly, while we have shown quality improvements across the board for errors, we have not separated the relative contribution of these errors from pre-processing and FC model prediction versus the RRG model output. Finally, the phrasal grounding is currently using bounding boxes which only approximately localize a finding. The full-fledged segmentation of findings may lead to better results. Due to space limitations, we have not reported here the performance of our model in terms of the type of finding errors and their criticality. Finally, the LLM-based report correction can be continually improved with the design of more specific prompts per finding further specializing the templates. Since their output is not guaran-teed to be the same in each run, variability could still exist in the reports. These issues will be addressed in future work. 

## 6. Conclusions 

In this paper, we have presented a novel method of im-proving the quality of generative AI reports for chest X-rays by using APO training at inference time. The pre-ferred responses are automatically generated by using a fact-checking model to detect and analyze errors and using relevant instructional prompts for correction with LLMs. Working across data sets and report generators, we have shown an average improvement in report quality ranging from 17-39% across report generators. 28261 References
