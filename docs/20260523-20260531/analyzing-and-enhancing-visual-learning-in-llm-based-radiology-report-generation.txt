Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) have recently advanced ra-diology report generation (RRG) by improving fluency and contextual reasoning. However, achieving clinically reli-able generation remains challenging, as current systems of-ten produce fluent yet clinically inaccurate reports. To un-derstand this limitation, we present a systematic mechanis-tic analysis of the training dynamics in LLM-based RRG and reveal two fundamental bottlenecks in visual seman-tic learning: (i) causal attention leaves visual tokens text-ignorant during forward decoding, and (ii) the visual en-coder receives only indirect gradients from the language modeling objective during backpropagation. Motivated by these findings, we introduce a training-time semantic en-hancement framework that strengthens visual–textual inter-action through two complementary modules: semantic in-jection, which transfers diagnostic semantics from reports into visual representations, and semantic alignment, which enforces consistency between visual features and LLM-interpretable text embeddings. Together, these modules mit-igate the text-ignorant and indirect-supervision issues iden-tified in our analysis, enhancing visual semantic learning and clinical accuracy without modifying inference architec-ture. Experiments on IU-Xray and MIMIC-CXR demon-strate improvements in both linguistic quality and clinical accuracy, while cross-domain evaluation on MS-COCO in-dicates the potential generality of our framework. 

1. Introduction 

Automated radiology report generation (RRG)—a long-standing goal in medical vision–language research—aims to translate subtle visual evidence in medical images into long-form diagnostic narratives. Early approaches [6, 21, * Corresponding author. 45] established the feasibility of automatically describ-ing radiological findings, and recent advances have intro-duced large language models (LLMs) as powerful decoders within an end-to-end vision-to-text paradigm, where a vi-sual encoder—often coupled with a lightweight projec-tor—extracts image representations that are subsequently interpreted by the LLM to generate the report text [19, 39, 44]. The integration of LLMs into the RRG framework has rapidly become a key trend in this field, as highlighted by several recent surveys [22, 27, 31, 36]. However, despite their enhanced linguistic fluency and contextual reasoning, these models still exhibit limited clin-ical accuracy, suggesting that they may depend excessively on linguistic priors rather than grounding their generation in visual evidence. As a result, the produced reports often read fluently yet lack factual correspondence to the underlying medical images. Although recent efforts—such as object detection–based feature extraction [10, 32], external knowl-edge incorporation [14, 17, 30], and auxiliary module en-hancement [12, 40]—have enhanced clinical accuracy, they tend to introduce additional architectural complexity and inference overhead. More importantly, none of the exist-ing studies systematically investigate why LLM-based RRG models struggle with clinical accuracy, leaving their funda-mental limitations insufficiently understood. This raises a question: what mechanisms cause such models to under-perform in visual reasoning? As a step toward addressing this question, we conduct a systematic mechanistic analysis of the training dynamics in LLM-based RRG, examining both the forward and back-ward processes. Our analysis reveals two inherent limita-tions: (i) in the forward pass, causal attention prevents vi-sual tokens from accessing report tokens, leaving them text-ignorant during decoding; and (ii) in the backward pass, the visual encoder receives only indirect supervision domi-nated by the language modeling objective, since the cross-entropy (CE) loss applies exclusively to predicted report to-This CVPR Findings paper is the Open Access version, provided by the Computer Vision Foundation. Together, these factors constrain effective visual se-mantic learning and ultimately hinder clinical accuracy. Motivated by these insights, we propose a semantic en-hancement framework to strengthen visual–textual interac-tion in LLM-based RRG, consisting of two complementary modules: (1) Semantic Injection module strategically po-sitions ground-truth report tokens before visual tokens and leverages the LLM decoder to transfer report semantics into the visual representations, forming a text-to-vision learn-ing branch. The enriched visual representations then pro-vide direct supervision for the visual encoder, guiding it to learn diagnostically meaningful features. (2) Semantic Alignment module enforces consistency between visual fea-tures and textual representations within a shared space, en-suring the visual encoder captures semantics interpretable by the LLM. Together, these modules enhance visual se-mantic learning, addressing the text-ignorant and indirect -supervision limitations identified in our analysis and im-proving clinical reliability of the generated reports. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: 1. Mechanistic Analysis . We present a dual-perspective analysis of training dynamics in LLM-based RRG, reveal-ing two limitations constraining visual semantic learning: (i) causal attention renders visual tokens text-ignorant in the forward process, and (ii) the visual encoder receives indirect 

supervision from the CE loss in the backward process. 2. Enhancement Framework . We design a training-time framework aimed at reinforcing visual semantic learning. It introduces two complementary modules—semantic injec-tion and semantic alignment—that enrich the supervision of the visual encoder and align visual representations with re-port semantics. These modules are only used during train-ing, adding no extra inference overhead. 3. Extensive validation . Experiments on two RRG datasets demonstrate substantial improvements in both linguistic quality and clinical accuracy, while cross-domain evalua-tion on image captioning indicates potential cross-domain applicability of our framework. 

2. Related Work 2.1. LLM-based RRG 

Recent large language models (LLMs) have shown strong capabilities in medical text generation, bringing new oppor-tunities for improving radiology report generation (RRG). For example, RAG [30] formulates RRG as a retrieval-augmented generation task, using GPT-3.5/4 to synthesize reports from a retrieved corpus. R2GenGPT [39] adopts a frozen LLM and maps visual features into its embedding space for report generation, which enhances linguistic flu-ency but still struggles with clinical accuracy. MedXChat [44] and LLM-CXR [19] go a step further by instruction-tuning LLMs to handle multiple tasks such as image-to-text generation, visual question answering (VQA), and text-to-image synthesis in medical domains. MambaXray [37] is a large model for RRG, trained using a multistage strat-egy. However, most of these approaches focus on improv-ing generation capability or linguistic richness, without ex-amining the underlying training dynamics that limit visual learning in LLM-based RRG. 

2.2. Visual Learning in RRG 

Recent studies increasingly focus on enhancing visual learning to ensure that visual evidence contributes more ef-fectively to RRG. KCAP [13] enriches visual and textual modalities through knowledge-guided fusion. FITA [43] refines visual–text correspondence using saliency-guided supervision, while Liu et al. [20] employ sentence-level contrastive learning to strengthen image representations. RadAlign [12] improves visual learning by aligning image features with concept embeddings, enabling the encoder to capture diagnostically meaningful semantics before report generation. MedM2G [47] facilitates cross-modal align-ment transfer through a unified framework representing CT, X-ray, and report data within a shared latent space. Wang et al. [40] further promote diagnostic feature learn-ing through cooperative task optimization, jointly training disease classification and report generation. Different from existing studies, our framework is derived from a mechanistic analysis of training dynamics in LLM-based RRG, which guides the design of two complementary modules. While the semantic alignment module adopts a contrastive mechanism, it differs from conventional align-ment strategies by deriving textual features from the frozen LLM embedding layer, which improves the compatibility between visual representations and the LLM. 3.3. Semantic Enhancement Framework 

From the above analysis, the insufficient visual learning in LLM-based RRG mainly stems from text-ignorant vision tokens and indirect visual supervision. To address these is-sues, we introduce a semantic enhancement framework that enriches visual learning through two training-time mod-ules: Semantic Injection , which transfers report semantics to vision tokens for direct supervision, and Semantic Align-ment , which enforces visual–textual consistency within the LLM’s embedding space. 

Semantic Injection. To overcome the text-ignorant nature of vision tokens in LLM-based RRG branch, we introduce Semantic Injection (SI), which transfers report semantics into vision representations through the causal attention of the frozen LLM, as illustrated at the top of Fig. 2. Instead of altering the autoregressive structure, we reposition the re-port tokens before the image tokens during training, form-ing a text-to-vision branch where preceding report embed-dings naturally inject semantics into subsequent vision po-sitions under the causal attention mechanism. A key design choice is to equip the textual side with a trainable text en-coder g t (·) and projector Pt (·),

E t = Pt (g t (R)) , E t 2 RL⇥d L ,

rather than directly using lookup embeddings of the LLM. Lookup embeddings are discrete and largely fixed, and thus 9330 provide no learnable interface to select and shape clinically salient semantics from R for guiding vision. In contrast,

Implementation. We adopt R2GenGPT [39] as the base RRG system, which uses a Swin Transformer [23] as vi-sual encoder and a frozen LLaMA2 [33] as the LLM de-coder. All models are trained with AdamW and mixed precision on 2 ⇥A100 GPUs. Ad-ditional hyperparameter and training details are provided in Appendix B.1.

Ablation Study. Table 3 analyzes the contributions of SI and SA and their internal components on the frontal-view MIMIC-CXR subset with region–sentence labels (row 1: R2GenGPT baseline). 

Effect of SI. Rows 2–4 focus on SI. Using only the LLM decoder without a trainable text encoder (row 2) leads to severe degradation (BLEU-4 = 0.031, RadCliQ = 1.827), showing that lookup embeddings alone are insufficient to drive semantic supervision. Using only the text encoder (row 3) yields performance similar to the baseline, indicat-ing that text features must be propagated through the LLM to influence vision tokens. Combining a trainable text en-coder and the LLM decoder (row 4) produces clear gains (BLEU-4: 0.128 !0.137, ROUGE-L: 0.293 !0.315, F1-RadGraph: 0.192 !0.208), confirming that SI relies on the synergy between these two components. 

Effect of SA. Rows 5–7 examine SA. Global alignment alone (row 5) improves both NLG and CE metrics over the baseline, while local alignment alone (row 6) yields further gains, especially on CE metrics (F1-RadGraph = 0.204). Using both global and local alignment (row 7) gives the best performance among SA-only variants, supporting the view that holistic CXR–report alignment and region–sentence alignment are complementary. 

Full Model. Rows 8–10 combine SI and SA. Adding SA on top of SI yields consistent improvements, and en-abling both global and local alignment (row 10) achieves the overall best results across all metrics. This shows that SI and SA provide complementary supervision: SI injects report semantics into visual tokens through the LLM, while SA enforces multi-level consistency between visual features and LLM-based textual embeddings. Overall, the ablations indicate that both SI and SA are necessary for realizing the full benefit of the proposed framework. 

Generalization across LLMs. To assess robustness across different LLM decoders, we instantiate our framework with OpenChat3.5-7B [35], Mistral-7B [16], and LLaMA2-7B [33]. Similar trends are observed for Mistral-7B and LLaMA2-7B. These results suggest that the SI+SA framework is com-patible with diverse LLM architectures and can serve as a plug-in strategy to improve LLM-based RRG.

5. Conclusion 

We analyzed the training dynamics of LLM-based RRG and identified two fundamental issues: vision tokens are text-ignorant under causal attention, and the visual encoder is trained only via indirect language supervision. To ad-dress these, we proposed a semantic enhancement frame-work comprising Semantic Injection (SI) and Semantic Alignment (SA), which enrich visual representations and strengthen visual–textual consistency without affecting in-ference efficiency. Experiments on MIMIC-CXR, IU-Xray, multiple LLM backbones, and MS-COCO show consistent gains in both NLG and CE metrics. These findings high-light the importance of improving visual–textual interaction for building more reliable LLM-based RRG systems. 9334 Acknowledgment
