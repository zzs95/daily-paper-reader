## Abstract 

Vision-language models (VLMs) have demonstrated sub-stantial promise in medical image segmentation by utiliz-ing radiology reports as prompts to segment regions of in-terest. However, VLM deployment in clinical settings is challenged by two intertwined issues: i) demographic bias, where performance varies across demographic groups, and ii) prompt sensitivity, where semantically similar prompts yield inconsistent outputs. These challenges are intercon-nected; demographic underrepresentation can worsen a model’s sensitivity to prompts, and prompt instability can more heavily affect certain demographic groups. In this study, we present FairVLM, a unified framework that ad-dresses both demographic disparity and prompt sensitiv-ity in VLMs. FairVLM integrates three key components: (1) Semantic-Retaining Counterfactual Prompting (SRCP), which generates clinically consistent and diverse prompt variations via large language models; (2) Demographic-Aware Feature Normalization (DAFN), a lightweight mod-ule that mitigates latent representation bias across demo-graphic groups; and (3) a Fairness-Calibrated Loss (FCL) that explicitly penalizes performance disparities while en-couraging prompt consistency. Extensive evaluations on the Harvard-FairSeg dataset show that FairVLM signifi-cantly improves equity-scaled segmentation. It also re-duces demographic disparity (DI) by over 65% and rel-ative performance gap (RPG) by over 60%, while main-taining or boosting overall accuracy. FairVLM is robust to prompt changes, with less than 0.5% performance drop across varied prompts, and also generalizes well on unseen datasets. These findings present FairVLM as a new state-of-the-art, robust, and adaptable framework for a fair, prompt-invariant vision-language model. Code and data are avail-able at https://github.com/Rahman-Motiur/FairVLM.

mechanism that generates clinically coherent prompt variations using large language models (LLM), (ii) 

Demographic-Aware Feature Normalization (DAFN) that aligns latent feature distributions across subgroups, and (iii) a Fairness-Calibrated Loss (FCL) that penalizes perfor-mance disparities during training. These modules collec-tively ensure that FairVLM provides equitable and prompt-invariant predictions without sacrificing segmentation accu-racy. 

Contributions: The major contributions of this work are: • We introduce a prompt counterfactual generation strategy (SRCP) using LLMs to improve robustness to linguistic variability while preserving clinical semantics. • We propose a lightweight demographic normalization module (DAFN) to reduce representation disparities across protected groups. • We design a fairness-calibrated loss (FCL) that explicitly penalizes inter-group performance gaps and encourages equitable segmentation quality. • We conduct extensive evaluations on the Harvard-FairSeg [31] dataset and out-of-distribution settings with en-tirely different prompts and/or entirely different datasets, demonstrating FairVLM’s effectiveness across both fair-ness and accuracy metrics. 

Positioning: Previous research has focused on either de-mographic fairness or prompt robustness, but rarely both together. For instance, GroupDRO [27] and FEBS [31] improve fairness but ignore prompt variation, while CoOp [44], CoCoOp [43], and PromptSmooth [8] boost robust-ness against variant prompts but overlook subgroup dispar-ities. Adversarial de-biasing [21] has been used in uni-modal settings but often reduces performance in multimodal VLMs. In contrast, FairVLM addresses all these concerns, offering a comprehensive approach for equitable, prompt-invariant vision-language models. 

## 2. Related Works 

This section reviews recent literature motivating our work, focusing on two key challenges: fairness in VLMs and their sensitivity to prompt variations. 

2.1. Fairness in Vision-Language Models 

Fairness in VLMs is vital as they are used in sensitive ar-eas like healthcare, where biases can cause harm. Con-cerns arise from imbalanced training data, spurious correla-tions, and societal biases in large datasets [35]. Models like contrastive language-image pre-training (CLIP) [24], medi-cal CLIP (MedCLIP) [36], segment anything model(SAM) [13], and Customized SAM (SAMed) [41] have strong zero-shot abilities but are vulnerable to biases related to gender, race, and age [29, 30]. They can unfairly associate certain jobs or diseases with specific groups, leading to unequal performance. Early efforts to address fairness in VLMs borrowed from unimodal de-biasing methods, such as adversarial training [35], latent space perturbations [25], and balanced data aug-mentation [30]. The De-biasing with Additive Residuals (DeAR) [29] method adjusts visual embeddings to suppress protected attribute information while preserving relevant features. Similarly, FairDeDup [30] suggested fairness-aware semantic de-duplication during data pre-processing, retaining underrepresented subgroups when pruning sam-ples. Other methods examine fairness through representa-tion learning. Ramaswamy et al. [25] used closed-form la-tent space manipulations to disentangle protected and target attributes, while D’Inca et al. [4] applied vision-language data augmentation to enhance demographic diversity dur-ing training. More recent work, Fair Error-Bound Scal-7451 Figure 2. 2.2. Prompt Sensitivity in Vision-Language Models 

Prompt sensitivity presents a major obstacle for implement-ing VLMs in critical areas like clinical imaging, where vari-ability in natural language is prevalent among users and re-ports. Minor syntactic or semantic changes in prompts can lead to significant model prediction shifts [15, 35]. Prompt learning methods like CoOp [44] and CoCoOp [43] replace templates with learnable embeddings to stabi-lize performance, but compromise interpretability and may not generalize well with limited data. Another work, called MVP (modeling variants of prompt) [17], decouples tem-plates from class semantics using variational autoencoders to improve robustness. Historical prompt calibration aids knowledge transfer across tasks [10], and prompt-based memory banks support dynamic updates to prevent forget-ting [34]. Adversarial prompt perturbations offer new ro-bustness avenues. A few-shot adversarial prompt learn-ing method was developed in [45], to enhance cross-modal alignment with limited data. In the medical imaging do-main, PromptSmooth [8], a framework combining prompt learning with randomized smoothing, was proposed to guar-antee robustness against adversarial attacks in Med-VLMs. While previous research has separately examined fair-ness and prompt robustness, there is considerable potential for further contributions. Our FairVLM framework extends by addressing both demographic fairness and prompt sensi-tivity together within a unified segmentation framework.

Let  x ∈ X  denote a medical image,  y ∈ Y  the correspond-ing pixel-level ground truth segmentation mask, and  p ∈ P

Our framework, FairVLM , improves fairness in vision-language models, as illustrated in Figure 2. ## 4. Experiments 

4.1. Datasets 

We use the Harvard-FairSeg dataset [31], which is consisted of 10,000 Scanning Laser Ophthalmoscopy (SLO) fundus images with expert-verified optic cup and rim masks, along with curator- and ChatGPT-generated medical notes. The dataset contains five demographic attributes, but we use four: sex, race, ethnicity, and language. We adopt the curator-defined test split, allocate 10% of the training data for validation, and use the validation set only for model se-lection, early stopping, and hyperparameter tuning. Final results are reported on the held-out test set, unseen during training. See the dataset details in Appendix A. 

4.2. Evaluation Metrics 

We evaluate performance using Dice and Intersection over Union (IoU) metrics (in %). To ensure fairness, we apply equity-scaled (ES) metrics (ES-Dice, ES-IoU) in %, which address group disparities. We also calculate Standard De-viation (STD), Disparity Index (DI), and Relative Perfor-mance Gap (as RPG %) to measure segmentation dispar-ity across groups. These metrics capture both accuracy and fairness in model evaluation. See details in Appendix B. 

4.3. Training Parameters 

For all compared segmentation models, we used the same training configurations. The models were trained for up to 300 epochs, with early stopping based on the validation Dice score. We set the early stopping patience to 30 epochs and required a minimum improvement of 0.05. Optimiza-tion was performed using the AdamW optimizer with expo-nential learning rate decay, a learning rate of 0.001, weight decay of 1 × 10 −4, and momentum of 0.9. All models were trained with batch size of 16.

RQ4: How does FairVLM perform on out-of-distribution data? To assess FairVLM’s robustness in out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios, we perform two types of tests: (i) generalization across different prompt styles (manually written versus GPT-generated), and (ii) cross-dataset generalization from Harvard-FairSeg to two external medical image segmentation datasets, MosMedData+ [22] and QaTa-COV19 [3]. For prompt-style generalization, Table 5 shows that FairVLM (SAMed) maintains strong and consistent seg-mentation performance across prompt variations. A simi-lar trend is observed in the reverse setting (GPT-generated 

→ manually written). FairVLM (LViT) also exhibits com-parable robustness, maintaining both accuracy and fairness across prompt domains. For external domain generalization, FairVLM (SAMed) improves equitable segmentation scores (ES-Dice and ES-IoU) over the baseline by 1.5–2% on both MosMedData+ and QaTa-COV19 datasets, demonstrating enhanced fair-ness across domains (see Table 6). However, it shows a slight drop (1–2%) in overall segmentation accuracy (Dice and IoU) compared to the baseline. Similar trends hold for the LViT backbone, where FairVLM improves fairness-specific performance but lags slightly in raw segmentation accuracy. These results highlight FairVLM’s effectiveness in promoting fairness-aware generalization, even under var-ious data domains. To answer RQ4: Yes, these findings show that FairVLM is resilient to linguistic variations in prompts, maintaining both accuracy and fairness. They also demonstrate FairVLM’s capacity to generalize across datasets with different population characteristics and imag-ing conditions while achieving equitable performance. 

## 5. Conclusion 

We presented FairVLM, a unified and model-agnostic framework for medical vision-language segmentation that jointly mitigates demographic bias and prompt sensitivity; two critical challenges limiting VLM reliability in clini-cal settings. FairVLM combines semantic counterfactual prompting, demographic-normalized representation learn-ing, and fairness-calibrated loss optimization into a unified pipeline that is adaptable to existing architectures. Exten-sive evaluations demonstrate that FairVLM significantly re-duces disparities in underrepresented demographic groups and enhances robustness to prompt variations without sac-rificing segmentation accuracy. Consistent improvements across fairness metrics, prompt variations, and external datasets emphasize FairVLM’s strong generalization abil-ity.
