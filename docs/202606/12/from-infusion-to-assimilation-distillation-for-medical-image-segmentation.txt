# Abstract 

Although foundation models (e.g. SAM) perform remark-ably in medical image segmentation, its high computational complexity limits deployment. Knowledge distillation (KD) allows lightweight models to inherit the representational capabilities of large models, thereby mitigating this issue. Existing KD methods enhance student performance, but due to teacher-student different feature advantages, they neglect to internalize and integrate student’s semantic information adaptively after knowledge transfer, causing poor knowl-edge assimilation and limiting gains and generalization. To address this limitation, we propose a novel medical im-age segmentation framework, which is infusion to assimila-tion distillation (IAD). In Knowledge Infusion Stage (KIS), to semantically align teacher-student prediction distribu-tions, soft-label distillation is combined with class-weighted prototype alignment strategy. In Knowledge Assimilation Stage (KAS), to promote adaptively semantic assimilation, a contrastive semantic self-optimization strategy refines stu-dent predictions through positive and negative sample pairs and imposes reverse constraints on encoder features to en-hance semantic consistency. IAD achieves DICE gains of 4.32% on Synapse, 1.85% on ACDC, and 2.42% on Polyp datasets, and delivers an average 4.16% generaliza-tion gain on ISIC2018, PH2, BUSI, and STU datasets, out-performing mainstream KD methods. Based on these, we can infer that due to the distinctive feature learning advantages of the student model and the teacher model, mainstream KD methods likely overlook semantic internalization and inte-gration of student model after knowledge transfer , leading to limited performance gains and generalization capability. To address the above issue, we propose a novel two-stage distillation framework for medical image segmen-tation, spanning from knowledge infusion to assimilation (IAD ). IAD first injects teacher knowledge and then assim-ilates it, enabling student to align with teacher’s information while preserving its own specificity. By this way, adaptive integration of teacher and student strengths can be achieved to further optimize the prediction quality (Fig. 2( f)). Specif-ically, in Knowledge Infusion Stage (KIS) , we combine soft-label distillation with a class-weighted prototype align-ment strategy, enabling student to learn teacher prototypes, mitigate class imbalance, and align prediction semantics. Our key contributions are as follows: • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to link the poor generalization of transfer datasets to insuffi-cient knowledge internalization in existing KD methods. Accordingly, we propose IAD, a progressive knowledge infusion and assimilation framework that preserves and leverages the student’s useful capacity. • We develop a two-stage distillation strategy: KIS aligns teacher-student semantics via soft-label distillation with class-weighted prototype strategy, while KAS employs contrastive semantic self-optimization with reverse fea-ture constraints for adaptive semantic assimilation. More-over, KAS is a plug-and-play strategy that can be inte-grated into existing KD methods to boost gains. • Our method yields strong gains, improving DICE by 4.32% on Synapse [28], 1.85% on ACDC [3], and 2.42% on Polyp [2, 25, 41, 43], and enhances generalization by 4.16% on transfer datasets (ISIC2018 [9], PH2 [34], BUSI [1], STU [73]), surpassing popular KD methods. 

# 2. Related Work 

## 2.1. Medical Image Segmentation 

MIS aims to extract organs or lesions accurately. Convo-lutional Neural Network [21, 36, 38, 65, 69–71] (CNN)-based methods like ConDSeg [29], nnWNet [68], and Zig-RiR [5] address boundary ambiguity, feature discon-tinuity, and global-local modeling. The rise of Trans-former has given rise to approaches [46, 59, 61]. E.g., FocalTransNet [31], CTRCL [54], and STPNet[40], en-hance precision via feature integration, knowledge transfer, and visual-text fusion. For 3D images, methods such as SegMamba-V2 [57], MambaDiff [32], and Mamba-Sea [8] employ downsampling, global slice-aware modeling, and multi-level semantic representation to efficiently capture global dependencies [14, 55, 72]. Recently, SAM [6, 7, 26, 30, 51] has been widely applied due to its strong generaliza-tion ability, thus we use it as our teacher model. WeakMed-SAM [47] uses weak supervision, E-SAM [27] adds vision-language prompts, and CCSSP [64] applies a star-shape combination. Overall, above methods face trade-offs be-tween accuracy and number of model parameters, which IAD addresses.

## 3.1. Knowledge Infusion Stage 

Response distillation is a classic and effective distillation strategy. Existing methods [12, 19, 42, 48] mainly align pre-diction distribution of teacher but lack discriminative class-level semantic modeling. Notably, CSW-KD [37] focuses on inter-class relationships via implicit knowledge transfer, while IFVD [50] emphasizes intra-class differences at fea-ture level. Although both involve class-level semantic mod-eling, they are designed for natural scenarios rather than medical. Our approach differs significantly by explicitly handling intra-class semantics at prediction level and inte-grating soft labels to enhance student’s perception of each class semantic information, thereby achieving more com-prehensive and precise knowledge transfer while addressing semantic alignment and class imbalance. ## 3.2. Knowledge Assimilation Stage 

Student and teacher models differ in scale, leading to dis-tinct feature advantages. In relatively easily represented regions, student may even outperform teacher. Preserv-ing students’ inherent useful information during feature learning is therefore crucial. Existing response-, feature-, and hybrid-based distillation methods [16, 19, 22, 48], however, generally neglect post-transfer semantic assimila-tion. To address this, we propose KAS, which employs a contrastive semantic self-optimization strategy with reverse feature constraints to retain valuable information of student under teacher guidance. KAS is a plug-and-play strategy that can be seamlessly integrated into mainstream KD meth-ods to boost their gains (see experiments section 4.4). Specifically, we first project student encoder output Fl ∈

RB×C1×H1×W 1 to match channel dimension and spatial resolution of prediction Os ∈ RB×C×H×W , and then com-pute L2 distance loss between them. In addition, Dice loss [35] is incorporated for joint supervision to form re-verse feature constraints loss Lrfc . The L2 distance loss encourages global feature consistency, while Dice loss en-hances pixel-level semantic alignment within target regions, thereby improving discriminative capability of encoder’s semantic feature representation, as follows:

## 3.3. Training Strategy 

Our method adopts a two-stage training strategy, with the first stage being KIS. In this stage, pretrained teacher model is loaded and frozen, with its output Ot serving as distilla-tion target to guide student output Os. Meanwhile, student output Os is also supervised by ground truth. The first stage loss Lst1 is defined as follows: 

Lst1 = αLkis (( Ot, Os), (Pt, Ps)) + βLsgs (Os, Ogt ), (7) where Lsgs is segmentation loss, using CE loss and Dice loss for multi-label segmentation, and BCE with logits for binary segmentation. O gt is ground truth. α and β are hyper-parameters to balance loss terms. After completing KIS stage, training proceeds to the sec-ond stage, KAS. In this phase, we impose both Lrfc and Lcss 

on student representations to promote discriminative feature refinement and semantic assimilation. Meanwhile, supervi-sion from ground truth is preserved to ensure task fidelity. The stage loss Lst2 is formulated as: 

Lst2 = γLkas (( Fl, Os), (Os, F±)) + δLsgs (Os, Ogt ), (8) where γ and δ are hyper-parameters to balance loss terms.

# 5. Conclusion 

This paper proposes IAD, a novel two-stage KD frame-work for MIS, which adaptively internalizes and integrates student’s semantic representations after knowledge trans-fer, effectively preserving respective feature advantages of both teacher and student. The infusion stage employs a class-weighted prototype-alignment soft-label distillation to mitigate class imbalance and facilitate efficient knowl-edge transfer. The assimilation stage incorporates a con-trastive semantic self-optimization strategy with a reverse consistency constraint, promoting encoder-decoder seman-tic alignment and adaptive internalization of learned repre-sentations. Extensive experiments validate the superiority of our IAD comparing to mainstream KD methods. Model complexity comparison is not provided since all KD meth-ods use the same baseline.
