## Abstract:

The correct and timely identification of lung diseases such as COVID-19, Pneumonia and Tuberculosis is among the most crucial clinical phenomena, particularly in the constrained health care facilities. The current article recommends the adaptive attention-guided deep-texture fusion framework to be applied in classification of multi-class lung diseases as a result of relying on the chest X-ray images. The method is a combination of semantic feature extractor based on transfer learning with enhanced local texture feature based on a dynamic adjustment of feature contribution as represented by a learnable attention system. There is also the achievement of the optimal separation between the classes without affecting the computation efficiency; this is unlike the traditional concatenation methods by the proposed adaptive fusion strategy. The model is trained on categorical cross-entropy loss and Adam optimization and trained on Grad-CAM to visualize the explanations of its decisions. Experimental-based evaluation finds the overall accuracy rate to be 98.3 percent and high rates of precision and recall rates in all classes. These results substantiate the opinion that semantic and micro-texture data may be incorporated to significantly enhance the capacity to discriminate and the ascertainment of the strength, and accordingly, the suggested framework can be implemented adequately in real-time and clinically comprehendible capillary pulmonary disease screening systems. As opposed to current hybrid CNN-texture models, this paper proposes an adaptive attention-guided fusion method that dynamically integrates deep semantic and texture features to enhance multi-class classification.

### I. Introduction

The illnesses of the lungs remain one of the significant health issues that pose high morbidity, mortality, and burden of health in the world. The respiratory diseases such as COVID-19, Pneumonia and Tuberculosis (TB) continue to affect millions of people annually and their diagnosis is usually late causing serious complications and outbreak. According to the recent world health assessment, tuberculosis alone has been considered one of the primary infectious causes of death particularly in the developing and resource constrained regions. Similarly, the COVID-19 pandemic has demonstrated the urgency of the need to rapid, accurate, and scalable screening tools that can assist clinicians in the diagnosis of pulmonary anomalies when patients are many [1], [2]. Further pulmonary diagnosis is complicated by pneumonia and bacterial and viral complications since it radiographically looks like other pulmonary infections and the distinction between the two is yet to be discovered.
