Title: RadGazeGen: radiomics and gaze-guided chest X-ray generation using diffusion models

URL Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/

Markdown Content:
## [](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/)Abstract.

### Purpose

We present RadGazeGen, a framework for integrating experts’ eye gaze patterns and radiomic feature maps as controls within text-to-image diffusion models to enable high-fidelity medical image generation. Although recent text-to-image diffusion models have achieved impressive success, textual descriptions alone often fail to capture disease-specific details necessary for generating clinically accurate and anatomically faithful images.

### Approach

To address these limitations, RadGazeGen leverages radiologists’ eye gaze trajectories and radiomics feature descriptors as spatial and semantic controls in the diffusion process. Eye gaze patterns encode visuo-cognitive attention and spatial localization of subtle disease cues, whereas radiomics features capture subvisual phenotype characteristics such as texture, intensity, and shape. By combining these multimodal cues, the proposed framework guides the generative model toward anatomically consistent and disease-aware image synthesis. The quality of the generated images were evaluated using a board-certified radiologist.

### Results

RadGazeGen was evaluated on the REFLACX dataset for image generation quality and diversity. Furthermore, to assess its downstream clinical utility, the generated images were used for disease classification tasks on the CheXpert test set () and for long-tailed learning evaluation on the MIMIC-CXR-LT test set (), demonstrating high fidelity and diagnostic relevance of the synthesized images.

### Conclusions

By jointly conditioning on gaze and radiomic representations, RadGazeGen bridges the gap between human visual cognition and machine perception, improving both realism and clinical validity in medical image generation. This framework underscores the importance of incorporating anatomically grounded and disease-aware controls in diffusion-based medical image synthesis.

**Keywords:** chest X-rays, diffusion models, disease classification, eye gaze, radiomics

## [](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/)1. Introduction

Current clinical applications of AI encounter significant hurdles such as data scarcity, missing modalities, and dataset imbalances, highlighting the importance for enhancing the generation of high-quality medical images to bolster automated patient diagnosis, prognosis, and to expedite clinical research. In the last few years, diffusion models have gained prominence in image generation.[1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r1),[2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r2) More recently, these models have made remarkable progress in conditional image generation, mainly in text-to-image (T2I) generation.[3](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r3)

These models take text descriptions or captions as input and attempt to synthesize images that have maximum resemblance with the provided description.

Recent T2I models trained on huge amounts of data have achieved state-of-the-art performance.[4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r4) However, this type of textual conditioning has several limitations. In the case of complex textual structuring, i.e., when the text has a description of complex objects or ill-defined contents, the T2I diffusion models show poor image generation capabilities.[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r5) In addition, vice versa, to generate a complex image, the text description design is often ill-defined. Even in the case of a well-defined text description, the generated images from T2I models occasionally have missing objects, incorrect shapes, color, position, etc.[6](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r6) This problem stems from the fact that there is an inherent ambiguity of obviousness between text description and real-life images. A simple example is “an elephant and a bird flying” intuitively means that only the bird is flying. However, the ambiguity can be that in an unrealistic scenario both the elephant and the bird are flying. Hence, instead of generating an image where there is an elephant on the ground and a bird in the sky, the generated image shows both the elephant and bird flying.[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r5) This particular problem is in fact more common in the case of medical images. For example, if the radiologist text indicates that there is a presence of a certain abnormality, “enlarged cardiac mediastinum,” but it is not very specific about the location and severity, the generated CXR can have enlargement is any direction and may have less severe enlargement than anticipated. Hence, location and severity are critical to clinically accurate image generation. Even after several iterations of prompt design, the exact desired image is extremely difficult to generate. Another important problem is that radiology reports contain common disease related diagnostic information and often exclude any global anatomical or patient demographic information. Moreover, description of local changes in image patterns, and their relationship to the disease, are also not present in such reports. This limitation is a real challenge to medical image generation. This motivates us to explore other clinically relevant conditioning approaches.

Generating images with the desired structure, texture, and attribute location may require several trials to design an unambiguous and clearly instructed prompt. To address this, additional controls are often fed to diffusion models to generate the desired structure.[7](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r7) These controls are fine-grained information that contain required structural, textural, and positional features to generate specific desired images. Images of edge maps, depth maps, pose skeletons, and segmentation maps can all be used as controls. Recently, remarkable progress has been made in this domain where more fine-grained additional controls such as spatial maps have demonstrated superior image generation capabilities.[8](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r8) However, in the case of medical image generation, these controls are not well-defined, and the field is unexplored.

Medical image generation is a complex task because apart from generating high-quality images, the quality of generated anatomical structure and, more importantly, the precise representation of disease patterns are extremely critical. Current text-conditioned diffusion models fall short in effectively addressing these critical aspects of medical image generation. In medical imaging, radiomics features have long been used for various tasks involving disease diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment response assessment. Radiomics features are image transformations that capture a variety of properties of medical images pertaining to local and global changes in intensity distributions—in this process, the textural patterns of disease manifestation are quantified. In this work, we hypothesize that these different clinically-relevant radiomic feature maps can be used as specialized controls for accurate medical image generation.

In clinical domains such as radiology and pathology, experts’ visual patterns have been shown to have an impact on medical image interpretation.[9](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r9)_–_[11](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r11) These eye gaze patterns harbor contextually rich visuo-cognitive data related to the occurrence and patterns of diseases.[12](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r12) Recently, several methods have shown that integrating gaze patterns into deep learning frameworks helps improve the model performance for several diagnostic tasks.[9](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r9),[10](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r10) In summary, eye gaze patterns may play a crucial role as controls for diffusion models, complementing text-based conditioning to generate disease pathologies in the relevant regions. Our next hypothesis is that the human visual attention (HVA) maps computed from these eye gaze patterns can be used as relevant controls to translate the disease occurrence and visual patterns into a diffusion model. In this work, we propose to integrate these eye gaze patterns into diffusion models for medical image generation. Previous works have explored medical image generation from text conditions where disease patterns generated can be unrealistic and not located in the desired regions. By integrating these gaze patterns, we aim to generate medical images with not just realistic disease patterns but ones where such patterns are in the regions where an expert is focusing during diagnostic interpretation.

### 1.1. Summary of Motivation

Owing to the ambiguity between the radiology text description and images, diffusion models face difficulty in generating realistic images with accurate disease patterns. In recent works in the nonmedical domain, the addition of spatial features as controls improves T2I generation capabilities. In medical image generation, by adding such controls, anatomical structure of the CXRs can be accurately generated; however, translating the disease patterns still remains a challenging problem.[13](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r13) Our work is based on the premise that, to translate the disease patterns and locations, the radiologists’ eye gaze fixations and radiomic feature maps can be used as clinically relevant controls.

In this work, the radiomics filter maps are combined with HVA maps and are provided as controls to diffusion models. However, composing these filters as a single control is difficult; hence, we propose separate frameworks for radiomics filters maps and HVA maps. For training diffusion models with the radiomic filters maps, we design Radiomics ControlNet (Rad-CN) and for training diffusion models with the HVA maps, we design and Human Visual Attention ControlNet (or HVA-CN). The combined Rad-CN and HVA-CN make up our RadGazeGen architecture. We demonstrate the efficacy of our approach for several tasks. RadGazeGen outperforms other diffusion baselines, highlighting the importance of radiomic filters and HVA maps as controls to improve the clinical diagnostic quality of generated images. Furthermore, we use a pretrained disease classification model for inference on the generated images. We show that our generated images contains the disease patterns as in the training dataset distribution and achieve comparable AUCs on different disease pathologies; this is further qualitatively corroborated by our collaborating radiologists. Finally, we also show results on a long-tailed disease classification task where we generate additional CXRs of the medium and tail classes and train the classifier on the re-balanced data distribution. We observe that injection of our generated images improve the balanced accuracy and other metrics when compared with the injection of baseline generated images to the data distribution.

## [](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/)2. Related Works

### 2.1. Eye-Gaze in Medical Image Analysis

The eye gaze of experts has demonstrated immense potential in several medical applications such as disease classification [9](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r9),[10](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r10) and segmentation,[14](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r14) for various modalities, including, radiology,[15](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r15) pathology,[16](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r16) retinopathy,[17](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r17) and ECG.[18](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r18),[19](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r19) After successfully leveraging eye gaze patterns in general computer vision tasks such as object detection,[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r20),[21](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r21) image segmentation,[22](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r22)_–_[24](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r24) action localization,[25](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r25),[26](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r26) and activity recognition,[27](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r27),[28](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r28) recent applications have ventured into utilizing them in the clinical domain. Understanding gaze patterns of medical professionals, such as radiologists/pathologists, and providing this information as an auxiliary signal to ML models can potentially reduce misdiagnosis.[29](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r29) This claim was validated by recent works,[9](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r9),[10](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r10) which employed teacher–student knowledge distillation models to improve disease diagnosis. In this work, we propose a new framework of diffusion models that generate gaze-guided clinically relevant medical images.

### 2.2. Text-to-Image Diffusion

Since their introduction,[30](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r30) diffusion models have been applied extensively to image generation tasks.[1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r1),[2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r2) Latent diffusion models (LDMs) deal with latent spaces of the images to perform the diffusion steps.[3](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r3) Text-to-image diffusion models integrate text embeddings with image latent space embeddings using CLIP,[31](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r31) achieving state-of-the-art performance in image generation tasks. Recent commercial applications of text-to-image generations are DALL-E2[32](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r32),[33](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r33) and Midjourney.[34](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r34) Recently, LDMs have been used for different medical imaging modalities for a wide range of applications such as image-to-image translation,[35](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r35),[36](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r36) reconstruction,[37](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r37)_–_[44](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r44) denoising,[45](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r45),[46](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r46) registration,[47](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r47),[48](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r48) segmentation,[49](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r49)_–_[54](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r54) and classification.[55](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r55)_–_[57](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r57) Despite their overall success, the ambiguity between text descriptions and images in text-to-image diffusion models can severely impact the quality of generated images and often lead to suboptimal generations.[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r5)

### 2.3. Controllable Diffusion Models

Recently, diffusion models in which one or more controls are used are gaining traction. These controls help in generating custom and task-specific images. The controls such as color variation[58](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r58) and inpainting[33](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r33),[59](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r59) can be directly provided by the image diffusion process. Controllable diffusion models can be broadly classified into two categories: models trained from scratch[60](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r60) and finetuned lightweight adapters that are finetuned by freezing the pretrained T2I diffusion models.[7](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r7),[61](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r61) Composer[60](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r60) uses single or multiple conditions to generate images by training large diffusion models. T2I-Adapter,[61](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r61) GLIGEN,[62](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r62) and ControlNet[7](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r7) finetune an adapter from frozen large pretrained diffusion models. More recently, several methods such as UniControl[63](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r63) and UniControlNet[64](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r64) have been proposed where multiple controls are unified to generate more specific images with different styles and contents. Att-Adapter[65](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r65) and Multi-ControlNet[7](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r7) are variations of T2I-Adapter and ControlNet, respectively, where models trained with different conditions are fused to generate images from composite conditions. However, for medical image generation, the use of additional controls is still in its infancy. Given the diversity of modalities and downstream tasks in medical images, the design of controls in this domain depends largely on the specific modality and task. Medical image translation[13](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r13) is an important field of research that has immense clinical importance with applications ranging from generating multimodality images to data augmentations. However, this domain of image translation using diffusion models with additional controls is relatively unexplored.

### 2.4. Radiomics

Radiomics encompasses the computerized extraction of quantifiable data from radiological images, typically subtle radiographical features. These features, which capture morphological and textural attributes, have been shown to be effective in several clinical applications involving radiographs.[66](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r66),[67](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r67) The role of such clinically relevant descriptors in generative models is unexplored.

In summary, there is no existing work that studies clinically relevant controls for radiology image generation. To address this limitation, we propose RadGazeGen, which uses radiomics filter maps and radiologists’ eye gaze patterns as controls to generate clinically accurate medical images.

## [](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/)3. Method

An overview of the proposed method is shown in [Fig.2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#f2). In this work, we first train individual ControlNet (CN) models with different radiomics maps and segmentation masks as a control [shown in [Fig.2(a)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#f2)]. Then, we perform zero-shot inference by combining two or more of these controls [shown in [Fig.2(b)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#f2)]. The proposed method RadGazeGen comprises two main modules, namely, Rad-CN and HVA-CN. For Rad-CN training, the radiomics maps and the lung segmentation mask are combined to form unified controls. HVA maps are computed from the eye gaze patterns of radiologists. For HVA-CN training, these HVA maps in a specified manner are fed as controls to the trained Rad-CN.

### 4.1. Implementation

#### 4.1.1. SD training

We use CXR images of size  from the MIMIC-CXR dataset to finetune an SD v1.5 model. We train for 50,000 steps with a learning rate of  and batch size of 4. #### 4.1.2. RadGazeGen training

To train the different components of RadGazeGen, the two eye gaze datasets are used. For training, we use  CXRs by combining the entire first dataset with  CXRs and the samples for which only single radiologist eye gaze are collected from REFLACX (). We remove the overlapping samples from the first dataset. For testing, we use the remaining  from REFLACX dataset. First, we extract different radiomics filter maps using OpenCV and Sklearn libraries and lung segmentation from.[71](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r71) We then train Rad-CN with these radiomics filters and lung masks. The HVA maps are computed, as discussed in Sec.[3.2.2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#sec3.2.2). We train the HVA-CN with these HVA maps. For training, we use  image size as input and train for 10 epochs with a batch size set to 4 and a learning rate of . We use mixed precision of “fp16,” and the gradient accumulation steps are set to 4.

#### 4.1.3. Classification with generated images as test set

We generate the captions for the test set of CheXpert using RGRG.[72](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r72) Radiomics maps are computed as described above, followed by the computation of HVA maps. The captions, radiomics filter maps, and HVA maps computed from the test set of CheXpert are used to generate CXRs using RadGazeGen. The pretrained DenseNet-121 model from TorchXRayVision[73](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r73) is used to predict the classes for the generated images.

#### 4.1.4. Long-tailed (LT) classification

For long-tailed classification, we use the MIMIC-CXR-LT dataset. We identify the medium and tail classes and randomly select text reports for these medium and tail classes from the MIMIC-CXR dataset. Note that there is no potential concern about data overlap. Then, we randomly select sets of radiomic filter maps and HVA maps and combine them with the texts. For each disease pathology in the medium and tail classes, we generate CXR images using this set as controls such that for each pathology the CXR count is , where  is the different medium and tail classes. In this way, all the classes in the MIMIC-CXR-LT dataset have  CXR images. A ResNet-50[74](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r74) model is then trained for LT classification.

### 4.4. Radiologist Evaluation

To assess the clinical relevance and visual fidelity of the generated images, a radiology fellow with 4 years of experience and an attending radiologist with 8 years of experience independently scored the outputs from our proposed method and three baseline models—stable diffusion, ControlNet, and MultiControlNet. Each generated image was evaluated based on three criteria: anatomical plausibility (0 to 4), pathological realism (0 to 4), and overall image quality (0 to 2). As summarized in [Table 7](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#t008), our method achieved the highest mean scores across all three evaluation criteria: 3.81 for anatomy, 3.44 for pathology, and 2.00 for image quality. Compared with stable diffusion, our method showed improvements of 10.8% in anatomy, 10.3% in pathology, and 14.3% in image quality. Relative to ControlNet, the gains were even more substantial: 45.4% (anatomy), 37.6% (pathology), and 44.9% (quality). Compared with MultiControlNet, our method improved by 64.9% in anatomy, 31.3% in pathology, and 88.7% in quality. These results underscore the clinical utility of our approach in generating diagnostically meaningful synthetic images.

## [](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/)5. Discussion

The findings presented in Sec.[4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#sec4) showcase the potential of RadGazeGen in generating high-quality, clinically relevant CXR images from eye gaze patterns of radiologists and other control signals such as radiomics features. This discussion will delve deeper into the implications of these results, highlight the strengths and limitations of our approach, and suggest avenues for future research.

### 5.1. Key Findings

#### 5.1.1. Quality and controllability

The quantitative results, as detailed in [Tables 1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#t002) and [3](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#t004), unequivocally demonstrate that RadGazeGen outperforms state-of-the-art models such as T2I-Adapter, ControlNet, and Multi-ControlNet in generating images with superior FID scores, higher SSIM, and mIoU. Eye gaze patterns of radiologists add clinically relevant information about disease pathology and fine-grained location to the generative models. Furthermore, the integration of radiomics filter maps with eye gaze enables the diffusion model to generate medical images with enhanced clinical fidelity, surpassing the capabilities of SDs. This multicontrol composability is crucial for generating images that are not only visually realistic but also rich in clinically relevant details. Although the composability of multiple controls increases computational cost, the generated images demonstrate significant improvements in precision and reliability. Note that although we experimented with three radiomics filters due to computational restrictions, it can be extended to multiple other filters. In clinical scenarios, where these qualities are critical, our method offers a substantial benefit.

#### 5.1.2. Pulmonary disease classification performance

The classification results on the CheXpert dataset, summarized in [Table 4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#t005), demonstrate that the images generated by RadGazeGen are highly effective for disease classification tasks. The significant improvement in AUC scores for seven out of 12 disease categories showcases the diagnostic utility of the synthetic images. This suggests that RadGazeGen captures and preserves critical diagnostic features in the generated images, making them suitable for training and validating machine learning models.

#### 5.1.3. Long-tailed pulmonary disease classification performance

The results on the MIMIC-CXR-LT dataset, shown in [Table 5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#t006), highlight RadGazeGen’s potential in addressing class imbalance. By generating additional images for underrepresented medium and tail classes, our model enhances the classification accuracy across these categories. This approach effectively mitigates the class imbalance problem, a common challenge in medical imaging datasets. The improvement in balanced and imbalanced test set performance demonstrates RadGazeGen’s capability to generate diverse and representative synthetic data that can bolster the robustness of classification models.

#### 5.1.4. Clinical relevance

The qualitative comparisons, depicted in [Figs.5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#f5)–[7](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#f7), further validate the clinical relevance of the images generated by RadGazeGen. The generated images accurately reflect disease patterns and are consistent with the ground-truth annotations provided by experienced radiologists. This level of clinical fidelity is essential for applications in medical education, diagnostic support, and research. The model’s ability to produce images that are both diagnostically and anatomically indistinguishable from real images represents a significant advancement in medical image synthesis.

### 5.2. Limitations

Despite the promising results, our approach has certain limitations. The reliance on specific control signals, such as HVA maps, may restrict the generalizability of the model to other imaging modalities or clinical tasks where such data are not readily available. In addition, the computational complexity associated with generating and training using multiple control signals requires substantial resources, which may limit the accessibility of our approach in resource-constrained environments. Although our method is readily applicable to 3D imaging modalities, the computational cost of generating HVA maps increases significantly in 3D, thereby adding to the overall complexity of the proposed approach.

### 5.3. Future Directions

Incorporating eye gaze from radiologists into text-to-image diffusion models offers promising avenues for enhancing medical image generation. By aggregating gaze data from multiple experts, we can integrate diverse expertise into the generation process, potentially improving the accuracy and relevance of the produced images. Moreover, utilizing gaze patterns to generate images at different time points could provide insights into disease progression and treatment efficacy, offering a dynamic tool for personalized medicine and longitudinal studies. These advancements could significantly enhance the utility of generative models in medical imaging, contributing to better treatment decisions and patient outcomes.

Furthermore, although RadGazeGen demonstrates strong performance in thoracic disease classification and long-tailed learning tasks, its effectiveness has not yet been validated across other medical imaging domains,[75](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r75)_–_[77](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/#r77) which remains an important direction for future work.

Finally, incorporating additional control signals, such as patient demographics, clinical history, or genetic information, could further refine the specificity and accuracy of the generated images. This integration would allow for the creation of more contextually relevant medical images, enhancing their utility in precision medicine and patient-specific diagnostics.

## [](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13285692/)6. Conclusion

In this work, we present RadGazeGen, a visual attention and radiomics-guided diffusion model for clinically accurate medical image generation. Unlike traditional text-conditioned image generation approaches, which have suboptimal information on the structure, anatomy, and disease-relevant patterns in the medical images, our approach uses radiomics filter maps and eye gaze to generate better structured, anatomically accurate, and clinically relevant disease patterns. The efficacy of our model is demonstrated in the quality of image generation and downstream disease classification problems.
