# Abstract 

# Background 

Noninvasive preoperative assessment of isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH) status and survival risk is clinically important in glioma. This study aimed to develop and externally validate a voxel-wise probabilistic MRI habitat framework for IDH status assessment and survival risk strati fi cation. 

# Methods 

This retrospective multicenter study included 269 adults with pathologically con fi rmed diffuse glioma: 174 patients from Center A for training and 95 from Center B for external validation. Preoperative multiparametric MRI included contrast-enhanced T1-weighted imaging, T2-weighted imaging, T2-FLAIR, apparent diffusion coe ffi cient maps, and cerebral blood  fl ow maps. Tumor and peritumoral edema were segmented using nnU-Net v2. Three-dimensional sliding-window radiomics generated voxel-wise feature maps, and Gaussian mixture models produced probabilistic habitat maps. Label-independent habitat metrics and tumor-edema differential features were combined with clinical variables for IDH classi fi cation. Survival value was assessed using Kaplan-Meier analysis and Cox regression. 

# Results 

The tumor-edema differential habitat model achieved an external validation AUC of 0.907 for IDH prediction. The combined clinical-imaging model achieved the highest discrimination (AUC, 0.938; 95% CI, 0.884–0.979), outperforming the clinical-only model (AUC, 0.695). Calibration was favorable (Brier score, 0.103), and decision curve analysis showed higher net bene fi t than clinical or habitat-only models. SHAP analysis identi fi ed perfusion- and diffusion-related entropy metrics as the dominant contributors. The habitat signature was independently associated with overall survival in multivariable analysis (hazard ratio, 4.829; 95% CI, 2.382–9.790; P < 0.001) and showed good prognostic discrimination (Harrell C-index, 0.847). 

# Conclusions 

Voxel-wise probabilistic habitat modeling of multiparametric MRI characterized spatial tumor heterogeneity and showed value for noninvasive IDH assessment and survival risk strati fi cation in glioma. 

# Background 

Isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH) status is central to the molecular classi fi cation, risk strati fi cation, and treatment planning of diffuse glioma[ 1–4]. In the current integrated diagnostic framework, IDH mutation 

> Page 2/20

status is closely associated with tumor biology, therapeutic response, and patient prognosis, and it directly in fl uences clinical decision-making before and after surgery. Although IDH status is ultimately determined by histopathology and genetic testing, preoperative noninvasive assessment may support surgical planning, biopsy targeting, treatment selection, and patient counseling, especially because tissue sampling can be affected by spatial tumor heterogeneity⁵. 

Conventional MRI-based radiomics and deep learning have shown promise for IDH prediction by extracting quantitative imaging features from structural, diffusion, and perfusion MRI[ 5–10 ]. These methods provide a potential bridge between macroscopic imaging phenotypes and underlying molecular alterations. However, many existing approaches summarize the tumor as a single region of interest and use global intensity, shape, or texture descriptors for model construction. Such region-level representations may overlook spatially distinct subregions within the enhancing tumor, nonenhancing tumor, and peritumoral edema, thereby underrepresenting intratumoral and peritumoral ecological variation. 

Habitat imaging has emerged as a promising strategy for characterizing spatial tumor heterogeneity by partitioning tumors into imaging phenotypes that may re fl ect differences in cellularity, perfusion, edema, angiogenesis, and microenvironmental gradients [ 11 –13 ]. In glioma, habitat-based analysis may be particularly relevant because molecular alterations such as IDH mutation are associated not only with tumor cellular composition but also with vascular remodeling, in fi ltrative growth patterns, and changes in the surrounding microenvironment. Nevertheless, conventional habitat approaches often rely on hard clustering or region-level summaries, which can compress continuous spatial information and assign variable label meanings across patients [ 14 ]. These limitations may reduce biological interpretability and weaken cross-patient comparability. 

Voxel-wise radiomics habitat modeling addresses these limitations by extracting local texture features around each voxel and modeling habitat membership probabilistically [ 15 ]. Compared with hard partitioning, probabilistic habitat modeling allows each voxel to retain graded memberships across multiple latent phenotypes, thereby better re fl ecting transitional tissue states and spatially continuous microenvironmental variation. We hypothesized that probabilistic voxel-wise habitat metrics derived from multiparametric MRI would improve preoperative IDH classi fi cation and provide prognostic information beyond routine clinical variables. 

The aim of this study was to develop and externally validate a multicenter voxel-wise probabilistic radiomics habitat framework using structural, diffusion, and perfusion MRI for IDH status assessment and overall survival risk strati fi cation in glioma. 

# Methods 

# Study design and patients 

> Page 3/20

This retrospective observational study included consecutive adults with diffuse glioma treated at two tertiary hospitals between January 2016 and December 2025. Inclusion criteria were age 18 years or older, pathologic con fi rmation after resection or stereotactic biopsy, complete preoperative multiparametric MRI within 2 weeks before surgery, and available clinical data. Exclusion criteria were previous treatment before MRI, severe artifacts or major protocol inconsistency, and missing or indeterminate IDH testing. Of 345 initially identi fi ed patients, 269 were included: 174 from Center A for training and 95 from Center B for external validation. The study was approved by the Ethics Committee of the First Hospital of Qinhuangdao (approval number: 2023KZ065; approval date: 17 May 2023), and informed consent was waived because of the retrospective design. 

IDH status was assessed using immunohistochemistry for IDH1 R132H. Immunohistochemistry-negative cases underwent Sanger sequencing or next-generation sequencing for IDH1/IDH2 mutations. Clinical variables included age, sex, WHO tumor grade, and tumor location. Tumor location was assigned by registering tumor masks to MNI152 space ( https://fsl.fmrib.ox.ac.uk/fsl/fslwiki/MNI ) and determining overlap with atlas-de fi ned brain regions( https://fsl.fmrib.ox.ac.uk/fsl/fslwiki/Atlases ); tumors involving two or more lobes were categorized as multilobar. All procedures complied with the Declaration of Helsinki. The overall data processing work fl ow is illustrated in Fig.  1.

# MRI preprocessing and segmentation 

All patients underwent preoperative MRI on 1.5-T or 3.0-T scanners from Siemens, GE, or Philips. Sequences included contrast-enhanced T1-weighted imaging (T1CE), T2-weighted imaging (T2WI), T2-FLAIR, diffusion-weighted imaging with derived apparent diffusion coe ffi cient (ADC) maps, and arterial spin labeling-derived cerebral blood  fl ow (CBF) maps (Supplementary Table  S1 ). 

Preprocessing was performed in Python using SimpleITK and ANTsPy. Structural sequences underwent N4 bias correction. All modalities were rigidly registered to T1CE space, visually inspected, resampled to 1 x 1 x 1 mm 3 resolution, and z-score normalized within brain parenchyma. Masks were resampled using nearest-neighbor interpolation. Skull stripping was performed using HD-BET [ 16 ]. Tumor core and peritumoral edema were automatically segmented using nnU-Net v2 ( https://github.com/MIC-DKFZ/nnUNet/tree/master/nnunetv2 ) trained with the 3D full-resolution con fi guration on BraTS 2021 data, followed by visual quality inspection. 

[17 ]. 

# Voxel-wise Feature Mapping 

Voxel-wise radiomics feature maps were generated using a three-dimensional sliding-window strategy. For each voxel within the tumor core and edema regions, local radiomics features were calculated from a 7 × 7 × 7 voxel neighborhood. When the window extended beyond the segmentation mask, only voxels inside the mask were used. Features were extracted independently for each MRI modality using a 

> Page 4/20

customized PyRadiomics-based pipeline and mapped back to the original MRI space to generate three-dimensional feature maps. A total of 33 voxel-wise radiomics features were extracted for each modality (Supplementary Methods S1). The implementation of the radiomics habitat pipeline is publicly available on GitHub ( https://github.com/zt3226073/radiomics-habitat-pipeline ). 

# Probabilistic Habitat Construction 

Voxel-wise habitat modeling was performed separately for each MRI modality using Gaussian Mixture Models. For each modality, voxel-wise radiomics feature vectors from the tumor and edema regions were modeled using a diagonal-covariance Gaussian Mixture Model, generating posterior probability maps that represented probabilistic habitat membership rather than hard cluster labels. 

The number of habitats was determined in the training set using the Bayesian Information Criterion across K = 2–8. To avoid information leakage, model parameters were estimated only from the training data and then  fi xed when applied to the external validation set. The posterior probabilities were reconstructed in MRI space to generate K-channel three-dimensional habitat probability maps (Supplementary Methods S2). 

Because habitat labels were not assumed to be directly comparable across patients, label-independent patient-level metrics were extracted from the probability maps. These included habitat entropy, dominance ratio, effective habitat number, Moran’s I, fragmentation index, and Gini coe ffi cient. Tumor– edema gradient features, including Δentropy, ΔMoran’s I, and Δfragmentation index, were further calculated to quantify spatial differences between the tumor core and peritumoral edema (Supplementary Methods S3). 

# IDH Prediction Modeling 

After Z-score standardization, ComBat harmonization was applied to reduce scanner-related batch effects, with scanner model de fi ned as the batch variable. The scanner with the largest sample size in the training set was used as the reference batch. To avoid information leakage, IDH status and outcome variables were not included during harmonization, and external validation features were mapped to the training-de fi ned reference distribution (Supplementary Methods S4)[ 18 ]. 

Feature selection was performed in the training set using Elastic Net logistic regression. Habitat metrics were combined with pretreatment clinical variables, including age, sex, and tumor location, for IDH classi fi cation. The regularization and mixing parameters were optimized using nested cross-validation, and features with nonzero coe ffi cients were retained for subsequent modeling. 

The model combining clinical variables and habitat metrics, termed Clin+TEΔ-ALL, was de fi ned as the primary model. Comparative models were constructed to assess the contributions of tumor-region features, edema-region features, tumor–edema differential features, and modality-speci fi c feature 

> Page 5/20

groups. Candidate classi fi ers included logistic regression, random forest, support vector machine, XGBoost, multilayer perceptron, and decision tree. Hyperparameters were optimized using Optuna with the Tree-structured Parzen Estimator algorithm, using cross-validated AUC as the objective function[ 19 ]. Model development used nested strati fi ed 5-fold cross-validation in the training set, and the external validation set was reserved for  fi nal evaluation. 

A habitat signature was constructed from the selected TEΔ-ALL features by linearly combining retained habitat metrics weighted by their Elastic Net coe ffi cients. This signature was used to quantify patient-level spatial heterogeneity and for subsequent survival analysis. Model performance was evaluated using AUC, accuracy, sensitivity, speci fi city, and F1 max . AUC 95% con fi dence intervals were estimated using 1,000 bootstrap resamples, and DeLong tests were used for model comparisons. Calibration was assessed using calibration curves and the Brier score, clinical utility using decision curve analysis, and interpretability using Shapley Additive Explanations (SHAP). 

# Survival Analysis 

Overall survival was de fi ned as the interval from diagnosis to death or last follow-up. Patients without death were censored at the last follow-up, with a follow-up cutoff date of February 2026. Survival analysis was performed in patients from the external validation cohort with available follow-up data (n = 61). Patients were strati fi ed into high- and low-risk groups according to the optimal cutoff value of the habitat signature determined using X-tile software, which identi fi es the cutoff by maximizing the log-rank χ² statistic. Kaplan–Meier curves were used to compare survival between the two risk groups, and differences were assessed using the log-rank test. Associations between the habitat signature and overall survival were further evaluated using univariable and multivariable Cox proportional hazards models, with hazard ratios and 95% con fi dence intervals reported. Variables with P < 0.10 in univariable analysis were included in the multivariable model. The proportional hazards assumption was assessed using Schoenfeld residuals. 

# Statistical Analysis 

All statistical analyses were performed in a Python environment. Continuous variables were tested for normality using the Shapiro–Wilk test. Normally distributed variables were expressed as mean ± standard deviation and compared using the independent-samples t test; nonnormally distributed variables were expressed as median and interquartile range and compared using the Mann–Whitney U test. Categorical variables were expressed as frequency and percentage and compared using the chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test. All tests were two-sided, and P < 0.05 was considered statistically signi fi cant.

# Feature Selection 

Patient-level BIC analysis in the training set supported K = 3 as the optimal habitat number for most MRI sequences and ROIs; although several sequences favored K = 4, K = 3 was selected for overall model 

> Page 7/20

stability (Supplementary Table S2). After ComBat harmonization, the proportion of habitat metrics with signi fi cant scanner-related differences decreased from 62.7% to 9.3% (Supplementary Figures S2 and S3). 

A total of 75 habitat metrics were entered into Elastic Net feature selection. At the optimal penalty parameter, 15 features were retained, including 13 habitat imaging features and 2 clinical variables, age and tumor location (Supplementary Figure S4). The selected imaging features were mainly derived from ADC and CBF and included tumor-region, edema-region, and tumor–edema differential metrics. 

Among the evaluated classi fi ers, logistic regression achieved the best performance, with the highest cross-validated AUC in the training cohort and the highest AUC in the external validation cohort, outperforming SVC, random forest, XGBoost, MLP, and decision tree models (Supplementary Table S3). 

# Model Performance and Comparison 

Multiple models were constructed to evaluate the contributions of spatial regions, imaging modalities, and tumor–edema differential features. In the external validation set, the Clin+TEΔ-ALL model achieved the best performance, with an AUC of 0.938 (95% CI: 0.884–0.979), outperforming the clinical-only model (AUC = 0.695) and demonstrating the incremental value of habitat-based imaging features (Fig.  2,Supplementary Table S4, Supplementary Figure S5). 

Among habitat-based models, TEΔ-ALL signi fi cantly outperformed TE-ALL (P = 0.024), indicating that tumor–edema differential features improved discrimination. In contrast, no signi fi cant differences were observed between TE-ALL and T-ALL (P = 0.100) or between E-ALL and T-ALL (P = 0.070). In modality-based comparisons, functional models showed numerically higher AUCs than structural models, but the differences were not signi fi cant. However, TEΔ-ALL signi fi cantly outperformed both TEΔ-FUNC (P = 0.030) and TEΔ-STRUCT (P < 0.001), suggesting complementary contributions of functional and structural habitat phenotypes for IDH prediction. 

# Model Calibration and Clinical Utility 

In the external validation set, the Clin+TEΔ-ALL model showed good calibration, with predicted probabilities closely aligned with observed outcomes and a Brier score of 0.103 (Fig.  3). Decision curve analysis demonstrated that Clin+TEΔ-ALL provided the highest net bene fi t across a broad range of threshold probabilities, outperforming the clinical-only model and other imaging-based models (Fig.  3). 

# SHAP Interpretability Analysis 

SHAP analysis showed that functional habitat features were the main contributors to the Clin+TEΔ-ALL model, with CBF_Tumor_habitat_entropy_mean and ADC_Tumor_habitat_entropy_mean ranking as the most in fl uential predictors. Edema-region features and tumor–edema differential metrics, including ΔMoran’s I and Δentropy, also contributed to model prediction (Fig.  4). 

> Page 8/20

Representative cases showed distinct voxel-wise habitat architectures between IDH-mutant and IDH-wildtype gliomas (Fig.  5). IDH-wildtype tumors exhibited more complex perfusion- and diffusion-related heterogeneity and fragmentation, whereas IDH-mutant tumors showed relatively more homogeneous habitat distributions, consistent with the SHAP  fi ndings. 

# Survival analysis 

Patients were strati fi ed into high- and low-risk groups using the optimal habitat signature cutoff identi fi ed by X-tile software in the survival cohort (cutoff, 1.09). Kaplan–Meier analysis showed signi fi cantly poorer OS in the high-risk group than in the low-risk group (log-rank P < 0.0001; Fig.  6). 

In multivariable Cox analysis, the habitat signature was independently associated with OS (HR = 4.829, 95% CI: 2.382–9.790, P < 0.001). Age was also independently associated with OS (HR = 1.109, 95% CI: 1.057–1.164, P < 0.001). Compared with frontal lobe tumors, parietal/occipital, other-region, and multilobar tumors were associated with higher mortality risk, whereas temporal lobe tumors showed no signi fi cant difference (Supplementary Table S5). The model achieved a Harrell’s C-index of 0.847, suggesting favorable prognostic discrimination. 

# Discussion 

This study developed and externally validated a voxel-wise probabilistic radiomics habitat framework for noninvasive molecular and prognostic assessment of diffuse glioma. By capturing spatially resolved tumor–edema habitat organization from multiparametric MRI, the proposed framework improved preoperative IDH classi fi cation beyond clinical variables and showed favorable calibration and decision curve bene fi t. The independent prognostic value of the habitat signature further suggests that spatial habitat architecture encodes biologically meaningful heterogeneity, providing an interpretable imaging bridge between glioma molecular status, microenvironmental organization, and survival risk. 

Previous imaging-based studies have predicted IDH status using whole-tumor radiomics, deep learning models, or ROI-level habitat analysis, but these approaches may insu ffi ciently characterize continuous spatial heterogeneity and often have limited interpretability[ 20 –24 ]. Although MRI habitat studies based on perfusion, ADC, or diffusion kurtosis metrics have demonstrated differences between IDH-mutant and IDH-wildtype gliomas[ 9, 25 –29 ], most rely on discrete region-level clustering and global feature extraction, which may obscure voxel-scale microenvironmental variation and reduce cross-patient comparability[ 25 , 27 , 29 , 30 ]. The present voxel-wise probabilistic habitat framework addressed these limitations by combining sliding-window radiomics feature mapping with Gaussian Mixture Model– based soft clustering to generate continuous habitat probability maps[ 15 ]. This strategy achieved strong external validation performance without deep learning and provided interpretable spatial heterogeneity and tumor–edema gradient metrics for characterizing glioma microenvironment organization. 

Model interpretability analysis showed that IDH prediction and survival strati fi cation were mainly driven by perfusion–diffusion habitat heterogeneity and its spatial organization. The most in fl uential features 

> Page 9/20

included CBF- and ADC-derived entropy, fragmentation, Moran’s I, and Gini metrics, together with tumor location. Biologically, CBF re fl ects tumor perfusion, angiogenesis, and microvascular remodeling, whereas ADC is associated with cellular density and microstructural alterations[ 31 ]. Previous studies have reported higher perfusion parameters in IDH-wildtype gliomas and supported ADC as an important biomarker for IDH classi fi cation[ 32 , 33 ]. Our  fi ndings extend these conventional parameter-based observations by showing that IDH-related imaging phenotypes are re fl ected not only by mean perfusion or diffusion values, but also by their voxel-wise spatial heterogeneity. Habitat entropy and Gini coe ffi cient quantify phenotype diversity and imbalance, whereas Moran’s I and fragmentation index describe spatial autocorrelation and subregional disorganization. These metrics may capture IDH-associated differences in vascular heterogeneity, cellular architecture, in fi ltrative growth, and tumor–edema microenvironmental gradients[ 26 , 27 , 34 ]. 

The peritumoral edema region may also contain important molecular and prognostic information. Previous habitat analyses based on dynamic susceptibility contrast (DSC) perfusion imaging have partitioned the tumor microenvironment into multiple subregions, including in fi ltrative edema and vasogenic edema. Differences in rCBV across these habitats have been shown to be associated not only with IDH status but also with patient prognosis[ 26 ]. Furthermore, multimodal studies have demonstrated that the spatial distribution of subregions characterized by varying vascular permeability and cellular density can re fl ect the heterogeneity of the tumor microenvironment and is associated with disease progression and survival outcomes[ 27 ]. Similarly, vascular heterogeneity–based habitat analyses have indicated that the hemodynamic characteristics of the peritumoral region can predict recurrence patterns and clinical outcomes[ 35 ]. 

The habitat signature remained independently associated with OS after adjustment for age and tumor location, and the model achieved a Harrell’s C-index of 0.847, indicating good survival discrimination. Age and non-frontal or multilobar tumor locations were also associated with poorer outcomes. Previous studies have shown that perfusion- and diffusion-de fi ned habitats, including low-perfusion or diffusion-restricted subregions, are related to treatment resistance and survival outcomes [ 36 ]. Multimodal MRI habitat studies further suggest that the volumetric distribution of subregions with different vascular permeability and cellular density is prognostically relevant, possibly re fl ecting hypoxia, metabolic stress, and aggressive tumor microenvironments [ 27 ]. These  fi ndings support the concept that the spatial organization of the glioma microenvironment carries prognostic information and may complement conventional clinical risk strati fi cation. 

This study has several limitations. First, the maturity of follow-up was moderate: the median follow-up duration was 26 months, and the overall median OS was also 26 months, indicating that the set is statistically at an intermediate level of maturity. The median OS of the low-risk group (42 months) may still be in fl uenced by censoring, and longer follow-up is required. Second, the retrospective study design may introduce potential selection bias, as the inclusion of patients with complete imaging data may favor individuals who were more likely to undergo surgery or had higher imaging quality. Future studies should employ prospective sets with consecutive enrollment and explicitly report the mechanisms of 

> Page 10/20

missing data. Third, external validation for survival analysis remains limited. Although external validation was performed for IDH classi fi cation, the OS model still lacks multicenter external validation for survival outcomes, which warrants further investigation in larger independent sets. 

# Conclusions 

Multiparametric MRI–based voxel-wise probabilistic habitat modeling enables noninvasive characterization of tumor and peritumoral heterogeneity, supports preoperative IDH prediction, and provides prognostic information for survival risk strati fi cation in glioma.
