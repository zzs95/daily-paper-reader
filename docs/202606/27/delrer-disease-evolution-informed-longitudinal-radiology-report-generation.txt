## Abstract 

Auto Radiology Report Generation (RRG) can effectively help relieve the workload of radiologists. Based on this, 

Longitudinal Radiology Report Generation (LRRG) utilizes patients’ longitudinal follow-up radiology examinations to provide a comprehensive description of patients’ current status. In clinical practice, radiologists often estimate a patient’s current expected state based on previous radiol-ogy images and disease evolution patterns. Inspired by this clinical thought process, we propose a novel LRRG method, namely Disease Evolution-informed Longitudinal radiology Report gen ER ation ( DELRER ). Given longitudi-nal data, our basic idea is to model the disease-specific evo-lution represented by radiology images, then estimate the current status of diseases as direct auxiliary signals. Exper-imental results show that by imitating the thought process of estimating the status of diseases, D ELRER can exceed other state-of-the-art methods in diagnostic accuracy, while gen-erating high-quality diagnostic reports. 

## 1. Introduction 

Radiology Report Generation (RRG) aims to mitigate the heavy diagnostic workload of radiologists [1, 36, 44] by de-veloping intelligent methods to generate reports automat-ically. In clinical practice, radiologists usually need to conduct an assessment of a patient’s historical examination records to write a diagnostic report [33]. Based on this be-havior, Longitudinal Radiology Report Generation (LRRG)

is an emerging research area, which takes the patient’s pre-vious radiological image and report as additional auxiliary inputs to generate the current diagnostic report. By refer-encing the patient’s previous status, LRRG can provide a comprehensive description of the patient’s current status. Recently, many LRRG methods have been proposed, and they mainly apply the historical images as auxiliary data from various perspective. One approach directly takes the historical images as inputs into the “black box” model [51]. Based on this, researchers further incorporate temporal em-bedding into the images to distinguish the previous and cur-rent images [3, 29, 50] and the order of patient-level images [43]. On the other hand, some methods [30, 39] explic-itly capture the differences between historical and current images, which enables the model to know the changes that have occurred in the current image compared to history. Referring to the previous literature [5, 13, 16, 40], in clinical practice, radiologists tend to estimate disease evo-lution patterns from longitudinal radiology images before writing reports. Typically, they estimate the expected status of diseases based on the previous image and as a reference to the current image. The expected status of diseases can re-duce the possibility of radiologists misdiagnosing patients. Inspired by this, we raise a question: Can the report genera-tion model also benefit from the expected status of diseases? 

In this paper, we explore this idea of adding information of expected disease status to the report generation process, and propose Disease Evolution-informed Longitudinal ra-diology Report gen ER ation ( DELRER ). The main idea of our method is to use the expected statuses of diseases to-gether with real radiology images to generate diagnostic re-ports. In the first stage, we estimate the severity of diseases in radiology images. In the second stage, we construct image sequences of disease development based on their severity, and train neural ODE models [6, 49] to model remission and aggravation of dis-eases, and estimate the current status. Finally, in the last stage, we use both real X-ray images and estimated im-ages to generate text reports. Experimental results show that D ELRER outperforms other methods on all datasets. It describes disease status more accurately, while simultane-ously preserving linguistic fluency and correctness. The main contributions of this paper are as follows: • We propose a novel LRRG method, D ELRER , that uses the expected disease status as an auxiliary signal to help the model generate more accurate diagnostic reports. • We propose a method to predict the fine-grained sever-ity to construct the disease evolution process sequences and use a neural ODE-based model to learn the disease-specific evolutionary patterns. • Extensive experiments demonstrate that our proposed DELRER achieves state-of-the-art performance in clinical efficacy metrics and the effectiveness of the estimated ex-pected disease status. 

## 2. Related Works 

Longitudinal Radiology Report Generation: LRRG is a type of radiology report generation task that generates pa-tients’ reports based on X-ray images of both past and cur-rent examinations, i.e., longitudinal X-ray images. Current LRRG methods mainly apply the historical images as auxil-iary data from various perspectives. One line of research di-rectly takes the historical images as inputs into the network with memory matrices [51]. Based on this, many recent methods incorporate temporal embeddings into the images to distinguish the previous and current images [3, 29, 50] and the order of patient-level images [43]. Another line of research explicitly captures the differences between histori-cal and current images through time-specific features [30] and a difference-aware model [39]. Different from the above methods, the D ELRER learns the disease evolution patterns and estimates the expected status of diseases as an auxiliary signal to help generate diagnostic reports. 

Dynamics Modeling by Neural ODE: Dynamics mod-eling tries to learn how system properties change over time and is a fundamental task in science and engineering [14, 45]. As a primary method for solving this task, neural ODE [6] is proposed and extends the residual network [18] to continuous time and parameterizes the state derivative through the neural network. This characteristic makes neu-ral ODE more suitable for the dynamics modeling of irreg-ular sampling. Neural ODE solves for state values through an ODE solver [17], thus enabling forward and backward propagation. Neural ODE [6] is used to model dynamics in a variety of domains [11, 12, 23, 27, 31, 32, 37]. On this basis, NDCN [49] uses an encoder to encode the states, improving the model’s representational ability. Inspired by this, we use the NDCN model to model the disease evolu-tion patterns and estimate the expected disease status in this work.

3.1. Problem formulation 

Given a previous X-ray image xp, a historical diagnostic report yp, and a current X-ray image xc, the objective of LRRG is to learn a function Fθ (·) that automatically gener-ates a diagnostic report ˜yc. Denoted as 

Fθ (xc, xp, yp) → ˜yc. (1) The method optimizes the parameters θ of Fθ (·) by mak-ing the generated reports ˜yc closer to ground truth reports 

yc. The results are obtained by selecting the optimal model based on its performance on the validation set, and then evaluating it on the test set. 

3.2. Overview of D ELRER 

The main idea of D ELRER is using both real X-ray images, i.e., xc and xp and the expected X-ray images of current disease status, i.e., ˜xc, to generate diagnostic reports for pa-tients, in order to align with radiologists’ diagnostic pro-cesses. As shown in Figure 1, the whole pipeline consists of three main stages. The Disease Severity Regression stage trains disease-specific models to predict the fine-grained severity of each disease in X-ray images. The Disease Evo-lution Learning stage constructs image sequences of disease development and uses neural ODE models to model pat-terns of disease evolution based on severity predicted from the first stage. In the Diagnostic Report Generation stage, the generative model uses both real X-ray images and ex-pected images predicted from the ODE models to generate diagnostic reports.

The objective of this stage is to develop a model that can predict disease-specific severity for X-ray images, allowing us to use the ODE model with images sorted by severity in the next stage. ## 4. Experiments 

4.1. Experimental Settings 

Datasets. 1) Longitudinal-MIMIC [51](L.M.) is a sub-set of the MIMIC-CXR [22] dataset, containing only longi-tudinal cases. Its training set contains 26,156 patients and 92,374 samples, the validation set contains 203 patients and 737 samples, and the test set contains 266 patients and 2,058 samples. To further validate the effectiveness of our method across different organ types, we divided the L.M. dataset into three datasets: 2) Lung-Longitudinal-MIMIC (L.L.M.) dataset contains samples whose previous and current images show at least one disease in Lung Opacity, Lung Lesion, Edema, Consolidation, Pneumonia, and Atelectasis. The training set contains 10,554 patients and 29,127 samples, the vali-dation set contains 1,319 patients and 3,755 samples, and the test set contains 1,319 patients and 3,582 samples. 3) Pleural-Longitudinal-MIMIC (P.L.M.) dataset contains samples of at least one disease in Pneumothorax, Pleural Effusion, and Pleural Other. The training set contains 5,694 patients and 16,831 samples, the validation set contains 712 patients and 2,133 samples, and the test set contains 711 pa-tients and 1,961 samples. 4) Heart-Longitudinal-MIMIC (H.L.M.) dataset contains samples of at least one disease in Enlarged Cardiomediastinum and Cardiomegaly. The train-ing set contains 2,950 patients and 5,370 samples, the val-idation set contains 369 patients and 670 samples, and the test set contains 369 patients and 646 samples. Following previous works [4, 8, 25, 28, 42], we treat the “Findings” section in radiology reports as the reference reports. When building the L.L.M., H.L.M. , and P.L.M. datasets, we shuf-fle all samples in the L.M. and split them into training sets, validation sets, and test sets with a ratio of 8 : 1 : 1 . This is because the original validation and test sets lack sufficient samples of specific organs. All samples from the same pa-tient are in the same subset. 

Evaluation metrics. Following previous works [25, 39, 43, 46], we use Clinical Efficacy (CE) metrics and Natural Language Generation (NLG) metrics to evaluate the meth-ods. CE metrics evaluate whether the generated reports cor-rectly include clinical entities, and NLG metrics evaluate linguistic similarities between generated and reference re-ports. To evaluate the accuracy of generated reports, we use the corresponding disease types from the CheXpert dataset [20]. By comparing the diseases detected in ˜yc with those identified in the ground truth report yc, we can compute the accuracy of the generated reports. In line with [39, 51], we use CheXbert [38] to label the generated reports with 14 ob-servations [20] and compute the sample-average Precision, Recall, and F1 score. We use BLEU score [34], METEOR [2], and ROUGE-L [26] as the NLG metrics. All metrics are computed using official libraries [7, 38], and higher values indicate better performance. 

Implementation details. We use the RAD-DINO [35] as our vision encoder to extract features from images in the first stage. We adopt a normalized graph Laplacian diffu-sion operator [49] in NDCN and use the dopri5 algorithm [48] as the ODE solver. The settings of the text generative model follow the baseline method [39]. We use the vision encoder of the MLRG [29] as the second-stage encoder and pre-train it by removing the global features. The best model 5361 is identified as the one with the highest F1 score on the vali-dation set, and we report its performance on the test set. All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 GPU. 

4.2. Main Results 

We compare D ELRER with seven state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods, i.e., R2Gen [8], R2GenCMN [9], LR2Gen [51], PromptMRG [21] DDaTR [39], R2GenGPT [47] and HC-LLM [30], on all datasets. Experimental results are shown in Table 1. DELRER outperforms other baseline methods on all datasets in CE metrics. Specifically, the F1 scores of D ELRER in the four datasets are 0.541 , 0.553 , 0.593 ,and 0.557 , demonstrating consistent superiority across all organ-specific datasets. In addition, the improvement in precision and recall shows that D ELRER has a lower missed diagnosis rate than other methods without sacrificing accu-racy. For different organs and different numbers of train-ing samples, D ELRER performs best in all three CE metrics. Among them, D ELRER performs best for pleural diseases. DELRER is comparable to the SOTA methods in terms of NLG metrics, indicating that the predicted auxiliary sig-nal does not lose the text fluency of the model-generated report. It is worth noting that, due to the advantages of LLMs in terms of parameter quantity and fluency of lan-guage expression, LLM-based methods (R2GenGPT, HC-LLM) have high NLG metrics. However, they fall short in CE metrics, which indicate that LLM cannot accurately un-derstand the clinical entities in radiology images. 

4.3. Ablation Study 

We conduct an ablation study by comparing the perfor-mances of D ELRER with and without the expected disease status images. The CE metrics results on all datasets are shown in Table 2. Adding expected disease status images improves Precision, Recall, and F1 score metrics on all datasets, indicating that using expected disease status im-ages as an auxiliary signal helps D ELRER generate more clinically accurate reports.

## 5. Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduce a new LRRG method called DELRER . Our approach is inspired by the way doctors think when estimating a patient’s current expected state during the diagnostic process. It leverages estimated disease sta-tus to enhance the generation of diagnostic reports. The method includes three stages. The Disease Severity Regres-sion stage predicts the severity for each radiology image. The Disease Evolution Learning stage estimates disease sta-tus images based on previous images and learned disease development patterns. The Diagnostic Report Generation stage generates diagnostic reports using the estimated im-ages as an auxiliary signal. Extensive experimental results on datasets of different organs demonstrate that considering the expected disease status as an auxiliary signal can make a more accurate diagnostic report, and our method provides a more precise description of clinical observations. Future work will focus on using development cycles of diseases to replace historical images.
