Abstract 

Recent multimodal large language models (MLLMs) are predominantly evaluated on aggregate accuracy across per-ception benchmarks, with far less attention to how stable 

their performance is across tasks, domains, and evaluation factors. This accuracy-centric focus yields a limited and sometimes misleading picture of multimodal perception ca-pability. In this paper, we take a step toward systematically studying perception stability and analyzing how it scales across pre-training, post-training, and inference. We be-gin by decomposing 11 perception benchmarks into 6 core abilities and evaluating 18 MLLMs with two stability met-rics that measure ranking volatility and performance disper-sion across heterogeneous subtasks. Our findings show that perception stability is a fundamental yet long-overlooked dimension of MLLM behavior. Specifically: (i) although recent open-source MLLMs can match or surpass strong closed-source models in accuracy, they exhibit a substantial gap in perception stability; (ii) training dynamics reveal an ability conflict phenomenon, where gains in one or more abilities coincide with significant declines in another abil-ity’s accuracy and stability; and (iii) stability is shaped by different factors at different stages: during pre-training and post-training it is primarily driven by the data mixing ra-tio and LLM size, whereas at inference time it is strongly influenced by the model’s reasoning style. Finally, targeted fine-tuning and model merging partially mitigate ability con-flicts, but fully resolving multiple concurrent conflicts re-mains an open problem. Our code is available at https: //github.com/Chenfeng1271/AbilityLens .

1. Introduction 

Recent multimodal large language models (MLLMs) [ 4, 15 ,33 ] are primarily evaluated by aggregate accuracy on per-ception benchmarks, with far less attention to perception sta-bility —the fine-grained consistency of performance across tasks, domains, and evaluation conditions. As shown in Fig-ure 1, the best- and worst-performing models differ across MMBench [ 36 ], MME [ 8], and SeedBench [ 14 ] on enriry recognition, underscoring that accuracy alone can mask insta-bility. This accuracy-centric view yields an incomplete and sometimes misleading picture of model capability, especially for real-world applications where consistency is critical. Except for this watermark, it is identical to the accepted version; the final published version of the proceedings is available on IEEE Xplore. 3050 therefore investigate the fine-grained perception stability of MLLMs and examine how it evolves across stages of model development. Our study begins by decomposing a broad-coverage suite of 11 image perception benchmarks [ 6 , 8, 12 – 14 , 20 , 23 ,32 , 36 , 40 ]—spanning diverse domains, instruction formats, question types, and metrics—into six core perception abili-ties (counting, OCR, attribute recognition, entity extraction, grounding, and structured-data understanding). We define perception stability as the consistency of a model’s relative standing and absolute performance across heterogeneous subtasks (domains, formats, metrics), quantified by a rank-entropy score (RES) and a dispersion score (DVS), respec-tively. Building on this setup, we observe that although recent open-source MLLMs [ 4, 33 ] often match or surpass strong closed-source counterparts [ 9 ] in accuracy, they still exhibit a substantial gap in perception stability. To explain this discrepancy, we analyze how stability scales across train-ing and inference and report three findings: (i) training dy-namics reveal an ability conflict phenomenon—gains in one or more abilities coincide with significant declines in another ability’s accuracy and stability; (ii) during training, stabil-ity is driven chiefly by data-mixture ratios and LLM size, with larger models and more balanced perception-focused mixtures tending to reduce conflicts; and (iii) at inference, a model’s reasoning style (e.g., Chain-of-Thought [ 35 ], self-reflection [ 28 ], self-consistency [ 34 ]) strongly modulates stability, significantly improving performance consistency among multiple subtasks. Finally, we explore simple strate-gies to mitigate ability conflicts and improve performance: targeted fine-tuning and lightweight model merging yield partial gains, but fully eliminating multiple concurrent con-flicts remains an open challenge. Our contributions are as follows: (1) A principled formu-lation of fine-grained perception stability with two comple-mentary metrics—RES and DVS—capturing ranking volatil-ity and performance dispersion across heterogeneous sub-tasks. (2) A large-scale evaluation over 11 image-perception benchmarks, 6 abilities, and 18 MLLMs, revealing a persis-tent stability gap between open-source and strong closed-source models, and documenting an ability conflict during training. (3) A stage-wise analysis showing that stability is driven by data mixtures and LLM size during pre-/post-training, and by reasoning style at inference; simple fine-tuning and model merging partially mitigate conflicts but do not eliminate them. 

2. Related Work 

Perception-based MLLM benchmarks. In parallel with architectural advancements [ 4, 15 ], considerable effort has been dedicated to improving benchmarks for MLLMs, which play a crucial role in steering the development of the next generation of models [ 37 ]. As a fundamental capability, per-ception is essential to the performance of MLLMs. To eval-uate this skill, benchmarks such as MME [ 8 ], MMStar [ 6], MMBench [ 36 ], and SeedBench [ 14 ] have been introduced, aiming to capture data from diverse domains and cover a wide range of question types. However, these differences in-evitably introduce evaluation variance, motivating our study of perception stability. 

Ability-perspective MLLM benchmarks. To enable a sys-tematic evaluation of MLLM advancements, recent bench-marks [ 1, 25 ] aim to expose granular weaknesses within specific capabilities by regrouping benchmarks [ 16 , 24 , 37 ]according to model abilities. For instance, Unibench [ 1]categorizes 53 vision-language benchmarks by different ca-pabilities, though most focus on traditional recognition tasks and are limited in evaluating state-of-the-art MLLMs. EU-REKA [ 5 ], on the other hand, concentrates on non-saturated samples within each ability, offering a more targeted view of areas most ripe for improvement. However, these ability-centric efforts remain largely accuracy-oriented; we comple-ment them with an ability-aligned evaluation that baseline-corrects heterogeneous submetrics and explicitly quantifies stability via rank-entropy (RES) and dispersion (DVS) across 18 MLLMs, enabling stage-wise analyses and revealing an open–closed-source stability gap. 

Multimodal large language models. Building upon the suc-cess of LLMs such as GPT [ 29 ], LLaMA [ 31 ], and Qwen [ 3], and vision foundation models including DINOv2 [ 27 ], CLIP [ 30 ], and SigLIP [ 38 ], MLLMs [ 15 , 18 , 21 , 22 ]have emerged as powerful tools for multimodal understand-ing and reasoning. However, training a versatile MLLM presents significant challenges [ 11 ]. Current state-of-the-art approaches [ 7, 17 ] primarily rely on more powerful LLMs and vision encoders coupled with extensive training data, requiring thousands of GPU hours for each training run. For example, Qwen2.5-VL [ 33 ] adopts a stronger LLM backbone and dynamic-resolution vision encoders, together with large-scale, multi-stage training (image–text pretraining, multi-task post-training, and RL-based post-training) on cu-rated datasets. While these resource-intensive solutions have yielded improvements, we still lack a clear understanding of how these models evolve during training and how different abilities interact with each other. This opacity in model be-havior impedes us to develop more effective strategies for advancing MLLM capabilities.

5. Scaling Law of Stability 

Because our pool of 18 MLLM candidates does not fully span the current performance landscape, we adopt dispersion-based variance stability (DVS) as the primary stability metric in this section, which is less sensitive to the number and composition of model candidates than rank-based measures.

Observation 3. During pre-training, model stability is primarily driven by the data-mixture ratio and LLM size. 

Reason behind ability conflict. To investigate the causes of ability conflict, we evaluate combinations of visual encoders, LLMs, and datasets (Figure 5, 6, 7). We find that conflicts are mainly attributable to model size and data mixing: dif-ferent encoder–LLM pairings exhibit similar trajectories on the same dataset, whereas varying the mixture ratio or the LLM size markedly changes the magnitude, timing, and frequency of conflicts. In particular, smaller LLMs tend to incur more conflicts during training. These conflicts induce pronounced fluctuations in stability and often stall progress, making further improvement difficult despite continued op-timization. Moreover, as shown in Fig. 4 (c)(d), increasing LLM size does not yield across-the-board gains at the abil-ity level; unlike aggregate accuracy, the improvements are heterogeneous and concentrated in only a subset of abilities.

Observation 4. Targeted fine-tuning and model merg-ing can partially improve perception stability, but fully resolving multiple ability conflicts remains an open chal-lenge. 

Simple solutions to alleviate ability conflict. As shown in Table 7 and 6, when a conflict arises in a single ability during training, continuous fine-tuning or model merging can mitigate it. Task Arithmetic [ 10 ] generally offers a better accuracy–stability trade-off than continuous fine-tuning: it raises OCR to 78.19 (vs. 81.77 with fine-tuning), lifts the average accuracy to 63.90 (vs. 63.41), and reduces the aver-age instability to 42.57 (vs. 44.93), while being less sensitive to data-mixture ratios. However, resolving conflicts that span multiple abilities remains open, as multi-model merg-ing often fails to preserve each model’s per-ability optimum without regressions. 

5.3. Test-time Scaling Law 

Observation 5. At inference, a model’s reasoning style strongly modulates stability, sometimes improving con-sistency even when mean accuracy changes little. 

We study reasoning as a mainstream form of test-time scal-ing, instantiated by three inference-time strategies: Chain-of-Thought (CoT) [ 35 ], self-reflection (critique–revise) [ 28 ], and self-consistency (sampling multiple reasoning paths with voting) [ 34 ]. We apply these to frozen models and keep prompts unified across abilities. As shown in Figure 8, on both GPT-4o and Qwen2.5-VL-72B, adding test-time reasoning produces small, mostly positive accuracy shifts across abilities, whereas the stability changes are larger and ability-dependent. Self-consistency tends to be unchanged on OCR and structured-data abili-ties, while counting often shows better stability under rea-soning. CoT delivers steady, modest improvements, and self-reflection helps curb brittle failures in several abilities, but effects vary by model. Overall, these results indicate a decoupling between accuracy and stability at inference time and highlight reasoning style as a strong knob for test-time scaling. 

6. Conclusion 

We studied perception stability of MLLMs beyond accuracy, decomposing 11 benchmarks into 6 abilities and evaluating 18 models with two complementary metrics (ranking and dis-persion). Our results show: (1) open-source models, while often matching or surpassing closed-source in accuracy, still lag in stability; (2) stability scales differently from accuracy, with gains driven primarily by reducing ability conflicts; and (3) stability’s drivers vary by stage—data mixing and LLM size during pre/post-training, and reasoning style at infer-ence. Simple tactics (targeted fine-tuning, model merging) yield partial improvements, but resolving multiple concur-rent ability conflicts remains open. We advocate reporting stability alongside accuracy and designing training/decoding strategies that explicitly optimize for stable perception. We hope this study will support researchers in drawing well-founded conclusions about effective strategies for advancing the fine-grained capability of MLLMs, avoiding overwhelm-ing development testing. 

Limitations and future work. Our study has several limitations. First, benchmark coverage remains restricted in domain breadth and metrics. Second, we focus primarily on perception abilities, leaving reasoning and instruction following largely unaddressed. In addition, our stabil-ity metrics (RES/DVS) capture ranking volatility and dispersion but not other reliability dimensions such as calibration or robustness under controlled perturbations. In future work, we will expand coverage to video/temporal, 3D, interactive, and multilingual settings with addi-tional metrics (e.g., calibration, factor invariance, and tail-risk), enlarge the model pool with denser size check-points, and develop methods that explicitly optimize stability. 3057 References
