## Abstract 

Radiology report generators often produce fluent text yet miss crucial details, leading to local semantic conflicts or flipped findings that require stronger penalties. Cross-entropy (CE) merely increases the probability of the ground-truth token y∗ without directly suppressing the model’s current wrong choice ˆy, and treats all positions uniformly, so corrections are not prioritized. We introduce a 

self-adaptive optimization framework that dynamically ad-justs token-level gradients based on semantic discrepancy cues derived from a frozen LLM referee. The LLM itself is not the contribution—it merely provides weak supervi-sion to trigger the adaptive learning process. Within this framework, (i) semantic conflicts between the predicted and reference reports are automatically localized and tagged with <e>...</e> (used only during training), and (ii) 

adaptive, stronger penalties are applied within these sparse but critical spans. Updates follow a push–pull scheme: error spans are pushed down, while non-error tokens are reinforced. The update strength is governed by two com-plementary signals— normalized entropy (for uncertainty calibration) and focal-style confidence (for handling over-and under-confident predictions). On MIMIC-CXR and IU-Xray, our framework consistently improves both language metrics (BLEU-4, ROUGE-L, METEOR) and clinical met-rics (RadGraph F1, CheXbert), and remains robust to noisy or imperfect error tags. 

## 1. Introduction 

Automating radiology report generation (RRG) promises to reduce radiologists’ workload and improve diagnostic con-sistency. Recent encoder–decoder models and large lan-guage models (LLMs) [2–4, 7, 34, 35] can produce flu-ent narratives from images, yet they frequently miss clin-ically salient details: flipped findings, stray negations, or

local contradictions. A central reason is that standard train-ing with cross-entropy (CE) treats all positions uniformly and only increases the likelihood of the ground-truth token ,without directly pushing down the model’s current wrong choice . Consequently, models lack a built-in mechanism for semantic self-consistency, making them unable to iden-tify or prioritize corrections where they matter most[13]. Prior work explores token-level feedback in general NLG via reinforcement learning [43], contrastive objec-tives [17], or post-hoc correction. However, these ap-proaches often depend on hand-crafted rewards, extra an-notations, or auxiliary networks, which limits scalability in medical NLP where expert supervision is costly. Such re-liance on coarse, sequence-level feedback leaves models in-sensitive to token-level semantic conflicts—a key challenge our method addresses. We propose SAT-RRG , a self-adaptive training frame-work that dynamically modulates token-level gradients using semantic discrepancy cues derived from a frozen LLM referee. The referee only provides weak supervi-sion during training, highlighting phrase-level conflicts via 

<e>...</e> tags to yield sparse, conflict-aware token masks. Guided by these masks, SAT-RRG converts learn-ing into a closed loop that concentrates updates on suspect spans to correct what is likely wrong while consolidating stable evidence elsewhere .At the objective level, SAT-RRG adopts a push–pull 

scheme on the model’s current choice: inside error spans, the loss explicitly drives the probability of the chosen token down; outside errors, it reinforces correct tokens. The up-date strength is adaptively governed by two complementary signals, focal-style confidence and normalized-entropy un-certainty , which jointly balance caution and correction. The custom loss is normalized over masked regions, balanced with CE, and introduces no inference-time overhead.

## 2. Related Work 

Radiology Report Generation. Radiology report gen-eration (RRG) aims to produce clinically accurate nar-ratives from radiological images. Traditional encoder– decoder methods [3, 19] rely on word-level cross-entropy loss, which penalizes clinically valid paraphrases and lacks semantic flexibility. To overcome this, recent works ex-plore domain knowledge integration and architectural inno-vations—METransformer [37] introduces expert tokens to guide attention, EKAGen [2] injects instance-level super-vision, and KiUT [7] aligns multi-modal features through U-shaped clinical pathways. Meanwhile, LLM-based mod-els such as R2GenGPT [38] and Bootstrapping [18] lever-age visual mappers and domain-specific prompts to adapt LLaMA [33] or Qwen [39] for report generation. De-spite these advances, both traditional and LLM-based ap-proaches treat generated reports uniformly during train-ing and lack token-level feedback mechanisms to distin-guish semantically correct from incorrect content. Our model addresses this gap through a self-adaptive opti-mization framework that leverages weak semantic discrep-ancy cues from a frozen LLM to guide token-level gradient modulation—enabling precise, fine-grained, and meaning-preserving training without explicit supervision. 

Token-Level Supervision and Feedback Learn-ing. Token-level feedback in general NLG has been explored through reinforcement learning (e.g., SCoRe, S2R, Reflexion [12, 23, 31, 43]), contrastive objectives [17], or correction-based post-hoc tuning. However, these meth-ods rely on manually defined rewards, additional labeled data, or separate corrector models, limiting scalability and differentiability. Multi-pass decoding (e.g., Self-Refine [24]) improves outputs via iterative prompting but increases latency, while heuristic token modification (TAPO [21]) lacks semantic feedback and parameter learn-ing. In contrast, SAT-RRG offers a fully differentiable, training-time mechanism that directly modulates gradients at the token level—promoting semantically consistent tokens and penalizing incorrect ones without reinforcement learning or external correctors. 

Token-Level Supervision in Medical NLP. In medical NLP, few RRG methods apply token-level semantic super-vision due to annotation costs and limited modeling strate-gies. To address this, we use in-context prompting of a frozen LLM to obtain weak semantic discrepancy cues, which are then transformed into a differentiable, adaptive optimization signal. This dual-loss framework penalizes overconfident errors ( ETAPL ) and reinforces correct predic-tions ( CTAL ), aligning training dynamics with clinical ac-curacy. Intuitively, CTAL functions as a semantic stabilizer, and ETAPL as a semantic corrector. 

## 3. Methodology 

An overview of our SAT-RRG framework is illustrated in Fig. 1, which consists of three key components: (a) report generation, (b) error token identification, and (c) inference process. The training procedure is detailed in Algorithm 1, and a step-by-step example is provided. 

3.1. Report Generation 

Our report generation pipeline consists of three key com-ponents: (1) a visual encoder that extracts representative features from the input image, (2) a visual mapper that projects these features into the LLM’s word embedding space, and (3) an LLM decoder that generates the final re-port conditioned on both visual and textual inputs. Given a chest X-ray image Xv , we employ the Swin Transformer [20] to extract visual features, denoted as Zv =Swin( Xv ), where Zv represents the final-layer feature map capturing image regions relevant to report generation. To align the visual features with the LLM’s text embedding space, a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) is used as a visual mapper to transform them into Hv = MLP( Zv ), where Hv

denotes the projected high-dimensional representation com-patible with the LLM. The mapped features Hv are then concatenated with a textual prompt P and the ground-truth report RGT , both of which are tokenized for LLM processing. The final LLM input sequence is defined as: 

X = Concat( Hv , Tokenizer( P ), Tokenizer( RGT )) .

where P provides the context: “Generate a compre-hensive and detailed diagnosis report for this chest X-ray image.” The LLM generates a sequence of tokens y =

{y1, y 2, . . . , y T }, predicted under cross-entropy loss.

3.3. Error-Aware Training Objectives 

Our framework enables the model to learn from its own pre-dictions by conducting an online semantic self-check during training. A frozen LLM compares the generated report with the reference report and tags semantically conflicting spans using <e>...</e> (Sec. 3.2). These tagged spans yield sparse token masks merr and mcor , which drive a push–pull 

optimization objective: tokens inside error spans receive re-pulsive gradients to suppress the model’s current choice, while correct tokens are reinforced. The full objective integrates three components: (i) En-tropy–Confidence Weighting (Sec. 3.3.1), which offers a dual-scale uncertainty signal through token-level entropy and global calibration. (ii) CTAL: Correct Token Adaptive Loss , a local confidence-guided reinforcement term based on token probability; and (iii) ETAPL: Error Token Adap-tive Penalty Loss , a local confidence-guided penalty term that suppresses both overconfident and ambiguous errors. These losses are normalized over their respective masked regions, scaled to account for the sparsity of errors, and then combined with cross-entropy (CE) into a single objective (Sec. 3.3.2). This formulation explicitly routes gradient to clinically important tokens, while introducing no inference-time overhead .

4.2. Implementation Details 

We leverage LLaMA3-3B as the large language model (LLM) and Swin Transformer as the visual encoder. The loss balancing coefficient λ is set to 0.5. Training is con-ducted on two NVIDIA A6000 GPUs (48GB each) with a mini-batch size of 24 and a learning rate of 5e-5. For in-ference, we apply beam search with a beam width of 3 to balance computational efficiency and output quality. 

Evaluation Metrics We assess the generated reports us-ing NLG metrics, including BLEU [27], METEOR [1], and ROUGE-L [16], following the standard evaluation proto-col. For clinical relevance evaluation, CheXBert [8] is ex-cluded due to past inconsistencies in the calculation (e.g., micro/macro/weighted variants) and lack of transparency in prior works, hindering fair comparisons. Instead, we adopt more robust metrics—RadGraph F1 [9], BERTScore [41], and RadCliQ [40]—that better align with experts’ assess-ments. Notably, RadCliQ integrates CheXBert in a stan-dardized manner. To better assess semantic fidelity and clinical grounding beyond traditional metrics, we report two LLM based metrics: RaTEScore [42] and GREEN Score [26].

We compare our model with state-of-the-art (SOTA) meth-ods on the MIMIC-CXR and IU-Xray datasets, shown in Table 2. Table 3 presents clinical metric comparisons.

Two distinct error modes emerge: (1) Over-confident errors — low-entropy, high-confidence predictions that require strong penalization through ETAPL; and (2) Uncertain errors — high-entropy, low-confidence predictions that are softly corrected via entropy weighting. The co-existence of both modes empirically validates the necessity of our joint focal–entropy adaptive scheme. 

Methods marked with † are quoted from their original pa-pers, while others were re-run using publicly released code 35369 on the same training-test partition as ours. As shown in Table 2, our model outperforms these SOTA methods across almost all metrics on both datasets, including image captioning models [5, 22], transformer-based encoder-decoder [2–4, 7, 19, 36], and LLM-based methods [18, 28, 38]. Specifically, our model surpasses the LLM-based models R2GenGPT [38] and Bootstrap-ping [18] in BLEU score, with improvements of 7.5% and 12.5%, despite these two methods using larger LLMs (7B) compared to ours (3B). Additionally, methods lever-aging disease knowledge [2, 7] lack feedback mechanisms for guided learning, while Multi-Grained [17] employs sentence-level contrastive learning but lacks token-level su-pervision, limiting its effectiveness. EKAGen, which uses 300×300 images instead of the standard 224×224, is not di-rectly comparable but still underperforms our approach. On IU-Xray, our model consistently demonstrates strong performance. Beyond NLG metrics, overall, it achieves bet-ter performance on clinical relevance metrics—RadGraph F1, BERTScore, GREEN, RaTEScore, and RadCliQ. This improvement stems from our self-generated token-level su-pervision, which dynamically refines predictions by rein-forcing correct tokens and suppressing incorrect ones.

## 5. Conclusions 

We present SAT-RRG , a token-level self-corrective train-ing framework for radiology report generation. By leverag-ing an auxiliary LLM to localize semantic inconsistencies in the model’s own predictions, SAT-RRG applies targeted reinforcement and suppression through two adaptive losses, CTAL and ETAPL, modulated by entropy- and confidence-based weighting. This design enables precise correction of clinically meaningful errors beyond what standard CE train-ing can capture.
