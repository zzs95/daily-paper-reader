# Abstract 

Forecasting fetal growth from sequential ultrasound exami-nations is essential for personalized prenatal care. Existing medical vision-language models (MLLMs) are limited to single-phase/organ evaluations and qualitative reasoning, neglecting longitudinal history and precise continuous bio-metric values. To address this gap, we introduce the novel task that integrates multi-modal ultrasound examination re-sults at early pregnance stage to predict the subsequent fetal health status. To support this task, we first present GrowthFetus, the interesting multi-phase, multi-organ fe-tal ultrasound dataset to date, containing 9,280 examina-tions from 2,000 fetuses. Based on this dataset, we pro-pose F 2-Assist, a unified MLLM framework with three key components: (i) a Cross-Phase Organ Alignment module for heterogeneous multi-organ feature fusion across phases, (ii) a History-Aware Temporal Encoding module for mod-eling irregular temporal dynamics, and (iii) a Growth Pa-rameter Adapter that encodes continuous biometric values as differentiable tokens for numerically precise reasoning. Extensive experiments show that F 2-Assist achieves tempo-rally coherent predictions and clinically consistent reports, significantly outperforming state-of-the-art MLLMs. Our study establishes a practical framework for longitudinal ul-trasound analysis, bridging growth forecasting and report generation in a unified model.

Crucially, these sequential exami-nations encode individualized growth trajectories necessary for detecting abnormalities and guiding preemptive clinical decisions [13, 26]. However, existing computer vision methods, including recent MLLMs for report generation [14, 29, 31], are fun-damentally constrained to single examinations and suffer from two critical shortcomings (Fig. 1a, left): (i) Isolated (Single-Phase) Focus: Existing techniques are limited to single-phase and often single-organ evaluations ( e.g. , adult CXR/ultrasound reports) [17]. They treat each examination in isolation, fundamentally ignoring the patient’s essential longitudinal history. (ii) Inability for Quantitative Rea-soning: Current approaches are primarily qualitative, fo-cusing solely on textual description while neglecting crucial continuous biometric values, which carry significant diag-nostic and predictive weight. Consequently, the field criti-cally lacks a framework that can model multi-phase evolu-tion and predict future growth status. In this paper, we pioneer the novel and clinically cru-cial task of Multi-Phase Fetal Growth Forecast and Re-port Generation (Fig. 1a, right), which integrates multi-modal ultrasound examination results at early pregnance stage to predict the subsequent fetal health status. This task is uniquely challenging, demanding solutions to three in-trinsic technical hurdles: (1) Heterogeneous Multi-Organ Alignment : A single screening involves multiple, diverse organs from different imaging planes. This requires robust feature alignment and fusion across heterogeneous visual modalities before temporal modeling can begin. (2) Irreg-ular Temporal Modeling : Fetal growth is longitudinal, yet examinations occur at variable, unevenly spaced intervals. Current sequential models struggle to effectively capture the underlying continuity and individualized growth trends across these irregular histories. (3) Quantitative Reason-ing Limitation : Subtle numeric variations in biometry are clinically essential. Existing MLLMs lack the mechanism to ingest and precisely reason over these continuous, nu-merical values, limiting predictive accuracy [9, 27]. To address these challenges, we first contribute Growth-Fetus , the first large-scale multi-phase, multi-organ fetal ultrasound dataset, comprising 9,280 examinations from 2,000 unique fetuses. GrowthFetus dataset was compiled over a three-year period, tracking 2,000 fetuses through all ultrasound examinations from pregnancy to delivery. Each participant underwent a minimum of four prenatal ultra-sound scans. See more details in Section 4. Built on this foundation, we propose F 2-Assist, a unified MLLM framework specifically designed for this longitudinal task. GFGPT seamlessly integrates three novel, tightly coupled components that directly map to the aforementioned chal-lenges: (a) Cross-Phase Organ Alignment ensures seman-tic consistency of heterogeneous features; (b) Multi-Phase Growth Encoder models temporal continuity across irreg-ular phase histories; and (c) Growth Parameter Adapter 

enables the LLM to perform numerically precise quantita-tive reasoning via differentiable tokenization of continuous biometric values. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We pioneer the Multi-Phase Fetal Growth Forecast and Report Generation task and introduce GrowthFetus , the first large-scale multi-phase, multi-organ dataset to enable this crucial longitudinal research. • We propose F 2-Assist, a novel MLLM framework that unifies organ-level alignment, irregular temporal model-ing, and precise quantitative biometric reasoning. • Extensive experiments demonstrate that F 2-Assist pro-duces clinically consistent, temporally coherent predic-tions, significantly outperforming state-of-the-art MLLM baselines across both quantitative (biometry prediction) and qualitative (report generation) metrics. 

# 2. Related Work 

2.1. Vision–Language Models 

Contrastive vision–language pretraining aligns images and texts in a shared space, laying the foundation for multi-modal reasoning [12, 16, 18, 25]. Instruction-following MLLMs further interleave visual tokens with LLMs to support open-ended grounding and dialog [2, 6, 21, 33]. For temporal content, Video-LLMs inject frame-wise en-coders and video-aware query modules to capture dynam-ics [37, 39]. In medical imaging, temporal-aware vi-sion–language pretraining explicitly leverages prior images and reports to improve downstream performance [1]. Re-cent instruction-tuned medical VLMs (e.g., LLM-CXR) ex-plore bidirectional alignment and clinical instruction fine-tuning for chest X-ray understanding and reporting [14]. Despite the progress, most general VLMs are trained on static image-caption corpora and lack dedicated mecha-nisms for longitudinal patient modeling or numeric trend reasoning that are crucial in prenatal assessment. 

2.2. Spatio-Temporal Modelling 

Spatio-temporal learning has been widely studied in video understanding via 3D CNNs, temporal Transformers, and video-LLMs [39]. In biomedicine, temporal pretraining and graph Transformers model disease progression, with lever-aging prior images/reports. Ultrasound and clinical time-series use local–global temporal Transformers to capture motion from cine/4D data [30]. Beyond encoding, methods like RECAP reason across phases by predicting changes for reporting, HERGen integrates longitudinal data via group causal Transformer [29], and STREAM combines spatio-temporal modeling with retrieval [38]. However, existing approaches focus on visual dynamics and cross-phase re-trieval, while explicit modeling of numeric trajectories (e.g., fetal growth curves) and their fusion with visual/linguistic tokens remains under-explored. 35342 2.3. Ultrasound / Radiology Report Generation 

Classical report generation used CNN/LSTM or Trans-former decoders on single-phase images [5, 7, 8, 11, 16, 28, 36, 40]. With the advent of MLLMs, instruction-tuned medical models improve clinical correctness and control-lability for chest X-ray reporting [14, 15, 23, 32]. Tem-poral and longitudinal reporting has recently emerged: RE-CAP leverages predicted spatio-temporal progressions [10]; HERGen fuses historical and current studies for history-enhanced reporting [29]; STREAM augments temporal rea-soning with retrieval of patient-similar priors to boost fac-tuality and follow-up consistency [38]. Complementary to chest radiography, ultrasound-specific spatio-temporal models focus on cine dynamics or deformation estimation (e.g., TransVFS) [30], yet most works target classifica-tion/assessment rather than end-to-end multi-organ, multi-phase eport generation. Very recent frameworks further strengthen bidirectional image–report reasoning and multi-stage tuning, but they still seldom encode patient-specific numeric growth signals. 

Summary. Prior studies either (i) align images and texts without longitudinal structure, or (ii) model temporal changes without explicitly representing numeric growth, or (iii) handle ultrasound but stop at single-phase assessment. Our work bridges these gaps by jointly fusing multi-view ultrasound sequences with temporal and numeric embed-dings inside an MLLM, enabling personalized fetal-growth prediction and next-phase report generation.

3.2. History-Aware Temporal Encoding 

Fetal growth is nonlinear, patient-specific, and often irregu-lar due to variable clinical phases. To model these complex dynamics, we propose a History-Aware Temporal Encoding (HATE), a transformer-based module that extracts patient-specific growth signatures from variable-length phase his-tories. To handle irregular phase histories and nonlinear fetal growth, each cross-phase-aligned token {v1, . . . , vT } is augmented with a gestational-age-aware positional embed-ding πt, which informs the model of the relative timing of each phase. A causal temporal mask casual-mask (T ) en-sures that the encoding at each phase only attends to previ-ous phases, preserving chronological order and enabling the model to reason about progressive development. Formally, the phase-wise encoding is: 

zt = HATE (vt + πt, casual-mask (T )) . (9) HATE also employs multiple attention heads that focus on distinct developmental phases (early, mid, late gesta-tion). The final fused token zT encodes a compact, patient-specific growth signature that conditions future biometric regression and report generation. 

3.3. Growth Parameter Adapter 

Large language models (LLMs) excel at textual reason-ing but are insensitive to subtle numeric variations that carry strong clinical significance. To bridge this nu-meric–semantic gap, as shown in Fig. 2 (c), we introduce the Growth Parameter Adapter, which encodes continu-ous biometric vectors as differentiable soft tokens, enabling LLMs to jointly reason over textual and quantitative infor-mation.

3.4. Training Strategy 

To stabilize interactions among the visual encoders, tempo-ral module, and LLM, we adopt a two-stage curriculum: 

Stage I — Frozen LLM. All our introduced mod-ules, e.g ., Cross-Phase Organ Alignment, are optimized while keeping the LLM frozen, ensuring that organ-level tokens and temporal growth representations are temporally consistent. 

Stage II — LoRA-based LLM Refinement. The LLM attention layers are fine-tuned using LoRA, conditioned on the fused temporal-growth features. The model is trained end-to-end with a joint objective: 

L = Ltxt + λnum Lnum , (13) where Ltxt is the standard text-generation loss [20] and Lnum 

enforces precise numeric prediction. This curriculum im-proves convergence, stabilizes early training, and ensures that both textual and quantitative predictions are accurate. 

# 4. Experiments 

4.1. Experimental Setup 

Datasets and splits. 

Our GrowthFetus cohort strictly adheres to the Interna-tional Society of Ultrasound in Obstetrics and Gynecology (ISUOG) guidelines [13, 26] for routine prenatal screen-ing. According to these clinical protocols, each pregnant woman typically undergoes at least 3 to 5 scheduled ul-trasound examinations throughout pregnancy at key gesta-tional milestones: 11–14 weeks for early anatomy assess-ment, 18–22 weeks for mid-trimester anomaly screening, 28–32 weeks for fetal growth evaluation, and 36–40 weeks for pre-delivery assessment. Our dataset comprehensively covers gestational weeks 11 to 40 as shown in Fig. 3, spanning the entire prenatal pe-riod from the first trimester to term. All scans were acquired by the qualified sonographers in prenatal ultrasound screen-ing using clinical-grade ultrasound systems from multiple manufacturers (e.g., Samsung and Sonoscape) in compli-ance with standardized imaging protocols. Each examina-tion includes three standard anatomical views: the transven-tricular view at the level of the thalami for head biome-try (HC and BPD), the transverse view of the upper ab-domen for AC, and the longitudinal axis of the femoral dia-physis for FL. The complete cohort comprises 9280 exam-inations from 2000 unique fetuses collected between 2020 and 2023, with maternal age ranging from 20 to 45 years (mean: 32.3 ± 4.43 years), with an average of 4.43 phases per patient (range: 3–8 phases). We adopt patient-level 

splits to prevent leakage across phases: 70% /15% /15% for train/val/test. Unless stated otherwise, the default history length is N > =3 .

Implementation details. Images are resized to 448 2 and encoded by a CLIP-L vision backbone; Continuous biom-etry is z-normalized per gestational week and embedded by the Growth Parameter Adapter . Historical phases are aggregated by a Temporal Fusion Transformer with 4 lay-ers and 8 heads. We use a 7B Qwen as decoder, LoRA fine-tuning (rank 16) on the language layers and projector, AdamW with LR 2×10 −5, cosine decay, batch size 16, and 5 epochs. All models are trained on A100 GPUs under iden-tical hyper-parameters for fair comparison. 

Evaluation metrics. For report quality, we report BLEU-1/2/3/4 (B1–B4), METEOR (M), ROUGE-L (R-L), and CIDEr (CI). Together, they quantify both lexical and se-mantic fidelity between generated and ground-truth re-ports. For biometric forecasting, we measure MAE (cm), Accuracy@ ±5% and @ ±10% relative error, and R2 re-flects how well predicted growth trajectories explain the variance of actual measurements.
