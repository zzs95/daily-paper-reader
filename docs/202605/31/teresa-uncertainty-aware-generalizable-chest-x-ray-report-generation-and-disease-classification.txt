## Abstract 

Medical report generation (MRG) is vital for reducing workload of radiologists and improving clinical efficiency. However, generating accurate reports remains challenging due to the need for precise disease labeling and the uncer-tainty in medical image interpretation. Existing methods often struggle with ambiguous cases, especially when dif-ferentiating between normal and abnormal presentations. To this end, we present Teresa , a novel approach that enhances medical report generation and disease classifi-cation by explicitly addressing uncertainty at the sample level, emphasizing rare cases, and refining feature repre-sentations. Teresa employs an encoder-decoder architec-ture with an added disease classification branch, where di-agnostic results are transformed into textual prompts that guide the report generation. Additionally, Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence is used as a regularizer to minimize the di-vergence between the text decoder’s output distribution and a reference distribution. To evaluate robustness and domain generalization, we train the model on MIMIC-CXR dataset and directly test it on IU X-Ray dataset without fine-tuning, demonstrating strong cross-domain performance. Experi-ments on both datasets show that Teresa achieves state-of-the-art disease classification and report generation per-formance. 

## 1. Introduction 

Reports of X-ray images are vital in diagnostics, but man-ual reporting is time-consuming, error-prone, and burdens medical staff. Automated medical report generation (MRG) aims to address these issues [18, 22], but challenges remain due to the complexity of medical images, the imbalance of classes, and the uncertainty in radiographs. Such uncer-tainty may lead to false positives or false negatives in MRG, hence affecting diagnostic accuracy. Existing methods treat uncertainty as a separate class [17] or assign uncertain sam-ples directly to positive [11, 12] or negative cases, This may degrade performance and generate unreliable reports, re-quiring a refined approach to manage uncertainty and en-hance accuracy. Our goal is to mitigate uncertainty to en-hance model robustness, not to eliminate it from the output. Since training labels, whether generated by CheXbert or de-rived from higher-level uncertainty in the ground truth, in-herently contain ambiguity, these labels supervise the clas-sification branch, which then helps guide the final predic-tion of the model. As a result, while we reduce the im-pact of noisy labels during training, the generated reports continue to capture and reflect diagnostic uncertainty. This preserved uncertainty is not only realistic, but also clini-cally valuable, as it mirrors the ambiguity present in real-world expert interpretations and supports more cautious, in-formed decision-making. To this end, we propose Teresa ,a framework for the generation of chest radiograph reports and disease classification that systematically addresses un-certainty. Our Certainty Enhancement Module (CEM) cre-ates Aether Positive Codex (APC) using certain samples as anchors, enabling similarity-based labeling of uncertain samples. CEM eliminates ambiguity by assigning the pos-itive, negative or uncertain labels to the uncertain samples. When our proposed CEM is applied, the uncertainty is eradicated by assigning certain labels to the samples, as shown in Fig-ure 1(f). ## 2. Related Works 

Generating medical reports is similar to image captioning tasks in which the content observed in an image is de-scribed as textual information [13, 16, 28, 43]. Tradition-ally, encoder decoders have been used for image captioning [21, 26, 29]. Medical report generation (MRG) is nevertheless more challenging as it has multiple descriptive sentences. MRG has not only the challenge of long sentences but also the challenge of accuracy. Therefore various scientists have proposed LSTM based methods to tackle this problem [9, 39]. In some studies [1, 4, 32, 37], researchers have used the transformer architecture for more reliable reports. In medical reports, image and text matching [15, 30, 42] is essential and can be helpful for an accurate report gen-eration process. Therefore, some papers [5, 35] have used the extra memory knowledge to learn the cross-modal pat-terns. Knowledge graph [36, 41] is another approach used in the medical report generation process. Few works [10, 20] have investigated reinforcement learning in the medical report generation process. As medical report gen-eration is closely tied to disease detection and classification [15, 17, 37], recent methods have explored prompt-based strategies where classification outputs guide report gener-ation [17, 37]. However, the challenge of uncertainty in disease labels remains underexplored. Some works attempt to handle uncertainty by treating it as a separate class [17], but this often fails to resolve the confusion caused by am-biguous visual cues in complex radiographs. Others have proposed labeling uncertainty as either positive [11, 12] or negative, but both strategies introduce misclassifications, ei-ther increasing false positives or false negatives. Since un-certainty inherently includes aspects of both positive and negative cases, rigid labeling schemes often degrade model performance. While current approaches perform reasonably well even with large proportions of uncertain samples Figure 7(b), the presence of uncertainty still adversely affects the overall re-port quality and classification accuracy. To address this, we proposed a model that systematically handles uncertainty, resulting in efficient report generation and disease classifi-cation.

3.1. Certainty Enhancement Module (CEM) 

Prioritizing positive samples provides high-quality informa-tion about medical conditions, enabling the model to cap-ture class characteristics. CEM leverages this to define class-representative information and reduce sample uncer-tainty. Given an image set X = {x1, . . . , x n} and correspond-ing expert report set R = {r1, . . . , r n} with J types of dis-eases as given in labels set Y = {y1, . . . , y n} where each 

y ∈ RJ has one of the four statuses (Present:1, Absent:2, Uncertain:3, and Blank:0). The positive loader as shown in Figure 3 selects positive samples from Dtrain , which con-tains both positive and negative examples, where Dtrain is the entire set of training data. For each class indexed as i,we first select anchors, indicating the positive conditions for the given class. 

3.1.1. Generating Aether Positive Codex (APC) 

The anchors from the positive loader pass through a pre-trained ResNet-18 to construct APC, representing feature embeddings of certain positive classes. APC is formed by selecting positive samples per disease index (14 classes in our case), ensuring that each class is represented by one or more high-confidence samples whenever available. ## 4. Experiments 4.1. Datasets 1) MIMIC-CXR dataset [19], is a large-scale MRG dataset with chest X-rays and reports. The dataset has 270,790 training samples, 2,130 validation samples, and 3,858 test-ing samples. 

2) IU X-Ray dataset [6], is a relatively smaller dataset hav-ing 2,955 samples in total. Following existing literature [17], we evaluate the complete IU-X-Ray dataset using our model trained on MIMIC-CXR Dataset. 

4.2. Metrics 

We evaluate the classification model using clinical efficacy (CE) metrics, including precision, recall, and F1. For report quality, we use standard natural language generation (NLG) 3236 Figure 4. Comparison of per-disease F1 scores between PromptMRG and Teresa . Solid bars denote PromptMRG, while hatched bars represent Teresa . Higher values indicate better performance for each disease category. metrics like BLEU [33], METEOR [2], and ROUGE-L [23]. 

4.3. Implementation Details 

We use a pre-trained ResNet-101 [8] as the image encoder and BERT-base [7] as the text decoder. The AdamW opti-mizer [27] is applied with a 0.06 weight decay and an ini-tial learning rate of 5 × 10 −5, using a Cosine scheduler. Key hyperparameters include a 256 projection dimension, 0.07 temperature, and a contrastive weight λc of 0.1. The positive loader length is 14, ensuring coverage for all dis-ease classes. We use two similarity thresholds: samples with scores ≥ 0.6 are considered positive , those with scores 

≤ 0.4 are considered negative , and values in between are treated as uncertain , with normalized cosine similarity as: Cos. Sim. = (cos( θ)+1) /2. The number of training epochs is 10, batch size is 16 on a single NVIDIA A6000 GPU. 

4.4. Comparison with State-of-the-Art (SOTA) 

We compare Teresa with SOTA methods on MIMIC-CXR and IU X-Ray datasets Table 1 [5, 11, 14, 25, 31, 34, 38, 40]. On MIMIC-CXR, Teresa outperforms exist-ing SOTA methods on all CE metrics and two of the NLG metrics. On the IU X-Ray dataset, Teresa outperforms existing methods on all evaluation metrics. Compared to PromptMRG [17], Teresa demonstrates consistent per-formance gains on both the MIMIC-CXR and IU X-Ray datasets. On MIMIC-CXR, Teresa improves Precision by 5.0%, Recall by 12.4%, F1 by 15.1%, BLEU-1 by 4.0%, BLEU-4 by 3.6%, METEOR by 3.8%, and ROUGE by 1.1%. On IU X-Ray, Teresa achieves a 12.2% gain in Precision, 9.6% in Recall, 16.1% in F1, 6.2% in BLEU-1, 45.9% in BLEU-4, 20.6% in METEOR, and 17.1% in ROUGE. More comparisons with PromptMRG in Figure 4 show Teresa ’s higher F1-scores on individual disease classification, demonstrating its importance over the base-line. 

4.5. Analysis and Discussion 4.5.1. Ablation 

We conducted a thorough ablation study by training on the MIMIC-CXR dataset and directly evaluating on the IU X-Ray dataset to assess the impact of different components of Teresa on Clinical Evaluation (CE) and Natural Lan-guage Generation (NLG) metrics, as shown in Table 2. Starting with the baseline model, we progressively added Aether Positive Codex (APC), Contrastive Loss (CL), Fo-cal Loss, and KL divergence regularization (KL). Adding each component improves the performance over the base-line. However, the best performance is achieved when all components of Teresa are present during training, indi-cating their complementary contributions and their role in improving robustness under cross-dataset evaluation.

## 5. Conclusion 

We present Teresa , a unified framework for chest X-ray disease classification and medical report generation that explicitly addresses label uncertainty during training. The proposed Certainty Enhancement Module (CEM) re-fines ambiguous labels by leveraging high-confidence pos-itive samples as anchors, forming the Aether Certainty Codex (ACC) to provide more reliable supervision. This strategy improves label quality while still preserving clini-cally meaningful uncertainty within generated reports. To further enhance robustness, we incorporate focal loss to mitigate class imbalance and supervised contrastive learn-ing to strengthen feature representation, particularly for rare disease categories. Experiments on the MIMIC-CXR and IU X-Ray datasets demonstrate that Teresa 

achieves state-of-the-art performance across key classifi-cation and natural language generation metrics. More-over, cross-dataset evaluation shows that the proposed framework maintains strong performance under distribu-tion shifts, highlighting its effective generalization capabil-ity.
