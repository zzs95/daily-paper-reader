# Abstract 

Automated radiological report generation using artifi-cial intelligence can increase clinical throughput by creat-ing an assistive tool for physicians. In this study, we intro-duce llamaRG, a unified vision-language architecture built upon the LLaMA3.2-11B model that generates detailed and context rich free-form text reports from chest X-rays. Prior work commonly relies on dedicated image encoders paired with language decoders, requiring additional alignment or fusion stages. In contrast, llamaRG is built on a natively multimodal LLaMA3.2-11B backbone, allowing us to di-rectly use the advanced language understanding and rea-soning capabilities of a pretrained LLM. This unified de-sign supports end-to-end optimization and efficient cross-modal integration without external encoders. Additionally, the model is designed for multi-view radiology report gen-eration, enabling the model to capture the anatomical in-formation from different views of a chest X-ray. This, in turn, helps the model to behave similarly to how radiolo-gists create diagnostic reports based on multi-view evidence in real-world clinical workflow. To further enhance clinical fidelity and semantic coherence, we incorporate a second stage of training with Self-Critical Sequential Training as a reinforcement learning paradigm. In this phase, we utilise diagnostic and linguistic quality metrics to develop a hybrid reward function that further improves the generated reports. Comprehensive experiments performed on the benchmark-ing dataset MIMIC-CXR demonstrate the superiority of lla-maRG in terms of both textual fluency and clinical accuracy

# 1. Introduction 

Radiology images, such as chest X-rays, play a cru-cial role in the diagnosis of thoracic diseases [31]. Accu-rate interpretation of these images to write diagnostic re-ports is fundamental to clinical decision-making. How-ever, the process of creating detailed hand-written reports is time-consuming and susceptible to errors, often due to factors such as cognitive overload and fatigue in radiolo-gists [1, 19]. Moreover, the global shortage of trained ra-diologists further intensifies these challenges, contributing to diagnostic delays and longer patient wait times. In re-sponse to these challenges, automated Radiology Report Generation has garnered significant interest, aiming to gen-erate comprehensive diagnostic reports that are accurate and summarize all important radiological findings from the medical images [7, 36]. These tools can assist radiologists by generating preliminary diagnostic reports, thereby mean-ingfully reducing workload, improving clinical efficiency, and shortening patient wait times. Inspired by image captioning algorithms [13, 42, 49, 55], most existing studies for report generation generally com-bine an image encoder with a text decoder to generate re-ports. While these methods are effective in generating brief captions, these architectures often struggle with gen-erating long texts and structured medical narratives [30]. Subsequently, domain-specific architectures with memory-augmented or knowledge-enhancing modules were pro-posed to better capture the hierarchical narratives required This CVPR Findings paper is the Open Access version, provided by the Computer Vision Foundation. Except for this watermark, it is identical to the accepted version; the final published version of the proceedings is available on IEEE Xplore. 5745 for medical reporting [2, 9, 18]. The rationale behind mem-ory and knowledge augmentation is that many clinical find-ings recur across patients and are described using similar narrative templates; thus, retrieving and leveraging prior representations can guide the generation of consistent and accurate text. However, empirical results highlight the lim-ited text generating ability of these methods, often resulting in the use of repeated phrases and the omission of subtle diagnostic keywords. To address these limitations, researchers have explored the capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) to en-hance the quality of the generated text, with recent stud-ies demonstrating the ability of LLMs to better comprehend the semantics and syntax of multi-sentence texts [47]. Fur-thermore, LLMs have displayed potential as a decision sup-port tool for medical image analysis that can minimize di-agnostic delays and increase throughput [10]. Recent ap-plications of LLMs [11, 16, 45] in X-ray report generation have demonstrated that LLM-based systems outperform tra-ditional image-captioning approaches. Additionally, exist-ing implementations are typically restricted to a fixed num-ber of X-ray views when generating reports [3, 16, 35, 45], generally only the posteroanterior (PA) view. Clinically, radiologists integrate findings across all available views to ensure a complete assessment of anatomical structures and subtle abnormalities. To address this gap, our approach pro-vides a flexible framework capable of incorporating an arbi-trary number of views within an X-ray study, enabling more comprehensive and clinically faithful report generation. With the motivation to develop an LLM-based assistive tool for X-ray report generation, we have proposed a unified framework, llamaRG, which is built on the LLaMA3.2-11B Vision model. This model is fine-tuned using Low-Rank Adaptation [8] (LoRA) to generate semantically accurate, coherent, and clinically relevant reports from multi-view chest X-ray images. LlamaRG employs the LLaMA3.2 Vision backbone’s native cross-modal reasoning capability to integrate multi-view radiographs directly with free-form textual generation. This unified design eliminates modality bottlenecks and ensures end-to-end alignment between vi-sual and linguistic representations. We have incorporated a custom cross-attention mask into the LLaMA3.2 Vision model’s visual encoding pipeline. This aims to enable sep-arate attention between each view in a study and the gen-erated report, allowing the model to reason across comple-mentary anatomical perspectives and thereby enhancing the richness and accuracy of the resulting reports. Additionally, in contrast to many recent works that rely on auxiliary supervision data—such as diagnostic priors, prior study images, or disease labels to improve report ac-curacy [14, 16, 20, 23, 26]—our approach focuses solely on the images from the current study. This mirrors the realistic clinical workflow, in which previous scans are not always available and radiologists typically base their assessments on the multi-view images obtained during the current visit. Consequently, llamaRG offers better diagnostic practicality and a generalizable solution for real-world scenarios. Furthermore, as reinforcement learning has been shown to align LLMs more closely with the user’s intent [27, 58], we applied it to ensure that the generated reports better re-flect the clinician’s perspective and exhibit stronger linguis-tic fluency. To this end, llamaRG was further optimized using reinforcement learning with a custom reward that integrates both linguistic quality and clinical performance metrics, applied through Self-Critical Sequence Training. Our findings show that this approach yields substantially improved performance, surpassing multiple state-of-the-art baselines through multi-view alignment without relying on structured priors, as demonstrated in our experiments. Our main contributions in this study are as follows: • We propose a new multi-view report generation frame-work, where we have introduced all available views of a chest radiograph in a study to the LLaMA3.2 Vision model to generate free-form reports. • We have further used reinforcement learning to refine the generated reports to increase clinical relevance. • Our proposed framework demonstrates promising per-formance on the largest publicly available benchmark dataset, MIMIC-CXR, over various state-of-the-art meth-ods. 

# 2. Related Works 

Image Captioning. The goal of the traditional image captioning problem is to generate a sentence summary for the provided image. Over the last few years, researchers have made successful advances in this domain, with various methods [5, 24, 42, 49, 55, 57]. Most of these methods fol-low an encoder-decoder paradigm, where they utilize con-volutional neural networks (CNN) as the encoder and recur-rent neural networks (RNN) with a precise attention module to generate text from encoded image features [24, 28, 49]. Although these methods perform exceptionally well for im-age captioning, radiographic images contain fine-grained details that need to be interpreted in long, clinically appro-priate paragraphs instead of single sentences, which can be challenging for the model’s attention module. 

Medical report generation. Over recent years, auto-mated radiology report generation (RRG) has evolved in various directions. Early efforts focused on adapting hierar-chical LSTM architectures to better understand radiological images and produce detailed reports [50, 51, 53]. Further-more, people have utilized knowledge built from the reports and the radiology images to further aid the report genera-tion process. Chen et al. [3] and Wang et al. [44] intro-duced additional knowledge blocks that store key informa-5746 tion, which can be used to generate important insights in the report. The use of knowledge graphs has also been ex-plored in many works [9, 17, 18, 52, 56] to incorporate the relationships between diseases and organs and to mitigate data bias. Another tangent of research is to incorporate se-mantic information from the medical domain to guide the generation of free-text reports [11, 39, 54], such as the use of multi-label disease classification using RadGraph to as-sist in report generation [38]. Despite achieving exceptional results, these methods are trained to handle only single chest X-ray images and have access to limited anatomical charac-teristics. 

Large Language Models for Medical report genera-tion. Recently, there has been a significant advancement in RRG, largely driven by growing interest and remark-able progress in Large Language Models (LLMs) [22, 43]. Algorithms like R2GenGPT [45] improved radiology re-port generation by using frozen LLMs to decode the fea-tures extracted from X-ray images using a pre-trained im-age encoder. Furthermore, Jin et al. [11] proposed a method that incorporates diagnosis-driven prompts combined with adaptive loss along with image features to guide a pre-trained language decoder in generating clinically consistent texts. The ability of LLMs to handle multiple images has been explored in recent studies, where longitudinal X-ray data along with prior reports or clinical indications are provided to enhance contextual grounding [20, 22, 26]. Although multi-view learning has been shown, in general machine-learning settings, to improve cross-view consistency and representation quality [48], most medical-report-generation approaches remain limited to a single projection—typically the posteroanterior (PA) view. In practice, however, a sin-gle PA view may be insufficient—or even infeasible—for certain patients, necessitating additional projections to ob-tain a complete diagnostic picture. Moreover, reliance on auxiliary information such as disease labels or prior med-ical records limits the clinical applicability of some exist-ing methods which use longitudinal data. To address these challenges, we propose llamaRG, which captures spatial and anatomical information from all available projections within the current study and leverages these features to fine-tune an LLM to generate reports without requiring auxiliary data.

## 3.1. Overview 

We formulate automated report generation as a se-quence modelling task conditioned on multiple X-ray im-ages. Given N input X-ray images, I = {I1, I 2, . . . , I N }

with Ii ∈ RH×W where (H, W ) is the resolution of the image, our objective is to generate a free-form report, Y =

{y1, y 2, . . . , y T } where yt is the tth token in the report. We propose llamaRG, a unified multimodal framework with a backbone of LLaMA3.2 Vision model. In this method, we use the integrated vision-language cross atten-tion modules to jointly process visual and text tokens. This approach enables end-to-end optimization of the model and avoids modality fragmentation. As shown in Figure 1, our model llamaRG consists of two training stages: 1. Supervised fine-tuning: the pre-trained LLaMA3.2 Vision model is adapted to the radiology domain with cross-entropy optimization on image-report pair, 2. Reinforcement learning: the fine-tuned llamaRG model is further refined using Self-Critical Sequential Training with a composite reward function. 

## 3.2. llamaRG 

The first stage of training involves fine-tuning the base language model for which we have adopted the LLaMA3.2-11B Vision model. LLaMA3.2-11B Vision model is a large autoregressive, transformer-based language model devel-oped by Meta [25] capable of handling both image and tex-tual inputs with 11 billion parameters. 

Vision-Language Model Architecture. LlamaRG is built on the LLaMA3.2 Vision architecture, which com-bines a ViT-based visual encoder (vision tower) with an LLM backbone. In our setup, all available projections from a study are arranged into a single multi-tiled grid and passed through the vision encoder, enabling a joint representation of complementary spatial and anatomical cues. The result-ing visual tokens are then provided to the language model for report generation. As discussed in the next section, we further introduce a custom cross-attention mask to regulate interactions between different tiles of the image-grid tokens and text tokens. For every study, input images (I1, I 2, . . . , I N )—which represent the various available views of the chest X-ray— are first concatenated together to form a single composite tiled grid Igrid . This grid is then encoded by the vision tower, ensuring that this encoding incorporates information from all the different views while preserving the relative spatial arrangement. ## 3.3. Reinforcement Learning 

Following the supervised fine-tuning stage, we employ Reinforcement Learning (RL) stage to further optimize the proposed llamaRG model towards diagnostically meaning-ful reports.

# 4. Experiments 

## 4.1. Dataset 

For this experiment, we utilized the largest publicly available dataset MIMIC-CXR [12] for medical report gen-eration, which comprises paired free-form textual reports and chest radiographs. This dataset comprises 377,110 chest X-ray images and 227,835 reports from 64,588 pa-tients of the Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center exam-ined between 2011 and 2016. Each report in the dataset is composed of ‘FINDINGS’, ‘IMPRESSION’ and ‘INDI-CATION’ sections, along with multiple views of the chest X-ray. In this work, we adhere to the MIMIC-CXR’s offi-cial split of 7:1:2 of the entire dataset, as followed by the study [3]. Similar to the existing methods [21, 22, 45], we trained llamaRG to generate the ‘FINDINGS’ and ‘IM-PRESSION’ sections of the report from X-ray images. Ad-ditional details about the pre-processing of data are pro-vided in Appendix A.1. 

## 4.2. Experimental Settings 

Evaluation metrics. We evaluated the quality of the generated radiological reports using NLG metrics, adher-ing to the standard evaluation protocol 1. The NLG metrics used to evaluate the linguistic similarity between generated and reference reports, includes BLEU [29] scores, ROUGE-L [15], METEOR [6], and CIDEr [41]. To gauge the clini-cal accuracy of the generated reports, we followed [3, 4, 18] and further reported CE metrics. We utilized Precision, Re-call and F1 score to evaluate the model performance for di-agnostic accuracy using the CheXpert labeller [37], which automatically annotates reports into 14 different diagnostic categories. 

Implementation Details. All the input images were re-sized to 256 × 256 for this study. The training process was carried out on a single H200 70GB GPU (using mixed pre-cision) in two stages. In the first stage, we fine-tuned the llamaRG model for 8 epochs. We initialized the supervised fine-tuning from the official checkpoint for LLaMA3.2-11B Vision provided by Meta. The llamaRG model was paired with LoRA optimization, with a rank and alpha of 16. The Adam optimizer was employed for training with a learning rate of 2e-4. In the second stage, SCST training was em-ployed with a batch size of 8. During the testing phase, we implemented a beam search strategy with a beam size of 3.

## 5.2. Ablation Study 

LoRA-based finetuning layer selection. We conducted an ablation study to investigate the effect of including dif-ferent layers of the llamaRG model with LoRA-based fine-tuning. As illustrated in Figure 3, we studied three con-figurations for fine-tuning: A. finetune only the input to-ken embedding layer and the final language model head; B. finetune the input token embedding, image embedding layer, and the final language model head; C. finetune the projection matrices in all multi-head attention layers; and D. finetune both the attention projections and the embedding plus LM head. Results demonstrate that the setup ‘D’ pro-vides the best balance between capturing multi-view visual-textual feature alignment and adapting the vocabulary of the LLM to the radiology domain, yielding significantly higher performance than other setups across both NLG metrics, BLEU-1 and ROUGE-L. Figure 3(a) also shows that up-dating both the attention projection and language layers al-lowed faster convergence of the validation loss, highlighting improved training efficiency.

However, when the sample size was increased to 15k, per-formance deteriorated, indicating that excessive RL updates may lead to overfitting. 

# 6. Conclusion 

In this work, we present llamaRG , a framework de-signed to generate radiology reports from multi-view chest X-ray studies under realistic clinical conditions. Similar to a radiologist, llamaRG generates reports by capturing complementary spatial and anatomical information across different views and leveraging this information to generate clinically accurate reports. We first adapt the model to the radiological domain using supervised finetuning, and then further improve its performance using RL with a custom reward function. This training strategy enables llamaRG to generate accurate reports from the current study alone, without relying on auxiliary information such as disease la-bels or prior examinations, supporting broader applicability in real-world workflows. Experiments on the MIMIC-CXR dataset demonstrated that llamaRG outperformed the SOTA baselines, highlighting its potential as an assistive tool ca-pable of improving the diagnostic workflow in a practical clinical environment.
