# Abstract 

Visual Instruction Tuning (VIT) endows Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) with powerful generalization capabilities across diverse visual tasks. However, the re-dundancy present in VIT data leads to substantial computa-tional overhead, making data pruning critical for achieving efficient VIT. Existing pruning methods struggle to achieve a favorable balance between performance and efficiency. Sample influence-based methods rely on external validation data, causing the selected data to be biased toward samples that match the format of external data. Moreover, the com-putational overhead of calculating sample level influence function even exceeds the cost of training on the full dataset. To overcome these limitations, this paper casts data pruning as an optimization problem over subset mixing proportions and proposes the Mix-to-Max method. This method requires no external data and computes influence across data subsets to build an Influence Transfer Matrix (ITM). The ITM cap-tures the directional knowledge flow, which allows for a pre-cise estimation of synergy and redundancy among subsets to significantly improve data efficiency. Experimental results on the LLaVA-665K and Vision-Flan-186K datasets demon-strate that with just 20% of the data, Mix-to-Max achieves 98.82% and 98.45% of the full-dataset performance respec-tively, and reduces the total wall-clock time by over 70%. Our code is publicly available at: https://github.com/ew-zhao/Mix-to-Max.git.

Instead of determining which individual samples to keep, our work focuses on identifying the optimal mix-ture proportions across different data subsets. Furthermore, the computational burden of sample level evaluation is a significant concern. The resources con-sumed during this data selection phase may even exceed those required for training on the full dataset. Consequently, the practical cost-effectiveness of such approaches for data-efficient learning is highly questionable. Mix-to-Max operates at the sub-set level influence as depicted in Fig. 1(b). It employs subset influence functions to quantify the bidirectional knowledge transfer relationships among data subsets, thereby identify-ing key knowledge sources and redundant links. This en-ables us to directly and efficiently optimize the data mix-ture proportions for enhanced VIT data efficiency. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: • Mix-to-Max Data Mixture Framework. We propose a data-efficient VIT framework that reformulates data prun-ing as a subset mixture optimization problem. It enables principled data re-mixing by adjusting proportions based on quantified subset relationships, without external task data or complex filtering mechanisms. • Subset Influence Transferability Perception. To im-plement this framework, we introduce a subset influ-ence method to estimate learning dependencies. Through a Focused Influence Scores (FIS) strategy, our approach constructs an Influence Transfer Matrix (ITM) to effi-ciently quantify bidirectional knowledge transfer and re-dundancy across data subsets. Crucially, operating with-out external target-task data avoids the influence bias in-herent in prior influence-based methods. • State-of-the-Art Performance-Efficiency Balance. We demonstrate that Mix-to-Max achieves a new state-of-the-art balance between performance and efficiency. On the LLaVA-665K and Vision-Flan-186K, our method retains 98.82% and 98.45% of full-data performance using 20% of the data, while achieving a 70% reduction in wall-clock time and an 8.2 × speedup over ICONS, the primary influence-based competitor. 

# 2. Related Work 

Data Selection. Current research in VIT primarily fo-cuses on data selection techniques to address the data effi-ciency challenge. These methods aim to optimize data effi-ciency and maintain model performance by selecting high-quality and informative coresets from large-scale datasets. Given the significant parallels in SFT data selection for both Large Language Models (LLMs) and LVLMs, we dis-cuss these domains concurrently. Existing work has primar-ily developed along two main technical approaches. The first leverages external model scoring to optimize dataset quality and diversity, exemplified by InsTag [27] and Alpa-Gasus. The second utilizes model-internal information to measure candidate data value, including IFD [19], Super-Filtering [18], and Self-Filter [7]. However, some single-score methods often introduce significant biases, substan-tially compromising data diversity [37]. 

Data Mixture. The composition of data during VIT is a critical yet underexplored factor that dictates model perfor-mance. While leading open-source models like LLaVA se-ries [17, 23], Qwen-VL series [5] and InternVL [8] achieve state-of-the-art results by training on carefully curated data mixtures, the methodology for determining these optimal proportions remains a black box. This lack of a principled framework creates a significant barrier, forcing researchers into costly, ad-hoc experimentation and highlighting the need for an efficient method to optimize VIT data mixtures. Early empirical studies, such as the work by Dong et al. [9], were foundational in identifying the complexities of data mixing. They systematically revealed that naive data mixing leads to performance conflicts and that mix-ture ratios significantly impact model capabilities. How-ever, this work was purely observational, lacking a prescrip-tive method to proactively optimize these proportions. More recent data selection studies aim to reduce the bias introduced by single-score filtering by selecting represen-tative samples from multiple subsets, which is conceptu-ally similar to forming a data mixture. A prime example is COINCIDE [16], which introduces the valuable concept of transferability to create an effective coreset. However, COINCIDE operates at the fine-grained sample level, mak-ing binary keep/discard decisions based on inter-sample dis-tances in the representation space. This sample-centric ap-proach focuses on representativeness in the feature space but does not consider from a holistic dataset perspective 2938 Figure 2. Illustration of the Mix-to-Max framework. # 3. Methodology 

3.1. Preliminaries: Influence Function 

Influence functions [15, 31, 33] quantify how a training sample affects model predictions and have become a fun-damental tool for understanding data contributions in deep learning. Based on first-order gradient approximations [33], the influence of a training point z on a reference validation point z′ can be expressed as: Inf (z, z ′) = X

Higher influence scores im-ply z benefits learning on z′, highlighting its task relevance. Recent studies have leveraged this theoretical framework for data pruning. Although influence functions are effective for selecting data relevant to specific target tasks, such as in LESS [39], they are less suitable for achieving broad gener-alization across diverse tasks. ICONS [38] attempts to mit-igate this by defining multiple, diverse target tasks. How-ever, the requirement for corresponding external datasets substantially increases implementation complexity. Fur-thermore, due to the influence bias illustrated in Fig. 1, the selection of external data exerts a significant impact on the final pruning outcomes. To address these challenges, we first aim to eliminate the dependency on external data. One potential solution is to perform data pruning by computing influence functions among samples within the training dataset itself, thereby simplifying the implementation pipeline. However, since the training data also contains diverse formats, this internal-only approach remains susceptible to influence bias. To fur-ther refine the method, we treat data from different sources as distinct subsets, since samples originating from the same source tend to exhibit higher similarity. By computing in-fluence functions only between these subsets, we hypothe-size that the bias can be effectively mitigated. Building on this insight, we propose Mix-to-Max, a simple yet efficient SFT data re-mixing framework that directly optimizes data mixture proportions through subset influence functions. 

3.2. Proposed Method 

The Mix-to-Max framework is illustrated in Fig. 2. In the following sections, we describe its three core stages: (1) computing proxy gradients for each data subset (Sec. 3.3), (2) estimating subset influence using FIS (Sec. 3.4), and (3) optimizing mixture proportions and performing data re-mixing based on the computed subset influence (Sec. 3.5). 

3.3. Compute Proxy Gradients 

Following Equation 1, computing influence function values requires gradient information for each data point. How-ever, the massive parameter scale of LVLMs makes direct gradient computation and storage extremely costly. LoRA-based approximation and random projection can compress gradients into a lower-dimensional space and substantially reduce memory usage [38, 39]. Nevertheless, processing gradients for every sample in large scale VIT datasets re-mains computationally intensive. As a result, the pruning process itself may demand more resources than training the model on the full dataset. In Mix-to-Max, we only require influence estimation 2939 between data subsets, rather than across individual sam-ples. This enables a more efficient proxy strategy. Specif-ically, given a dataset D partitioned into N subsets 

{D1, D2, . . . , DN }, we first train a lightweight LoRA adapter on a small (5%) random portion of the training cor-pus as a proxy model, following [39]. Then, from each sub-set Di, we sample m proxy examples to form ˜Di and com-pute their gradients using the proxy model. Finally, each gradient is compressed via random projection to obtain a compact set of proxy gradients Gi = {gx | x ∈ ˜Di}.

3.4. Subset Influence Transferability Perception 

The computation of subset influence is a pivotal stage in the Mix-to-Max framework, forming the foundation for evalu-ating each subset’s contribution to the final pruned dataset. A natural strategy is to aggregate sample level influence values between two subsets by averaging them. However, this approach essentially measures only the average proxim-ity of their gradients in the representation space, capturing similarity rather than true knowledge transfer, as illustrated in Fig. 3(b). Consequently, it fails to uncover the directional and asymmetric dependency structures among data subsets. Moreover, simple averaging introduces an over-smoothing effect that may obscure the crucial transfer relationships be-tween subsets. To better elucidate the knowledge transfer dynamics between different data subsets, we propose the focused influence method. This approach captures the bidi-rectional learning dependencies between subsets by empha-sizing their most significant transfer connections.

3.5. Optimize Mixture Proportions 

Given the quantified ITM , the key challenge is how to ef-fectively leverage this information to optimize the mixture proportions of the data subsets. Intuitively, the optimization 2940 should prioritize subsets with high knowledge contribution by increasing their weights, while reducing the weights of those exhibiting high information redundancy. Following this principle, we design the following optimization strat-egy for determining the optimal mixture proportions. 

Subset Value Scoring. According to the directionality of 

ITM , cross-subset transfer capability can be decomposed into two directions: outgoing transferability and incoming transferability. If ITM ij denotes the transfer strength from subset i to subset j, this value simultaneously represents the outgoing transferability of subset i (toward j) and the incom-ing transferability of subset j (from i). Outgoing transfer-ability measures the average contribution of a data subset to improving the performance of models trained on other sub-sets, reflecting its role as a knowledge provider. Conversely, incoming transferability measures the extent to which a sub-set can be explained or represented by others, indicating its degree of informational redundancy within the overall dataset. Building on this analytical framework, we define two key indicators: • Knowledge Transfer Potential (KTP) : This metric quantifies the total outgoing transferability of a subset. It is calculated as the i-th row sum of the ITM : Ti =P

A high KTP indicates that a subset serves as a powerful knowledge source, whose contents broadly enhance the performance of models trained on other data. • Information Redundancy Index (IRI) : This metric quantifies the total incoming transferability of a subset, calculated as the column sum: Ri = P

Proportion Optimization. To guide the optimization of mixture proportions, we first formulate a subset value scor-ing mechanism that balances the KTP and IRI. The objec-tive is to assign higher proportions to subsets with strong knowledge contribution and low redundancy. Accordingly, we define the initial value score Si for each subsets as the difference between its KTP and IRI: Si = Ti − Ri. This score Si reflects a subset’s relative value as a knowledge provider versus its tendency to be a knowledge receiver. To utilize these scores as effective weighting coefficients in data re-mixing, we normalize them to [0 , 1] interval via a sigmoid function. In practice, the empirical range of the ini-tial scores Si (theoretically bounded within [−1, 1] ) is often concentrated in a very narrow sub-interval. A naive appli-cation of the sigmoid function to such clustered scores fails to produce weights with sufficient differentiation. To overcome this limitation, we introduce Dynamic Range Mapping (DRM). This procedure centers the initial scores Si relative to their mean and dynamically scales them to fit the sigmoid function’s effective input range. The final normalized score S∗

# 5. Conclusion and Limitations 

In this paper, we introduced Mix-to-Max, a simple yet pow-erful framework that boosts data efficiency simply by re-allocating mixture proportions across pre-defined subsets. Mix-to-Max demonstrates that, even without complex fil-tering pipelines, judiciously remixing data alone can re-cover full-data performance using only a fraction of sam-ples, achieving 98.82% of full-data accuracy with just 20% of the dataset and reducing training time by over 74.4%. The framework is also compatible with any score-based sampler. While random sampling performs surprisingly well, Mix-to-Max can readily incorporate stronger scoring methods, offering potential for further improvement. 

Limitations. Mix-to-Max faces two main constraints. First, it operates on subsets within a mixed dataset, requir-ing the dataset to be partitioned into subsets in advance. While large-scale datasets typically comprise multiple nat-urally occurring subsets, this requirement may still limit the method’s applicability. Second, the current implementation learns static optimal mixing ratios, which may not fully cap-ture evolving data utility during fine-tuning. Future work could address these limitations by exploring automatic sub-set partitioning strategies and developing adaptive mixing approaches that adjust dynamically throughout training, fur-ther enhancing the method’s flexibility and effectiveness. 2944 Acknowledgments
