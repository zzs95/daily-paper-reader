Abstract

Introduction:

Retained surgical items represent a critical yet preventable failure in surgical safety systems worldwide, reflecting systemic weaknesses that threaten patient outcomes. Persistent postoperative fistulae are a potential clinical marker of such retained foreign bodies, but they are often underrecognized, particularly in resource-limited settings. By reporting this case series, we aim to underscore the diagnostic value of wound fistulae as a sentinel sign of retained surgical items and to highlight the ongoing gaps in surgical safety systems.

Case presentation:

A

Two Black African women presented with chronic, non-healing wounds and persistent fistulous drainage following surgery. The first case involved a 46-year-old woman with abdominal pain and wound discharge persisting for five months after hysterectomy. Imaging revealed a retained abdominal pack that had migrated into the bowel, resulting in an ileo-ileal fistula. The second case described a 45-year-old woman with six months of neck wound drainage after thyroidectomy, despite multiple unsuccessful surgical attempts; exploration uncovered retained gauze within a fistulous tract. Both patients underwent surgical removal of the foreign bodies with full symptom resolution.

Conclusion

These cases expose the systemic deficiencies in operative safety that permit the occurrence of retained surgical items, leading to severe patient harm and prolonged morbidity. They highlight the urgent need to reinforce surgical safety culture, ensure strict compliance with established checklists, and implement reliable detection protocols. Addressing these systemic gaps is essential to prevent such avoidable complications and improve patient safety outcomes globally.

Introduction

Retained surgical items (RSI) are defined as unintended surgical items left inside a patient’s body after surgery, representing a serious but preventable complication with potentially devastating clinical and medicolegal consequences [1, 2]. Despite advances in surgical safety measures, including standardized sponge and instrument counts and the widespread adoption of the World Health Organization (WHO) Surgical Safety Checklist, RSI events continue to occur across diverse healthcare settings worldwide, ranging from high-resource to low-resource environments [3, 4]. These “never events” typically result in chronic postoperative complications such as persistent wound infections, non-healing wounds, fistula formation, and occasionally life-threatening sepsis [5].

The presence of a wound fistula, an abnormal communication between the surgical site and skin, especially when associated with serous or purulent discharge that fails to resolve with conventional treatment, should raise high suspicion for a retained foreign body [6]. However, the diagnosis may be delayed due to nonspecific symptoms, lack of awareness among healthcare providers, and variability in access to advanced imaging modalities, leading to repeated consultations, unnecessary treatments, and increased patient morbidity [7, 8].

Importantly, RSI occurrences are usually attributable to failures in established intraoperative protocols, such as erroneous or incomplete surgical counts, breakdowns in communication within the surgical team, and deficiencies in safety culture [9]. In regions affected by prolonged armed conflict and resource limitations, challenges to maintaining consistent patient safety practices are magnified, compounding the risk of such adverse events [10, 11].

The global surgical community recognizes the critical need for strengthening patient safety culture through multidisciplinary teamwork, standardization of operating room procedures, ongoing staff training, rigorous adherence to checklists, and the incorporation of adjunct technologies when feasible [3, 9]. These measures are vital not only to prevent RSI, but also to restore patient trust and reduce the considerable financial and emotional toll imposed by these avoidable complications [2, 12].

A

In this context, we present a case series of two patients who developed persistent wound fistulae secondary to retained surgical items after abdominal and neck surgical interventions. By highlighting the clinical features and surgical outcomes, our report emphasizes the recognition of fistula formation as a potential red flag for RSI and stresses the need for strict adherence to surgical safety protocols. This advocates for strengthening the patient safety culture to prevent such avoidable “never events”, particularly in resource-constrained and conflict-affected healthcare environments.

Case Series Description

Case 1

A 46-year-old female presented with a five-month history of intermittent colicky abdominal pain accompanied by persistent serous discharge from an abdominal wound site. The symptoms began two weeks after the patient underwent a total abdominal hysterectomy. The patient reported multiple prior consultations with different healthcare providers and had undergone various empirical treatments without clinical improvement. She denied systemic symptoms such as fever, constipation, diarrhea, or respiratory complaints. Her past medical history was unremarkable.

On physical examination, the patient’s vital signs were stable and within normal limits Abdominal examination revealed a soft, non-distended abdomen with mild tenderness localized to the lower quadrants; no guarding or rigidity was elicited. A small (0.5 x 0.5 cm) wound was identified 3 cm inferior to the umbilicus, exuding serous fluid (see Fig.1). Bowel sounds were present and normal on auscultation.

Image of the abdominal wound, the yellow arrow indicates the site of the fistula opening.

Laboratory workup noted a mild anemia (hemoglobin 10.4 g/dL), while white blood cell count, electrolytes, blood glucose, and renal function tests were all within normal ranges. Initial abdominal ultrasonography suggested a retained foreign body, which was corroborated by contrast-enhanced computed tomography (CT) showing a foreign object penetrating the ileal lumen (see Fig.2)

Fig. 2

Contrast-enhanced computed tomography in axial (A), sagittal (B), and coronal (C) planes reveals a retained surgical pack, partially projecting into the bowel lumen, highlighted by a yellow arrow.

Based on clinical examination and investigations, the diagnosis of a retained abdominal surgical item was made, and the patient was scheduled for surgery.

Following preoperative optimization, informed consent was obtained, and the patient underwent an exploratory laparotomy under general anesthesia. Intraoperative findings revealed a retained abdominal pack lodged approximately 85 cm proximal to the ileocecal valve, and an ileo-ileal fistula proximal to the pack. The fistulous tract also communicated externally through the abdominal wound (see Fig.3). Surgical management involved en-bloc resection of the involved ileal segment containing the foreign body and fistula, followed by end-to-end ileo-ileal anastomosis. Following copious irrigation of the abdominal cavity, the fascia was closed and the skin was left approximated, with delayed primary closure planned.

Postoperative recovery was uneventful. The patient resumed oral sips on postoperative day 1, received analgesics for five days and antibiotics for seven days. Wound closure was completed on day 5, and the patient was discharged on day 9. Case 2

A 45-year-old female was referred with a persistent swelling and serous discharge from a neck wound. The problem persisted for six months following right thyroid lobectomy. The patient underwent three distinct local wound explorations in response to a persistently non-healing wound, two of which were performed abroad in separate countries, all without resolution. Her medical history was notable for two previous cesarean sections but otherwise unremarkable.

Physical examination revealed a healed collar scar with a focal 1 x 1 cm wound demonstrating granulation tissue and serous exudate (see Fig.4). Vital signs were within normal limits. An ultrasound examination suggested a subcutaneous nodule, and thyroid function tests were normal.

Fig. 4

Neck wound with granulation tissue

A provisional diagnosis of a chronic wound associated with a persistent tract was established, and after obtaining informed consent, the patient underwent surgical exploration. Intraoperatively, a fistulous tract was identified extending to a retained small gauze, which was thoroughly removed. The wound was debrided, irrigated, and closed primarily (see Fig.5)

Intraoperative visualization of the retained gauze, identified by a yellow arrow, retrieved through a neck collar–type incision. Postoperatively, the patient received analgesics and antibiotics for 48 hours and was discharged on postoperative day 3. By day 8, the wound showed adequate healing, permitting suture removal. Discussion

Retained surgical items are a critical source of postoperative morbidity, leading to severe complications that negatively impact patient recovery, health outcomes, and overall healthcare costs [9, 13]. Beyond the clinical consequences, retained surgical items also trigger serious medicolegal ramifications. Healthcare providers and institutions face significant legal actions, financial liabilities, and damage to their reputations [2, 9, 14]. The two cases presented exemplify not only the clinical challenges in diagnosing RSI, but also the systemic failures in patient safety protocols that continue to allow these events to occur. Both cases shared a common clinical presentation, chronic non-healing wounds accompanied by fistulae draining serous secretions, which should raise suspicion for RSI, especially when standard treatments fail to produce improvement.

RSI often presents with nonspecific symptoms such as pain, infection, or fistula formation, which can mimic other more common postoperative complications [5]. The patients in this series initially sought care multiple times from various clinicians and facilities without resolution or diagnosis, illustrating the diagnostic difficulties encountered. This delay often results from low clinical suspicion compounded by incomplete histories, lack of awareness, inadequate imaging, and nonspecific symptoms [5, 15]. In this small series, the diagnosis for the first patient was established only with the help of imaging - initially ultrasound, followed by CT scan. For the second patient, the diagnosis was made intraoperatively during surgical exploration. Although plain radiographs are traditionally considered the gold standard for detecting retained surgical items, given that such objects typically appear as radiopacities with associated mass effect, mottled air, or increased density in surrounding soft tissues [16], this approach has limitations in our context. In several hospitals within our region, locally made small gauze and abdominal packs used during surgery are not radio-opaque. Consequently, CT scanning is the preferred diagnostic modality, as it allows for the identification of both radiopaque and non-radiopaque foreign bodies, along with associated inflammatory changes or fistulous tracts [8, 17].

Both patients endured repeated healthcare visits and interventions, including unnecessary procedures, incurring financial costs and psychological distress. This cycle reflects a critical gap in the healthcare system’s ability to promptly identify and manage RSI, prolonging patient suffering and increasing resource utilization. Such scenarios are particularly devastating in resource-limited settings or regions where patients bear significant out-of-pocket expenses [18, 19].

Retention of surgical items is considered a "never event" in modern surgical practice and generally reflects failures in established intraoperative safety measures, such as surgical count protocols and instrument tracking [1]. Despite global initiatives like the World Health Organization Surgical Safety Checklist, adherence varies widely, and lapses continue to occur, especially in regions with strained healthcare infrastructures [20, 21]. These cases highlight systemic vulnerabilities in operative safety processes that allowed the retention of surgical materials.

In the context of regions affected by prolonged armed conflict, like the setting of these cases, healthcare systems face additional challenges including resource scarcity, workforce shortages, and disrupted training programs [10, 22]. These factors compound the risk of safety breaches and necessitate targeted approaches to reinforce patient safety culture through training, implementation of standardized protocols, audits, and multidisciplinary teamwork.

Enhancement of patient safety culture must be prioritized, with strict enforcement of surgical count protocols, use of adjunct technologies where feasible and continued education regarding the clinical signs suggestive of RSI such as non-healing wounds and persistent fistulae.

Conclusion

Retained surgical items cause preventable chronic wounds and fistulae, leading to patient harm and increased healthcare costs. Strengthening adherence to safety protocols and raising clinician awareness, especially in resource-limited settings, is critical. Future efforts should focus on implementing advanced tracking technologies and promoting a robust patient safety culture to eliminate these avoidable errors.
