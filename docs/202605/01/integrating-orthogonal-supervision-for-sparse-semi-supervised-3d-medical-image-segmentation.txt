## Introduction

Volumetric medical image segmentation has seen significant improvements due to the success of deep learning techniques (Litjens et al., 2017). While volumetric imaging data is abundant, obtaining full 3D annotations remains a time-consuming and labor-intensive task, as it requires pixel-wise annotation across all slices (Çiçek, Abdulkadir, Lienkamp, Brox, Ronneberger, 2016, Shen, Peng, Wang, Wang, Cen, Jiang, Xie, Yang, Tian, 2023). To alleviate this burden, semi-supervised learning approaches have been widely adopted. These methods train segmentation models using a small amount of labeled data along with a large pool of unlabeled data, effectively reducing the reliance on full annotations (Kumari and Singh, 2023). However, most existing semi-supervised segmentation methods still assume access to full 3D annotations for each labeled volume, which is inefficient-especially since adjacent slices in a volume often contain highly redundant information, making it redundant to annotate every slice (Cai, Li, Qi, Yu, Shi, Gao, 2023a, Çiçek, Abdulkadir, Lienkamp, Brox, Ronneberger, 2016).

Due to the high similarity between adjacent slices in volumetric medical data, radiologists typically annotate only a few representative slices within a volume, leaving the rest unlabeled (Wang, Huang, Yuan, Shen, & Kang, Yan, Cai, Zhang, Cao, Liu, 2025, Zhang, Liu, Duan, Wang, Cai, Li, Dong, Zhang, 2024). Building on this practice, several methods have been developed that utilize fully volumetric data while relying on annotations from only a limited number of slices per volume (Cai, Qi, Yu, Shi, Gao, 2023b, Çiçek, Abdulkadir, Lienkamp, Brox, Ronneberger, 2016, Li, Cai, Qi, Yu, Shi, Gao, 2022a). However, these approaches still require manual labeling for every individual case, which remains a significant burden for radiologists. Furthermore, many existing methods are originally designed for 2D segmentation tasks and are not well suited to fully exploit the spatial continuity inherent in 3D volumetric data (Chen and Hong, 2022).

To address the aforementioned limitations, recent methods have emerged that focus on learning from sparse annotations in semi-supervised setting, focusing on a more challenging but clinically realistic scenario. In particular, these approaches leverage a small number of labeled samples alongside a large pool of unlabeled data, following a typical SSL paradigm. However, in this setting, each 3D labeled volume contains only a few annotated slices, while the remaining slices remain unlabeled. This setup is referred to as the Sparsely Annotated Semi-Supervised Framework (Yan et al., 2025) or the Barely Supervised Framework (Cai, Li, Qi, Yu, Shi, Gao, 2023a, Cai, Qi, Yu, Shi, Gao, 2023b, Wu, Xu, Tong, 2025). In line with these methods, we refer to our approach as Sparsely Annotated Semi-Supervised Medical Image Segmentation. The aim of these methods is to reduce annotation effort while maintaining competitive segmentation performance.

In this context, **Desco** (Cai et al., 2023a) uses two orthogonal slices per volume in a semi-supervised setting and employs registration techniques to propagate labels across the volume. It further introduces a co-training strategy, where one model receives true supervision from axial slices and another from coronal slices (Figure 1 (a)), while pseudo-labels generated from each model are used to supervise the unlabeled slices in the other. However, relying on only two orthogonal slices can lead to an incomplete representation of the volume’s data distribution (Yan et al., 2025). To address this, **SGTC** (Yan et al., 2025) proposes annotating three orthogonal slices per volume in a subset of volumetric samples. SGTC introduces a dense tri-view co-training scheme using three separate models, as shown in Figure 1 (b). Each model is supervised with labels from two of the three anatomical planes, for example _E s_(_x l, x u_) with coronal and axial slices, _E c_(_x l, x u_) with sagittal and axial slices and so on. For unlabeled volumes, the segmentation predictions from each model act as cross-supervision signals for the others. In addition, SGTC leverages a pre-trained CLIP model to enable semantic-aware and fine-grained segmentation.

Despite the success of existing methods, the aforementioned approaches use only a subset of the available anatomical supervision to train each model and subsequently generate pseudo labels for the entire volume. This strategy exposes each model to only a partial, view-specific perspective of the anatomy, which can lead to incomplete and biased learning. Given that the available supervision is already sparse, we argue that it is more effective to combine all supervisory signals from orthogonal views in a unified and cohesive manner. By leveraging the complementary information present in axial, sagittal, and coronal planes simultaneously, the model can develop a richer and more holistic understanding of the volume’s data distribution-even under limited supervision. Based on this insight, we propose Integrating Orthogonal Supervision (IOS), a framework that employs a single encoder to learn from all anatomical views and construct a unified knowledge representation.

Moreover, to effectively learn from both unlabeled slices within sparsely labeled volumes and completely unlabeled volumes, we adopt a Tri-Decoder Framework (Figure 1). This design offers three key benefits. First, to learn from unlabeled slices, we employ a cross-decoder supervision strategy (Chen et al., 2021), where one decoder _D_(_x l_; _ϕ_) guides the learning of the other decoder _D_(_x l_; _ξ_), and vice versa. This mutual learning framework enhances the model’s ability to leverage information from unlabeled slices. Second, the pseudo-labels collaboratively generated by the two decoders _D_(_x l_; _ϕ_) and _D_(_x l_; _ξ_) are used to supervise a third decoder _D_(_x l_; _θ_), which is trained solely on unlabeled data. These collaboratively generated pseudo-labels are more reliable and complementary than those produced by a single decoder, as each decoder refines the other’s predictions from different representational perspectives-thereby enhancing the overall consistency and robustness of unlabeled supervision. Third, the framework establishes a disentangled data flow (Zhang et al., 2025) that separates the training streams of sparsely labeled (_D_(_x l_; _ϕ_) and _D_(_x l_; _ξ_)) and unlabeled data (_D_(_x l_; _θ_)). This separation enables the model to effectively extract semantic features from partially labeled data while mitigating the adverse influence of noisy unlabeled predictions on segmentation performance.

The main contributions of our work are as follows: (1) We propose an Integrating Orthogonal Supervision (IOS) training strategy that learns from all orthogonal anatomical views simultaneously, enabling the model to develop a richer and more holistic understanding of the volumetric data distribution-even under limited supervision. (2) We employ a Tri-Decoder Framework to effectively learn from unlabeled slices within sparsely labeled volumes as well as completely unlabeled volumes. A cross-decoder supervision strategy is applied between two decoders to enhance learning from unlabeled slices, while the pseudo-labels jointly generated by these decoders are used to guide learning on unlabeled 3D volumes. The combined pseudo-labels provide more reliable supervision, and the overall design establishes a disentangled learning flow between sparsely labeled and unlabeled data. Unlike existing methods, our approach does not rely on any external registration libraries or pre-trained vision-language models such as CLIP, yet it outperforms state-of-the-art alternatives. We validate the effectiveness of our method on the LA, KiTS19 and pancreas datasets, where experimental results demonstrate superior performance over existing approaches.

## Orthogonal vs. Parallel Annotation

Orthogonal annotation (OA), where a few slices-or even a single slice-are labeled in the axial, coronal, and sagittal directions, has been shown to be more effective and efficient than parallel annotation, where slices are labeled only along a single direction. This superiority arises from OA’s ability to preserve anatomical disparity and enhance feature consistency (Cai et al., 2023a).
