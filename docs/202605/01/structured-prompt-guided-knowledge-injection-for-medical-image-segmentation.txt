## Abstract

Accurate medical image segmentation remains a longstanding challenge due to anatomical variability and limited annotations. In this paper, we propose Structured Prompt-guided Knowledge Injection Network (SPKI-Net), a novel framework that integrates structured clinical knowledge into visual representations to enhance segmentation performance. SPKI-Net employs a training strategy that exploits both general and task-specific structured attribute texts to learn semantically informed visual representations, while retaining only a category-level text cue during inference for efficient deployment. Specifically, we propose a Structured Prompt Fusion (SPF) module that encodes hierarchical clinical priors derived from structured attribute texts. These attribute texts are constructed using a large language model under a predefined structured prompt template and are encoded via CLIP to provide fine-grained semantic guidance during training. Then, a Cross-modal Prompt Injection (CPI) module is introduced to inject text-derived semantic embeddings into the final-stage encoder features, thereby enhancing cross-modal representation learning. Moreover, we present a Morphological Boundary Refinement (MBR) module that employs morphological operations to improve boundary delineation. Extensive experiments on multiple benchmark datasets demonstrate that SPKI-Net achieves state-of-the-art or strongly competitive performance medical image segmentation methods. The code is available at [https://github.com/YiyangZhao-NJUST/SPKI-Net](https://github.com/YiyangZhao-NJUST/SPKI-Net).

## Introduction

Medical image segmentation is a fundamental component in clinical workflows, offering quantitative anatomical information critical for disease diagnosis, surgical planning, and prognostic evaluation [1]. Segmentation accuracy is clinically critical: inaccuracies at lesion boundaries have been associated with increased intraoperative complication rates, while precise anatomical delineation is essential for radiotherapy dose planning and control. Therefore, developing accurate and robust automated segmentation algorithms is imperative for enhancing procedural safety, treatment reproducibility, and overall clinical outcomes.

Recent advances in medical image segmentation have been dominated by convolutional neural networks (CNNs) [2], [3] and Transformer-based architectures [4], [5], [6]. While CNNs effectively capture hierarchical spatial features, their limited receptive fields and static convolutional kernels constrain modeling flexibility. Transformers address this limitation through self-attention mechanisms that model global context, enhancing adaptability. However, both architectures typically specialize in individual segmentation tasks, exhibiting performance degradation when applied across diverse anatomical structures, lesion characteristics, or clinical contexts. This task-level generalization gap presents a fundamental barrier to developing unified segmentation frameworks for diverse clinical applications.

Recently, vision-language frameworks, particularly Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP), have emerged as versatile backbones for medical image segmentation, alleviating the brittleness of task-specific architectures. By fusing free-form text with visual cues, they infuse dense semantic priors that soften reliance on any single modality. For example, MCL [7] enhances pathological representation via paired image-text inputs, while MedCLIP [8] enables joint training on unpaired data sources across modalities. Beyond these representative studies, existing methods have explored several cross-modal fusion strategies to bridge textual semantics and visual representations, including direct concatenation, feature modulation, and cross-attention-based interaction. These paradigms improve visual-text coupling to different extents, but the injected semantics are often generic and are not explicitly tailored to lesion attributes or morphology-aware guidance. However, existing CLIP-based segmentation methods face several critical limitations. First, most approaches perform coarse-grained semantic alignment at the image or region level, lacking mechanisms to map fine-grained textual cues to specific pixel-level regions. Second, textual knowledge is typically integrated in a static and predefined manner, which constrains the model’s adaptability to different imaging modalities, anatomical heterogeneity, and task-specific requirements. Third, semantic injection mechanisms typically introduce high computational costs, limiting their applicability in real-time deployments. These limitations are illustrated in Fig. 1, which provides a comparative visualization of representative segmentation approaches. In Fig. 1(a), conventional CLIP-based models that rely solely on coarse class-level prompts (_e.g._, “tumor”) tend to produce diffuse and semantically ambiguous attention patterns, highlighting their limited capacity for precise localization.

In this paper, we propose a novel Structured Prompt-guided Knowledge Injection Network (SPKI-Net) that explicitly integrates clinically structured knowledge into visual features to enhance medical image segmentation. As shown in Fig. 1(b), our method incorporates structured priors, including general and task-specific structured attribute texts. This structured attribute design enables the model to adaptively capture detailed lesion characteristics, such as anatomical location, boundary clarity, and internal morphology, thereby providing fine-grained guidance that steers attention toward clinically relevant regions. In this paper, “fine-grained semantic alignment” refers to region-level correspondence between slot-wise textual semantics and spatial lesion responses, rather than coarse image-level matching. In our framework, this property is realized through the CPI-based visual-text interaction and is reflected by more focused qualitative localization as well as improved downstream Dice and IoU scores. The term “modality-adaptive” means that the same prompt scaffold and overall network design are retained across modalities, while only the modality-dependent attribute vocabulary is redefined according to the target imaging scenario. Specifically, we present a Structured Prompt Fusion (SPF) module to encode hierarchical clinical priors derived from structured attribute texts into semantic embeddings. Then, a Cross-modal Prompt Injection (CPI) module is proposed to integrate these embeddings into visual features via attention-based alignment to enhance semantic consistency. Moreover, we propose a Morphological Boundary Refinement (MBR) module, which explicitly models anatomical contours using prior-guided structural enhancement. This modular design enables SPKI-Net to deliver accurate and reliable segmentation across diverse tasks, offering strong adaptability to varied clinical scenarios.
