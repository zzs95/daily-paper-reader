## 1. Introduction

### 1.1. Background and development trends of medical MLLMs

In recent years, general-domain MLLMs have advanced rapidly. Representative models, such as GPT-4o [[1]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0001), Qwen-VL [[2]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0002), and LLaVA [[3]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0003), achieve cross-modal information fusion by aligning visual and textual inputs within a unified vector space. By combining fine-grained visual feature extraction with strong language understanding and generation, these models have substantially improved cross-modal semantic understanding and generation performance [[4]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0004). Building on these advances, Medical MLLMs have emerged to handle complex medical data, including electronic health records, consultation records, medical images, and physiological signals, thereby improving the efficiency of medical information processing and clinical question answering while advancing intelligent healthcare services. Recent studies have adapted general-domain MLLMs to medical scenarios through continued pre-training (CPT) and instruction fine-tuning (IFT), leading to representative Medical MLLMs. For example, Med-Flamingo [[5]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0005), based on OpenFlamingo-9B [[6]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0006), applies CPT to medical image–text pairs and interleaved image–text data to acquire generative medical question-answering capability. Moreover, LLaVA-Med [[7]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0007) uses GPT-4 to construct medical instruction data and performs IFT on LLaVA-7B [[3]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0003), significantly improving performance in multitask medical question answering and medical text generation.

Current research on Medical MLLMs reflects several major trends, including deeper multimodal integration, transparent reasoning processes, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and stronger privacy protection. First, because medical scenarios inherently involve multi-source heterogeneous data, models may overcome the inherent “information silo” limitation of traditional models through cross-modal alignment, cross-validation, and complementary feature fusion, thereby supporting the development of intelligent cross-modal diagnosis and treatment. Second, to satisfy clinical demands for interpretability and traceability, Medical MLLMs are shifting from merely generating correct results toward transparent reasoning processes. For example, supervised reasoning fine-tuning [[8]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0008) and deep reinforcement learning (Deep-RL) [[9]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0009) can enhance both the transparency of reasoning and the logical consistency of responses. Meanwhile, to improve the standardization of medical terminology and the reliability of medical facts, the RAG framework has been widely adopted to retrieve and integrate evidence from external authoritative knowledge bases, thereby generating more standardized and verifiable responses [[10]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0010). In addition, given the sensitivity of patient data and the need for privacy protection, Medical MLLMs should also incorporate techniques such as data anonymization, reinforcement learning (RL) safety constraints, and encryption to reduce privacy leakage risks and improve system security [[11]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0011), [[12]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0012). To facilitate readers’ understanding of the core concepts and abbreviations used in this survey, the key terms and their definitions are summarized in [Table 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0001).

### 1.2. Advantages of medical MLLMs

Traditional medical AI models are mainly designed for recognition and prediction in a single modality and a single task. For example, Zhu et al. [[13]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0013) proposed an improved YOLOv8-BCD model for pulmonary nodule detection on CT images. Huang et al. [[14]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0014) used 3D U-Net for brain tumor segmentation on MRI images. Akhtar et al. [[15]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0015) developed RSG-Net for diabetic retinopathy grading from fundus photographs, and Liu et al. [[16]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0016) applied a CNN model to single-lead electrocardiograms for arrhythmia classification. Although these models achieve strong performance in specific scenarios, they are generally trained for particular tasks and single data modalities, and thus remain limited by task isolation, strong dependence on annotated data, weak cross-task generalization, and difficulty in incorporating complex clinical context.

Compared with traditional medical AI models, Medical MLLMs demonstrate more evident advantages in clinical application potential and task adaptability. First, they support natural language interaction, enabling communication with clinicians in clinical language and allowing follow-up questions, clarification, and supplementary information exchange, which makes them better aligned with real-world clinical workflows than the fixed-label or single-value outputs of traditional models [[17]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0017), [[18]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0018). Second, Medical MLLMs are more versatile, as a single model can support multiple tasks, including medical visual question answering (Med-VQA), medical report generation (Med-RG), case summarization, structured information extraction, disease coding, and triage recommendation. This overcomes the fragmented “one model for one task” paradigm and facilitates the sharing of medical knowledge and representations across tasks [[19]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0019), [[20]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0020). In addition, by leveraging the attention mechanism to model contextual information and long-range dependencies, Medical MLLMs can more effectively integrate multimodal information, such as medical images, text, medical history, and medical test results, thereby enhancing comprehensive understanding and decision support in complex clinical scenarios [[21]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0021). Finally, unlike traditional medical AI models, which are often trained in an end-to-end ”black-box” manner, Medical MLLMs can output intermediate reasoning processes, diagnostic evidence, and clinical reasoning paths in text form, thereby improving the interpretability and clinical reference value of their outputs [[22]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0022).

### 1.3. Objectives of this survey

Several review studies have presented the progress of MLLMs in the medical domain from different emphases. For example, Xiao et al. [[23]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0023) systematically reviewed the evolution from large language models (LLMs) to MLLMs, covering technical foundations, application scenarios, challenges, and future directions. Yi et al. [[18]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0018) focused on the applications of Medical MLLMs in radiology, particularly Med-RG and Med-VQA. AlSaad et al. [[21]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0021) emphasized multimodal data fusion and summarized the applications, technical challenges, and development trends of Medical MLLMs. Although these studies have provided valuable insights into the functions of different modules and their collaborative mechanisms, they still lack vector-level analysis of the core modules, as well as a systematic summary of methods for activating the reasoning capabilities of Medical MLLMs and overcoming the limitations of superficial token matching in self-supervised learning. In contrast, as illustrated in [Fig. 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0001), this survey provides a comprehensive analysis of the three core modules of Medical MLLMs from input to output, reveals the detailed flow of feature vectors across modules, and systematically categorizes medical datasets according to model requirements to help researchers efficiently identify relevant resources. In addition, this survey explores methods for enhancing the reasoning capabilities of Medical MLLMs to improve the transparency, interpretability, and safety of their outputs, and summarizes advanced training and evaluation strategies aimed at overcoming the bottleneck of superficial symbolic matching. The specific objectives and main contributions are as follows:

*   •
This survey provides a vector-level analysis of the core framework of Medical MLLMs, systematically organizes and categorizes existing medical datasets, and comprehensively discusses training methods, including supervised learning, RL, and parameter-efficient fine-tuning. It aims to help researchers rapidly identify relevant resources and select appropriate training strategies according to task requirements, thereby supporting more effective model development.

*   •
This survey explores in detail the key approaches for activating the reasoning capabilities of Medical MLLMs, including supervised reasoning fine-tuning and Deep-RL techniques. Its objective is to improve the interpretability and accuracy of the reasoning process, thereby improving their ability to handle complex medical tasks effectively.

*   •
This survey systematically examines the major challenges currently faced by Medical MLLMs and discusses corresponding potential solutions. Additionally, it offers a forward-looking analysis of the future development of Medical MLLMs from three perspectives: technical pathways, verifiable hypotheses, and expected progress, aiming to provide insights for future research and theoretical support for their further application in clinical settings.

### 2.1. Medical report generation

Med-RG is a core component of clinical decision support systems, and its workflow generally consists of two stages, as shown in [Fig. 2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0002). The first is the Findings stage, in which lesions are detected and quantitative features are extracted from multimodal medical data, such as CT, MRI, and ultrasound images. The second is the Impression stage, in which the abnormalities identified in the Findings stage are combined with medical knowledge for diagnostic reasoning and the generation of structured medical reports [[24]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0024). This technology is valuable for improving report generation efficiency and healthcare quality, and reducing diagnostic variability caused by human factors. Its detailed workflow is described below.

#### 2.1.1. Findings stage

As shown in [Fig. 2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0002), the Findings section is used to describe observations from medical images, including organ structures, lesion morphology and location, the extent of pathological changes, and relevant radiological features. This stage requires both careful image observation and accurate identification of abnormalities. Owing to the comprehensive and fine-grained nature of medical imaging, the Findings stage plays a central role in Med-RG, serving as the key stage for extracting medical information and generating descriptive observations. By using the visual encoder to extract information from medical images, the text encoder to capture patients’ clinical text data, and the text decoder to generate descriptions, Medical MLLMs are able to identify and express these medical findings [[40]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0040). In addition, given the complexity of medical images and the need for fine-grained analysis, the Findings stage can incorporate not only raw medical images but also region-level prompts, such as bounding boxes or segmentation masks annotated with Regions of Interest (ROI) (see [Section 4.2.2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#sec0038)). These additional prompts can improve the accuracy and completeness of medical findings [[41]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0041).

#### 2.1.2. Impression stage

Compared with the Findings stage, the Impression stage is intended to summarize and synthesize the observations, transforming complex imaging details into clinically meaningful diagnostic conclusions. This stage requires strong medical reasoning ability to derive disease classification, identify urgent abnormalities, and formulate accurate diagnoses based on the findings. It also requires strong comprehension and extensive medical knowledge, while ensuring logical consistency and interpretability in clinical decision-making. Since Medical MLLMs may lack domain-specific terminology and standardized diagnostic expressions, RAG can be introduced at the impression stage [[10]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0010) (see [Section 4.2.3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#sec0039)). By retrieving medical knowledge using key information from the Findings stage and selecting the most relevant entries as contextual references, Medical MLLMs can standardize medical terminology and further improve their outputs.

### 2.2. Medical visual question answering

Med-VQA aims to address clinical problems by leveraging the complementary information of multimodal data. Such tasks require not only cross-modal semantic understanding, but also accurate comprehension of user questions and effective application of medical knowledge to generate accurate answers [[47]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0047). Med-VQA can be broadly divided into two categories: Open VQA and Close VQA. These two paradigms complement each other and jointly drive the clinical translation and maturation of intelligent Med-VQA systems [[48]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0048).

#### 2.2.1. Close VQA

Close VQA provides a rigorous benchmark for assessing models’ mastery of medical knowledge. Its main characteristics are as follows: (i) A well-defined answer space: because the number of candidate answers is limited, Close VQA enables more precise evaluation of a model’s medical judgment and memorization ability. The model is required to rapidly match and select an answer from a predefined answer space [[49]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0049). (ii) Lower annotation cost: Close VQA only requires establishing correspondences between questions and fixed answer options, without manually writing long-form answers. This makes dataset construction more efficient and cost-effective, and thus suitable for building large-scale training and test sets [[50]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0050), [[51]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0051). (iii) Limited clinical applicability: Compared with Open VQA, Close VQA is more suitable for large-scale benchmark evaluation, but is less aligned with real-world clinical tasks, because most clinical questions cannot be fully constrained to a fixed set of answer options. Therefore, its value lies primarily in evaluation rather than direct clinical decision-making [[52]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0052).

#### 2.2.2. Open VQA

Open VQA refers to a task in which the model generates free-form and relatively detailed answers based on the input medical images and question, without relying on predefined answer options. Medical Open VQA has the following characteristics: (i) Open-ended responses: As shown in [Fig. 2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0002), answers in open-ended medical tasks are not restricted by a predefined set of candidate options. Compared with Close VQA, these questions are more complex, requiring Medical MLLMs to integrate information from medical images, question text, and clinical records to understand the question and generate free-form responses [[5]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0005), [[53]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0053). (ii) Terminological rigor: Because commonly used evaluation metrics, such as BLEU, recall, and ROUGE-L, emphasize token-level overlap with reference answers, the responses generated by Medical MLLMs must not only be semantically consistent with the correct labels, but also use medical terminology precisely. This imposes stricter requirements on both accuracy and expression [[54]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0054). (iii) Closer clinical relevance: Compared with Close VQA, Open VQA better supports multi-turn interaction and contextual understanding, making it more consistent with real-world clinical communication and diagnostic needs. It can be applied to diverse scenarios, including medical research, clinical decision-making, and medical education [[55]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0055).

### 2.3. Performance comparison and discussion of clinical value

To more comprehensively evaluate the practical value of Medical MLLMs, this survey, in its analysis of the results presented in [Tables 3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0003) and [4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0004), considers not only commonly used metrics such as BLEU, METEOR, ROUGE-L, accuracy, and recall, but also factors including parameter efficiency, clinical utility, robustness, and potential failure modes. Overall, the performance of Medical MLLMs on Med-RG and Med-VQA has shown a steady upward trend, but it does not increase strictly with model size. Across different tasks, the visual encoder, cross-modal alignment modules, and the quality of domain-specific instruction data often have an equally important or even greater impact on performance. In addition, these evaluation methods are largely based on textual overlap between predictions and reference answers, which cannot fully reflect the clinical value of outputs generated by medical MLLMs. Therefore, beyond quantitative metrics, this section further examines model performance in terms of clinical factual consistency, cross-scenario robustness, and failure modes, allowing for a more clinically meaningful comparison.

#### 2.3.1. Performance comparison of med-RG models

In recent years, Medical MLLMs have achieved notable progress in Med-RG tasks, with continuously improved performance on the MIMIC-CXR dataset [[56]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0056). MIMIC-CXR is one of the largest publicly available chest X-ray datasets, containing a large number of chest X-ray images and corresponding radiology reports, and has been widely used for radiology report generation and medical image analysis. In this section, the performance of different models is compared using the text similarity metrics described in [Section 6.1.2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#sec0062), including BLEU, METEOR, and ROUGE-L.

[Table 3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0003) compares several representative Medical MLLMs. Overall, Med-RG performance on MIMIC-CXR has shown a gradual upward trend over time, but this improvement does not correspond to a simple linear increase in parameter scale. Early methods, such as CMN, CGPT2, and MET, achieved relatively stable BLEU and ROUGE-L scores with smaller model sizes, suggesting that Med-RG performance depends not only on model scale, but also on image-text alignment and decoder design. In the era of LLMs, although models such as LLaVA-Med, XrayGLM, ClinicalBLIP, MAIRA-1 and EVOKE have substantially more parameters, their gains are not consistent across metrics. For example, ClinicalBLIP and EVOKE achieve relatively high ROUGE-L scores, whereas MAIRA-1 performs better on BLEU-4 without leading on ROUGE-L. Likewise, although LLaVA-Med and XrayGLM exceed 7B parameters, they do not show a clear overall advantage over smaller models on the reported metrics. This indicates that performance improvement in Med-RG arises from the joint effect of parameter scaling and architectural optimization, rather than from parameter expansion alone.

From the perspective of evaluation metrics, [Table 3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0003) also reveals distinct performance profiles across models. BLEU emphasizes local n-gram matching and is therefore more sensitive to consistency in report phrasing and template-like expressions. By contrast, METEOR and ROUGE-L are more sensitive to lexical variation, recall, and long-sequence matching, and thus better reflect semantic coverage and textual organization. As a result, some models show limited gains on BLEU but perform better on METEOR or ROUGE-L, suggesting that their improvements are more related to enhancing the overall completeness and semantic coherence of generated reports, rather than merely optimizing local lexical overlap [[63]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0063).

#### 2.3.2. Performance comparison of med-VQA models

The Med-VQA task is intended to evaluate the comprehensive capabilities of a model in medical image understanding, domain knowledge transfer, and text generation. In this section, several representative Medical MLLMs are compared, as shown in [Table 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0004). These models have been systematically evaluated on benchmark datasets such as VQA-RAD [[64]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0064), SLAKE [[65]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0065), and PathVQA [[50]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0050). The general evaluation metrics adopted in [Table 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0004) are as follows: for Close VQA, accuracy is used as the evaluation criterion; for open-ended questions, recall is employed to assess the degree of lexical coverage between model outputs and reference answers [[3]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0003), [[7]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0007), [[40]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0040).

As shown in [Table 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0004), relatively strong performance can be achieved on Close VQA even by models with smaller parameter sizes, suggesting that such tasks impose strong constraints on the answer space and mainly rely on stable visual discrimination and cross-modal matching. For example, small and medium-scale models such as Q2ATransformer, PubMedCLIP, BiomedCLIP, and LaPA have already reached relatively high performance on Close VQA, whereas some large general-purpose models do not show clear advantages in medical settings. In contrast, Open VQA places greater demands on language generation, instruction following, and medical knowledge integration. Models must not only recognize image content, but also generate answers that are semantically complete, terminologically accurate, and highly aligned with the question, leading to more pronounced performance differences. As [Table 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0004) shows, improvements in Open VQA generally lag behind those in Close VQA. In particular, on datasets such as PathVQA, early models achieved relatively low Open VQA performance, whereas recent models such as LLaVA-Med++ and SigPhi-Med have shown more substantial gains, reflecting the gradual improvement of Medical MLLMs in complex generative tasks. It should also be noted that Med-VQA performance is not determined by parameter scale alone, but by the combined effects of visual representation quality, cross-modal alignment strategies, training objectives, and task matching. Domain-pretrained visual encoders and more effective alignment modules can improve the mapping of medical image features into the language space, thereby enhancing answer accuracy and consistency. Meanwhile, high-quality medical instruction data, question-answer template design, and task coverage directly influence generation quality in Open VQA. This also explains why some small- and medium-scale models can approach or even outperform larger models on specific datasets: when model architecture is better matched to the task scenario, parameter efficiency is often higher. Overall, the performance differences shown in [Table 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0004) reflect the combined effects of model scale, domain adaptation, and architectural design, rather than simple competition in parameter quantity.

#### 2.3.3. Clinical value and discussion

Although metrics such as BLEU, METEOR, ROUGE-L, accuracy, and recall in [Tables 3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0003) and [4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0004) can reflect the overall performance trends of Medical MLLMs on Med-RG and Med-VQA, they mainly measure similarity between predicted and reference texts and therefore cannot fully capture more clinically important factors, such as clinical utility and robustness. Existing studies have shown that traditional lexical-overlap metrics are only weakly correlated with clinical value, whereas evaluation methods that capture clinical failure modes align more closely with expert judgment, indicating that surface-level textual similarity alone is insufficient to fully assess clinical utility [[66]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0066). In addition, manual review of MAIRA-1 further suggests that automatic metrics may overlook important failure modes. On the one hand, model performance may be overestimated by automatic evaluation metrics due to noise in the training data. On the other hand, models may generate plausible but unsupported quantitative descriptions, such as numerical statements without actual measurement evidence. These issues are difficult to detect using conventional text-similarity-based metrics. Therefore, the evaluation of Medical MLLMs should also consider clinical utility, robustness, and failure modes.

As reflected in [Table 3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0003), improvements in Med-RG are not only shown by higher metric scores, but also by their potential value in real reporting workflows. Representative models such as MAIRA-1 and EVOKE demonstrate that recent advances are no longer confined to expanding generator parameters, but increasingly emphasize directions such as domain-specific visual encoders, multi-view information modeling, and the use of patient-specific information, which better align with Med-RG’s requirement for contextual integrity. Recent clinical studies also suggest that multimodal models have practical assistive value in radiology. For example, the clinical evaluation of CheXagent showed that its draft reports could reduce report processing time for resident physicians by about 36%, without an obvious decline in overall report quality. This suggests that evaluation frameworks for Med-RG should incorporate the efficiency and quality gains brought by human-AI collaboration, as this may better reflect the model’s true clinical value than relying solely on automatic evaluation metrics such as BLEU and ROUGE.

For the Med-VQA task shown in [Table 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0004), clinical risk is not limited to incorrect answers, but also includes two more subtle problems: correct answers based on unreliable evidence and instability under variations in question phrasing. Recent work on HEAL-MedVQA [[67]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0067) showed that medical multimodal models in VQA often exhibit a phenomenon of ”getting the answer right for the wrong reasons” in which the models may rely on language co-occurrence patterns or attend to irrelevant regions when answering, thereby suggesting that shortcut behavior may be present in answer generation. Consistently, RoMed [[68]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0068) shows that high scores on standard metrics such as accuracy and recall do not necessarily correspond to stronger robustness: under semantically equivalent paraphrases and multi-level perturbations, high-performing models represented by LLaVA-Med still exhibit a clear decline in answer consistency, indicating that high-scoring models may not remain stable under question rephrasing and changes in clinical expression. It also explains why the Open VQA results in [Table 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0004) should still be interpreted with caution. Although methods such as Med-MoE, SigPhi-Med, and LLaVA-Med++ have achieved improvements in parameter efficiency or benchmark performance, existing studies on these methods have mainly focused on architectural design, lightweight modeling, and the optimization of data or training strategies. Therefore, accuracy or recall alone remains insufficient to demonstrate their clinical value and robustness. Overall, the clinical evaluation of Med-VQA should go beyond conventional accuracy and recall, and further include evidence localization consistency, stability under question rephrasing, and grading of error consequences, so as to better reflect the usability and risk boundaries of these models in clinical question-answering settings.

## 3. Core architectures of medical MLLMs

### 3.1. Overall architecture of medical MLLMs

As shown in [Fig. 3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0003), the overall architecture of Medical MLLMs consists of three core components: a visual encoder, a modality alignment module, and LLMs [[69]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0069). Specifically, the visual encoder first transforms medical images into high-dimensional visual feature vectors, namely visual tokens. Subsequently, the modality alignment module further maps these visual tokens into a representation space that is semantically aligned with text, producing visually aligned tokens that can be understood and processed by the language model. Finally, the LLMs concatenate the visually aligned tokens with the instruction tokens encoded from the input text, forming a multimodal joint input, and then generate responses autoregressively based on this input [[5]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0005), [[7]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0007), [[70]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0070). During generation, the token prediction at each time step depends on visual information, instruction information, and the tokens that have already been generated. The currently predicted token is then successively appended to the generated token sequence. This process is repeated until an end-of-sequence token is produced, ultimately resulting in the complete answer [[7]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0007), [[71]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0071).

### 3.2. Visual encoder

In Medical MLLMs, the visual encoder is responsible for converting medical images into high-dimensional visual feature vectors and serves as the key component for providing visual information to the language model. Although current mainstream LLMs perform well in language understanding and generation, they lack visual encoders and therefore cannot process image data, resulting in a form of ”blindness” to visual information. For this reason, a visual encoder must be introduced to enable visual-language integration. Medical images can generally be divided into two modalities, namely 2D and 3D. The following sections separately discuss the design principles of visual encoders for these two types of images.

#### 3.2.1. 2D visual encoder

2D visual encoders are primarily designed for two-dimensional medical images, such as X-rays, pathological slides, and ultrasound images. Their input is a 2D pixel matrix of size H ± W, which is transformed into high-dimensional visual features through convolution or self-attention mechanisms, providing compact and expressive visual representations for subsequent multimodal alignment and language generation. In terms of architecture, classical convolutional networks such as ResNet [[72]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0072) and DenseNet [[73]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0073), as well as Vision Transformer (ViT) frameworks [[74]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0074), can be adopted. As shown on the left side of [Fig. 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0004), 2D ViT typically divides an image into fixed-size patches, which are then flattened, linearly projected into a visual embedding space, and combined with positional encodings to form a sequence of visual tokens containing positional information. Subsequently, these tokens are further processed by multi-head attention to enable global interaction and feature fusion. In terms of training, 2D visual encoders are commonly pretrained using contrastive learning, classification-task pretraining, and self-supervised learning [[5]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0005), [[75]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0075), [[76]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0076). Specifically, contrastive learning enhances the consistency between visual and textual representations by pulling positive image-text pairs closer and pushing negative pairs farther apart in the embedding space. Classification pretraining relies on manually annotated data to enhance the model’s discriminative ability. Self-supervised learning acquires general representations from unlabeled data through tasks such as masked reconstruction. During the subsequent IFT and supervised fine-tuning (SFT) stages, the visual encoder is usually kept frozen to preserve the visual knowledge obtained during pretraining.

#### 3.2.2. 3D visual encoder

3D visual encoders are mainly designed for volumetric medical images, such as CT and MRI, as well as medical videos. Their input typically consists of stacked 2D slices, while in some cases the temporal dimension can also be treated as the third axis to capture dynamic changes. Compared with 2D encoders, 3D visual encoders not only extract intra-slice features such as textures, edges, and structures, but also model inter-slice or temporal relationships, thereby providing richer contextual information for lesion localization, volumetric understanding, and disease progression analysis [[33]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0033). Common architectures include 3D convolutional neural networks (3D CNNs) [[77]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0077) and 3D Vision Transformers (3D ViT) [[78]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0078). As shown on the right side of [Fig. 4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0004), a 3D ViT typically first divides a 3D medical image into multiple blocks or slices, then flattens them into feature vectors, which are projected through a shared linear mapping and combined with the corresponding positional encodings before being fed into the visual encoder. Under the multi-head attention mechanism, these vectors interact and fuse with other block vectors, ultimately producing visual representations that encode the information of the entire image. In terms of training, contrastive learning, classification pretraining, and self-supervised pretraining commonly used in 2D encoders are also applicable to 3D visual encoders. However, due to the scarcity of large-scale 3D medical pretraining data, 3D encoders are often not fully frozen during the subsequent IFT and SFT stages, and partial unfreezing or end-to-end optimization is generally more suitable for further enhancing volumetric understanding.

### 3.3. Modality alignment module

In Medical MLLMs, the effective integration of visual and textual information is essential. Without modality alignment, directly feeding visual signals into LLMs may lead to severe semantic misalignment and hallucinated responses [[79]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0079). The core challenge of modality alignment lies in achieving cross-modal semantic alignment, namely, establishing accurate semantic mappings between different modalities such as vision and text. This section systematically reviews four mainstream alignment methods from three perspectives: technical principles, optimization for medical applications, and comparative analysis with selection recommendations.

#### 3.3.1. MLP alignment

As shown in the Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) alignment module in [Fig. 5](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0005), each high-dimensional feature vector produced by the visual encoder is individually mapped by a shared-weight MLP into a semantic space consistent with text embeddings, generating aligned visual vectors that are then used as prompts together with the encoded textual instruction as input to LLMs, thereby achieving cross-modal feature alignment. Since the features of different patches are projected independently while sharing the same MLP parameters, this method reduces the training cost of modality alignment while preserving parallel computation and parameter sharing [[7]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0007), [[24]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0024). In the medical domain, the application of MLP alignment has gradually evolved from simple feature projection to multi-granularity semantic alignment tailored to medical data. For example, Med-MoE [[38]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0038) trains only the MLP projection layer after the visual encoder during multimodal medical alignment, enabling a preliminary mapping of medical image tokens into the language semantic space with low parameter cost. Furthermore, MedPLIB [[41]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0041) extends this process to region-level and even pixel-level prompts, supporting various region inputs such as points, bounding boxes, and free-form shapes, so that MLP alignment not only performs visual-language mapping but also integrates local lesion evidence, thereby enhancing the model’s perception and expression of fine-grained medical findings.

#### 3.3.2. Cross-attention alignment

The cross-attention alignment follows the cross-attention mechanism in the original Transformer architecture [[71]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0071). As shown in the Cross-Attention module in [Fig. 5](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0005), its core idea is to map image features produced by the visual encoder into keys (K) and values (V), while the text decoder provides queries (Q) during token generation and performs attention computation with the keys and values, enabling the text side to dynamically retrieve and integrate relevant visual information throughout the decoding process. With stacked cross-attention layers, image-text interaction is progressively deepened. In Medical MLLMs, this usually enables LLMs to continuously inject visual evidence while preserving contextual semantics, thereby achieving deeper fusion of visual and textual tokens. In the medical domain, this alignment strategy is mainly applied to multi-granularity feature modeling and local lesion enhancement. For example, Li et al. [[80]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0080) use hierarchical cross-attention to separately extract global organ features and local lesion features, strengthening organ-lesion relationship modeling. Wang et al. [[81]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0081) further proposed a multi-granularity cross-modal alignment framework, which achieves fine-grained alignment between local image patches and text tokens through bidirectional cross-attention, and demonstrated superior performance over traditional methods on the Med-RG task.

#### 3.3.3. Q-former alignment

As shown in the Q-Former alignment module in [Fig. 5](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0005), Q-Former [[82]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0082) employs a set of learnable query vectors as soft prompts to extract key information from the high-dimensional features produced by the visual encoder through cross-attention, and maps them into visual aligned vectors with the same dimensionality as text embeddings. These vectors are then concatenated with textual instruction embeddings and fed into LLMs, enabling efficient bridging and multimodal fusion between visual information and language models. In the medical domain, the optimization of Q-Former mainly focuses on visual token compression and adaptation to 3D medical images. For example, Moor et al. [[5]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0005) proposed Med-Flamingo, which introduces Q-Former to compress visual features into more compact prompts, thereby reducing the context length input to LLMs and improving inference efficiency, with an approximately 20% improvement on human-evaluated generative Med-VQA. Chen et al. proposed MedBLIP [[70]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0070), which freezes both the visual encoder and LLMs, employs contrastively trained MedQFormer for multimodal alignment, and adapts 3D medical data to a 2D image encoder through sub-volume partitioning, linear projection, and positional encoding, thereby enabling effective 3D medical image understanding and achieving state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on Alzheimer’s disease brain imaging VQA tasks.

#### 3.3.4. Textual transformation alignment

As shown in the text transformation alignment module in [Fig. 5](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0005), medical images, such as X-rays, MRI, and CT, are first processed by multiple computer-aided diagnosis (CAD) models [[83]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0083). Serving as the image-perception front end for LLMs, these models generate structured textual descriptions based on predefined prompt templates and use them as contextual inputs to the LLMs. The LLMs then integrate the visual diagnostic information provided by different CAD models to generate medical reports or diagnostic recommendations [[84]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0084). In the medical domain, this alignment strategy has evolved from an early one-step pipeline that directly converts CAD outputs into natural language prompts to a more complex framework integrating multi-CAD diagnosis, retrieval augmentation, and task adaptation. For example, Wang et al. proposed ChatCAD [[85]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0085), which reformulates the outputs of CAD networks for diagnosis, lesion segmentation, and report generation into natural language text, and then uses LLMs for summarization, explanation, and interactive generation, thereby improving the interpretability and usability of medical image analysis results. Zhao et al. further proposed ChatCAD+ [[86]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0086), which introduces a domain recognition module on top of ChatCAD, enabling the model to adaptively select appropriate CAD models for different types of medical images and combine local report experience with authoritative medical knowledge retrieval to further improve reliability and generalization.

#### 3.3.5. Comparative analysis and selection recommendations for modality alignment modules

**Comparative analysis:** Overall, MLP alignment, Cross-Attention alignment, Q-Former alignment, and text transformation alignment represent different trade-offs between efficiency and performance. MLP alignment structure is simple and incurs relatively low parameter overhead, making it suitable for mapping visual features into the language space. However, it cannot compress the number of visual tokens, which may lead to higher inference costs [[38]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0038), [[41]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0041). Cross-Attention enables deeper cross-modal fusion through dynamic interaction between visual and textual representations, making it more effective for modeling the relationship between local lesions and global semantics, but at the cost of higher computational and memory demands [[87]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0087), [[88]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0088). Q-Former uses a small set of learnable query vectors to compress visual tokens, achieving a good balance among parameter efficiency, inference efficiency, and transfer flexibility, although fixed-length compression may result in the loss of local details and spatial information [[82]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0082), [[89]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0089). Text transformation alignment converts imaging information into structured text through CAD models before feeding it into LLMs, offering strong interpretability and modularity, but its performance depends heavily on the front-end CAD models and may suffer from the loss of original visual details during transformation [[85]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0085), [[86]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0086).

**Selection Recommendations:** From the perspective of medical task adaptation, MLP alignment is preferable for lightweight models and coarse-grained tasks such as medical question answering and description generation [[38]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0038). Cross-Attention is more suitable for tasks that emphasize local lesion recognition, regional evidence association, and deep image-text interaction [[87]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0087), [[88]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0088). When a balance between performance and efficiency is required, especially under the setting of frozen visual encoders and LLMs, Q-Former is generally a more appropriate choice [[82]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0082). In contrast, text transformation alignment is more valuable when clinical interpretability, system scalability, and compatibility with existing CAD pipelines are of greater concern [[85]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0085), [[86]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0086). Therefore, the choice of modality alignment modules in Medical MLLMs should comprehensively consider task granularity, input complexity, computational budget, and clinical deployment requirements.

### 3.4. LLMs architectures in medical MLLMs

In the architecture of Medical MLLMs, LLMs integrate medical imaging information with user instructions and generate accurate responses by leveraging the medical knowledge acquired during training. At present, three main architectures are commonly used: encoder-only, decoder-only, and encoder-decoder. [Fig. 6](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0006) illustrates the attention mechanisms of these three architectures. The following sections analyze their architectural characteristics and applications in medical scenarios.

#### 3.4.1. Encoder-only

The Encoder-Only architecture is built on bidirectional self-attention and is typically pretrained with the masked language modeling (MLM) objective, in which parts of the text are randomly masked and predicted from both left and right context, enabling the model to learn global semantic representations. BERT [[90]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0090) is a representative example of this architecture. By modeling bidirectional context, it possesses strong word-level and sentence-level semantic understanding capabilities, making it more suitable for understanding tasks. Its main advantage lies in modeling medical terminology, abbreviations, and their contextual relationships, which enhances fine-grained semantic understanding and supports effective few-shot transfer after domain-specific pretraining. However, its limitation is the lack of a native autoregressive generation mechanism, resulting in relatively weak long-text generation ability, while the MLM objective is also not fully consistent with downstream generation tasks. Therefore, Encoder-Only models are more appropriate for tasks such as medical named entity recognition, clinical semantic understanding, and clinical text similarity prediction. In medical applications, encoder-only architectures have demonstrated strong performance in clinical text understanding tasks. For example, BioBERT [[91]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0091), after domain pretraining on PubMed corpora, improves performance on biomedical named entity recognition, relation extraction, and question answering; ClinicalBERT [[92]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0092) enhances the model’s ability to handle abbreviations, template-style expressions, and contextual constraints in clinical narratives by learning domain-specific clinical representations; and the comparison by Chen et al. [[93]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0093) of BioBERT, BlueBERT [[94]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0094), and ClinicalBERT on clinical text similarity prediction further shows that Encoder-Only models are better at distinguishing lexical similarity from semantic similarity, mainly due to their stronger holistic modeling of clinical terminology and contextual relations.

#### 3.4.2. Decoder-only

The Decoder-Only architecture is based on causal masked self-attention and generates text autoregressively from left to right, typically pretrained with the next-token prediction objective. This makes its pretraining paradigm highly consistent with downstream generation tasks, leading to strong performance in dialogue, text continuation, and report generation. Compared with traditional sequence models such as RNNs, LSTMs, and CNNs, it offers better training parallelism and stronger long-range dependency modeling, and is therefore widely adopted in mainstream large models such as ChatGPT [[1]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0001), Qwen [[2]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0002), and Llama [[95]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0095). Its main limitation is that it cannot directly exploit future global context and remains constrained by token-by-token decoding during inference. Therefore, Decoder-Only is particularly suitable as the core architecture for medical generative tasks such as Med-RG, multi-turn medical question answering, and clinical dialogue generation [[52]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0052). In medical applications, its unified autoregressive paradigm is especially advantageous for long-text continuation, contextual coherence, and instruction following. For example, MAIRA-1, designed for chest X-ray report generation, combines a CXR-specific image encoder with a Vicuna-7B-based language model and achieves improvements on RadCliQ and multiple lexical metrics, indicating that Decoder-Only is well suited for terminology-intensive and cross-sentence coherent generation tasks. Meanwhile, HuatuoGPT [[96]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0096) has demonstrated excellent performance in Chinese medical multi-turn dialogue, indicating that medical consultation tasks generally require the continuous integration of symptoms, disease progression, and medication information across historical turns, which is highly consistent with the characteristics of the autoregressive generation mechanism in Decoder-Only architectures.

#### 3.4.3. Encoder-decoder

The Encoder-Decoder architecture consists of an encoder and a decoder, where the encoder models the global semantics of the input sequence and the decoder generates the output sequence step by step based on the encoded representation, forming a typical conditional generation framework [[71]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0071). Compared with traditional sequence-to-sequence models such as RNNs, LSTMs, and GRUs, it offers stronger training parallelism and better long-range dependency modeling [[97]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0097), [[98]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0098). Its main advantage lies in the encoder’s ability to fully exploit the complete input context for semantic understanding, while the decoder generates more coherent and semantically complete target text, making this architecture particularly effective for tasks with relatively fixed input-output mappings. However, because it includes both encoding and decoding modules, the overall structure is more complex, with greater parameter and computational cost, and its inference process is still constrained by token-by-token generation. In medical applications, encoder-decoder architectures are more suitable for tasks such as medical information querying, medical text translation, and medical text normalization or rewriting. For example, MedT5SQL [[99]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0099), which is fine-tuned from T5 on medical information retrieval datasets, can convert natural language questions into structured SQL queries, thereby supporting electronic health record retrieval and interactive data access. In addition, MNMT [[100]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0100) is capable of translating biomedical texts in French, German, Italian, Korean, and Spanish into English, and achieves near SOTA performance on biomedical benchmark datasets. This further indicates that encoder-decoder architectures are well suited for cross-lingual medical text transformation and specialized terminology translation.

## 4. Model optimization and prompt engineering

### 4.1. Optimization methods for medical MLLMs

Because MLLMs typically contain a large number of parameters but relatively low knowledge density, they often suffer from unnecessary parameter redundancy. This not only increases computational and storage costs, but also poses significant challenges to training and inference efficiency. In response, researchers have proposed various methods to optimize the architecture and computational efficiency of LLMs. Several promising optimization strategies are outlined below.

#### 4.1.1. Mixture of experts models

Traditional LLMs usually adopt dense architectures, in which all parameters are activated for every input. As model size increases, this design substantially raises the computational, storage, and memory costs of training and deployment, while also reducing inference efficiency in long-sequence scenarios. To address this issue, Fedus et al. introduced the mixture of experts (MoE) framework [[101]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0101) into LLMs, enabling only a subset of experts to be activated during each forward pass through a routing mechanism, thereby reducing the number of active parameters and computational cost while preserving model capacity. Jiang et al. further proposed Med-MoE [[38]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0038), which integrates MoE into Medical MLLMs and achieves performance comparable to or better than LLaVA-Med-7B while activating only 2.0B-3.6B parameters, demonstrating the efficiency advantage of sparse activation in medical scenarios.

#### 4.1.2. Model distillation

Model distillation is a classical model compression method that aims to transfer knowledge from a large teacher model to a lightweight student model, enabling the student to retain performance close to that of the teacher while substantially reducing parameter size, training cost, and inference cost. This idea was first proposed by Hinton et al. [[102]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0102) and has been widely applied to language model compression and capability transfer. Its technical foundation is typically based on a teacher-student framework, where the teacher is usually a more powerful pretrained large model and the student is a smaller, more deployment-efficient counterpart. According to the accessibility of the teacher model, model distillation can be further divided into black-box distillation and white-box distillation: the former mainly relies on the teacher’s generated outputs or pseudo-labels for learning [[103]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0103), whereas the latter can additionally access intermediate information such as prediction distributions, allowing more sufficient knowledge transfer [[104]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0104). During training, soft labels provided by the teacher are usually combined with hard labels from the ground-truth dataset and optimized through a weighted loss function, so as to balance the generalization ability of transferred knowledge with the accuracy of supervised signals [[105]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0105). In terms of training paradigms, model distillation mainly includes supervised distillation and reinforcement learning distillation: the former generally uses pseudo-labels generated by the teacher model to conduct SFT of the student model, while the latter constructs distillation data from the teacher’s responses and corresponding reward signals, and employs reinforcement learning to optimize the student’s policy for maximizing expected rewards and further improving performance [[106]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0106).

#### 4.1.3. Model quantization and pruning

**Parameter quantization:** Parameter quantization reduces storage, bandwidth, and computational costs, and improves inference efficiency by compressing the weights of Medical MLLMs from 32-bit high-precision representations to 8-bit or even lower precision. This method is broadly applicable to most models and hardware platforms, but low-precision representations may introduce quantization errors and thus degrade model performance. To alleviate this issue, quantized models usually require calibration training. Among existing methods, Quantization-Aware Training is one of the most widely used, as it gradually introduces quantization operations during both forward and backward propagation, thereby improving the stability and performance of quantized models [[107]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0107).

**Model pruning:** Model pruning constructs more compact network structures by removing redundant or less important connections, neurons, or channels, thereby reducing storage and computational costs. Typical methods include connection pruning based on weight importance [[108]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0108), which first trains the model, then removes weights with small absolute values, and finally restores performance through fine-tuning; loss-sensitivity-based pruning [[109]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0109), which estimates the increase in loss caused by removing parameters and preferentially preserves those with greater impact on performance; and Taylor-expansion-based importance estimation [[110]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0110), which uses first- or second-order Taylor approximation to estimate the loss change caused by removing a parameter, neuron, or channel, and then iteratively prunes the least important components. Overall, these methods all aim to remove redundancy while preserving model performance as much as possible.

### 4.2. Prompt engineering for medical MLLMs

In Medical MLLMs, prompt enhancement techniques can improve the accuracy and professionalism of generated outputs without modifying model parameters by guiding the model to focus on key information and incorporate external knowledge. These methods help enhance the reliability of Medical MLLMs in tasks such as medical diagnosis, Med-RG, and Med-VQA, while improving their alignment with clinical needs [[111]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0111). This section systematically analyzes related prompt engineering methods from three perspectives: technical principles, clinical significance, and comparative analysis with selection recommendations.

#### 4.2.1. In-context learning

To address the scarcity of annotated medical data and the need for rapid adaptation to new tasks, as shown in the In-context learning (ICL) part of [Fig. 7](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0007), ICL enables LLMs or MLLMs to infer task rules from contextual prompts without updating model parameters by incorporating task descriptions and a few example-answer pairs, including output format, reasoning patterns, and response style, thereby generating results consistent with task objectives [[112]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0112). In medical multimodal settings, these examples may extend from text-answer pairs to cross-modal demonstrations, such as image, report fragment, and structured prompts to outputs, allowing rapid task alignment and format control at the prompting stage. In clinical applications, ICL is particularly suitable for scenarios where large-scale annotated data are unavailable or model fine-tuning is impractical, as a few examples can quickly align model outputs with the target report structure, wording preferences, and narrative style, thus reducing post-processing and manual revision costs. For example, Yan et al. [[113]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0113) combined RadGraph content with few-shot prompting to generate reports consistent with the style of specific radiologists, while Huang et al. [[114]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0114) proposed FineRadScore, which uses few-shot evaluation examples to guide LLMs in line-by-line report correction and to provide error severity scores together with supporting evidence, demonstrating that ICL can extend LLMs into tools for automatic evaluation and quality control without additional training.

#### 4.2.2. ROI annotation

ROI annotation explicitly localizes key anatomical structures or lesion regions in medical images through bounding boxes, masks, or segmentation contours, thereby providing Medical MLLMs with directly usable spatial priors [[115]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0115). As shown in the ROI part of [Fig. 7](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0007), this strategy transforms the ”evidence location to be attended to” from implicit attention into explicit prompt signals, guiding the model to organize factual descriptions and diagnostic conclusions around specified regions while reducing erroneous attention and redundant generation caused by global background interference. ROI can be annotated by clinical experts or automatically generated by detection or segmentation models, and is then fed into the model together with the original image and task prompt. Its core process involves first feeding medical images into a dedicated lesion segmentation model or lesion detection model to generate the corresponding ROI annotations for each image. Subsequently, the medical images, together with their ROI annotations and relevant textual information, are input into the Medical MLLMs to facilitate the identification and localization of pathological regions, thereby improving the accuracy of the model outputs. In clinical applications, the main value of ROI annotation lies in enhancing spatial alignment, so that key information in the question can be stably aligned with imaging evidence regions, thereby supporting more reviewable region-level conclusions and explanations. For example, MedTrinity-25M [[40]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0040) incorporates ROI location, scale, and regional descriptions into a multi-granularity annotation system to support region-related vision-language tasks, and the results show that explicit ROI information improves regional understanding and question-answering performance. MedVP [[115]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0115) further injects ROI as an explicit visual prompt into image inputs to enhance regional focus in medical VQA, thereby improving the accuracy and reliability of region-related question answering, while also demonstrating the positive effect of ROI annotation on region-level attention and VQA performance.

#### 4.2.3. Retrieval-augmented generation

RAG is designed to address the rapid evolution of medical knowledge, the lag of parametric knowledge in models, and the high risk of hallucination. Its core idea is to combine the parametric knowledge of Medical MLLMs with dynamically updated external non-parametric knowledge bases without modifying model parameters, so that retrieved evidence can constrain the generation process and improve factual consistency and reliability [[10]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0010). As shown in the RAG part of [Fig. 7](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0007), the typical pipeline includes cleaning and chunking clinical guidelines, textbooks, PubMed literature, or institutional knowledge, mapping these knowledge chunks into a vector space with embedding models, and building a vector database for indexing. During inference, the user query is encoded into a query vector, and the top-k relevant knowledge passages are retrieved through vector similarity retrieval or semantic similarity retrieval. These passages are then organized together with the original query into a structured prompt and fed into the generation model, forming a process of ”retrieval-evidence injection-constrained generation”. In clinical applications, the key value of RAG lies in aligning model outputs with retrievable authoritative knowledge, making it particularly suitable for tasks that rely on up-to-date guidelines, drug contraindications, institutional protocols, and professional consensus, thereby improving factuality and traceability. For example, KG-RAG proposed by Soman et al. [[116]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0116) constructs prompts using retrieved knowledge-graph context and outputs answers together with source information, making responses more verifiable and better aligned with clinical requirements for evidence chains. In radiology contrast agent consultation, Wada et al. [[117]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0117) combined locally deployable LLMs with RAG to constrain responses through authoritative retrieved references, significantly improving consultation quality and reducing hallucination risk under privacy-friendly local deployment, which highlights the value of RAG for knowledge alignment in medication question answering.

#### 4.2.4. Multi-round retrieval augmented generation

Multi-round retrieval-augmented generation (MRAG) is a further extension of traditional RAG. Its core idea is to transform the one-shot ”retrieval-generation” process into an iterative closed loop of ”retrieval-reading-reasoning-re-retrieval,” enabling the model to assess whether current evidence is sufficient, identify the remaining uncovered knowledge gaps, and continuously refine queries to supplement missing information, thereby making it more suitable for complex clinical questions and long-chain reasoning scenarios [[118]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0118), [[119]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0119). Technically, MRAG extends the single-round RAG shown in [Fig. 7](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0007) into the closed-loop iterative process illustrated in [Fig. 8](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0008), upgrading retrieval from one-time evidence supplementation to active evidence seeking during reasoning. As shown in the DeepSearch part of [Fig. 8](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0008), the model first analyzes the clinical question and generates an initial query, retrieves candidate evidence from webpages, medical knowledge bases, or medical datasets, and then filters, reads, and extracts relevant information, which is written into working memory and organized into a chain of thought. On this basis, the model further checks the sufficiency of the current answer, such as whether key conditions are supported by evidence, whether conflicts exist among retrieved evidence, and what information is still missing, and then dynamically rewrites queries or raises follow-up subquestions to trigger the next round of retrieval until stopping criteria, such as the maximum number of rounds, token budget, or evidence sufficiency threshold, are met [[118]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0118). Building on this, DeepResearch further introduces a structured writing framework, typically by first generating a report-style section outline, then invoking DeepSearch for targeted retrieval and reasoning for each section, and finally integrating and refining all sections to ensure consistency in terminology, logic, and structure, thereby extending MRAG from answering individual questions to generating long-form structured reports [[119]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0119).

In clinical settings, MRAG is particularly suitable for information-intensive tasks that require long evidence chains, repeated supplementation, and iterative verification. In radiology question answering, for example, Wind et al. [[120]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0120) proposed a radiology Retrieval and Reasoning framework that answers complex questions through multi-step retrieval, evidence integration, and iterative reasoning, and the results show that it further reduces hallucinations and improves response reliability compared with conventional single-round RAG. For liver disease clinical decision support, Hu et al. [[121]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0121) proposed a self-correcting Agentic Graph RAG framework, which combines knowledge graphs with self-correcting multi-round retrieval to construct more complete evidence contexts while constraining generated conclusions, demonstrating the closed-loop evidence-seeking and self-verification capability of MRAG in clinical decision-making. In multi-turn diagnostic dialogue, Sun et al. [[122]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0122) proposed MRD-RAG, which gradually refines differential diagnosis through retrieval-driven follow-up questioning and iterative information completion, making the model more consistent with the real clinical workflow of ”asking, retrieving, and reasoning simultaneously,” and achieving better performance on multiple medical datasets and physician evaluations. In addition, for tasks requiring long, structured, and well-supported clinical reports, medical DeepResearch agents such as DeepMed [[123]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0123) first construct section outlines and then perform multi-hop retrieval and controlled iterative reasoning to organize evidence chains, while introducing monitoring mechanisms to suppress excessive retrieval and noise accumulation, highlighting the potential of MRAG for long-chain evidence organization and reliable generation.

#### 4.2.5. Comparative analysis and selection recommendations for prompt engineering

**Comparative analysis:** From the perspective of alignment objectives, ICL, ROI, RAG, and MRAG correspond to four distinct needs: pattern alignment, spatial alignment, knowledge alignment, and iterative evidence verification. ICL enables rapid control of output structure, expression style, and reasoning patterns through a few demonstrations without updating model parameters, making it suitable for low-resource settings and cross-institution template transfer. However, its effectiveness is sensitive to the quality, relevance, and ordering of examples, and it may introduce considerable token cost and inference latency in long-text or multimodal scenarios [[124]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0124), [[125]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0125). ROI provides explicit spatial priors for lesions or anatomical regions, reducing background interference and improving region-level interpretability, which is particularly beneficial for fine-grained image understanding tasks, but it relies heavily on ROI quality, while expert annotation is costly and automatically generated ROI may introduce additional errors and computation overhead [[115]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0115), [[126]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0126). RAG supports generation with updatable and traceable external evidence, making it more suitable for medical tasks requiring rapidly updated guidelines and strong normative constraints. Nevertheless, its performance is strongly affected by retrieval quality, embedding consistency, and chunking strategies, and it also raises additional privacy and security concerns [[10]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0010), [[127]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0127). MRAG further extends RAG with an iterative ”retrieval-reasoning-re-retrieval” loop, allowing continuous completion of evidence gaps and verification of conclusion consistency, and is therefore more advantageous for tasks involving long evidence chains, multiple constraints, and complex clinical reasoning, although it incurs higher token consumption, longer inference time, and a greater risk of noise accumulation [[120]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0120), [[128]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0128).

**Selection Recommendations:** When the task mainly emphasizes output format, standardized template adherence, or consistency of expression style, such as standardized report generation, writing style transfer, or prompt-based automatic evaluation, ICL should be preferred; when such tasks also require stronger factuality and traceability, ICL + RAG is generally more appropriate, with ICL constraining structure and expression and RAG providing external evidence support [[5]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0005), [[125]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0125). When the main challenge is to localize the question to specific image regions, as in region-level question answering, subtle lesion analysis, or fine-grained localization explanation, ROI should be prioritized; if standardized medical explanation of local regions is also required, ROI can be further combined with RAG to form a joint prompting strategy of ”spatial evidence + knowledge evidence” [[40]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0040), [[115]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0115). When the task depends heavily on external knowledge such as updated guidelines, drug contraindications, or institutional protocols, and requires timely, standardized, and traceable outputs, RAG should be preferred [[10]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0010). For tasks with multiple constraints, uncertain information gaps, and repeated evidence-seeking needs, such as complex medical question answering, multi-turn diagnostic dialogue, and long-form structured report generation, MRAG is generally more suitable. In practice, however, it should be coupled with noise filtering, stopping strategies, and self-correction mechanisms to control additional latency and reduce the risk of error accumulation [[120]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0120), [[122]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0122).

**Pre-training datasets:**Pre-training datasets usually come from large-scale general-domain text corpora and image-text aligned data, such as Wikipedia, BooksCorpus, OpenWebText, COCO [[147]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0147), and LAION-5B [[148]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0148). In terms of data format, they mainly include two types: pure text samples for learning language modeling and general reasoning abilities, and image-text paired samples for learning general visual concepts and cross-modal mappings. Through this stage, the model typically acquires strong general language generation ability and basic vision-language alignment, providing a stable initialization for subsequent domain-specific training.

**Continual pre-training datasets:** CPT datasets mainly come from public medical literature, electronic health records, and medical image-text paired data. In terms of format, they also include two main types: medical text-only data, which strengthen medical terminology and professional knowledge representation, and medical image-text aligned samples, which further improve visual representation and cross-modal alignment for medical imaging. Through CPT, the model can better acquire medical knowledge and clinical narrative patterns, while enhancing its understanding of anatomical structures and lesion characteristics, thereby providing stronger domain adaptation for subsequent fine-tuning and downstream tasks.

**Instruction fine-tuning datasets:** IFT datasets are typically constructed from electronic health records, medical reports, and related metadata, and mainly contain key information such as symptom descriptions, diagnostic results, and imaging modalities. Their data format generally follows an ”instruction-multimodal input-standardized response” triplet structure and can support either single-turn or multi-turn interactions. Training on such datasets enables Medical MLLMs to better understand diverse task instructions and improve their performance on tasks such as Med-RG, Med-VQA, and case summarization, thereby making outputs more professional, standardized, and clinically relevant. For example, LLaVA-Med-Instruct [[7]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0007) is an instruction dataset generated by GPT-4 based on image-text data and related descriptions from PubMed Central, with the aim of enhancing multimodal instruction understanding and execution capabilities.

**Medical report generation datasets:** Med-RG datasets are typically derived from ethically approved and de-identified electronic health records, and contain medical images together with their corresponding diagnostic reports. These reports usually consist of two parts, Findings and Impression: the former focuses on objective descriptions of imaging findings, while the latter summarizes the main observations and provides diagnostic impressions or clinical interpretation. Training on such datasets enables Medical MLLMs to learn how to extract key information from images and generate clinically standardized diagnostic reports. For example, MIMIC-CXR [[56]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0056) contains 377,110 chest X-ray images and 227,835 corresponding reports, and can be used to train models to extract key information from chest radiographs and generate structured clinical report text.

**Medical question answering datasets:** Med-VQA datasets are designed for tasks that answer clinical questions based on medical images. Their sources mainly include medical literature and textbooks, medical examination question banks, and real or simulated physician-patient dialogues. The data format typically consists of medical images, clinical questions, and correct answers, and can support both single-turn question answering and multi-turn dialogue. Training on such datasets enables Medical MLLMs to learn cross-modal medical vision-language reasoning, combining lesion-related visual features with clinical context to generate accurate answers, while further improving multi-turn interaction and contextual understanding. For example, PathVQA [[50]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0050), derived from pathology textbooks and digital repositories, contains 4998 pathology images and 32,799 question-answer pairs, and can be used to train pathology image question-answering models.

**Limitations of supervised learning datasets:** Although the supervised learning datasets listed in [Table 5](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0005) are essential for general alignment, medical knowledge injection, and downstream fine-tuning of Medical MLLMs, they still share several common limitations. First, many clinical imaging and Electronic Health Records (EHRs) datasets come from only a few hospitals or a single healthcare system. For example, MIMIC-CXR and MIMIC-IV are both derived from Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, CheXpert is collected only from Stanford Health Care, and PadChest comes from San Juan Hospital in Spain, with differences in report language and imaging devices compared with U.S. datasets [[56]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0056), [[131]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0131), [[132]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0132), [[138]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0138). Because institutions differ in equipment, case distribution, and documentation standards, models often suffer performance degradation when deployed across hospitals. Second, public resources remain imbalanced in both modality and task distribution, with large-scale datasets largely concentrated on scenarios such as chest X-rays and head CT scans. Examination question banks and paper-based image question answering are also generally closer to standardized answer paradigms and figure-caption contexts, leaving a gap from real clinical applications. As a result, models may achieve strong benchmark performance while still showing limited clinical utility [[51]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0051). In addition, synthetic instruction datasets may introduce distribution bias and lack realism. For instance, LLaVA-Med expands IFT data by sampling PMC image-text resources and using GPT-4 to generate instruction-response pairs; although this improves instruction following and dialogue ability, insufficient quality control may lead to templated expressions, inaccurate medical details, or even fabricated clinical evidence, thereby introducing noise into training and weakening clinical reliability [[7]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0007). Finally, due to privacy regulations and access restrictions, clinical data are usually de-identified and often incomplete, making external reproduction and cross-institutional validation costly [[56]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0056). To address these issues, [Table 6](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0006) further summarizes representative datasets according to source and population coverage, modality distribution, and potential risks, thereby providing a basis for training strategy design and evaluation.

**RLHF Datasets:** RLHF datasets usually consist of preference rankings or quality ratings of model outputs annotated by medical experts, and are used to guide models toward responses that are more clinically logical, safe, and professional. For example, the Zhongjing-RLHF dataset [[151]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0151) contains 20,000 manually annotated preference pairs, each consisting of a preferred response and a rejected response. The model is trained to increase the probability of generating preferred responses while reducing the probability of generating rejected ones, thereby producing outputs more aligned with human preferences. Similarly, the Aquila-Med RLHF [[149]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0149) adopts direct preference optimization (DPO) [[158]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0158) (see [Section 5.2.2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#sec0048)) to increase the probability of generating preferred responses and suppress rejected ones, thus improving output safety and professionalism.

**RLAIF Datasets:** RLAIF datasets replace human annotators with AI models to provide preference labels or reward scores for RL training. During data construction, AI models can both generate candidate samples, such as dialogues, treatment plans, or reports, and rank or score the outputs of Medical MLLMs to provide reward signals. For example, Yang et al. [[159]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0159) constructed comparative preference data based on 94,844 chest CT reports, in which Qwen and radiologists jointly ranked model-generated outputs, and these rankings were then used to train a reward model and perform RL alignment. Experimental results showed that the ranking agreement between Qwen and radiologists reached 77.9%, indicating that AI can, to some extent, replace human annotators in providing reward signals to align LLMs with radiologist preferences.

**Limitations of RL datasets:** Overall, RL datasets for Medical MLLMs still face several common challenges, including high annotation cost, limited scale, noisy preference signals, construction bias, insufficient coverage, and difficulties in compliant sharing. First, RLHF relies heavily on medical experts to rank candidate outputs and annotate preferences, which keeps most public datasets at only tens of thousands of samples. For example, in the RLHF stage of Zhongjing, only six medical experts ranked about 20,000 model outputs [[151]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0151), while the preference alignment data of LongReward contains only 10,000 preference pairs [[154]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0154). Such scales remain inadequate compared with the diversity of diseases, examination types, and multidisciplinary variation in real clinical settings. Second, even larger manually annotated preference datasets may still exhibit imbalanced coverage across medical subdomains, modalities, and languages. For instance, MM-RLHF provides 120k fine-grained preference pairs, but its data show a strong structural bias toward short-form question answering: short-form QA accounts for about 83.68%, whereas long-form QA represents only 12.17%, limiting its coverage of long-text reasoning and Med-RG scenarios [[153]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0153). In addition, preference annotation may suffer from subjectivity and inconsistency, as disagreements often arise from under-specified tasks, stylistic preferences, differences in refusal strategies, and annotation errors. For example, Aquila-Med introduces GPT-4 for generation and review when constructing subjective preference data for DPO, which may implicitly inject the reviewer model’s own preferences and expression patterns into the dataset, making preference signals not fully equivalent to clinical value [[149]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0149). Finally, medical data are restricted by privacy regulations and usage agreements, making preference datasets difficult to share publicly and training procedures hard to reproduce. For example, PhysioNet explicitly prohibits restricted datasets such as MIMIC-III, MIMIC-IV, and MIMIC-CXR from being processed through third-party online services or APIs, which objectively limits dataset expansion, team-level reproducibility, and secondary development of existing resources.

### 5.2. Training methods

Supervised learning and RL are the two main training paradigms for Medical MLLMs. Supervised learning constructs supervision signals from the data itself and minimizes the distribution gap between model predictions and predefined labels. In contrast, RL aims to maximize expected rewards, thereby increasing the probability of generating high-quality outputs through continual reward optimization. This section introduces the applications of these two methods in Medical MLLMs, compares their differences, and finally provides recommendations for method selection.

#### 5.2.1. Supervised learning

The core objective of supervised learning is to minimize prediction errors on training data without requiring manual annotation, thereby enabling the model to automatically learn meaningful feature representations from large-scale unlabeled data. As shown in [Fig. 9](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0009), the supervised learning process of Medical MLLMs can be divided into four training stages.

Fig. 9. Supervised training pipeline of Medical MLLMs. A general vision-language model is first adapted through CPT on medical image-text and textual data to obtain a medical vision-language model, which is then further optimized via instruction tuning and downstream fine-tuning for tasks such as Med-RG and Med-VQA.

**Pre-training:** Pre-training is a crucial stage for Medical MLLMs to acquire fundamental capabilities. Its goal is to develop stable representation capabilities, cross-modal alignment, and text generation abilities through supervised learning on large-scale medical or general multimodal data. Although pre-training corpora may contain some noise, their scale and diversity still enable the model to accumulate general language knowledge, medical domain knowledge, and visual representation priors [[22]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0022). By minimizing the discrepancy between predicted token distributions and ground-truth tokens, the model learns to generate appropriate textual outputs from multimodal inputs and to establish deep alignment between visual and linguistic modalities. When labeled medical data are limited, pre-training also improves generalization and transferability, providing an essential foundation for the overall performance of subsequent fine-tuning stages [[21]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0021).

**Continual Pre-training:** CPT incrementally updates the model using domain-specific medical data beyond the original pre-training corpus, enabling it to continuously acquire new medical knowledge and clinical experience [[60]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0060). Through this process, the model can better understand medical terminology, imaging features, and the complex relationships among multimodal medical information while retaining previously learned knowledge. It also helps alleviate the effects of noisy pre-training data and limited medical annotations, thereby providing a more targeted foundation for subsequent IFT and SFT [[5]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0005).

**Instruction Fine-tuning:** IFT further optimizes the model on top of pre-training by using data in the form of ”instruction-multimodal input-response,” with the goal of improving its ability to understand, follow, and respond to task instructions [[160]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0160). A single IFT dataset may include various instructions, such as ”briefly describe the image,” ”describe the lesion location in detail,” or ”what is the evidence of right pleural effusion?” Each instruction sample explicitly defines the task objective. Multimodal inputs usually include medical images together with related textual information, while the response is the task-specific answer generated by the model according to the instruction [[7]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0007). Through IFT, the model learns the correspondence between different task formulations and output requirements, enabling it to better understand user intent and generate responses that are more consistent with the target medical task.

**Supervised Fine-tuning:** SFT further optimizes the model on top of CPT or IFT using task-specific annotated data to improve output accuracy and task adaptability for specific medical tasks. This stage usually relies on datasets for downstream tasks such as medical named entity recognition, Med-VQA, and Med-RG, and enables the model to learn the mapping between inputs and target outputs by minimizing task-specific loss functions [[161]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0161). Through SFT, the model can generate results that are more consistent with task requirements and medical standards, thereby improving its performance, stability, and reliability in specific clinical scenarios.

#### 5.2.2. Reinforcement learning

RL is an important post-training optimization method for Medical MLLMs. Its core idea is to continuously adjust and optimize the generation policy based on feedback signals derived from model outputs under given multimodal inputs. Unlike training methods that fit static labeled data, RL focuses more on the overall quality of outputs and uses reward mechanisms to guide the model toward responses that are more accurate, useful, and clinically appropriate. In medical scenarios, such reward signals may come from preference comparisons provided by physicians or experts, from reward models or external evaluators, or from automated evaluation mechanisms constructed with expert rules. According to how feedback signals are built and how policies are optimized, RL methods in Medical MLLMs can generally be divided into three categories: DPO, which directly uses preference comparison data; Critic Network optimization, which updates policies based on learnable reward models; and Deep-RL, which can generate reward signals by combining expert scoring rules with the evaluation capability of LLMs [[162]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0162). The following sections introduce the basic principles of these three methods and their representative medical applications.

**Direct Preference Optimization:** In medical scenarios, physicians and clinical experts often show clear preferences among different model-generated outputs for the same instruction. DPO aligns models using such preference comparison data, where each sample typically consists of one instruction and two candidate responses, with the better response identified by experts. In clinical tasks, preference pairs can be constructed from scenarios such as Med-RG and Med-VQA; for example, two diagnostic reports may be generated for the same medical image, or two candidate answers may be produced for the same clinical question, and experts then select the response that is more accurate and clinically valuable. Based on these data, DPO updates model parameters by increasing the probability of preferred responses and suppressing non-preferred ones, thereby making outputs more consistent with human preferences [[158]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0158). For example, STLLaVA-Med proposed by Sun et al. uses GPT-4o to evaluate candidate answers in Med-VQA and applies DPO for model alignment, achieving competitive zero-shot performance while using only about 9% medical data [[163]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0163). Zhu et al. further proposed MMedPO, which conducts multimodal preference optimization by constructing clinically relevant preference data and significantly improves factual accuracy and clinical alignment in medical question answering and Med-RG tasks [[164]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0164).

**Critic Network Optimization:** Critic Network methods introduce a learnable evaluator to provide feedback signals for Medical MLLMs and optimize generation quality within an RL framework. The procedure usually involves two steps. First, a reward model is trained on RLHF or RLAIF preference data so that it can output scalar reward scores for given instructions, medical images, and candidate responses, thereby assessing their accuracy, relevance, and clinical usefulness. Second, during the policy optimization stage, the medical MLLM acts as the actor to generate responses online, while the reward model scores these outputs and uses the feedback to update the model parameters. In this way, the model increases the probability of high-reward responses and suppresses low-reward ones, thereby improving its generative capability for downstream tasks. For example, Yang et al. [[159]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0159) adopted a three-stage framework of ”SFT-reward modeling-Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)” to align medical LLMs for chest CT report impression generation. In that study, a reward model was trained on comparative preference data and was then used to generate feedback signals for further optimizing the policy model. Experimental results showed that the reward-aligned policy model outperformed the unaligned model on Precision, Recall, and F1, indicating that Critic Network-based optimization can effectively improve the clinical consistency and usability of Med-RG.

**Deep Reinforcement Learning:** Traditional supervised learning is typically centered on cross-entropy loss, minimizing the discrepancy between model predictions and reference answers through token-level matching. However, in the medical domain, open-ended tasks often permit multiple semantically equivalent and clinically valid expressions, making surface-level token alignment insufficient to capture such semantic diversity and thereby limiting generalization and generation quality. At the same time, conventional RL methods struggle to flexibly incorporate medical scoring rules into the reward process. To address this issue, Deep-RL in Medical MLLMs typically adopts LLMs-based evaluation mechanisms (see [Section 6.1.2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#sec0062)) and combines them with prompt engineering to construct multidimensional reward rules. These rules assess complete responses from multiple aspects, including answer correctness, clinical value, semantic consistency, output format, and cross-modal alignment, and then update policy parameters according to the reward signals. In this way, the optimization objective shifts from ”matching reference answers” to ”improving the quality of open-ended clinical reasoning.” For example, MediX-R1 proposed by Mullappilly et al. [[9]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0009) introduces this strategy into Medical MLLMs and designs a composite reward consisting of response accuracy reward, medical-embedding semantic reward, format reward, and modality reward, thereby enhancing feedback stability and clinical relevance in open-ended medical reasoning. Experimental results show that, with about 51,000 instruction samples, its 8B model achieves an average accuracy of 68.8%, exceeding the 68.4% of MedGemma 27B, while the 30B model reaches 73.6%. These results indicate that Deep-RL combining LLMs evaluation and multidimensional rewards can effectively improve the open-ended reasoning ability and task adaptability of Medical MLLMs.

#### 5.2.3. Comparative analysis and selection recommendations for supervised learning and reinforcement learning

**Comparison of technical principles:** Supervised learning and RL represent two typical training paradigms for Medical MLLMs. Supervised learning is essentially closer to imitation learning: it relies on static labeled data and achieves knowledge injection and task behavior learning by minimizing the cross-entropy loss between ground-truth answers and model predictions. Given a multimodal input _X_ and the previously generated response prefix $Y_{\left[\right. : i - 1 \left]\right.}$, the predicted token distribution at position _i_, denoted as _Y_[_i_], can be written as(1)$Y_{\left[\right. i \left]\right.} = \text{LLM} \left(\right. X , Y_{\left[\right. : i - 1 \left]\right.} \left.\right)$The corresponding cross-entropy loss is(2)$\text{Loss} = - \sum_{i = 1}^{N} log p \left(\right. y_{i} \mid Y_{\left[\right. i \left]\right.} \left.\right)$where _N_ denotes the output sequence length, and _y i_ is the ground-truth token at position _i_. This objective essentially emphasizes relatively shallow token-level alignment: by minimizing the discrepancy between the predicted distribution and the reference token at each position, the model learns to reproduce the response patterns in the training samples, thereby achieving knowledge injection and imitation of task behavior.

In contrast, RL is more analogous to an interactive cognitive-behavioral learning paradigm. Instead of directly relying on ground-truth answers for token-level alignment, the model generates a complete response sequence _Y_ online under a given multimodal input _X_ based on the current policy _π θ_. The generated response is then evaluated as a whole by a reward model RM _ϕ_ or preference signals, which outputs a scalar reward _R_. On this basis, the model is updated by maximizing the expected reward while incorporating policy constraints, which can be formulated as:(3)$Y = \text{LLM}_{\theta} \left(\right. X \left.\right) , R = \text{RM}_{\phi} \left(\right. X , Y \left.\right)$(4)$\text{Loss} = - R + \beta \cdot \text{KL} \left(\right. \pi_{\theta} \left(\right. \cdot \mid X \left.\right) \parallel \pi_{\text{ref}} \left(\right. \cdot \mid X \left.\right) \left.\right)$where _π_ ref is usually the model from the previous training stage and serves as the reference policy. The KL divergence constrains the update magnitude during RL training, thereby preventing the model from deviating excessively from its original language capability and safety boundary. Minimizing the above loss is equivalent to maximizing $R - \beta \cdot \text{KL} \left(\right. \cdot \left.\right)$. In practice, stable optimization is often achieved using policy-gradient methods such as PPO, DPO, and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO).

**Comparison of advantages and limitations:** The main advantages of supervised learning are its clear training objective, stable optimization process, and strong reproducibility. Under the framework of teacher forcing and maximum likelihood, the model can efficiently acquire general language abilities and medical domain knowledge, making this paradigm particularly suitable for rapidly injecting capabilities such as template-based expression, basic vision-language alignment, and instruction following [[4]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0004). However, its limitations are also evident. On the one hand, supervised learning is centered on token-level maximum likelihood, emphasizing local conditional probability fitting and lexical coverage. As a result, it is more effective at learning how to express answers according to annotations, but less capable of directly modeling and optimizing clinically important objectives, such as semantic fidelity, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) quality, and clinical value [[7]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0007), [[17]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0017). On the other hand, open-ended medical generation tasks often allow multiple reasonable solutions or expressions, and relying on a single reference answer may restrict output diversity and suppress valid alternatives. In addition, teacher forcing introduces a mismatch between training and inference, since the model must rely on its own previous outputs during generation, causing errors to accumulate along the sequence, especially in long-form medical text generation [[5]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0005).

In contrast, the main advantage of RL lies in its ability to align optimization directly with clinical goals and preferences. Rather than fitting annotated answers token by token, RL first allows the model to generate a complete response, which is then evaluated as a whole by a reward model that outputs scalar feedback. This shifts the optimization objective from reproducing labels to maximizing clinical value, making it easier to explicitly incorporate requirements such as diagnostic accuracy, risk warnings, logical consistency, and formatting standards into the reward function, and to optimize them stably under KL constraints using policy-gradient methods such as PPO [[165]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0165). RL can also integrate multiple sources of evaluation signals: in addition to RLHF, stronger models can be used to generate RLAIF signals, thereby alleviating the bottleneck of manual annotation and improving scalability [[159]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0159), [[166]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0166). However, RL also comes with substantial costs and risks. High-quality preference data depend on clinical experts, making annotation expensive and subject to stricter compliance requirements. Training is also more sensitive to hyperparameters such as reward scaling, KL coefficients, and sampling strategies, resulting in lower stability and reproducibility. Moreover, because the reward model is only a proxy metric, excessive optimization may lead to reward hacking, where the model learns to satisfy the reward model rather than genuinely improving clinical correctness and practical value [[167]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0167).

**Selection Recommendations:** In the training of Medical MLLMs, SFT is generally more suitable for tasks with well-defined answers and stable evaluation criteria, such as medical terminology normalization, clinical relation extraction, structured information filling, case classification, risk stratification, and coding. These tasks usually have relatively fixed reference answers and can often be evaluated with automatic metrics, allowing SFT to efficiently inject medical knowledge and standardized expression through token-level supervision. Existing studies have shown that, for relatively simple and highly structured tasks such as medical classification, SFT alone can already achieve strong performance, while the additional gains from preference learning are often limited; therefore, prioritizing SFT is usually more economical and robust [[168]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0168). In addition, Med-PaLM improves response quality through instruction alignment in the medical domain, further highlighting the importance of SFT for medical knowledge injection and instruction following [[169]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0169).

By contrast, for open-ended generation tasks such as clinical reasoning, differential diagnosis explanation, triage, clinical summarization, patient dialogue, and medication recommendation, where multiple valid answers may exist and where safety, effectiveness, clinical value, and interpretability are of greater concern, it is more appropriate to introduce preference learning or RL on top of SFT. Related studies show that, in complex tasks such as clinical reasoning, summarization, and triage, further preference optimization after SFT can still bring clear improvements [[168]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0168). For example, in chest X-ray report generation, CLARIFID first learns the Findings-Impression writing process and then applies PPO using the CheXbert F1 score of the Impression section as the reward signal, thereby directly optimizing the clinical correctness of diagnostic conclusions and achieving better clinical performance than baseline methods on MIMIC-CXR [[166]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0166). This demonstrates that RL can shift the training objective from merely imitating expression to improving the quality of clinical conclusions.

Overall, this survey recommends a two-stage strategy of SFT first, followed by RL alignment. In the first stage, SFT is conducted on high-quality multimodal medical annotated data to establish foundational capabilities, including medical knowledge injection, basic vision-language alignment, terminology and format standardization, and instruction following. In the second stage, methods such as DPO, PPO, or GRPO are introduced to explicitly encode requirements such as clinical correctness, completeness of risk warnings, uncertainty expression, compliance and safety boundaries, and usability in clinical workflows into reward or preference signals, and to optimize the model stably under KL constraints. This strategy combines the stability and reproducibility of SFT with the direct optimization ability of RL for clinical preferences and safety objectives, thereby improving clinical utility and controllability while maintaining output reliability.

### 5.3. Efficient fine-tuning strategies

Although full-parameter fine-tuning can improve task-specific performance, its application in Medical MLLMs is often limited by high training cost, scarce annotated data, and reduced generalization ability. On the one hand, the large number of model parameters substantially increases computational and memory costs during full updating. On the other hand, limited and distributionally heterogeneous medical data may lead to overfitting, weakened cross-domain generalization, and even catastrophic forgetting [[170]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0170). To address these issues, parameter-efficient fine-tuning strategies have been proposed. By freezing most pretrained parameters and updating only a small number of additional parameters, these methods reduce computational and storage costs while preserving general knowledge as much as possible, thereby enabling Medical MLLMs to adapt effectively to new tasks with far fewer trainable parameters [[171]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0171). The following sections further introduce the principles and medical applications of these methods, compare their differences, and provide practical selection recommendations.

#### 5.3.1. LoRA

As shown in [Fig. 10](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0010), LoRA [[172]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0172) does not directly update the original weight matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{out}} \times d_{\text{in}}}$ in the _Q_/_K_/_V_ projection matrices of the attention module or in the fully connected layers of the feed-forward network. Instead, it represents parameter updates as a low-rank increment Δ _W_, approximating the update required for full-parameter fine-tuning with the product of two trainable matrices:(5)$\Delta W = \frac{\alpha}{r} B A , W^{'} = W + \Delta W$where $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d_{\text{in}}}$, $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{out}} \times r}$, _r_ ≪ min (_d_ in, _d_ out), and _α_ is a scaling factor. During training, _W_ is frozen and only _A_ and _B_ are optimized, so the number of trainable parameters grows linearly with _r_, substantially reducing memory usage and computational cost. During inference, the scaled product _BA_ can be added to _W_ as a residual to obtain the fine-tuned weight _W_′, introducing almost no additional inference latency. This makes LoRA well suited for efficient domain adaptation and multi-task deployment in Medical MLLMs. In clinical applications, the value of LoRA lies not only in parameter efficiency but also in its ability to rapidly adapt general models into institution-ready tools under constraints of data privacy, limited computing resources, and fast iteration. For example, PeFoMed [[173]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0173) freezes the visual encoder and applies LoRA fine-tuning only to the language model, while further enhancing medical image-text alignment through a two-stage training strategy, thereby demonstrating the effectiveness of LoRA in medical multimodal understanding tasks. In addition, Gao et al. [[174]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0174) combine a task decomposition prompting strategy with LoRA fine-tuning for perioperative complication recognition and grading, highlighting its ability to balance local deployment, privacy protection, and performance improvement.

#### 5.3.2. Prompt tuning

Prompt Tuning [[175]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0175) achieves parameter-efficient adaptation by introducing a trainable continuous soft prompt at the beginning of the input sequence. Specifically, as shown in [Fig. 10](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0010), given a frozen pre-trained model _f θ_ with fixed parameters _θ_, a trainable prompt matrix $P = \left[\right. p_{1} , \ldots , p_{m} \left]\right. \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d}$ is introduced for each task, where _m_ is the prompt length and _d_ is the hidden dimension. For an input sequence $x = \left(\right. x_{1} , \ldots , x_{n} \left.\right)$ with embedding representation $E \left(\right. x \left.\right) \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$, the soft prompt is concatenated with the original input embeddings to form a new input:(6)$E^{'} = \text{concat} \left[\right. P , E \left(\right. x \left.\right) \left]\right. \in \mathbb{R}^{\left(\right. m + n \left.\right) \times d} , \hat{y} = f_{\theta} \left(\right. E^{'} \left.\right)$During training, the task loss $L o s s \left(\right. \hat{y} , y \left.\right)$ is minimized through backpropagation, while only _P_ is updated and _θ_ remains frozen. In this way, effective task adaptation can be achieved with only a very small number of trainable parameters. Since these prompt vectors are essentially virtual tokens, they do not need to correspond to readable text, but can continuously influence the generation distribution of subsequent tokens through the attention mechanism, enabling the model to learn task-specific implicit instructions and biases without altering the backbone knowledge. In clinical applications, prompt tuning is particularly suitable for medical text and multimodal tasks that require rapid experimentation, frequent iteration, and minimal changes to the foundation model. For example, Peng et al. [[176]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0176) developed a soft prompt-based learning framework for clinical concept extraction and relation extraction. Their experiments showed that using soft prompts while keeping the large language model frozen can achieve strong few-shot learning performance and cross-task generalization. In addition, SPeC [[177]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0177) calibrates discrete prompts through a soft prompt encoder, significantly reducing output variance caused by semantically similar but differently worded prompts, thereby improving the consistency and robustness of clinical summary generation.

#### 5.3.3. Prefix tuning

Prefix Tuning [[178]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0178) is a parameter-efficient adaptation method that injects task information into the self-attention mechanism of a frozen pre-trained model _f θ_ with fixed parameters _θ_ in the form of continuous prefixes. As shown in [Fig. 10](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0010), unlike soft prompts that are only concatenated at the input level, Prefix Tuning introduces trainable prefix key/value matrices $P_{l}^{K}$ and $P_{l}^{V}$ of length _m_ into the attention module of each layer, where _l_ denotes the layer index. In this way, subsequent tokens can continuously attend to these prefixes as if they were attending to virtual tokens, thereby enabling task-conditioned control over the generation distribution. Specifically, given the query, key, and value matrices _Q l, K l_, and _V l_ derived from the hidden states of the original sequence at layer _l_, the prefixes are concatenated with the original keys and values along the sequence dimension, leading to the following attention computation:(7)$\text{Attn}_{l} = \text{Attention} \left(\right. Q_{l} , \text{concat} \left(\right. P_{l}^{K} , K_{l} \left.\right) , \text{concat} \left(\right. P_{l}^{V} , V_{l} \left.\right) \left.\right)$During training, only the prefix parameters $P_{l}^{K}$ and $P_{l}^{V}$ of each layer are optimized, while the backbone parameters _θ_ remain fixed. In this way, task-specific biases are written into the key-value memory of attention with only a small number of trainable parameters, continuously influencing subsequent representations and generation distributions, and thereby improving task performance. In clinical applications, Prefix Tuning is particularly suitable for long-text generation, structured generation, and multimodal alignment, as task information can be injected more deeply into the model’s attention layers. For example, Sonsbeek et al. [[32]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0032) employed Prefix Tuning to map visual features into learnable prefixes and prompted the language model with these prefixes together with the question. Their method achieved competitive performance on benchmarks such as SLAKE, OVQA, and PathVQA, indicating that Prefix Tuning can enable effective vision-language alignment and open-ended generation on small-scale medical datasets with only a minimal number of trainable parameters. In addition, RadAdapt [[179]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0179) further shows that lightweight Prefix tuning can improve the quality of radiology report summarization and enhance the model’s ability to handle long clinical text generation tasks.

#### 5.3.4. Adapter

Adapter [[180]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0180) is a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method whose core idea is to insert small trainable Adapter modules into each layer of the Transformer block while keeping the backbone parameters of the pre-trained model _f θ_ fixed. Only the parameters of these newly added modules are optimized to adapt the model to downstream tasks. A typical Adapter combines an MLP with a residual connection. Given the hidden state $h_{l} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ at layer _l_, it is first compressed by a down-projection matrix $W_{\text{down}} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d}$, where _m_ ≪ _d_, then passed through a nonlinear activation function _σ_( · ), and finally restored by an up-projection matrix $W_{\text{up}} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times m}$. The adapted hidden state can be written as(8)$h_{l}^{'} = h_{l} + W_{\text{up}} \sigma \left(\right. W_{\text{down}} h_{l} \left.\right)$During training, only _W_ down, _W_ up, and optionally a small number of normalization parameters are updated, while the backbone weights remain unchanged. In this way, Adapter enables efficient task adaptation with very few trainable parameters, while reducing the forgetting risk and resource cost associated with full-parameter fine-tuning. In clinical applications, the main advantage of Adapter lies in its plug-in design and low intrusion into the backbone model, making it particularly suitable for institution-specific adaptation under privacy and compliance constraints, while also supporting flexible maintenance and fast switching across tasks. For example, MedAdapter [[181]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0181) trains lightweight adapters to rerank candidate answers and achieves efficient medical reasoning adaptation for both white-box and black-box models without fine-tuning the backbone or sharing raw data, demonstrating the engineering feasibility of Adapter in restricted deployment settings. In addition, MAKEN [[182]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0182) introduces adapter tuning and medical knowledge enhancement into the BLIP-2 framework, improving the accuracy and coherence of medical report generation by training only lightweight modules, which shows that Adapter can achieve effective medical domain adaptation under limited paired data and constrained training resources.

#### 5.3.5. Comparative analysis and selection recommendations for efficient fine-tuning strategies

**Comparative analysis:** In Medical MLLMs, the main differences among LoRA, Prompt Tuning, Prefix Tuning, and Adapter lie in where task-specific information is injected, namely, the weight space, input layer, attention prefixes, and internal layer modules, respectively. This leads to different trade-offs in performance, inference cost, maintainability, and deployment flexibility. LoRA performs low-rank updates on linear-layer weights and can usually achieve performance close to full-parameter fine-tuning with almost no additional inference latency, making it a practical general solution that balances accuracy and efficiency [[172]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0172). By contrast, Prompt Tuning learns only a small number of continuous soft prompts at the input embedding layer and leaves the backbone structure almost unchanged, resulting in minimal parameter cost and simple training, version control, and rollback. However, its effectiveness depends more heavily on the scale of the backbone model and the representation capacity of soft prompts, and it often has limited performance in multimodal medical alignment and complex reasoning tasks. It is also more sensitive to prompt form and may be more vulnerable to prompt injection or instruction override in prompt-driven deployment settings [[175]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0175). Prefix tuning injects learnable prefixes into the attention layers and, while keeping the backbone unchanged, provides stronger conditioning throughout the generation process. Therefore, it is generally more robust in low-resource settings and generation tasks, but it also incurs additional attention computation overhead, which is particularly important for long-form clinical text generation [[178]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0178). Adapter inserts plug-and-play bottleneck modules into Transformer layers to enable lightweight layer-wise adaptation. Its strengths lie in high modularity, natural support for multi-task isolation and rapid switching, and greater ease of long-term maintenance. In multimodal systems, it can also serve as an interface layer for modality alignment or domain adaptation. However, its performance is sensitive to insertion position and module capacity, and thus requires more careful architectural design and ablation analysis [[180]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0180), [[183]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0183). Overall, these four strategies can be viewed as different ways of injecting task-specific parameters while keeping the backbone frozen: through low-rank updates in weight space, lightweight prompts at the input layer, deep conditioning in attention layers, or modular updates within layers. In general, the closer the injection point is to the model interior, the greater the control over representation distributions and the higher the potential performance ceiling, but this is usually accompanied by higher inference cost and engineering complexity.

**Selection Recommendations:** The choice of parameter-efficient fine-tuning strategies for Medical MLLMs should be made by jointly considering target performance, inference latency, data scale, and long-term maintenance cost. When the goal is to achieve a balanced optimum in accuracy, efficiency, and deployment cost with minimal additional inference latency, LoRA is usually the preferred default option. By applying low-rank updates to linear-layer weights, it can achieve performance close to full-parameter fine-tuning while typically introducing almost no extra inference overhead, making it well suited for engineering deployment and large-scale use in resource-constrained settings. If the priority is to minimize trainable parameters and simplify version management and rollback while keeping the backbone almost untouched, such as in scenarios requiring rapid iteration, strict compliance auditing, or frequent backbone updates, Prompt Tuning can be adopted. This method learns only soft prompts at the input embedding layer and therefore has the lowest training and maintenance cost, although its performance depends more strongly on the backbone model and the capacity of prompt representations, which may limit its effectiveness in complex cross-modal alignment and reasoning tasks [[175]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0175). When annotated data are scarce but stronger end-to-end conditioning is required, such as for long-form clinical generation, structured outputs, or multimodal alignment tasks in which the model must continuously retain task priors, Prefix Tuning is a suitable choice. By injecting learnable prefixes into the attention mechanism, it enables later tokens to attend to prefix information as if reading virtual tokens, and is often more robust in low-resource settings. However, its consumption of context budget and additional attention computation must be carefully considered, especially in long-sequence generation. Finally, when the system must support long-term multi-task maintenance and rapid task switching, Adapter is often more advantageous. By introducing plug-and-play bottleneck modules for lightweight updates within layers, it facilitates modular deployment and task isolation. However, it usually adds extra module computation during inference, and its effectiveness is more sensitive to insertion position and capacity design, requiring task-specific architectural validation.

### 5.4. Reasoning activation in medical MLLMs

Large-scale self-supervised pre-training equips Medical MLLMs with rich general and medical knowledge, as well as strong task adaptability. However, because traditional training objectives mainly focus on semantic modeling and next-token prediction, these models often struggle to provide clear and transparent reasoning processes in complex tasks such as multi-step reasoning, diagnostic analysis, and treatment decision-making. To meet clinical demands for transparency and interpretability, researchers have introduced the CoT mechanism into Medical MLLMs to enhance their reasoning ability and intermediate-process expression [[184]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0184). To effectively integrate CoT into Medical MLLMs, two main approaches have been proposed. As shown in [Fig. 11](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0011), the first is supervised reasoning fine-tuning, which leverages the latent reasoning ability acquired during large-scale pre-training and adapts the model to the CoT framework through training on CoT-style demonstration data [[185]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0185). The second is GRPO, which is based on a Deep-RL framework and uses general LLMs as evaluators to score model outputs in terms of CoT format compliance, reasoning consistency, and answer correctness, and then dynamically optimize the policy model’s reasoning capability accordingly [[9]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0009).

#### 5.4.1. Supervised reasoning fine-tuning

**Basic Principle:** Ye et al. [[8]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0008) observed that LLMs already acquire logical reasoning ability and thinking patterns during pre-training. Therefore, the core of supervised reasoning fine-tuning is not to inject entirely new knowledge, but to activate the model’s latent reasoning ability and regulate its output format through carefully designed cognitive templates. This suggests that the development of Medical MLLMs can shift from knowledge acquisition to knowledge activation. As shown in [Fig. 11](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0011), this method inserts intermediate reasoning steps between the question and the answer in conventional SFT data, enabling the model to learn the structure and expression of CoT and thereby effectively activate its reasoning ability. In addition, the training methods and parameter-efficient fine-tuning strategies commonly used in conventional SFT are also applicable to supervised reasoning fine-tuning.

**Dataset Construction Process:** As shown in [Fig. 11](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0011), the key steps of supervised reasoning fine-tuning include: (i) collecting complex medical tasks requiring multi-step reasoning from clinical dialogue and question-answering datasets, together with their reference answers, and enclosing the answers with the tag <answer>...</answer>; (ii) for each example, having medical experts or strong medical reasoning models write detailed CoT for the given question and answer. Such CoT may include medical image interpretation, intermediate reasoning steps, and diagnostic conclusions, which are then enclosed with the tag <think>...</think>; and (iii) standardizing all examples into the format of ”medical image, question, <think> reasoning process </think>, <answer> answer </answer>.” These formatted examples are then used as the training set for SFT of Medical MLLMs, thereby activating the model’s reasoning ability.

**Medical Application:** Fan et al. proposed ChestX-Reasoner [[139]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0139) and conducted supervised reasoning fine-tuning on 59,000 visual question-answering samples and 301,000 clinically validated reasoning steps. The results show that the model outperforms multiple Medical MLLMs and general-domain MLLMs in both diagnostic accuracy and reasoning ability. Compared with the best-performing Medical MLLMs, the best-performing general-domain MLLMs, and its own base model without supervised reasoning fine-tuning, its reasoning ability improves by 16%, 8.5%, and 18%, respectively, while its answer accuracy increases by 3.3%, 24%, and 27%, respectively.

#### 5.4.2. GRPO

**Basic Principle:** GRPO [[186]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0186) is a policy optimization method based on Deep-RL and models medical reasoning tasks with an outcome reward model (ORM) [[187]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0187). Unlike methods that rely on intermediate-process feedback, ORM evaluates only the final generated CoT and answer as a whole. Its scoring can be binary, such as correct or incorrect, or extended to multidimensional assessments covering medical correctness, logical consistency, and format compliance. During training, GRPO performs multiple exploratory generations for the same medical task, producing a set of sampled outputs that include both CoT and final answers. These outputs are then compared by the reward model according to their relative scores within the group. High-scoring samples are encouraged, while low-scoring samples are suppressed. Through this iterative process of sampling, scoring, and policy updating, GRPO continuously improves the CoT quality and reasoning accuracy of Medical MLLMs, ultimately generating outputs that better conform to clinical logic and semantic standards.

**Key Process:** As shown in [Fig. 11](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0011), the main steps of GRPO include: (i) defining evaluation criteria, where medical experts establish multidimensional scoring standards for CoT and answers in terms of format compliance, reasoning accuracy, logical consistency, and medical correctness, so that reward signals align with clinical requirements; (ii) constructing the reward model, in which experts score multiple sampled answers to the same question according to these criteria, thereby generating structured reward data that include the question, CoT, answer, and dimension-specific scores, which are then used to train or build the reward model; (iii) group-based sampling, where Medical MLLMs generate multiple independent CoT-and-answer samples for the same task to ensure diverse exploration; (iv) selecting high-quality samples, where the reward model scores all samples in the group and those above the group average are treated as positive optimization signals, with higher-scoring samples assigned greater update weight; (v) policy updating, in which relatively high-scoring samples are treated as high-quality experience and their generation probability is increased, while that of low-scoring samples is reduced; and (vi) iterative optimization, in which the cycle of sampling, scoring, and updating is repeated to progressively improve reasoning ability and answer accuracy under the joint guidance of evaluation criteria and the reward model.

**Medical Application:** Lai et al. proposed the Med-R1 model [[188]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0188), which divides reward rules into two dimensions: format reward and accuracy reward. Specifically, (i) the model is required to produce outputs enclosed by reasoning tags <think>...</think> and answer tags <answer>...</answer>. If the format is correct, a reward of 1 is assigned; otherwise, the reward is 0. (ii) The generated answer is then checked against the reference answer. If it matches exactly, the reward is 1; otherwise, it is 0. Benefiting from the generalization advantage of RL in reasoning, Med-R1 achieved an average cross-task generalization accuracy of 74.64%, substantially higher than the 42.58% of the base model. In each experiment, one task was randomly selected from five categories—disease diagnosis, anatomical identification, lesion grading, modality recognition, and biomedical attribute analysis—for training, while the other four were used for testing. These results indicate that GRPO can improve the general medical reasoning ability of the model rather than merely enhancing adaptation to a single task.

## 6. Evaluation methods

### 6.1. Automatic evaluation methods

#### 6.1.1. Evaluation metrics for closed-ended tasks

In closed-ended tasks, commonly used evaluation metrics include precision, recall, accuracy, and F1 score. These metrics provide standard ways to assess model performance on specific tasks, each with its own emphasis and application scenario, thereby enabling evaluation from different perspectives.

**Precision:** Precision measures the reliability of positive predictions and is particularly important in high-risk tasks where false positives are costly, such as cancer screening. Because false positives may lead to misdiagnosis or unnecessary treatment, precision is especially critical in such scenarios. It is defined as the proportion of true positive samples among all samples predicted as positive, and can be calculated as(9)$\text{Precision} = \frac{T P}{T P + F P}$where _TP_ denotes the number of true positives, and _FP_ denotes the number of false positives, that is, samples with negative labels that are incorrectly predicted as positive.

**Recall:** Recall reflects the model’s ability to cover positive samples and is defined as the proportion of actual positive samples that are correctly predicted as positive. It is particularly important in tasks where missed detections may lead to serious consequences, such as early disease screening and lesion detection. In these medical scenarios, a high false negative rate may cause patients to miss timely diagnosis and treatment. Therefore, recall effectively measures the model’s ability to detect positive cases. It is calculated as(10)$\text{Recall} = \frac{T P}{T P + F N}$where _TP_ denotes the number of true positives, and _FN_ denotes the number of false negatives.

**Accuracy:** Accuracy is suitable for tasks with relatively balanced class distributions, as it directly reflects the overall classification performance of the model. However, under data imbalance, accuracy may be less informative. For example, in rare disease diagnosis, a model may achieve high accuracy by correctly predicting a large number of negative samples while failing to identify the relatively few positive cases, thereby showing poor diagnostic capability. Therefore, for imbalanced tasks, accuracy should be used together with other metrics to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of model performance. Accuracy is defined as the proportion of correctly predicted samples among all samples, and is calculated as(11)$\text{Accuracy} = \frac{T P + T N}{T P + F P + T N + F N}$where _TP_ denotes the number of true positives, _TN_ denotes the number of true negatives, _FP_ denotes the number of false positives, and _FN_ denotes the number of false negatives. The sum of these four terms equals the total number of samples.

**F1 score:** The F1 score [[189]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0189) combines the strengths of precision and recall and is particularly suitable for tasks that require a balanced trade-off between the two. When precision and recall differ substantially, the F1 score provides a more stable evaluation measure. In medical classification tasks, especially in scenarios where false positives and false negatives must both be carefully balanced, the F1 score is often used as a key indicator of overall model performance. A higher F1 score suggests that the model can identify positive samples effectively while reducing incorrect predictions, thereby providing more reliable support for clinical decision-making. The F1 score is defined as the harmonic mean of precision and recall, and is calculated as(12)$F 1 = 2 \times \frac{\text{Precision} \times \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}$

#### 6.1.2. Evaluation metrics for open-ended generation tasks

In open-ended generation tasks, the quality of generated text is commonly evaluated using two types of methods: text-similarity-based metrics and LLMs-based evaluation methods. The former mainly include BLEU, ROUGE-L, CIDEr, and METEOR, which evaluate generation quality by measuring the overlap between generated and reference texts at the word, phrase, and sentence levels. The latter mainly include CheXbert, RadCliQ, and LLMs-based evaluation (LLM-as-a-Judge), which evaluate the outputs of Medical MLLMs at the semantic level through fine-tuned or prompted LLMs. [Table 8](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#tbl0008) summarizes the principles, characteristics, and application scenarios of these evaluation methods.

**BLEU:** BLEU [[190]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0190) is an automatic evaluation metric based on n-gram overlap. It measures text quality by calculating the local lexical and phrasal matching between generated text and reference text. Its core design is based on modified n-gram precision, together with a brevity penalty to correct overly short outputs. BLEU is computationally efficient and suitable for large-scale automatic evaluation, but it is limited in capturing deep semantics, long-text coherence, and grammatical or semantic inconsistency. In medical scenarios, BLEU is commonly used for the rapid evaluation of medical question-answering tasks, such as SLAKE, VQA-RAD, and PathVQA, to assess lexical-level overlap between generated answers and reference answers.

**ROUGE-L:** ROUGE-L [[191]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0191) is an automatic evaluation metric based on the longest common subsequence (LCS), which measures the consistency between generated text and reference text in terms of content coverage and sequence order. Compared with metrics based on fixed n-gram matching, ROUGE-L can better capture sentence-level structural similarity while allowing non-consecutive matches, making it more suitable for long-text generation tasks. Because it emphasizes coverage of reference information, it is commonly used in scenarios such as radiology reports, disease screening records, and case descriptions to evaluate the completeness and overall coherence of model outputs.

**CIDEr:** CIDEr [[192]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0192) is an automatic evaluation metric based on TF-IDF-weighted n-gram similarity, used to measure the consistency between generated text and reference text. Compared with traditional metrics, CIDEr effectively reduces the influence of common high-frequency words, such as ”the,” ”is,” and ”and,” while placing greater emphasis on the accurate use of key medical terms, such as ”pneumothorax,” ”pleural effusion,” and ”coronary artery calcification.” This property makes CIDEr particularly useful for tasks that require precise use of domain-specific terminology. It is especially suitable for multimodal image description tasks, such as the Findings section of radiology reports, as well as other applications that demand accurate medical expression.

**METEOR:** METEOR [[193]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0193) evaluates the similarity between generated text and reference text by incorporating synonym matching, stemming, and word-order penalties, and computes an overall score based on the harmonic mean of precision and recall. Compared with metrics that rely only on surface-level word matching, METEOR is more tolerant of partial synonymy, for example, it can better handle differences such as ”nodule” and ”lesion.” Because it places greater emphasis on recall, it is more suitable for tasks that require both flexible terminology expression and complete coverage of key information. In medical scenarios, METEOR is commonly used in Open VQA, diagnostic reasoning, and case discussion tasks to assess the plausibility and information coverage of generated content.

**CheXbert:** CheXbert [[194]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0194) is an automatic evaluation method designed for chest X-ray reports. Built by fine-tuning BioBERT [[91]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0091), it can make structured predictions over 14 categories of imaging observations and evaluate report quality by comparing the label consistency between generated and reference reports. Compared with general text-similarity metrics, CheXbert is more clinically oriented and can more directly reflect agreement in diagnostic conclusions. However, its evaluation scope is largely limited to predefined chest imaging observations, which restricts its applicability to non-thoracic diseases or other imaging modalities. Therefore, CheXbert is mainly used to assess the quality of chest disease report generation and has shown strong accuracy and practical value in chest imaging analysis.

**RadCliQ:** RadCliQ [[195]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0195) is a composite evaluation metric for radiology report generation. It assesses overall report quality by integrating multiple metrics, including BLEU, BERTScore, CheXbert, and RadGraph-F1. Compared with single metrics, RadCliQ can flexibly adjust the weights of various evaluation metrics according to task characteristics, thereby achieving closer agreement with human judgment. However, it is computationally expensive and is mainly suited to complex medical text tasks such as radiology report generation. Therefore, RadCliQ is commonly used in comprehensive evaluation settings that require both linguistic quality and clinical utility, such as multimodal image report generation, clinical case summarization, and radiology report generation.

**LLM-as-a-Judge:** In recent years, general-purpose LLMs have increasingly been used for the automatic evaluation of open-ended medical generation tasks. By incorporating the original question, reference answer, and evaluation criteria into the prompt, these methods can assess the outputs of Medical MLLMs from dimensions such as semantic consistency and logical coherence, thereby compensating for the limited ability of traditional text-similarity metrics to capture deeper semantics. For example, Zhou et al. [[196]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0196) introduced an LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation framework that uses GPT-4o-mini as the judge model and incorporates fine-grained reasoning process analysis to evaluate the generation quality of medical large models. The correlation coefficient between its evaluation results and expert ranking reached 0.88, significantly outperforming traditional text-similarity-based methods, which indicates the strong potential of LLMs for evaluating the quality of medical reasoning processes.

Under the Deep-RL framework, the evaluation of MLLMs is no longer limited to token-level matching between generated content and reference answers, but instead places greater emphasis on the logical coherence of the reasoning process, the structural regularity of responses, and semantic consistency with the ground-truth answer. As shown in [Fig. 12](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0012), prompting can be used to construct a multidimensional reward mechanism, enabling general-purpose LLMs to better adapt to the requirements of medical evaluation tasks and to comprehensively assess dimensions such as reasoning logic, answer correctness, format compliance, and ethical safety. To better align with expert judgment, a small amount of expert-annotated data can further be used for fine-tuning or prompt optimization of the evaluator, thereby improving scoring reliability. On this basis, a closed-loop training process of ”sampling-evaluation-resampling” can be established, in which Medical MLLMs generate multiple candidate outputs for the same question, general-purpose LLMs provide reward signals, and algorithms such as GRPO or PPO are used to iteratively optimize the generation policy [[9]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0009). This process helps continuously improve the accuracy, structure, and logical coherence of model outputs, thereby enhancing their clinical applicability and trustworthiness.

### 6.2. Human evaluation

As a fundamental benchmark in medical AI research, human evaluation has gradually evolved into different assessment paradigms over time. As AI becomes increasingly integrated into medicine, human evaluation still plays a critical role in ensuring the reliability and clinical relevance of evaluation results.

**Development Stages:** Human evaluation in medical AI has roughly gone through three stages. The first stage is single-dimensional scoring, in which physicians mainly rate model performance based on clinical experience and overall impression. This approach is relatively coarse-grained and cannot adequately reflect model performance in specific tasks. The second stage introduces structured checklists, which enable evaluators to assess models systematically across multiple dimensions according to predefined standardized checklists, thereby addressing the limitations of single-dimensional scoring [[197]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0197). The third stage further develops toward weighted integration, in which different evaluation dimensions are assigned different weights according to task objectives and application scenarios, allowing more accurate reflection of model value in real medical practice [[198]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0198).

**Core Evaluation Dimensions:** Human evaluation usually focuses on clinical relevance and ethical compliance [[197]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0197), [[199]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0199). Clinical relevance mainly concerns the accuracy, usefulness, and decision-support value of Medical MLLM outputs in real clinical settings, and can be assessed by comparing model-generated diagnoses or management suggestions with expert opinions. Ethical compliance, in contrast, examines whether model outputs meet medical ethical and regulatory requirements, with particular attention to patient privacy, informed consent, fairness, and risk control, so as to ensure safety and trustworthiness in clinical use.

**Limitations:** Despite its progress, human evaluation still has several inherent limitations. First, it is inevitably influenced by subjective judgment, which creates challenges for consistency and reproducibility. Even with structured checklists and weighted evaluation strategies, this issue cannot be fully eliminated [[200]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0200). Second, human evaluation is costly, requiring substantial time and effort from physicians, and the heavy workload may negatively affect evaluation quality [[201]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0201).

### 6.3. Evaluation gaps and recommendations

Although the evaluation of Medical MLLMs has expanded from closed-ended classification metrics to text-similarity metrics, domain-specific automatic evaluators, and LLMs-based evaluation methods, the current evaluation framework still has clear limitations. In particular, automatic evaluation of open-ended generation remains heavily dependent on surface-level token matching and text coverage, with strong reliance on metrics such as BLEU, ROUGE, CIDEr, and METEOR. However, these metrics mainly reflect lexical, phrasal, or sequential overlap between generated text and reference answers, and cannot adequately assess whether the medical semantics are correct, whether key findings are omitted, or whether the conclusions are truly clinically useful. Previous studies have shown that traditional automatic metrics are not always highly consistent with radiologists’ judgments of report quality. In high-risk medical text generation tasks such as clinical summarization, n-gram coverage and general semantic scores are also insufficient to fully capture factual consistency, hallucination, omission, and clinical relevance [[202]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0202). Therefore, future studies should avoid relying on a single text-similarity metric as the primary basis for drawing evaluation conclusions in open-ended tasks. Instead, they should incorporate more specialized metrics for clinical semantics and factual correctness, together with expert-based spot checking, so as to build a multi-level evaluation framework that jointly considers text quality, medical correctness, and clinical value.

Second, although LLMs-based automatic evaluation is superior to surface-level token matching in semantic judgment and can assess the outputs of Medical MLLMs from higher-level aspects such as logical coherence, content completeness, and clinical plausibility, its reproducibility and cross-study comparability remain limited. On the one hand, LLMs-based evaluation results are often influenced by factors such as scoring criteria, prompt templates, few-shot examples, output format, and the evaluator model itself, so that even for the same evaluation target, different studies may reach conclusions that are not directly comparable because of differences in evaluation settings [[203]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0203). On the other hand, existing medical studies have also shown that current LLM-as-a-Judge methods still do not consistently achieve expert-level performance in some tasks, and their reliability often depends on additional prompt optimization and clinical constraint design [[204]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0204). Therefore, future use of LLMs for automatic evaluation should disclose evaluator model versions, prompt templates, scoring rules, and output parsing procedures as fully as possible, and should improve robustness and reproducibility through multi-prompt reassessment, cross-validation with multiple evaluator models, and repeated sampling.

Finally, the current evaluation framework still lacks mature and unified methods for assessing the ethics, safety, and privacy risks of Medical MLLM outputs. Most existing automatic evaluations mainly focus on accuracy, text similarity, and semantic consistency, while lacking systematic quantification of potentially harmful advice, sensitive health information leakage, group bias, and ethical violations. Relevant systematic reviews have pointed out that ethical analyses of LLMs in healthcare remain limited and heterogeneous, with inconsistent evaluation methods and insufficient standardization [[205]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0205). At the same time, studies involving protected health information have shown that the adequacy of current privacy-preservation measures remains to be validated, and standardized frameworks for bias auditing are also lacking [[206]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0206). In light of these limitations, future evaluation frameworks should go beyond conventional performance assessment and further incorporate dimensions such as safety, bias, fairness, privacy leakage risk, accountability, and ethical compliance. They should also establish graded evaluation mechanisms for high-risk medical scenarios, for example by auditing ”errors that may lead to misdiagnosis,” ”outputs that may reveal patient identity,” and ”responses that may result in unfair medical treatment” in separate modules, so as to improve the relevance of evaluation results for real clinical deployment.

## 7. Applications of medical MLLMs

### 7.1. Multimodal medical question answering

Med-VQA is one of the major application directions of Medical MLLMs. Its core goal is to jointly leverage medical images, clinical text, and other heterogeneous medical information to perform cross-modal understanding, medical reasoning, and answer generation, thereby supporting tasks such as diagnostic analysis, case discussion, and report verification. For example, HuatuoGPT-Vision [[129]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0129) constructed the PubMedVision dataset with 1.294 million Med-VQA samples and trained a 34B-parameter model, achieving accuracies of 68.1%, 76.9%, 63.5%, and 58.2% on VQA-RAD, SLAKE, PathVQA, and PMC-VQA, respectively, with an average of 66.7%. These results highlight its potential in complex case-oriented question answering and explanation generation. ELIXR [[207]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0207) further demonstrates the value of Med-VQA: by aligning a medical visual encoder with LLMs, it achieved accuracies of 58.7% and 54.8% on the chest subset of VQA-RAD and the MIMIC-CXR test set, respectively, and can be extended to tasks such as radiology report quality verification, suggesting that Medical MLLMs are beginning to move from benchmark validation toward more clinically meaningful case-based applications. However, real-world deployment of such systems still faces multiple challenges, including the scarcity of high-quality multimodal medical data, model hallucination and limited decision interpretability, strict privacy and regulatory requirements, and difficulties in deep integration with existing clinical workflows. Existing studies suggest that only by embedding these systems into practical workflows such as EHRs, together with privacy protection and continuous monitoring mechanisms, can stable and sustainable clinical application be more realistically achieved [[208]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0208).

### 7.2. Medical report generation

Med-RG has become one of the most clinically valuable applications of Medical MLLMs. Its core objective is to jointly leverage medical images, clinical text, and relevant contextual information to automatically generate diagnostic reports that are clinically accurate, structurally standardized, and linguistically readable, thereby supporting radiology interpretation, result communication, and documentation. Existing studies show that Medical MLLMs can not only perform conventional image-to-text generation, but also incorporate prior examinations, report context, and clinical indications to improve report completeness and verifiability. For example, expert evaluation of FlamingoCXR [[209]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0209) showed that, in 56.1% of ICU chest X-ray cases and 77.7% of emergency chest X-ray cases in India, its generated reports were judged by at least half of the reviewers to be better than or comparable to the original clinician reports, indicating strong potential for high-quality report generation. CheXagent [[29]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0029) further demonstrated its value in real workflows: using its draft reports saved radiology residents 36% of their time and improved writing efficiency for residents and attending physicians in 81% and 61% of cases, respectively, without reducing report quality. This suggests that Med-RG is gradually evolving from fully manual writing toward human-AI collaborative reporting. Nevertheless, the clinical deployment of Medical MLLMs in Med-RG still faces several challenges, including the mismatch between automatic evaluation metrics and real clinical quality, reduced generalization caused by cross-institutional data distribution shifts, persistent risks of hallucination and omission of key findings, and insufficient privacy protection, regulatory compliance, system integration, and post-deployment monitoring. Existing studies have pointed out that most current applications of medical large models remain at the proof-of-concept stage, and that more rigorous multi-center validation and workflow integration are still required before stable and sustainable clinical deployment can be achieved [[208]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0208).

### 7.3. Medical diagnosis

Medical diagnosis is an important application of Medical MLLMs. Its core objective is to integrate multimodal evidence from medical images, clinical text, patient history, and electronic health records to support disease recognition and differential diagnosis, thereby providing more comprehensive assistance for clinical decision-making. Existing studies show that Medical MLLMs are evolving from single-task recognition models into more general systems capable of handling complex diagnostic reasoning. For example, Med-Gemini [[35]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0035) achieved an accuracy of 93.5% on USMLE-MM and showed an average relative improvement of 44.5% over GPT-4V across seven multimodal medical diagnosis benchmarks, including the NEJM Image Challenge and MMMU, demonstrating strong cross-modal diagnostic understanding and medical reasoning ability. In addition, Med-MLLM proposed by Liu et al. [[161]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0161) further highlights the value of such models in low-resource and emerging disease scenarios. After large-scale training on general medical data, the model was able to provide effective diagnostic support using only a small amount of labeled Omicron data, suggesting the potential of Medical MLLMs for rapid adaptation and robust diagnosis in rare diseases or public health emergencies. Nevertheless, the real-world application of medical MLLMs in medical diagnosis still faces multiple challenges, including insufficient reasoning ability in complex clinical scenarios, unresolved risks of hallucination and diagnostic safety, and the lack of well-developed external medical knowledge bases and RAG support systems for evidence-grounded reasoning. Therefore, further improvements are still needed in terms of evidence grounding, reliability, and clinical usability [[210]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0210), [[211]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0211).

### 7.4. Medical agents

With the continued development of Medical Large Agent Models (Med-LAM), their applications are no longer limited to information understanding or decision support, but are gradually expanding toward more complete clinical service chains. On the one hand, Med-LAM can integrate multimodal perception with embodied execution and collaborate with systems such as surgical and nursing robots to accomplish complex tasks. On the other hand, it can enhance clinical analysis, planning, and execution by calling external tools, knowledge bases, and information systems. In addition, in scenarios such as out-of-hospital continuous care and chronic disease management, Med-LAM can provide personalized recommendations and risk alerts based on longitudinal health data. Overall, as shown in [Fig. 13](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0013), Med-LAM is gradually emerging as a multi-level application platform that spans embodied collaboration, tool use, and health management, representing an important direction for driving medical AI from knowledge assistance toward integrated healthcare services.

#### 7.4.1. Collaboration between agents and clinical robots

Deep collaboration between medical agents and robotic systems, such as surgical and nursing robots, is one of the most promising future directions for Med-LAM. In surgical settings, Med-LAM can integrate multimodal information, including intraoperative images, voice commands, and instrument states, to support task decomposition, procedural planning, and action scheduling, thereby providing navigation and operational guidance for robots. For example, Zargarzadeh et al. [[212]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0212) proposed an MLLM-based collaborative robotic framework for surgical scenarios, in which Medical MLLMs perform high-level task reasoning, while an RL module is responsible for low-level motion planning and execution. Applied to autonomous blood suction and hemostasis assistance, the framework further incorporated complex intraoperative conditions such as active bleeding and blood clots. The results showed that the framework could achieve more reasonable priority decision-making and more interpretable operational planning in dynamic environments. In nursing settings, Med-LAM can also collaborate with nursing and in-hospital logistics robots by integrating ward monitoring data, nursing records, voice instructions, and task lists to support material delivery, specimen transport, assistance with ward rounds, and nursing workflow scheduling, thereby reducing nurses’ burden in repetitive routine tasks. For example, Lim et al. [[213]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0213) evaluated the robotic nurse assistant Florence in a real inpatient ward. The system was able to autonomously perform vital sign measurement, medication delivery, and small-item transport. The results showed that about 80% of patients reported a positive interaction experience, and nurses also recognized its potential for routine nursing support. These findings suggest that collaboration between Med-LAM and nursing robots is more likely to be deployed first in low-risk and standardized nursing support scenarios.

#### 7.4.2. External tool utilization by medical agents

As an intelligent medical assistant, Med-LAM can actively call external tools, knowledge bases, and information systems based on user intent and clinical context, thereby handling more complex medical tasks. Compared with traditional Medical MLLMs that rely mainly on internal parameters for question answering or generation, such agents can further connect to clinical risk calculators, medical knowledge bases, gene databases, imaging analysis models, and web browsers, extending their capability from knowledge response to tool execution and task planning. For example, AgentMD proposed by Jin et al. [[214]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0214) demonstrates the practical value of external tool use in clinical decision support. The system can automatically organize and invoke 2164 executable clinical risk calculators from PubMed, achieving over 85% accuracy in tool quality checking and over 90% pass rate in unit tests, while significantly improving risk prediction accuracy over GPT-4 on the RiskQA benchmark. In addition, GeneGPT [[215]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0215) further illustrates the potential of Med-LAM in calling external databases and APIs. By invoking the NCBI E-utils and BLAST interfaces for genomics question answering, it achieved an average score of 0.83 across eight GeneTuring tasks, substantially outperforming previous retrieval-augmented baselines. These results suggest that Med-LAM is evolving from a passive knowledge-response model based on parametric memory into an active decision system capable of task planning, tool selection, and execution coordination.

#### 7.4.3. Personalized health management and risk alerts

Personalized health management and risk alerting represent an important application direction of Med-LAM for out-of-hospital continuous care and chronic disease management. By continuously integrating wearable-device data, clinical records, prior interactions, and patient-reported symptoms, Med-LAM can identify individual health patterns, generate personalized recommendations, and provide early warnings when abnormal trends emerge. In recent years, its role in long-term health management has gradually expanded from single-turn health question answering to continuous monitoring, individualized recommendation generation, and proactive risk alerting based on longitudinal multimodal data. For example, Google’s pH-LLM [[216]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0216), developed for sleep and fitness scenarios, achieved 79% and 88% accuracy on multiple-choice tests in sleep medicine and fitness, respectively. In a comprehensive evaluation on 857 real cases, it performed comparably to human experts on fitness-related tasks and outperformed the base Gemini model in personalized sleep insights, showing that Medical MLLMs can already generate high-quality individualized recommendations from longitudinal wearable data. PHIA [[217]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0217) further reflects the trend of agent-based systems toward health management: by combining multi-step reasoning, code generation, and web retrieval, it analyzes personal wearable time-series data and achieved significant improvements over non-agentic models in open-ended health reasoning across more than 6000 manually evaluated responses and 16,000 automatic evaluations. This suggests that Med-LAM is no longer limited to passive response generation, but can actively analyze, interpret, and provide recommendations based on individual data. In risk alerting, Health-LLM [[218]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0218) integrates multimodal wearable sensing data to predict and warn about multiple conditions, including stress, fatigue, sleep quality, and sleep disorders, indicating that Med-LAM has strong potential for personalized early risk detection and health warning based on longitudinal physiological and behavioral data.

### 7.5. Medical education

By integrating multimodal information such as images, text, and video with strong capabilities in understanding, reasoning, and generation, Medical MLLMs are driving medical education from traditional static knowledge delivery toward more intelligent, interactive, and scenario-based teaching. Compared with conventional digital teaching tools, Medical MLLMs can provide not only knowledge support and reasoning assistance, but also teaching content and training environments that more closely resemble real clinical settings. As a result, they show important application potential in case-based teaching, clinical reasoning training, and procedural skill development. At present, their representative applications in medical education mainly include virtual case generation, Knowledge support and procedural instruction.

#### 7.5.1. Virtual medical case generation

**Virtual Question Answer Generation:** Virtual question-answer generation is an important application of Medical MLLMs in medical education. Its core goal is to automatically construct clinically relevant and educationally valuable QA pairs from real electronic health records, medical reports, imaging data, and public medical literature, thereby supporting case-based discussion, diagnostic reasoning training, and the expansion of high-quality teaching resources. For example, Bai et al. [[219]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0219) proposed a synthetic data generation method for clinical QA, using LLaMA3-8B and GPT-4o in a zero-shot setting to automatically generate questions and answers from electronic health records. By adopting strategies such as generating questions that do not overlap with the original QA wording and guiding question generation with structured summaries, the method produced large amounts of medical QA data with varying difficulty levels. Experiments on RadQA and MIMIC-QA showed that the generated data more effectively improved downstream medical LLM fine-tuning than existing question-generation methods, and better evaluated deep understanding of clinical context. In multimodal settings, Huang et al. [[220]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0220) proposed the Med-VQA data synthesis framework MedVLSynther. Using images, captions, and in-text references from open-access PubMed Central articles as inputs, and a GLM-4.5V-108B-based generator to automatically produce questions, options, and reference answers, the framework constructed the MedSynVQA dataset, which contains 13,087 reviewed questions and 14,803 images. Experimental results showed that models trained on this dataset achieved better performance on six Med-VQA benchmarks, indicating that Medical MLLMs can already support high-quality and scalable virtual QA generation and provide new data support for question-bank construction and capability assessment in medical education.

**Virtual Clinical Interview Generation:** Unlike virtual question generation, which mainly focuses on item construction and knowledge training, virtual clinical interview generation emphasizes the simulation of real clinical communication. Its core objective is to build virtual patients based on real electronic health records, standardized cases, and clinical interview protocols, enabling medical students to engage in multi-turn questioning, symptom exploration, and history taking to develop their skills in history inquiry, clinical communication, and diagnostic reasoning. In recent years, this field has evolved from static case texts to interactive virtual patient systems with memory, retrieval, and feedback capabilities. For example, Yu et al. [[221]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0221) proposed AIPatient, a conversational virtual patient built on real electronic health records, which achieved an accuracy of 94.15% in EHR-based question answering and demonstrated comparable or superior fidelity, usability, and educational value to human standardized patients, suggesting that Medical MLLMs can support highly realistic virtual interview training. Furthermore, Yamamoto et al. [[222]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0222) used GPT-4 Turbo to simulate patients and generate feedback in a non-randomized controlled study involving Japanese medical students. The results showed that students who received AI-based virtual consultation training achieved significantly higher interviewing scores in standardized clinical skills examinations than those in the control group, indicating that medical MLLM-based virtual consultation systems can provide a repeatable and cost-effective training environment while also improving medical students’ practical interviewing performance to some extent.

#### 7.5.2. Knowledge support and procedural instruction

**Knowledge Support:** Knowledge support is an important application of Medical MLLMs in medical education. Its core goal is to combine medical images, clinical text, and external knowledge retrieval to answer standardized exam questions, case analysis problems, and image-text integrated questions, while also generating step-by-step explanations to help medical students understand diagnostic evidence and reasoning pathways. For example, Med-PaLM M [[19]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0019), as a general biomedical multimodal model, achieved SOTA or better performance on MultiMedBench across 14 tasks, including Med-VQA, Med-RG, and image interpretation, and demonstrated zero-shot medical reasoning ability. This indicates that unified multimodal models already have strong potential as educational tools for medical knowledge support. To further improve interpretability, MedCoT proposed by Liu et al. [[223]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0223) introduced multimodal CoT into Med-VQA. Through a hierarchical expert mechanism, it jointly generates answers and their reasoning paths, achieving accuracies of 87.50% and 87.26% on the closed-set tasks of VQA-RAD and SLAKE-EN, respectively, and significantly outperforming baseline prompting methods without CoT on VQA-RAD. These results suggest that Medical MLLMs can provide more interpretable knowledge support for medical students through explicit reasoning processes.

**Procedural Instruction:** Surgical skills training is another important application of Medical MLLMs in medical education. Its core goal is to combine surgical videos, intraoperative images, and procedural knowledge to provide step-by-step analysis, key-point guidance, and interactive instruction for clinical procedures, thereby helping medical students and residents understand complex workflows more intuitively. In recent years, this direction has evolved from static video explanation toward intelligent teaching systems with dialogue, feedback, and task-understanding capabilities. For example, LLaVA-Surg proposed by Li et al. [[224]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0224) structurally extracted knowledge from public surgical teaching videos, constructed the Surg-QA dataset containing 102,000 surgery video-instruction pairs, and trained a multimodal assistant capable of Open VQA on surgical videos. Experiments showed that it significantly outperformed existing general-purpose models in zero-shot surgical video question answering, indicating that Medical MLLMs can already generate high-quality step explanations and procedural guidance for surgical workflows. Furthermore, EndoChat proposed by Wang et al. [[225]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0225), was trained on the Surg-396K dataset and is capable of providing support for tasks such as intraoperative image understanding, scene-based question answering, and physician interaction. This reflects a shift in procedural education from ”video understanding” to ”interactive guidance”, and also indicates that Medical MLLMs have the potential to provide context-aware guidance in surgical training and procedural education.

## 8. Critical challenges and future directions

With the rapid development of Medical MLLMs, these models have shown great potential in medical diagnosis, treatment decision support, and clinical research. However, many challenges remain in their training, deployment, and application, including limited reliability, computational and efficiency bottlenecks, privacy and transparency issues, and ethical and regulatory risks. Despite these challenges, the future of Medical MLLMs remains promising. Emerging paradigms, such as multi-agent collaboration, innovative model architectures, and digital twin-driven applications, may further advance this field. ### 8.1. Critical challenges

#### 8.1.1. Hallucination and reliability issues

**Existing challenges:** During the training of Medical MLLMs, limitations in training data, such as noisy samples, incorrect annotations, and low-quality images, may weaken the model’s perception and reasoning ability. At the same time, newly introduced data may also interfere with existing knowledge representations. Together, these factors increase the risk that the model will generate content deviating from established medical facts, thereby leading to hallucinations. For example, the MedVH study by Gu et al. [[226]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0226) showed that medical vision-language models can still describe nonexistent lesions in chest X-ray report generation. Even for the relatively strong CheXagent, the instance-level hallucination rate remains close to 50%, indicating that current Medical MLLMs still face substantial challenges in reliability.

**Potential solutions:** To mitigate this issue, Deep-RL (as discussed in [Section 5.4.2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#sec0058)) and MRAG (as discussed in [Section 4.2.4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#sec0040)) can be combined to improve the response quality of Medical MLLMs. First, a multi-stage objective function can be constructed by integrating multiple evaluation criteria, such as retrieval relevance, reasoning coherence, answer accuracy, output format standardization, structural quality, and linguistic consistency. By assigning different weights to these criteria and applying Deep-RL, the model’s retrieval, reasoning, and generation capabilities can be optimized end to end. Second, within the same retrieval pool, content from authoritative medical websites or high-quality databases can be prioritized, and both retrieval quality and source reliability can be incorporated into the reward function. When new medical websites or datasets are introduced, the reward weights of the retrieval strategy can be further adjusted dynamically through RL, so as to continuously improve the credibility and timeliness of retrieved knowledge. This strategy not only strengthens the model’s judgment ability in the initial retrieval stage, but also provides more reliable knowledge support during subsequent reasoning and generation, thereby reducing hallucinations and improving overall response quality [[227]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0227).

#### 8.1.2. Deployment costs and efficiency bottlenecks

**Existing challenges:** Medical MLLMs often incur high computational cost during inference, which poses a significant barrier to clinical deployment. For example, Meditron-70B [[228]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0228) still required 332 h of training on 128 NVIDIA A100 80 GB GPUs. Although its training efficiency is relatively strong, its deployment and inference still demand substantial computational resources, making it difficult to support on edge devices such as dynamic CT scanners.

**Potential solutions:** Existing lightweight strategies mainly include the MoE architecture discussed in [Section 4.1.1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#sec0033). By dynamically allocating computational resources, MoE enables different expert modules to handle specific tasks or input modalities, thereby improving knowledge density while maintaining computational efficiency. For example, Med-MoE [[38]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0038) designs multiple expert branches for different types of medical images and selects appropriate experts through modality routing, achieving clear improvements over non-MoE models in image-text alignment and retrieval tasks. In addition, as discussed in [Section 4.1.2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#sec0034), model compression methods such as knowledge distillation can transfer the capability of large models into smaller ones. For instance, SigPhi-Med proposed by Zhou et al. [[39]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0039) uses only 26% of the high-quality training data from the LLaVA-Med-Alignment dataset for knowledge distillation, yet still outperforms larger models trained with more data, indicating that knowledge distillation based on high-quality data can help improve model knowledge density.

#### 8.1.3. Privacy and transparency

**Existing challenges:** The training and inference of Medical MLLMs usually rely on large amounts of sensitive patient data, creating multiple challenges in privacy protection and transparency. Such data often contain highly sensitive personal information, and without strict encryption, access control, and security safeguards, they may lead to privacy leakage and security vulnerabilities. These risks are further amplified in cloud deployment, where data upload, transmission, and third-party storage increase the possibility of unauthorized access, data leakage, and regulatory noncompliance. In addition, improper use of patient data may raise concerns related to discrimination, bias, or insufficient informed consent, thereby weakening trust in the clinical use of Medical MLLMs. At the same time, the decision-making process of Medical MLLMs still lacks sufficient transparency, and their outputs often fail to provide complete and traceable reasoning evidence, making it difficult for physicians and patients to understand how diagnostic conclusions are formed.

**Potential solutions:** In terms of privacy protection, data filtering and de-identification can effectively reduce the risk of information leakage. For example, the MIMIC-III dataset [[130]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0130) removed identity identifiers in accordance with HIPAA standards before public release. During deployment, input filtering and local deployment can further be combined so that model training or updating can be conducted without directly aggregating raw patient data, thereby reducing the privacy risks caused by centralized cloud storage and cross-institutional data transfer. In terms of transparency and interpretability, the supervised reasoning fine-tuning and GRPO methods introduced in [Section 5.4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#sec0056) can guide the model to explicitly provide intermediate reasoning steps before generating the final output, thereby enhancing the transparency and interpretability of the reasoning process while improving answer accuracy.

#### 8.1.4. Ethical and regulatory challenges

**Existing challenges:** The deployment of Medical MLLMs in clinical practice involves not only technical performance issues, but also complex ethical and regulatory challenges. First, the World Health Organization (WHO) has pointed out that factors such as biased training data, opaque data sources, and unequal population coverage may lead to unfair outcomes across different genders, ages, racial groups, or disease subpopulations, thereby amplifying existing healthcare inequalities [[229]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0229). Second, Ong et al. further noted that, because of their generative nature, Medical MLLMs may produce incorrect, overgeneralized, or insufficiently grounded recommendations with high confidence in clinical settings, which may threaten patient safety and also raise concerns regarding physician responsibility, informed consent, and accountability in clinical decision-making [[230]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0230). In addition, documents from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the European Union Artificial Intelligence Act (EU AI Act) indicate that Medical MLLMs are characterized by strong generality, blurred application boundaries, and continual updating, making them difficult to regulate under traditional frameworks designed for medical devices or conventional software. Existing regulatory systems remain insufficient, especially with respect to model iteration, cross-scenario transfer, and post-deployment performance drift [[231]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0231). Therefore, institutional arrangements concerning data ownership, responsibility allocation, transparency, fairness, and continuous oversight remain key bottlenecks in the clinical translation of Medical MLLMs.

**Potential solutions:** To address these challenges, progress is needed at three levels: governance frameworks, technical controls, and clinical oversight. First, at the governance level, the WHO’s principles for AI governance in health can serve as a useful reference for establishing a full-lifecycle framework covering model design, training, validation, deployment, and updating. Such a framework should clearly define the responsibilities of developers, deploying institutions, and clinical users, while preserving a human-in-the-loop mechanism in high-risk scenarios to strengthen informed consent, accountability, and patient safety [[229]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0229). At the technical level, further efforts are needed in data governance, bias identification and evaluation, external validation, interpretability records, audit trails, and standardized technical documentation, so as to improve the transparency, stability, and accountability of model outputs. Research by Tam et al. on human evaluation frameworks for LLMs in healthcare also suggests that current evaluation practices still have clear limitations in reliability, generalizability, and applicability, highlighting the need for more standardized and systematic evaluation and reporting paradigms [[197]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0197). At the application level, clinical AI systems with continuous-update characteristics should adopt dynamic deployment and continuous monitoring frameworks. On the basis of multi-center external validation, post-deployment performance tracking, and real-time clinical verification, a clinical governance pathway can gradually be established that balances innovation promotion with risk control [[232]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0232).

### 8.2. Future directions

#### 8.2.1. Multi-agent collaboration

For complex medical tasks involving multimodal input, long CoT reasoning, and cross-disciplinary decision-making, the multi-agent collaboration of Medical MLLMs is expected to evolve along the path of complexity-aware scheduling, role-based collaboration, and interactive feedback optimization, as shown in [Fig. 15](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0015). First, collaboration mechanisms may shift from fixed patterns to dynamic scheduling based on task complexity, so that the number, type, and collaboration depth of agents can be adaptively allocated to balance diagnostic performance, reasoning efficiency, and computational cost. Second, these systems are likely to further develop toward role-based specialization and knowledge integration in medical scenarios. By introducing specialty-specific roles, enabling multi-perspective reasoning, and incorporating historical experience memory, they may improve reasoning stability, evidence integration, and decision consistency in complex case analysis. More importantly, future systems may move beyond static role assignment toward continuous optimization through multi-round interaction and external feedback, allowing information-gathering strategies, reasoning paths, and decision outcomes to be iteratively refined in a dynamic closed loop. Overall, multi-agent collaboration in Medical MLLMs is expected to progress from simple combinations of multiple models to a highly adaptive collaborative paradigm integrating dynamic scheduling, expert coordination, experience accumulation, and autonomous iteration.

Based on the above technical route, future multi-agent collaboration in Medical MLLMs should not be regarded merely as a simple extension of ”multiple models answering together,” but should instead be studied through several testable hypotheses. First, complexity-aware dynamic scheduling may adaptively choose between single-agent and multi-agent reasoning paths according to task difficulty, thereby reducing inference cost and resource consumption while maintaining diagnostic performance. MDAgents [[233]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0233) showed that dynamic scheduling structures achieved the best performance on 7 of the 10 benchmark tasks and provided a better balance between accuracy and efficiency than fixed structures. Second, role-based multi-expert collaboration may improve knowledge integration, reasoning stability, and cross-task generalization in complex medical tasks. MDTeamGPT [[234]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0234), through consensus aggregation, residual-style discussion, and accumulation of experience in a knowledge base, achieved accuracies of 90.1% and 83.9% on MedQA and PubMedQA, respectively, demonstrating the effectiveness of multi-agent collaboration. Finally, interaction-feedback-driven continuous optimization may enhance key information collection and final decision quality in multi-turn consultations. DoctorAgent-RL [[235]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0235), through repeated interaction among doctor, patient, and evaluator agents, continuously optimized questioning strategies and outperformed existing methods in both multi-turn reasoning and final diagnosis, while also showing potential to reduce misdiagnosis risk and improve medical resource allocation.

#### 8.2.2. Novel model architectures

**Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs):** Traditional MLP usually rely on fixed activation functions, such as ReLU, Sigmoid, or Tanh, to fit complex nonlinear functions. In contrast, KANs [[236]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0236) place learnable activation functions on connections between nodes rather than inside the nodes themselves, thereby improving nonlinear representation ability while maintaining strong parameter efficiency and interpretability. In the medical domain, replacing the modality alignment module with KANs may help model complex cross-modal nonlinear semantic mappings more precisely and preserve richer multimodal information during feature fusion. Although the application of KANs in Medical MLLMs is still at an early stage, their potential advantages deserve close attention.

**State Space Models:** In Transformer architectures, the computational cost of self-attention usually grows quadratically with sequence length, leading to substantial computation and memory burdens in long-text modeling. To address this issue, Mamba [[237]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0237) introduces an architecture based on selective state space models (SSMs). By combining SSMs with selection mechanisms and hardware-friendly parallel algorithms, it enables efficient sequence modeling that scales linearly with sequence length, thereby significantly improving long-sequence processing efficiency and showing strong performance on long-context tasks. This suggests that SSMs may become an important future direction for the underlying architecture of LLMs.

**Introducing New Task Modules:** Recent studies suggest that the embeddings produced by the text decoder in Medical MLLMs contain not only global semantic information but also fine-grained lesion-related visual features. Based on this property, the output embeddings of the final decoder layer can be used as inputs to downstream task modules, thereby transferring diagnostic language understanding to tasks such as disease recognition and segmentation. For example, MedPLIB [[41]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0041) integrates a SAM-Med-based segmentation module into Medical MLLMs and uses the final-layer embedding corresponding to a specific control token (”<SEG>”) to guide lesion segmentation. In the future, Medical MLLMs may further incorporate new task modules, such as lesion detection and physiological signal monitoring, to continuously expand their multi-task capabilities.

#### 8.2.3. Medical MLLM driven digital twins

With the rapid development of MLLMs, the application of digital twins in healthcare is entering a new stage. The integration of MLLMs with digital twins enables dynamic adjustment and prediction based on real-time data, thereby advancing innovation in medical practice. This synergy shows particularly strong potential in applications such as real-time surgical support, clinical trial simulation and optimization, and continuous monitoring and health management.

**Real-time surgical support:** In real-time surgical support, as shown in [Fig. 15](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0015), medical MLLM-driven digital twins can follow a pipeline of patient-specific preoperative modeling, continuous intraoperative multimodal data integration, synchronized updating of physical and virtual states, and output of risk predictions and strategy recommendations. The system first constructs a patient-specific digital twin using preoperative imaging, medical history, and physiological parameters, and then continuously incorporates intraoperative video, instrument status, and vital signs to keep the virtual model synchronized with the real patient. Medical MLLMs further integrate these multimodal data to support joint understanding, risk interpretation, and strategy generation, thereby providing real-time decision support for surgeons. This paradigm reflects the evolution of surgical digital twins from static preoperative modeling to integrated intraoperative perception, prediction, and decision support. Based on this framework, future MLLM-driven real-time surgical support should move beyond shallow assistance based on visual reconstruction and passive prompting. First, if digital twins can continuously synchronize patient anatomy, instrument status, and physiological changes, their real-time risk prediction and surgical navigation capabilities may surpass those of systems relying only on static preoperative modeling. Asciak et al. [[238]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0238) proposed the concept of Digital Twin-Assisted Surgery (DTAS), emphasizing that surgical digital twins should consist of the patient entity, a virtual counterpart, and bidirectional data exchange to support preoperative planning, intraoperative navigation, risk assessment, and decision-making. Second, if Medical MLLMs can integrate multimodal intraoperative information in a synchronized physical-virtual environment and generate interpretable strategy recommendations, real-time surgical support systems may evolve from static assistance to continuous decision-making and even toward higher levels of automation. For example, Kim et al. [[239]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0239) proposed the autonomous surgical framework SRT-H, which adopts a two-level architecture in which the high-level policy is responsible for task planning and the low-level policy for robotic trajectory generation. The framework achieved a 100% success rate on eight previously unseen ex vivo cholecystectomy samples, demonstrating the potential for continuous decision-making and autonomous control in complex surgical workflows.

**Clinical trial simulation and optimization:** In clinical trial simulation and optimization, as shown in [Fig. 15](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0015), medical MLLM-driven digital twins can follow a pathway of patient-level digital twin construction, longitudinal disease trajectory prediction, intervention scenario simulation, and trial design optimization. Specifically, the system can build patient-specific digital twins from electronic health records, imaging, treatment records, and follow-up data, and then use Medical MLLMs to model and predict individual disease progression and treatment response. It can further simulate the effects of different drug regimens, surgical strategies, and follow-up schedules on trial outcomes, thereby supporting participant selection, identification of potentially benefiting populations, construction of virtual controls, and optimization of trial protocols. This paradigm reflects the shift in clinical trials from static experience-based design toward dynamic prediction and simulation optimization that accounts for individual heterogeneity [[240]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0240). Based on this framework, future MLLM-driven trial simulation should go beyond merely digitizing existing workflows. First, if patient-level digital twins can integrate multimodal clinical data and accurately predict individual disease trajectories and treatment responses, they may outperform traditional static stratification methods in participant selection, beneficiary identification, and virtual control construction. Akbarialiabad et al. [[241]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0241) suggested that dynamically updated virtual patients could simulate both the natural course under standard care and the potential response to new interventions, enabling efficacy and safety signals to be explored before real trials begin. Second, if Medical MLLMs can support scenario simulation across multiple regimens, endpoints, and follow-up schedules within a digital twin environment, clinical trials may achieve earlier optimization in dosing strategies, sample size, and statistical power, thereby shortening trial duration, reducing development costs, and lowering the risk of exposing participants to ineffective or potentially harmful treatments. For example, Wang et al. [[242]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0242) trained a generative digital twin model on historical trial and observational data to build individualized virtual controls for patients with Alzheimer’s disease, and validated its feasibility in the AWARE trial for evaluating alternative trial designs in advance. The results showed that, while maintaining statistical power, this approach could reduce the total sample size of future Alzheimer’s disease trials by approximately 9%-15% and the control group size by about 17%-26%.

**Virtual wards and health management:** In virtual wards and health management, as shown in [Fig. 15](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0015), medical MLLM-driven digital twins can follow a pathway of continuous sensing, synchronized state updating, risk prediction and early warning, and support for personalized intervention and resource allocation. Specifically, the system can integrate multi-source data from wearable devices, home monitoring instruments, electronic health records, and patient-reported information to construct and dynamically update patient-level digital twins. Medical MLLMs can then jointly interpret these multimodal temporal data to generate summaries of disease progression, deterioration alerts, treatment adjustment recommendations, and referral escalation plans, thereby supporting remote monitoring, care pathway optimization, and long-term health management. Overall, this paradigm reflects the evolution of virtual wards from passive monitoring toward continuous sensing, dynamic prediction, and proactive intervention. Based on this framework, future MLLM-driven virtual wards and health management should move beyond simple data aggregation and passive alerts. First, continuously updated patient-level digital twins combined with the interpretive capabilities of Medical MLLMs may outperform traditional threshold-based or rule-driven remote monitoring systems in early deterioration detection, acute event warning, and timely intervention. For example, Eslami et al. [[243]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0243) developed an ICU-oriented digital twin system based on LLaMA-3 and fine-tuned with LoRA on ICU physician notes, showing better treatment recommendation accuracy than zero-shot baselines, which highlights the potential of Medical MLLMs in patient state interpretation and clinical decision support. Second, multimodal digital twin systems that jointly incorporate physiological data, electronic health records, and home monitoring information may achieve more robust performance than single-source systems in readmission risk prediction, long-term disease assessment, and personalized treatment adjustment. Liu et al. [[244]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#bib0244) noted in their study of medical time-series LLMs that such models can integrate multimodal physiological temporal data from wearables, remote monitoring, and electronic health records, demonstrating strong potential for constructing patient-level digital twins in real-time monitoring, virtual wards, and long-term health management, while supporting disease progression modeling, risk warning, and personalized intervention.

#### 8.2.4. Future development priorities and expected progress

**Future development priorities:** We argue that future work on Medical MLLMs should not pursue all frontier directions in parallel, but should instead follow a staged pathway: first standardizing evaluation and enhancing reasoning, then advancing multi-agent collaboration and architectural innovation, and finally moving toward embodied medical systems. Specifically, the first priority should be the establishment of fair, comprehensive, and authoritative automatic benchmarking and auditing platforms for Medical MLLMs, together with the development of medical reasoning LLMs for clinical decision-making. The key reason is that the main bottleneck is no longer whether models can achieve high scores on exam-style medical benchmarks, but rather the lack of unified evaluation frameworks that closely reflect real clinical scenarios and can simultaneously assess safety, fairness, multi-turn interaction, and clinical reasoning. In addition, current Medical MLLMs for clinical decision-making still lack clear and auditable intermediate decision processes, making it difficult for them to integrate symptoms, examination findings, and differential diagnoses in a stepwise and clinically interpretable manner. The second priority should focus on multi-agent collaboration and new model architectures: the former can support distributed reasoning for complex cases, cross-specialty collaboration, and evidence integration, while the latter should aim to balance long-context understanding with low computational cost. In particular, state-space models represented by Mamba have shown potential for linear scaling with sequence length and higher inference efficiency, making them promising for longitudinal electronic health records, long clinical reports, and continuous physiological signal modeling. The third priority should be advanced surgical and nursing robotic systems with MLLMs as the cognitive core. Although embodied medical systems represent an important direction for future intelligent healthcare, they still face greater challenges in technical integration, cost control, regulatory evaluation, and ethics. Therefore, considering both medical and general MLLM development, it is more appropriate to first strengthen the foundational capabilities of being evaluable, reason-capable, and collaborative, and only then gradually move toward advanced robotic medical systems that are deployable, closed-loop, and potentially able to substitute for parts of professional clinical work.

**Expected Timeline of Progress:** As shown in [Fig. 15](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253526002654#fig0015), the future development of Medical MLLMs is expected to follow a progressive trajectory from the refinement of foundational capabilities to breakthroughs in efficient architectures, and ultimately to system-level intelligent healthcare applications. Over the next 1-2 years, research will likely focus on multi-agent collaboration, medical reasoning LLMs, and the development of fair, comprehensive, and authoritative automatic evaluation platforms for Medical MLLMs, with the goal of improving stepwise reasoning, cross-specialty collaboration, multi-turn interaction, and safety auditing in complex clinical tasks, while establishing standardized evaluation systems that better reflect real-world medical scenarios. Over the next 3-4 years, with continued advances in efficient architectures, Medical MLLMs are expected to maintain long-context understanding while significantly reducing parameter scale, memory usage, and inference cost, thereby better supporting integrated modeling of longitudinal electronic health records, long medical reports, continuous physiological signals, and cross-modal clinical data. Over the next 4-5 years, Medical MLLMs may be more deeply integrated with digital twins, surgical robots, and nursing robots, promoting the transition of intelligent healthcare from decision support to higher levels of automation, with broader applications in perioperative management, virtual wards, elderly care, and long-term health management. Overall, with continued progress in clinical reasoning, real-world evaluation frameworks, and efficient model architectures, Medical MLLMs are rapidly moving from methodological exploration toward clinical application, and will likely work together with digital twins and medical robotics to drive intelligent healthcare toward greater intelligence, automation, and system-level integration.

## 9. Conclusion

This survey systematically analyzes the foundations of Medical MLLMs, clarifies the key mechanisms through which they improve performance on medical tasks, and further emphasizes their application potential and performance advantages in two core tasks, namely Med-RG and Med-VQA. It also systematically reviews the core technical framework of Medical MLLMs, including visual encoders, modality alignment modules, and LLM backbones, and provides a detailed classification of related datasets according to different training stages. To further improve model performance, this survey reviews training methods such as CPT, IFT, SFT, RLHF, and RLAIF approaches, and conducts an in-depth analysis of the fundamental principles underlying parameter-efficient fine-tuning strategies. In addition, it introduces supervised reasoning fine-tuning and GRPO as two frameworks for activating reasoning abilities, highlighting their important roles in enhancing model reasoning capability, improving generalization performance, and increasing output transparency. Furthermore, it systematically analyzes existing evaluation methods, providing a useful reference for researchers assessing the performance of MLLMs across different medical tasks. Finally, this survey discusses the broad application prospects and future development directions of Medical MLLMs in the healthcare domain, and proposes potential solutions to current challenges, with the aim of providing theoretical support for future research and clinical practice.
