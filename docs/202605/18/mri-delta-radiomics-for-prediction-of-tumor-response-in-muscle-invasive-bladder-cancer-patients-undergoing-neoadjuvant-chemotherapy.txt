Title: MRI delta-radiomics for prediction of tumor response in muscle-invasive bladder cancer patients undergoing neoadjuvant chemotherapy

## Introduction

As quantitative imaging improves, dynamic imaging biomarkers have attracted attention for their potential to improve clinical prediction [[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR1 "Nardone V, Reginelli A, Rubini D et al (2024) Delta radiomics: an updated systematic review. Radiol Med 129(8):1197–1214. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1007/s11547-024-01853-4
                  
                "), [2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR2 "Tan D, Mohamad Salleh SA, Manan HA et al (2023) Delta-radiomics-based models for toxicity prediction in radiotherapy: a systematic review and meta-analysis. J Med Imaging Radiat Oncol 67(5):564–579. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1111/1754-9485.13546
                  
                ")]. Delta-radiomics, which captures temporal changes in radiomic features, has emerged as a potent tool to predict response and survival across multiple cancers [[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR3 "Cousin F, Louis T, Dheur S et al (2023) Radiomics and delta-radiomics signatures to predict response and survival in patients with non-small-cell lung cancer treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors. Cancers (Basel). 
                  https://doi.org/10.3390/cancers15071968
                  
                "), [4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR4 "Nasief H, Zheng C, Schott D et al (2019) A machine learning based delta-radiomics process for early prediction of treatment response of pancreatic cancer. npj Precision Oncology 3(1):25. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1038/s41698-019-0096-z
                  
                ")]. Through analysis of serial imaging, delta-radiomics can detect response-related changes earlier than conventional morphology [[5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR5 "Arita Y, Kwee TC, Akin O et al (2025) Multiparametric MRI and artificial intelligence in predicting and monitoring treatment response in bladder cancer. Insights Imaging 16(1):7. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1186/s13244-024-01884-5
                  
                ")].

Building on these advances, there is now growing interest in applying delta-radiomics to particular cancers, in which early treatment stratification could significantly influence patient outcomes [[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR1 "Nardone V, Reginelli A, Rubini D et al (2024) Delta radiomics: an updated systematic review. Radiol Med 129(8):1197–1214. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1007/s11547-024-01853-4
                  
                "), [5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR5 "Arita Y, Kwee TC, Akin O et al (2025) Multiparametric MRI and artificial intelligence in predicting and monitoring treatment response in bladder cancer. Insights Imaging 16(1):7. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1186/s13244-024-01884-5
                  
                ")]. Muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC) treated with neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NAC) represents a particularly compelling application. While NAC offers proven survival benefits, MIBC continues to pose therapeutic challenges due to the considerable variability in individual treatment response [[6](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR6 "Regmi SK, Kalapara A, Konety BR (2021) The need for interim assessment of neoadjuvant chemotherapy response for muscle invasive bladder cancer-can we help prevent definitive treatment delay in nonresponders? J Urol 205(2):327–329. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1097/ju.0000000000001381
                  
                "), [7](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR7 "Taber A, Christensen E, Lamy P et al (2020) Molecular correlates of cisplatin-based chemotherapy response in muscle invasive bladder cancer by integrated multi-omics analysis. Nat Commun 11(1):4858. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-020-18640-0
                  
                ")]. Identifying which patients will benefit from NAC remains a critical unmet need, as early prediction could guide personalized treatment, sparing non-responders from unnecessary toxicity and delays in definitive therapy [[8](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR8 "Boeri L, Soligo M, Frank I et al (2019) Delaying radical cystectomy after neoadjuvant chemotherapy for muscle-invasive bladder cancer is associated with adverse survival outcomes. Eur Urol Oncol 2(4):390–396. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1016/j.euo.2018.09.004

Evidence shows that delta-radiomics often outperforms baseline-only radiomics, improving accuracy in treatment response prediction [[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR3 "Cousin F, Louis T, Dheur S et al (2023) Radiomics and delta-radiomics signatures to predict response and survival in patients with non-small-cell lung cancer treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors. Cancers (Basel). 
                  https://doi.org/10.3390/cancers15071968
                  
                "), [9](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR9 "Guo L, Du S, Gao S et al (2022) Delta-radiomics based on dynamic contrast-enhanced MRI predicts pathologic complete response in breast cancer patients treated with neoadjuvant chemotherapy. Cancers (Basel). 
                  https://doi.org/10.3390/cancers14143515
                  
                "), [10](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR10 "Ahrari S, Zaragori T, Zinsz A et al (2024) Application of PET imaging delta radiomics for predicting progression-free survival in rare high-grade glioma. Sci Rep 14(1):3256. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-53693-x
                  
                ")]. In breast cancer patients undergoing NAC, a delta-radiomics model built on sequential MRIs (pre-treatment and after the first cycle) predicted NAC response, outperforming pre-treatment radiomics or conventional imaging alone [[9](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR9 "Guo L, Du S, Gao S et al (2022) Delta-radiomics based on dynamic contrast-enhanced MRI predicts pathologic complete response in breast cancer patients treated with neoadjuvant chemotherapy. Cancers (Basel). 
                  https://doi.org/10.3390/cancers14143515
                  
                ")]. In another study on lung cancer patients, the delta-radiomics model outperformed others in predicting treatment response, while the baseline radiomics signatures showed no significant ability to predict treatment response or patient survival [[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR3 "Cousin F, Louis T, Dheur S et al (2023) Radiomics and delta-radiomics signatures to predict response and survival in patients with non-small-cell lung cancer treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors. Cancers (Basel).

Recent systematic reviews further highlight its value for prognosis, toxicity risk, and individualized decision-making, especially in MRI-based applications [[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR1 "Nardone V, Reginelli A, Rubini D et al (2024) Delta radiomics: an updated systematic review.                   https://doi.org/10.1007/s11547-024-01853-4
                  
                "), [2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR2 "Tan D, Mohamad Salleh SA, Manan HA et al (2023) Delta-radiomics-based models for toxicity prediction in radiotherapy: a systematic review and meta-analysis. J Med Imaging Radiat Oncol 67(5):564–579. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1111/1754-9485.13546
                  
                ")].

Previous radiomics studies in MIBC patients treated with NAC have been limited to static, pre-treatment analyses [[11](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR11 "Mofid B, Abdollahi H, Khajetash B et al (2025) Machine learning-based radiomics using magnetic resonance images for prediction of clinical complete response to neoadjuvant chemotherapy in patients with muscle-invasive bladder cancer. J Clin Oncol 43(16):4581–4581. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1200/JCO.2025.43.16_suppl.4581

Front Oncol 12:878499. 
                  https://doi.org/10.3389/fonc.2022.878499
                  
                "),[14](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR14 "Choi SJ, Park KJ, Heo C et al (2021) Radiomics-based model for predicting pathological complete response to neoadjuvant chemotherapy in muscle-invasive bladder cancer. Clin Radiol 76(8):627.e613-627.e621. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1016/j.crad.2021.03.001

In this study, we assessed whether longitudinal changes from baseline in multiparametric MRI (mpMRI) radiomic features, measured at mid- and post-NAC timepoints, can reliably predict NAC response in patients with localized MIBC. This approach has the potential to refine clinical decision-making by enabling earlier, more accurate identification of responders during the treatment course.

## Materials and methods

### Patient cohort and study design

From July 2019 to July 2021, we conducted a prospective cohort study in two tertiary medical centers in Tehran, Iran, involving consecutive male and female patients with MIBC at clinical stages T2–T4a who had previously undergone transurethral resection of bladder tumor (TURBT) and were eligible for platinum-based NAC. Eligibility criteria also included adequate baseline bone marrow and hepatic function, as well as an ECOG performance status of 0–2. Patients with poor image quality that precluded reliable segmentation or feature extraction, prior pelvic radiotherapy, non-urothelial histology, and insufficient clinical or pathological data to define treatment response were excluded. The study protocol was approved by the ethics committee of Shahid Beheshti University of Medical Sciences (IR.SBMU.UNRC.REC.1399.015). All participants provided written informed consent in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki.

Figure[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#Fig1) depicts the study pipeline, which consisted of four sequential steps: first, patient image acquisition followed by tumor segmentation; second, radiomics feature extraction and calculation of delta-radiomics between predefined time points; third, feature selection using the least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO) algorithm; and fourth, model development and performance analysis with Support Vector Machine with RBF kernel (SVM-RBF), Random Forest (RF), and Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LGBM)**.**

### Treatment and imaging protocol

The details of the chemotherapy and imaging protocols have been previously published [[15](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR15 "Razzaghdoust A, Jafari A, Mahdavi A et al (2025) Diffusion-weighted MRI-derived ADC and tumor volume as predictive imaging markers for neoadjuvant chemotherapy response in muscle-invasive bladder cancer. BMC Med Imaging 25(1):3. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1186/s12880-024-01547-3
                  
                ")]. Briefly, all participants received NAC with either Gemcitabine/Cisplatin (Gem/Cis) or Gemcitabine/Carboplatin (Gem/Carbo) regimens, selected according to renal function, for up to four cycles. Multiparametric MRI (MP-MRI), including T2-weighted imaging (T2WI), diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI), and apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) mapping, was acquired at three time points (pre-, mid-, and post-NAC) following a VI-RADS–based protocol. Dynamic contrast-enhanced T1-weighted imaging (DCE-T1WI) was performed exclusively in the pre-treatment setting. All MRI examinations were performed using a 1.5 Tesla MRI machine (Magnetum Avanto, Siemens, Erlangen, Germany) with a pelvic phased array coil. Imaging parameters for different sequences are summarized in Table[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#Tab1). ### Tumor segmentation

Primary tumors were segmented at all three time points (baseline, mid-treatment, and post-treatment) by an abdominal radiologist with more than 10 years of experience, who was blinded to clinical and pathological outcomes. Regions of interest (ROIs) were delineated on T2WI and DWI/ADC maps, beginning with the initial image demonstrating tumor presence and extending to the final image, using ITK-SNAP version 4.2.0 [[16](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR16 "Yushkevich PA, Piven J, Hazlett HC et al (2006) User-guided 3D active contour segmentation of anatomical structures: significantly improved efficiency and reliability. Neuroimage 31(3):1116–1128. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuroimage.2006.01.015
                  
                ")].

### Feature extraction and delta-radiomics calculation

Radiomic features were subsequently extracted with ViSERA version 1.0.0 [[17](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR17 "Salmanpour MR, Shiri I, Hosseinzadeh M et al (2023) ViSERA: Visualized & Standardized Environment for Radiomics Analysis - A Shareable, Executable, and Reproducible Workflow Generator. In: 2023 IEEE Nuclear Science Symposium, Medical Imaging Conference and International Symposium on Room-Temperature Semiconductor Detectors (NSS MIC RTSD), 4–11 Nov. 2023. pp 1–2. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1109/NSSMICRTSD49126.2023.10338638
                  
                ")], an IBSI-compliant environment for radiomics analysis, under a standardized pipeline that included bias-field correction, resampling to isotropic voxels, intensity normalization, and fixed bin-width discretization. The resulting feature set encompassed first-order intensity/histogram descriptors (e.g., mean, median, percentiles, entropy, skewness, kurtosis), three-dimensional shape and size metrics (e.g., volume, surface area, compactness, sphericity), and texture families derived from gray-level co-occurrence, run-length, size-zone, dependence, and neighborhood difference matrices (GLCM, GLRLM, GLSZM, GLDM, NGTDM).

Delta-radiomics were computed within-patient between timepoints. The absolute and relative deltas are defined as follows:

$$\Delta {F}_{s}\left({T}_{i}\to {T}_{j}\right)={F}_{s,{T}_{j}}-{F}_{s,{T}_{i}}$$

$$\Delta {F}_{s}^{\%}\left({T}_{i}\to {T}_{j}\right)=100\cdot \frac{{F}_{s,{T}_{j}}-{F}_{s,{T}_{i}}}{{F}_{s,{T}_{i}}}$$

Here, F S,T denotes the value of radiomic feature F extracted from MRI sequence S at timepoint T; S ∈ {T2, DWI, ADC}; T ∈ {T0, T1, T2} with T0 = pre-treatment, T1 = on-treatment, and T2 = post-treatment. T i and Tj (i ≠ j) indicate two distinct timepoints defining the direction of change, Ti → T j. ΔF S (T i → Tj) is within-patient absolute change, and ΔF%S (T i → Tj) is the corresponding relative change scaled by 100 [[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR1 "Nardone V, Reginelli A, Rubini D et al (2024) Delta radiomics: an updated systematic review. Radiol Med 129(8):1197–1214. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1007/s11547-024-01853-4
                  
                ")].

### Endpoints

All patients underwent cystoscopic evaluation four weeks after completing NAC. When indicated, targeted biopsy of the previous tumor site was performed. The primary endpoint, clinical complete response (CR), was defined as the absence of detectable primary tumor (T0), confirmed by cystoscopy, negative urine cytology, and the lack of residual disease on supportive imaging [[18](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR18 "Lee HW, Kwon WA, Nguyen NT et al (2023) Approaches to clinical complete response after neoadjuvant chemotherapy in muscle-invasive bladder cancer: possibilities and limitations. Cancers (Basel). 
                  https://doi.org/10.3390/cancers15041323
                  
                "), [19](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR19 "Wu J, Xie R-Y, Cao C-Z et al (2022) Disease management of clinical complete responders to neoadjuvant chemotherapy of muscle-invasive bladder cancer: a review of literature. Front Oncol. 
                  https://doi.org/10.3389/fonc.2022.816444
                  
                ")]. The secondary endpoint was clinical overall response (OR), defined as downstaging to non-muscle-invasive disease (< cT2) on the same assessment [[20](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR20 "Hafeez S, Koh M, Jones K et al (2022) Diffusion-weighted MRI to determine response and long-term clinical outcomes in muscle-invasive bladder cancer following neoadjuvant chemotherapy. Front Oncol 12:961393. 
                  https://doi.org/10.3389/fonc.2022.961393
                  
                "), [21](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR21 "Hafeez S, Koh M, Jones K et al (2022) Assessing bladder radiotherapy response with quantitative diffusion-weighted magnetic resonance imaging analysis. Clin Oncol (R Coll Radiol) 34(10):630–641. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clon.2022.04.001
                  
                ")]. Restaging cystoscopy was performed by an independent, blinded urologist, and all patients with post-treatment cystoscopy were included in an available-case analysis.

### Feature selection

To obtain an unbiased estimate of generalization performance while performing feature selection and hyperparameter tuning, we employed nested stratified cross-validation. The outer loop consisted of 5 folds for performance estimation, and the inner loop consisted of 3 folds for model selection. For each outer split, all feature selection and hyperparameter optimization were conducted exclusively within the inner cross-validation of the outer training set; the held-out outer test fold was used only once for final prediction. Predictions from all outer test folds were aggregated to generate out-of-fold (OOF) scores for each subject, and performance metrics were computed based on these OOF scores. Nested cross-validation is recommended because using the same data for model tuning and evaluation leads to optimistically biased performance estimates, whereas nested cross-validation provides nearly unbiased error estimates [[22](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR22 "Varma S, Simon R (2006) Bias in error estimation when using cross-validation for model selection. BMC Bioinformatics 7:91. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1186/1471-2105-7-91
                  
                ")].

We applied ℓ1-penalized logistic regression (LASSO) to induce sparsity and select a compact subset of predictors. We used the lambda.1se rule as the primary penalty for parsimony; if no features were retained, we fell back to lambda.min. LASSO is a widely accepted approach for simultaneous regularization and feature selection, with efficient implementations via coordinate descent [[23](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR23 "Tibshirani R (1996) Regression shrinkage and selection via the Lasso. J Roy Stat Soc: Ser B (Methodol) 58(1):267–288. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1111/j.2517-6161.1996.tb02080.x
                  
                "), [24](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR24 "Friedman JH, Hastie T, Tibshirani R (2010) Regularization paths for generalized linear models via coordinate descent. J Stat Softw 33(1):1–22. 
                  https://doi.org/10.18637/jss.v033.i01
                  
                ")].

### Model development and validation

We retained numeric variables, imputed missing values by the median per feature, removed zero-variance predictors, and standardized features (z-scoring) using training-fold statistics only to avoid leakage. These steps were applied within each outer training split of cross-validation. After feature selection, we trained and tuned three complementary classifiers by inner cross-validation (grid search), then refit them on the full outer-training data with the best inner configuration:

*   **RF** bagged decision trees with random subspace sampling; we tuned mtry and min.node.size (1000 trees; probabilistic output [[25](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR25 "Breiman L (2001) Random forests. Mach Learn 45(1):5–32. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1023/A:1010933404324
                  
                ")].

*   **SVM-RBF** tuned penalty C and kernel width γ, with probabilistic outputs enabled [[26](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR26 "Cortes C, Vapnik V (1995) Support-vector networks. Mach Learn 20(3):273–297. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1007/BF00994018
                  
                ")].

*   **Gradient-Boosted Decision Trees (GBDT)** implemented as LGBM on our system. We tuned the learning rate, number of leaves (depth), minimum data in leaf, and feature fraction [[27](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR27 "Ke G, Meng Q, Finley T et al (2017) LightGBM: A Highly Efficient Gradient Boosting Decision Tree. In: Neural Information Processing Systems"), [28](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR28 "Chen T, Guestrin C (2016) XGBoost: A Scalable Tree Boosting System. Paper presented at the Proceedings of the 22nd ACM SIGKDD International Conference on Knowledge Discovery and Data Mining, San Francisco, California, USA,")].

### Model performance assessment

Primary discrimination was assessed with the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC) and 95% confidence intervals (95%CI) using DeLong’s method on OOF predictions [[29](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR29 "DeLong ER, DeLong DM, Clarke-Pearson DL (1988) Comparing the areas under two or more correlated receiver operating characteristic curves: a nonparametric approach. Biometrics 44(3):837–845")]. Other complementary metrics, including sensitivity, specificity, accuracy, precision, and F1 score, were also computed.

Model calibration was evaluated using the Brier score, calibration slope, and Expected Calibration Error (ECE) across probability bins. Clinical utility was assessed via Decision Curve Analysis (DCA) to compare net benefit against treat-all/treat-none strategies. Feature stability was quantified by selection frequency across cross-validation folds. All metrics used out-of-fold predictions to minimize bias.

## Results

### Patient cohort

The study cohort consisted of 52 patients with MIBC treated with NAC. Patient characteristics were reported previously by our group in a static radiomics study, in which pre-treatment MRI data were analyzed to predict clinical CR to NAC [[11](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR11 "Mofid B, Abdollahi H, Khajetash B et al (2025) Machine learning-based radiomics using magnetic resonance images for prediction of clinical complete response to neoadjuvant chemotherapy in patients with muscle-invasive bladder cancer. Egypt J Radiol Nucl Med 56(1):180. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1186/s43055-025-01603-0
                  
                ")]. In pursuit of a dynamic imaging biomarker, the present study evaluated longitudinal MRI acquired at pre-, mid-, and post-treatment time points to predict clinical CR and OR. Clinical CR occurred in 19 patients (36.5%), while 31 patients (59.6%) achieved OR. Detailed comparisons of clinical and pathological variables between the CR and non-CR cohorts were previously reported in our study on static radiomics. Briefly, most patients across both groups presented with high-grade tumors (84.2% in the CR group vs. 87.9% in the non-CR group). Patients who achieved CR demonstrated a significantly smaller mean baseline tumor volume (6.3 cm 3) compared to those without CR (22.9 cm 3). Furthermore, a higher proportion of patients in the CR group exhibited negative nodal status at baseline (63.2%) compared to the non-CR group (36.4%). However, chemotherapy regimen distribution was similar across groups.

### Discriminative performance

#### Prediction of clinical CR

Figure[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#Fig3) shows ROC curves for clinical CR prediction using mid- and post-treatment delta-radiomics derived from T2, DWI, ADC map, and their combination. Moreover, Fig.[4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#Fig4) indicates a heatmap summarizing the AUROC, specificity, sensitivity, accuracy, and F1 score for CR prediction using mid- and post-treatment delta-radiomics across RF, SVM, and LGBM models. Delta-radiomics derived from mid-treatment mpMRI (ΔRadiomics-mid) provided clinically meaningful discrimination for CR, with the best performance achieved by SVM-RBF using multi-sequence features (AUROC 0.817, sensitivity 0.850, specificity 0.719, accuracy 0.769, F1 0.739). RF and LGBM models trained on ΔRadiomics-mid showed moderate AUROC values, depending on the sequence, with a tendency toward higher sensitivity but lower specificity. It is worth noting that models based solely on T2WI demonstrated relatively lower AUROC values, ranging from 0.582 to 0.691. This indicates that mid-treatment changes are already informative for the early identification of CR, but that classifier choice and regularization strongly influence operating characteristics.

### Calibration analysis

Calibration tables for CR and OR are provided in Supplementary Files [5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#MOESM6) and [6](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#MOESM7), respectively. Overall, SVM-RBF and RF classifiers demonstrated generally acceptable agreement between predicted and observed event rates across most probability bins, whereas LGBM tended to generate more extreme probability estimates, necessitating cautious threshold selection to balance sensitivity and specificity. Complementary calibration metrics, summarized in Supplementary Files [1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#MOESM2) and [2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#MOESM3), including calibration slopes, Brier score, and ECE, indicated that DWI-based and multisequence models achieved comparatively better calibration for both CR and OR in the best-performing SVM-RBF and RF models. In contrast, LGBM models showed less favourable calibration, characterized by lower slopes and higher Brier and ECE values.

### Clinical utility

DCAs evaluating the net clinical benefit across threshold probabilities are presented in Supplementary Files [7](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#MOESM8) and [8](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#MOESM9) for CR and OR, respectively. DCA plots demonstrated superior net benefit for DWI-based models compared with treat-all and treat-none strategies. For CR prediction, ΔRadiomics-post DWI SVM-RBF yielded the highest net benefit. For OR prediction, both ΔRadiomics-post DWI LGBM and ΔRadiomics-mid multisequence SVM-RBF achieved the maximum and comparable net benefit across clinically relevant threshold probabilities.

## Discussion

This prospective study demonstrated that MRI-based delta-radiomics derived from longitudinal mpMRI can predict both clinical CR and OR to NAC in patients with MIBC with good discriminative performance and clinically meaningful calibration. By incorporating temporal changes in radiomic features acquired during and after treatment, the proposed models move beyond static baseline characterization and provide a dynamic assessment of tumor response that more closely reflects treatment-induced biological alterations.

### Possible biological rationale

A key finding of this study is that DWI-based delta-radiomics outperformed T2WI for CR prediction, consistent with the results observed in our static radiomics analysis [[11](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR11 "Mofid B, Abdollahi H, Khajetash B et al (2025) Machine learning-based radiomics using magnetic resonance images for prediction of clinical complete response to neoadjuvant chemotherapy in patients with muscle-invasive bladder cancer. Egypt J Radiol Nucl Med 56(1):180. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1186/s43055-025-01603-0
                  
                ")]. Delta features extracted from DWI achieved higher AUROC values and more stable performance across classifiers for both CR and OR. This observation is biologically plausible, as diffusion-weighted MRI is sensitive to changes in cellular density, membrane integrity, and extracellular space that occur in response to effective chemotherapy. Tumor cell death, necrosis, and stromal remodeling result in altered water diffusivity and increased intratumoral heterogeneity, which are more accurately captured by texture-based radiomic features than by simple intensity metrics [[30](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR30 "Le Bihan D, Iima M (2015) Diffusion magnetic resonance imaging: what water tells us about biological tissues.                   https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.1002203
                  
                ")]. In contrast, T2WI primarily reflects gross morphology and edema, which may lag behind true biological response or be influenced by non-specific treatment-related changes, thereby limiting its predictive specificity compared to DWI in the delta-radiomics setting [[31](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR31 "Tian X, Ma A, Jia Z et al (2025) MRI radiomics combined with delta-radiomics model for predicting pathological complete response in locally advanced rectal cancer patients after neoadjuvant chemoradiotherapy: a multi-institutional study. ### Clinical point of view

From a clinical perspective, the ability of mid-treatment delta-radiomics to predict response is particularly noteworthy [[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR1 "Nardone V, Reginelli A, Rubini D et al (2024) Delta radiomics: an updated systematic review. Radiol Med 129(8):1197–1214. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1007/s11547-024-01853-4
                  
                ")]. Accurate risk stratification during NAC offers a potential window for treatment adaptation, allowing early identification of non-responders who may benefit from alternative systemic therapy, treatment intensification, or expedited definitive local management [[6](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR6 "Regmi SK, Kalapara A, Konety BR (2021) The need for interim assessment of neoadjuvant chemotherapy response for muscle invasive bladder cancer-can we help prevent definitive treatment delay in nonresponders? J Urol 205(2):327–329. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1097/ju.0000000000001381
                  
                ")]. This is especially relevant in MIBC, where delays in radical cystectomy following ineffective NAC have been associated with adverse survival outcomes [[8](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR8 "Boeri L, Soligo M, Frank I et al (2019) Delaying radical cystectomy after neoadjuvant chemotherapy for muscle-invasive bladder cancer is associated with adverse survival outcomes. Eur Urol Oncol 2(4):390–396. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1016/j.euo.2018.09.004
                  
                ")]. In contrast, post-treatment models, while informative, are inherently less actionable, as therapeutic decisions are largely completed by that stage. Therefore, the strong performance of mid-treatment models highlights the potential role of delta-radiomics as an early decision-support tool in response-adapted treatment strategies.

### Strengths, limitations, and recommendations

An important methodological strength of this study is the comprehensive evaluation of model performance beyond discrimination alone. In addition to AUROC, we assessed calibration and clinical utility. These complementary metrics are critical for translational relevance. SVM-RBF models consistently demonstrated better calibration and lower Brier scores, indicating more reliable probability estimates. In contrast, LGBM models, despite achieving high AUROC values for OR prediction, tended to produce more extreme probabilities. This distinction is clinically meaningful, as well-calibrated models are essential for probability-based decision thresholds in individualized patient management [[37](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s43055-026-01765-5#ref-CR37 "Van Calster B, McLernon DJ, van Smeden M et al (2019) Calibration: the Achilles heel of predictive analytics. BMC Med 17(1):230. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1186/s12916-019-1466-7
                  
                ")].

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the relatively small sample size limits statistical power and generalizability, although the use of nested cross-validation and out-of-fold predictions mitigates optimistic bias in performance estimation. External validation in larger, multicenter cohorts with heterogeneous imaging protocols is necessary before clinical implementation. Second, tumor segmentation was performed manually, which may introduce interobserver variability and limit scalability. Future studies should explore automated or semi-automated segmentation approaches to improve reproducibility and workflow efficiency. Third, longitudinal DCE-T1WI was not available in our study, precluding evaluation of delta-radiomics from contrast-enhanced sequences, which demonstrated strong predictive value in prior baseline analyses. Incorporation of DCE-T1WI at multiple time points may further enhance predictive performance. Finally, while the present analysis focused on longitudinal variables, combining delta-radiomics with clinical parameters, pathological features, and molecular or genomic biomarkers in future studies could enable the development of more comprehensive and biologically informed predictive models.

## Conclusion

This study provides prospective evidence that MRI-based delta-radiomics can serve as a non-invasive, dynamic biomarker for predicting response to NAC in MIBC. Diffusion-based delta features, particularly when analyzed with appropriately calibrated machine-learning models, offer promising performance for both early and post-treatment response assessment. With further validation and integration into multimodal predictive frameworks, delta-radiomics has the potential to support personalized, response-adapted management strategies in bladder cancer.
