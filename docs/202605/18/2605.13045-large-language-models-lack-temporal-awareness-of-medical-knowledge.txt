Abstract 

The existing methods for evaluating the medical knowledge of Large Language Models (LLMs) are largely based on atemporal examination-style benchmarks, while in reality, medical knowledge is inherently dynamic and continuously evolves as new evidence emerges and treatments are approved. Consequently, evaluating medical knowledge without a temporal context may provide an incomplete as-sessment of whether LLMs can accurately reason about time-specific medical knowledge. Moreover, most medical data are historical, requiring the models not only to recall the correct knowledge, but also to know when that knowledge is cor-rect. To bridge the gap, we built TempoMed-Bench, the first-of-its-kind benchmark for evaluating the temporal awareness of the LLMs in the medical domain through evolving guideline knowledge. Based on the TempoMed-Bench, our evaluation analysis first reveals that LLMs lack temporal awareness in medical knowledge through the key findings: (1) model performance on up-to-date medical knowledge exhibits a gradual linear decline over time rather than a sharp knowledge-cutoff behavior, suggesting that parametric medical knowledge is not strictly bounded by knowledge cutoffs; (2) LLMs consistently struggle more with recalling outdated historical medical knowledge than with up-to-date recommendations: accuracy of historical knowledge is only 25.37% - 53.89% of up-to-date knowledge, indi-cating potential knowledge forgetting effects during training; and (3) LLMs often exhibit temporally inconsistent behaviors, where predictions fluctuate irregularly across neighboring years. We also show that the temporal awareness problem is a challenge that cannot be easily solved when integrated with agentic search tools (-3.15% - 14.14%). This work highlights an important yet underexplored challenge and motivates future research on developing LLMs that can better encode time-specific medical knowledge 1.

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance in various medical tasks, including clinical question answering, diagnosis, and specialty-specific knowledge asssessment [ 5,29 , 22 ]. These capabilities are often due to their strong ability to encode large amounts of medical knowledge during pre-training and post-training [ 34 ]. Because clinical applications are high-stakes, the LLMs’ internal medical knowledge must be evaluated rigorously. Most existing evaluations, however, assess medical knowledge in an atemporal way. Medical Benchmarks [ 14 , 26 , 43 ] typically test factual and reasoning ability using exam-style questions, but

To address this limitation, we study a crucial yet under-explored problem through the lens of temporal aware-ness : the ability of LLMs to correctly recall and align medical knowledge with a specified point in time. Tem-poral awareness is strongly grounded in real-world clin-ical practice: Much of medical data is inherently histor-ical and longitudinal [ 11 , 9], as they are recorded from past patient encounters and clinical trials. This requires the models not only to recall the correct knowledge, but also to know when that knowledge is correct. This need arises in multiple scenarios. First, users may ask LLMs health-related questions whose correct answers depend on recently updated evidence. Second, clinicians and researchers may need to retrospectively interpret prior studies, historical recommendations, or past treatment decisions. In both cases, the model must distinguish between current and outdated knowledge. To evaluate LLMs’ temporal awareness in the medical domain, we introduce TempoMed-Bench, a benchmark built from evolving clinical guidelines. Guideline knowledge is continuously released and updated by official organizations, and can therefore be naturally seen as snapshots of evolving medical knowledge. We collect over 20,000 guideline articles from the PubMed Central (PMC) database and organize them into 3,411 trajectories of guideline versions issued by the same organization on the same topic. For each trajectory, we extract differences between the up-to-date version and each of the outdated version, yielding 721 version-to-version difference pairs. Finally, for each pair, we construct a multiple-choice question (MCQ) to evaluate whether LLMs’ knowledge aligns more closely with the current or outdated guidelines. TempoMed-Bench aims to address four research questions. RQ1 : Have LLMs already acquired up-to-date medical knowledge? RQ2 : Can LLMs correctly recall historical (outdated) medical knowledge, and how consistent are their behaviors over time? RQ3 : Can LLMs maintain temporal consistency across different time periods? RQ4 : Can agentic RAG systems mitigate these failures? We evaluate more than 10 frontier LLMs on TempoMed-Bench. Our evaluation results first reveal that LLMs lack temporal awareness in medical knowledge, which is supported by the key findings below. In RQ1, we empirically observe that, across different guideline trajectories, strong models such as GPT-5 are not oracles in recalling all detailed up-to-date knowledge (Table 1); moreover, model performance on up-to-date medical knowledge exhibits a gradual linear decline over time (Figure 6), rather than a sharp knowledge-cutoff behavior. In RQ2, we found that, in a given guideline trajectory, LLMs consistently struggle more with recalling historical medical knowledge than with up-to-date recommendations: accuracy of historical knowledge is only 25.37% - 53.89% of up-to-date knowledge (Figure 7), indicating potential knowledge forgetting effects during training. In RQ3, we show that LLMs exhibit temporally inconsistent behaviors, where predictions fluctuate irregularly across neighboring years without coherent temporal transitions. We also show that the temporal awareness problem is a non-trivial challenge that cannot be easily solved when integrated with popular agentic search tools, such as ToolUniverse [ 10 ] and Biomni [ 13 ], which only bring limited improvements (-3.15% - 14.14%; see Table 3). To sum up, our contributions are: (1) Temporal-Aware Medical Knowledge Evaluation . We propose temporal context as a necessary dimension for evaluating medical LLMs, motivated by the inherently evolving nature of real-world medical knowledge. (2) TempoMed-Bench . We construct a large-scale collection of evolving clinical guideline trajectories and a benchmark of 721 temporally grounded medical MCQs. (3) Empirical Evidence of Limited Temporal Awareness in LLMs . We 2empirically show that current LLMs lack temporal awareness of medical knowledge and that current agentic RAG systems only partially address the problem, through four research questions. 

2 Related Works 

Medical Knowledge Evaluation. Prior work on medical knowledge evaluation has largely concen-trated on atemporal QA benchmarks [ 16 ], including MedQA [ 14 ], PubMedQA [ 15 ], and MedM-CQA [ 26 ], as well as broader benchmark collections such as MultiMedQA [ 34 ] and MedHELM [ 6]. More recent efforts have extended evaluation to explanation quality, clinical versatility, safety, and guideline adherence, as exemplified by MedExQA [ 18 ], MedS-Bench [ 38 ], and HealthBench [ 3]. However, these evaluations still mostly assume that medical knowledge is static. In contrast, medical knowledge is inherently evolving, with clinically important changes frequently formalized through updated guidelines. 

Temporal Knowledge Evaluation. Most knowledge in the real world evolves over time. Temporal awareness has recently been a popular dimension in evaluating the evolving knowledge of LLMs [ 19 ,37 , 8 , 42 , 35 , 24 , 23 ]. However, the existing works only focus on the general knowledge (e.g., “who served as the president of the United States from 2001 to 2009?”) from sources such as Wikidata [ 35 ,42 , 23 , 8] or industry documents [ 24 ]. In this paper, we particularly focus on the temporal knowledge evaluation in the medical domain. The problem remains largely unexplored, mainly due to the intrinsic challenges of collecting and curating temporally evolving medical knowledge, which is often unstructured and distributed across various clinical trial reports and guideline papers. [ 39 ] focuses on evaluating whether LLMs can endorse current and reject pseudo-outdated medical recommendations when some synthesized conflicting information is present in the context. Although we share a similar setting in evaluations with medical guidelines, the differences are also significant: (1) we focus on evaluating LLMs’ temporal awareness in the medical domains, which is fundamentally different; (2) their outdated knowledge is constructed using manually created pseudo-outdated versions, while ours focuses on the real evolution of medical guidelines. 

3 TempoMed-Bench 

In this section, we introduce TempoMed-Bench, a benchmark for evaluating how LLMs handle evolving medical knowledge. Specifically, we construct a dataset grounded in clinical guideline documents, which naturally capture snapshots of medical knowledge at different points in time and are regularly revised to reflect new evidence. Based on these guidelines, we identify differences across versions and construct multiple-choice questions (MCQs) that explicitly probe models’ preferences between up-to-date and outdated medical knowledge. In the following sections, we describe the dataset construction pipeline in detail and provide a comprehensive statistical analysis of the resulting benchmark. 

3.1 Dataset Preparation Pipeline Guideline Collection & Filtering. We collect the guideline papers from the PubMed Central (PMC) 2 database, which contains over 7.5 million life science and biomedical papers of various types, including reviews, clinical studies, guidelines, and research articles. In this step, we aim to build a rough classifier to minimize the risk of missing guideline papers. To this end, we adopt a relaxed filtering strategy that combines metadata from PubMed with naïve keyword matching. Specifically, for each article, if the term “guideline” appears in the title or in the <article-type> tag, we classify it as a guideline paper. Figure 8 visualizes the process. The articles in the PMC database are organized into three subsets: comm , noncomm , and other . Trajectory Difference Extraction. 

Given the guideline trajectories, the next step is to extract the evolving changes between the up-to-date guideline and each of its previous outdated versions. To achieve this, we prompt LLMs to identify these differences, and the extracted changes are subsequently verified by a separate, strict verifier LLM. Listing 3 and Listing 4 present the prompt to the LLM. As seen, the extracted difference pair between each up-to-date guideline and outdated guideline must represent a direct head-to-head comparison and be meaningfully different. Then, a verifier LLM scores the extracted difference pair with detailed rubrics. Note that in this step, we only consider the trajectory where all the guideline nodes are in the guideline set we prepared in {Guideline Collection & Filtering}, since otherwise we would not be guaranteed to get the full text of the paper. The process is visualized in Figure 3. We provide several examples of the extracted differences in the Appendix C.1. This process results in 721 difference pairs; each is an extracted difference between an up-to-date guideline and an outdated guideline.

4.1 RQ1: Have LLMs already acquired up-to-date medical knowledge? Experimental Setup. To investigate this problem, we evaluate multiple LLMs on each question instance in the TempoMed-Bench. Each question is designed to assess whether a model’s response aligns with the most up-to-date medical guidelines in a trajectory, as opposed to outdated guidelines or distractor options that reflect incorrect or irrelevant recommendations. To ensure a robust evaluation, for each original question, we generate two additional variants by perturbing the positional order of the answer options. This augmentation allows us to reduce the influence of positional bias [ 41 , 27 ]and obtain a more reliable estimate of model performance (see Appendix D.2 for details). 

Accuracy & Prediction Distribution. Table 1 presents the evaluation results for different models on the TempoMed-Bench, where the Accuracy column represents the percentage of correctly answering the option corresponding to the up-to-date knowledge. In general, models with larger parameter size (e.g., GPT-5 ) exhibit strong encoding of the up-to-date knowledge compared to the smaller models such as Qwen3-4B . Moreover, when zooming in on the option distributions, we found that smaller models (e.g., Qwen2.5-7B and Llama-3.1-8B ) tend to choose the distractor options more often than the larger models, partially suggesting that their parametric knowledge might be off the trajectory. 

Temporal Degradation on Up-to-date Medical Knowledge. Figure 6 and 13 present accuracy grouped by the year of the corresponding up-to-date guideline. As shown, all models tend to capture more recent knowledge less effectively , as evidenced by declining accuracy over the years. We further fit a trend line using the average accuracy values across years, and report the p-value from the Mann-Kendall test [ 21 ]. To analyze the trend, we mark each model’s knowledge cutoff date with a red dotted line. Interestingly, the performance degradation follows an approximately linear trend, rather than a sharp drop around the cutoff date. This suggests that the limitation is not solely due to the knowledge cutoff. One plausible explanation is that newer medical knowledge appears less frequently in pre-training corpora, whereas well-established guidelines from earlier years are more widely cited and thus more strongly encoded.

4.3 RQ3: Can LLMs maintain temporal consistency when recalling medical knowledge across different time periods? Temporal Consistency. We define temporal consistency as a qualitative concept that characterizes whether an LLM exhibits coherent and stable behavioral patterns when recalling medical knowledge across time. Specifically, temporally consistent behavior does not necessarily require the model to provide factually correct answers for every year; rather, it requires the model’s predictions to evolve in a logically consistent manner over time. In contrast, temporally inconsistent behavior refers to irregular or contradictory prediction patterns that fluctuate unpredictably across neighboring years without coherent temporal transitions. Such behaviors suggest unstable or weakly grounded temporal knowledge representations rather than coherent medical reasoning, thereby undermining the trustworthiness of medical LLMs. 

Experimental Setup. To evaluate temporal consistency in recalling the time-specific medical knowledge, we conduct a fine-grained analysis of LLM predictions using the time-specific question templates. Specifically, for each difference pair extracted in the TempoMed-Bench benchmark, we construct queries using the following template: 

According to the most recent guideline on or before {target year}, do you agree with the statement that {statement}? ,→

We vary the {target year} from 2000 to 2026 to simulate the temporal evolution of medical knowledge and evaluate whether models can correctly align their predictions with the appropriate guideline at each time point. We restrict our analysis to trajectories that contain exactly two guideline versions (i.e., one outdated and one updated version) to enable a controlled comparison of model behavior before and after a guideline change. The {statement} is constructed based on (1) the updated guideline and (2) the outdated guideline, allowing us to examine whether models can distinguish between up-to-date and outdated medical recommendations. Listing 6 shows an example of such questions. 

Diversified Consistency Patterns. For each question instance, this analysis probes the model’s encoded knowledge across different years, resulting in a two-row heatmap where each column represents the varying years and each row represents the up-to-date statement and the outdated statement, respectively. Ideally, the model’s responses should reflect the temporal validity of the statements. For the up-to-date statement, the model should answer “No” before the up-to-date guideline year while answering “Yes” thereafter; Conversely, for the outdated statement, the model should answer “Yes” before the update and “No” afterwards. This results in a zig-zag transition pattern centered around the guideline update year (named as Correct-Transition-Point ). Empirically, however, this ideal pattern rarely occurs. Instead, we observe several iconic temporal consistency patterns in the probing heatmaps: • All-True : the model answers “Yes” for both statements across all years. • All-False : the model answers “No” for both statements across all years. • Only-Know-Latest : the model consistently answers “Yes” for the up-to-date statement and “No” for the outdated statement, regardless of the year. • Only-Know-Prior : the model consistently answers “No” for the up-to-date statement and “Yes” for the outdated statement, regardless of the year. • Wrong-Transition-Point : the zig-zag transition pattern appears, but the transition occurs at a year that deviates significantly from the true guideline update year. All other heatmaps that do not belong to the above categories are deemed as Inconsistency . We present examples of each error pattern in Appendix E.3.1. Each error pattern reveals a distinct limitation in 8how LLMs encode and utilize temporally evolving medical knowledge: All-True indicates that the model exhibits a strong affirmative bias, accepting both outdated and up-to-date statements regardless of their temporal validity. All-False suggests that the model systematically rejects both statements, indicating a lack of confidence or failure to recognize valid medical knowledge. Only-Know-Latest 

shows that the model consistently favors the up-to-date statement across all years, ignoring temporal constraints. Only-Know-Prior indicates that the model consistently relies on outdated knowledge, failing to incorporate more recent updates. Wrong-Transition-Point reflects cases where the model captures the existence of a knowledge shift but misplaces its timing. 

Pattern Distributions. To analyze each LLM’s internal preference towards the temporal medical knowledge, we conduct a statistical analysis on the distributions of these patterns for each LLM model, with the results shown in Table 2. Out of all the error patterns, Only-Know-Latest and All-True 

are the top-2 major patterns, further indicating the conclusions in the previous sections: the LLMs might have encoded the latest knowledge but lack temporal awareness of the medical knowledge.

Analysis of Limited Improvements. Appendix E.3.2 presents a representative failure case in which the model produces an incorrect answer despite successfully retrieving and incorporating the relevant guideline into the context. We attribute this failure primarily to conflicts among multiple pieces of retrieved information. Specifically, although the oracle guideline (e.g., PMID 37129716) is correctly retrieved via the ‘PMC_search_papers’ tool, additional documents are introduced into the context through the ‘Web_Search’ tool. The presence of these potentially inconsistent sources increases the cognitive load on the model and may lead to interference between competing pieces of information, ultimately degrading prediction accuracy [2, 33]. 

4.5 Limitations and Future Work 

While our study provides a first-of-its-kind dataset for evaluating the temporal medical knowledge of LLMs, several limitations remain and could be addressed in future work: (1) We apply strict criteria when extracting guideline differences, resulting in a relatively limited dataset containing only 721 instances; (2) Although we observe limited improvements when using off-the-shelf agentic RAG tools, we have not yet explored how to further improve performance through techniques such as fine-tuning or more advanced agentic systems; (3) Due to limited full-text availability in the PubMed database (only the PMC provides full-text access), our trajectory dataset remains incomplete. 

5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduce TempoMed-Bench, the first benchmark for evaluating the temporal awareness of LLMs in the medical domain. Our empirical results demonstrate that even state-of-the-art models exhibit limited temporal awareness when handling evolving medical knowledge. Furthermore, integrating agentic RAG systems does not fully resolve this limitation. We hope this work highlights an important yet underexplored challenge and motivates future research on developing LLMs that can more effectively encode and reason over time-specific medical knowledge. 9Acknowledgments

D.2 Positional Perturbation 

We applied positional perturbations to each 5-option MCQ at the evaluation stage. Starting from the original item, we generated perturbed variants under three schemes. • Reorder-only permutes the displayed option order while preserving the original label–content bindings • Label Shuffling keeps the displayed slots fixed but permutes the option contents across positions, thereby changing the location of the correct, outdated, and distractor options. • Alternate Labels preserves the option contents but replaces the canonical option labels with an alternative label set (e.g., X, Y, Z, W).

F Broader impacts 

Our evaluation on TempoMed-Bench shows that even state-of-the-art LLMs lack temporal awareness in medical knowledge, and this limitation persists even when integrating agentic retrieval-augmented 34 generation (RAG) systems. These findings suggest that current LLMs are still far from being reliable assistants for time-specific medical decision-making. It should be emphasized that strong performance on TempoMed-Bench alone does not guarantee the safe deployment of LLMs in clinical settings. However, failure on this benchmark indicates a critical limitation in temporal medical reasoning that must be addressed before such systems can be trusted in real-world healthcare applications. In this sense, we believe that passing TempoMed-Bench should be considered a necessary—although not sufficient—condition for deploying LLMs in evolving medical environments. 35
