Abstract 

Glioma is the most common primary brain tumor, with high -grade glioma (HGG) posing significant clinical 

challenges due to its poor survival outcomes. One -year tumor recurrence indicates a poor prognosis, making 

accurate progression -risk prediction models c ritical for clinical decision -making. This study aimed to develop a 

novel combined model ( DL_com ) based on the MobileNet -based Hybrid Network (MobHy -Net), integrating clinical 

variables and deep learning features from both T2 -FLAIR and extracellular volume (ECV) images to predict one -

year progression risk. Preoperative multi -sequence MRI (T1WI, T1C, and T2 -FLAIR) from 193 HGG patients 

across two centers was analyzed. DL_com demonstrated superior predictive performance, with area under the curve 

(AUC) values of 0.954 (training), 0.911 (validation), and 0.919 (test), significantly outperforming other models 

(P < 0.05). Furthermore, decision curve analysis (DCA) confirmed its clinical utility, and Shapley Additive 

Explanations (SHAP) analysis enhanced its visua lization and interpretability. DL_com effectively predicts one -year 

progression risk in HGG, offering a valuable tool for risk stratification and clinical decision support. 

Keywords: High -grade glioma; Deep learning; Extracellular volume ; SHAP visualization analysis ; Progression 

prediction .

Introduction 

Glioma, a highly prevalent brain tumor, stands as the third leading cause of cancer -related mortality globally, with a 

mortality rate of 4.4 per 10,000 people 1. High -grade glioma (HGG ) include s World Health Organization ( WHO )

grades 3 and 4 tumors according to the 2016 WHO Classification of Tumors of the Central Nervous System .ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS Furthermore, based on the updated 2021 WHO classification criteria, certain WHO grade 2 gliomas are now 

reclassified as grade 3 or 4 after integrating molecular profiling into the diagnosis 2,3 . Represent ing 75% of primary 

brain tumours in adults 4, HGG s are known for their aggressiveness. Despite multimodal treatment involving maximal 

safe resection, radiotherapy, and chemot herapy —supplemented in recent years by tumor -treating fields 5— the 

prognosis remains dismal. The overall prognosis (OS) remains poor, with a 5 -year survival rate of 6.6% to 30.9% .

Critically, a substantial proportion of patients experience disease progress ion within the first year after initial treatment, 

which is strongly and independently associated with exceptionally poor survival outcomes. A previous study 6 indicates 

a marked disparity in median overall survival (mOS) between patients with and without progression within one year, 

at 15 months and 56 months, respectively. Early identification of this high -risk subgroup is therefore pivotal for 

optimizing clin ical management. However, reliable prediction of progression within one year remains a significant 

challenge. Conventional predictors based on clinical parameters and standard imaging findings have shown limited 

accuracy and robustness 7. Given the clinical imperative for improved prognostication, there is a pressing need to 

establish a more precise and reliable predictive model, especially concerning progression within one year. 

Radiomics emerges as a promising approach by computationally extracting high -di mensional quantitative features 

from medical images to characterize tumor heterogeneity, with emerging studies demonstrating superior performance 

to conventional imaging metrics 8,9 . An increasing number of studies in recent years have employed radiomics fo r

tumor diagnosis, recurrence monitoring, prognostic prediction, and therapeutic evaluation. For instance, Li et al. 10 has 

demonstrated that radiomics can effectively predict patient survival prognosis, but its accuracy still needs 

improvem ent. Current rad iomics research has mainly focused on demonstrating the feasibility of extracting 

quantitative features from computed tomography 11 (CT ), magnetic resonance imaging 12,13 (MRI ), and ultrasound 

images 14 . However, these features are largely limited to the morphological and textural information of conventional 

imaging modalities and do not fully capture the tumor microenvironment or spatial heterogeneity. In recent years, 

functional imaging based on multip arametric MRI, such as extracellular volume ( ECV ), and morphological feature 

extraction methods based on topology have gradually gained attention. These approaches can provide complementary 

information from dimensions such as microvascular density, stromal fibrosis, and tumor spatial configuration 15,16 .

Tang et al. 17 confirmed that combining ECV images can enhance the model ’s predictive capability for tumor budding 

grading in patients with rectal cancer, suggesting its potential to improve model prediction performance. Similarly, 

Yang et al. 18 developed a combined logistic model for brainstem gliomas to predict H3K27M mutation by extracting 

radiomic s and topological features. Therefore, extracting and integrating higher -dimensional, multi -source imaging 

features may further enhance the predictive capability of radiomics models. 

Recently, deep learning (DL) has been widely applied in glioma research du e to its powerful ability to automatically 

extract high -level features and identify complex patterns from medical imaging data. The Vision Transformer (ViT) 

represents a groundbreaking and milestone architecture 19 , which has also shown great potential in g lioma image 

analysis, such as biomarker analysis 20 , molecular marker expression prediction 21 , and subtype classification 22 .ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS However, the standard ViT framework primarily processes 2D image slices, whereas our study focuses on 3D brain 

tumor MRI volumes, wh ich require effective modeling of cross -sectional spatial context to capture tumor 

heterogeneity comprehensively. Therefore, in this study, we adopted the direct 3D extension of the ViT architecture, 

namely the 3D Vision Transformer (3D ViT) 23 , to construc t the deep learning models based on ECV images and multi -

sequence MRI aiming to enhance predictive performance by leveraging 3D global contextual features. 

This study constructed combined models by integrating pre -treatment multi -sequence MRI, ECV image s, and clinical 

features based on MobHy -Net, aiming to identify the optimal method for predicting one -year disease progression risk. 

This study aims to optimize clinical decision -making capabilities and facilitate dynamic treatment monitoring. These 

capabili ties collectively advance precision medicine in neuro -oncology.

To enhance the interpretability of the model in clinica l applications, the study also used the Shapley Additive 

exPlanations (SHAP) algorithm 30,31 to generate heatmaps (Fig. 6), visually showing the deep learning models focusing 

on tumor lesion areas. In the heatmap, red regions indicate areas with a significa nt positive contribution to the deep 

learning model ’s prediction, and blue regions indicate a negative contribution. In other words, the heatmap does not 

just point out "where the tumor is," but rather "which local structures/locations within the tumor are most important 

for prediction." A consistent pattern we observed is that the SHAP heatmap shows redder colors (higher contribution) 

closer to the center of the solid component of the lesion, and lower contribution or neutral/negative influence in 

necrotic areas, cystic changes, or surrounding edema. Notably, the red high -contribution region largely overlaps with 

the actual tumor location, suggesting that the deep learning model captures and utilizes tumor -related features for its 

decision -making. These vis ualization features help to clarify the key lesions in radiotherapy effect evaluation, 

effectively supp orting treatment decision -makers in the planning of patient stratification treatment in clinical work.

Individualized prognostic evaluation 

Based on the aforementioned findings, we determined t he optimal cutoff value of 0.48 using X‑tile software and 

utilized the optimal model —DL_com —to stratify all HGG patients into high‑risk and low‑risk groups for subsequent 

survival curve analysis. In the training cohort, the log‑rank test yielded statistica lly significant differences between 

the two groups (p < 0.01), demonstrating clear discrimination of the survival curves for both OS and PFS and 

indicating promising predictive performance of the combined model in this cohort. The high‑risk group demonstra ted 

a mOS of 26 months, whereas the low‑risk group showed prolonged survival with a mOS of 77 months. Consistently, 

patients classified as high risk also exhibited markedly shorter PFS, with almost all progression events occurring in 

this group (C‑index fo r PFS = 0.846, log‑rank p < 0.001). 

In the validation cohort, the separation trend between high‑ and low‑risk groups for PFS was preserved (C‑index = 

0.792), although the log‑rank p value did not reach statistical significance (p = 0.283), likely due to the smaller sample 

size and fewer events. In the independent test cohort, the combined model remained predictive of PFS (C‑index = 

0.793, log‑rank p = 0.003), with a similar pattern of shorter PFS in the high‑risk group. However, the log‑rank test ARTICLE IN PRESS

Discussion 

In this study, the predictive value of multi -sequence MRI combined wit h ECV for disease progression in patients with 

HGG was investigated using radiomics and deep learning. The research results confirm that the combined model, 

which integrates clinical variables with deep learning –derived imaging features from both T2 -FLAIR and ECV images ,

performs the best and can assist clinical decision -making. 

In fact, numerous studies have suggested that MRI information has significant predictive value in identifying high -

risk populations for short -term progression with HGG. For instance, Li et al. 32 developed machine learning models 

predicting HGG progression and recurrence by extracting radiomic s features from pre -radio therapy MRI sequences. 

However, it is crucial to note that traditional radiomics models typically rely on a single or a limited number of 

sequences for feature extraction and analysis, leading to incomplete tumor information response and inevitable 

informa tion omission. Therefore, recent research initiatives have started to evaluate the comparative effectiveness of 

single -sequence and multi -sequence models. Wang et al. 33 found that the radiomics model based on multiple sequences 

had better predictive perfor mance than that based on a single sequence ( p < 0.05). Wu et al. 34 developed a clinical -

radiomics nomogram based on multi -sequences and demonstrated optimal predictive performance (AUC=0.968) for 

the initial response to concurrent chemoradiotherapy in patients diagnosed with locally advanced nasopharyngeal 

carcinoma. Research on multi -sequence models has gradually emerged in glioma studies. Heo et al. 35 developed a

deep learning model based on multiparametric MRI to predict early local progression in ad ult -type diffuse glioma. 

Consistent with prior work 33 -36 , our study also compares and demonstrates that the combined model integrating deep 

learning features based on multiple sequences and clinical features may exhibit superior performance in identifying 

high -risk populations compared to models relying on any single m odality. Notably, DLFM underperformed compared 

to DL_com. Several factors may explain this discrepancy. First, T2 -FLAIR demonstrates high sensitivity and contrast 

for tumor extent, infiltrativ e edema, and lesion heterogeneity in high -grade glioma 37 -39 . It is the most stable and widely 

used sequence in clinical practice, serving as a reliable basis for reflecting tumor burden and infiltration, consistent 

with Kong et al. 40 , suggesting its potential to complement or replace conventional sequences in deep learning models. 

Second, because ECV is derived from T1WI and T1C through computational processing, potential feature overlap 

may exist between ECV images and T1WI/T1C image s. Therefore, careful selection and integration of clinical features 

are essential to avoid redundancy. Additionally, the lack of interpretation of deep learning features is a major obstacle 

to the practical application of deep learning models in clinical practice. To improve the interpretation of deep learning 

features, we adopted Shapley technology to generate heatmaps, visually showing the model's focus on tumor lesion ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS areas 41,42 . In summary, this visualization technique elucidates the model's decision -making mechanism for clinicians, 

aligning with international standards for explainable artificial intelligence ( AI ) in healthcare 43 .

Recently, using topology to extract morphological features from tumor imaging has been gradually adopted in the 

diagnosis, t reatment guidance, and prognostic analysis of tumors. Kodama et al. 44 extracted topological features from 

CT images to predict the recurrence rate in stage I non -small cell lung cancer patients. In the study, we extracted 

topological features and radiomic s features from multi -sequence MR I of HGG patients, and performed feature 

selection using LASSO. Unfortunately, the final results indicated that the contribution of topological features to the 

progression model for HGG was limited. This may be related to th e high intratumoral and intertumoral heterogeneity 

of HGG, making a global geometric characterization of tumor vasculature difficult 45 . A general issue with topological 

studies is the skeletonization process. Irregular surfaces and boundary perturbations c ause single voxel stubs in the 

skeleton, leading to a breakdown of topological clusters 46 .

In addition to standard structural MRI sequences, including T1WI, T1C, and T2 -FLAIR, we generated tumour ECV 

images using contrast -enhanced MR examination before the rapy in patients with HGG. The ECV includes the 

intravascular space and extracellular volume and thus can reflect the degree of microvessel density and stromal 

fibrosis 47 . Quantitative analysis of ECV images and ECV fraction was reported to be a promising quantitative metric 

in evaluating cardiac and hepatic fibrosis, and the prognosis of some tumors 48 -50 . Fukukura et al. ’s50 study has 

demonstrated that ECV fraction determined by equilibrium contrast -enhanced multidetector computed tomography is 

a prognostic factor in unresectable pancreatic adenocarcinoma treated with chemotherapy. Tang et al. 17 developed a 

deep learning -radi omic s model based on ECV images for predicting the preoperative tumor budding grade of rectal 

cancer patients . Our research demonstrates that the deep learning model established by extracting features from ECV 

images (AUC=0 .825 ) outperforms T2 -FLAIR (AUC=0 .759 ), T1WI (AUC= 0.785 ), and TIC images (AUC= 0.789 ) in 

progression analysis for HGG. This finding aligns with Tang et al. 17 observations, demonstrating that the ECV -based 

deep learning model (AUC=0.867) outperformed the model using venous -phase CT images (AUC=0.770) and Shi et 

al.'s 51 observations, demonstrating that the ECV -based deep learning model (AUC=0.894) outperformed the model 

using T1C (AUC=0.793). This may be attributed to the influence of the ECV in malignant tissue, where tumors with 

higher ECV fractions demonstrate greater anticancer drug uptake 52,53 and enhanced radiotherapy sensitivity. This 

finding indicates that, compared with models that rely on structural MRI sequence s, models based on ECV images 

have stronger diagnostic ability and can m ore comprehensively reflect the heterogeneity of HGG lesions. However, 

during the collection of baseline clinical data from the patients, hematological data such as hematocrit (Hct) were not 

collected. As a result, we used the average Hct value of 45% for normal males and females to generate ECV images, 

which may have introduced potential inaccuracies .

Our predictive model demonstrates encouraging accuracy (AUC=0.954), suggesting the ability to effectively identify 

HGG at high risk of progression. First, clinicians can utilize the model's risk stratification, combined with individual 

clinical characteristics and medical imaging findings, to develop personalized treatment plans for patients. Second, ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS the model's visualized outputs facilitate effective physician -patient communication regarding therapeutic options. 

Finally, the model enables regular monitoring of patient survival status and treatment efficacy, while optimizing 

follow -up procedures and enhancing management protocols. 

This study has some limitations that merit consideration. First, the sample size was relatively small and the study was 

retrospective. The limited sample size may be insufficient for adequately training the 3D ViT model, necessitating the 

inclusion of larger -scale datasets to enhance model robustness and generalizability. This limitation primarily arose 

from two factors: (1) challenges inherent to cross -center collaboration and workflow management in imaging, which 

prevented the acquisition of clinical data for some patients; and (2) the fact that initial MRI scans for some patients 

were performed at external institutions, where protocol heterogeneity precluded standardization and integration of the 

corresponding data. Prospective validation is therefore necessary to confirm it s generalizability and clinical utility. 

Secondly, the imaging protocol included a conventional cohort of structural sequences (T1 WI , T1C, T2 -FLAIR), 

lacking functional imaging sequences and other relevant imaging examinations such as PET -CT/DWI. Finally, the 

biological significance of the selected radiomics features remains to be elucidated, and future research integrating 

imaging modalities with molecular, epigenetic, or transcriptional data may provide greater insight into micro -

information and the relat ionships with other variables. Therefore, further research is needed to address the above 

concerns. 

In conclusion, the combined model integrating clinical and deep learning features from ECV and T2 -FLAIR 

demonstrated promising predictive performance within our study cohorts and appeared to generalize well to the 

independent test cohort. This model has th e potential to outperform the clinical model, radiomics model, and deep 

learning model based purely on ECV or single -sequence MRI. This study might offer new perspectives and references 

for identifying high -risk populations for early progression before tre atment in HGG patients. 

Methods 

Patient population 

A total of 239 patients with high -grade glioma were recruited from our center (n=133) and Sun Yat -sen Memorial 

Hospital, Sun Yat -sen University (n=60), of which 193 patients were included. The study recrui ted patients 

diagnosed with high -grade glioma confirmed by integrating the diagnosis from June 2016 to June 2023 at our center 

and an external center. We conducted a retrospective analysis and included patients who met the following criteria: 

1) patients w ith integrated diagnosis of high -grade glioma, 2) no treatment given prior to the confirmed diagnosis of 

glioma, 3) availability of complete pre -treatment MRI images of the HGG patients, including T1 -weighted images 

(T1WI), contrast -enhanced T1 -weighted im ages (T1C) and T2 -FLAIR, 4) availability of clinical information, and 5) 

availability of comprehensive molecular profiling da ta. The diagnosis of high -grade gliomas refers to the 2021 

WHO Classification of Tumours of the Central Nervous System (CNS) and in tegrates both histopathological and 

molecular profiling. This primarily includes the following four entities: 1) Glioblastoma, isocitrate dehydrogenase ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS (IDH) -wildtype (CNS WHO Grade 4); 2) Astrocytoma, IDH -mutant (CNS WHO Grade 4); 3) Astrocytoma, IDH -

mut ant (CNS WHO Grade 3); and 4) Oligodendroglioma, IDH -mutant and 1p/19q -codeleted (CNS WHO Grade 

3) 3,24 . Patien ts were excluded if they met any of the following conditions: 1) presence of blurred, artifact, or 

discontinuous MRI images, 2) history of previous malignancy or prior treatment for HGG, 3) lack of pre - and post -

treatment follow -up clinical data, and 4) la ck of complete imaging sequences, including T1WI, T1C, and T2 -FLAIR.

Research workflow 

1) The study incorporated data from two distinct medical centers, encompassing comprehensive processing steps 

including: medical image preprocessing, ECV images generation, feature extraction, feature selection, and model 

construction. Model performance was subsequently validated using an indepe ndent validation cohort. 2) We developed 

a clinical model by extracting clinical patient profiles and developing a radiomics model by extracting radiomics and 

topological features from preoperative MRI scans. 3) Four deep learning models were constructed u sing a 3D ViT 

architecture, leveraging both computationally generated ECV images and multi -sequence MR I. 4) Two combined

through the MobileNet -based Hybrid Network (MobHy -Net). 5) The final evaluation comprised Receiver Operating

performance quantification, supplemented by survival analysis of the combined model's prognostic capabilities. The

Data preprocessing 

Due to potential discrepancies in pixel spacing or resolution caused by varying scanning conditions or equipment, we 

performed spatial registration and resampling on all imaging data during the preprocessing stage. For each sequence, 

images are resampled to a uniform voxel size (3mm³) to ensure consistent volumetric resolution across the input 

multimodal sequence. Furthermore, uniform spatial resolution effectively reduces the cha llenges faced by the model 

in generalizing across different devices or centers. To eliminate intensity distribution biases between images from 

different patients caused by variations in equipment or scanning intensity ranges, the segmented images are 

stand ardized. Specifically, Z -Score 28 normalization is applied, where each image (on a single -modality basis) is 

subtracted by its mean value across the entire image. To enhance the model ’s robustness during training and mitigate 

the risk of overfitting due to insufficient data scale, we applied a series of data augmentation techniques to the 

preprocessed image data, including random cropping, image rotation, and flipping (within ±10°).

Radiomics and topological feature extraction 

On the Python 3.8 platform, we employed the open -source software package PyRadiomics 

(http://pypi.org/project/pyradiomics/ ) to extract radiomics features from the ROI delineated on T1WI, T1C, and T2 -

FLAIR sequences. In total, 3,045 radiomics features were extracted from the combined three sequences, comprising 

594 f irst -order statistical features, 726 Gray -Level Co -occurrence Matrix (GLCM), 528 Gray -Level Size Zone Matrix 

(GLSZM), 528 Gray -Level Run Length Matrix (GLRLM), 165 Neighbouring Gray Tone Difference Matrix 

(NGTDM), 462 Gray -Level Dependence Matrix (GLDM), a nd 42 shape features. In addition to the conventional 

radiomics features described above, we also derived a set of topological features based on surface curvature properties 

from the same three image sequences. These topological features totaled 32 and inc luded 8 Gaussian curvature features, 

4 min curvature features, 4 max curvature features, 4 principal curvature features, 4 curvature intensity features, 4 

sharpness features, and 4 shape index features. 

Feature selection 

To identify and eliminate redundant features, we employed a T -test, and features with p > 0.05 were eliminated. For 

highly correlated features, the Pearson Correlation Coefficient was calculated. If the correlation coefficient was greater 

than 0.9, one of each pair of features was retained. The Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (LASSO) 

algorithm was used to further screen; the optimal λ value was determined through tenfold cross -validation, and  ＞

0.0001 coefficient features were selected. Moreover, for the selected independent a nd stable radiomics features, a 

robust normalization method was used for standardization: each column of features was subtracted by its median and 

then divided by the interquartile range. 

Model building 

14 machine learning classification -clinical models are built in batches using the PixelMedAI platform based on Python 

3.8. These models include Logistic Regression (LR), Naive Bayes (NB), linear Support Vector Machine (linear_SVM), 

polynomial Support Vector Machine (poly_SVM), sigmoid Support Vector Machin e (sigmoid_SVM), radial basis 

function Support Vector Machine (rbf_SVM), Decision Tree(DT), Random Forest (RF), Extremely Randomized Trees 

(ExtraTree), eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost), Adaptive Boosting (AdaBoost), Multi -Layer Perceptron (MLP), 

Gradien t Boosting Machine (GBM), and Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM). The radiomics models were 

constructed in the same way. To reduce the risk of overfitting, we perform 5 -fold cross -validation on the classification 

models to select the optimal parame ters. ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS In total, we designed six deep learning models: 1) Four 3D ViT models based on T1WI (DL_t1), T1C (DL_t1c), T2 -

FLAIR images (DL_t2), and ECV images (DL_ecv) separately , 2) A combined deep learning model (DL_com) that 

further integrates T2 -FLAIR, ECV, and clinical features through MobHy -Net , and 3) A combined deep learning model 

(DL FM ) that further integrates all MR images , ECV, and clinical features also through MobHy -Net . We pr opose an 

adapted 3D ViT architec ture that extends the traditional 2D ViT framework to effectively process volumetric medical 

images. For the 3D ViT model, we used a patch size of 16×16×16, an embedding dimension of 512, 12 Transformer 

layers, 8 multi -hea d self -attention heads, an FFN hidden dimension of 4 times the embedding dimension, and 

LayerNorm with dropout (p = 0.1). The model processes input volumes by dividing them into overlapping 3D patches 

that are flattened and linearly projected into high -dim ensional embeddings while preserving spatial relationships 

through positional encoding. A learnable class token (cls token) is prepended to capture global features, with the 

complete sequence enhanced by 3D positional encoding and Dropout regularization. T he architecture employs stacked 

Transformer encoders, each containing multi -head self -attention (MSA) with LayerNorm and residual connections, 

followed by feedforward networks (FFN) with GELU activation. The MSA mechanism globally correlates patch 

embeddin gs through attention weights, while the FFN blocks perform nonlinear feature transformation. The complete 

framework maintains training stability through layer -wise residual connections and effectively models 3D spatial 

relationships in medical imaging data .

For the deep learning network training, the PyTorch framework (version 1.8) was used and run on an NVIDIA RTX 

4060 Ti GPU. The optimizer was AdamW, with an initial learning rate of 1e -4 and a weight decay of 1e -5. Cosine 

annealing learning rate schedulin g was employed, and ReduceLROnPlateau was used for further adjustment when the 

validation AUC stopped improving. The batch size was 16, the maximum number of training epochs was 200, and an 

early stopping strategy (patience = 30) was implemented. After eac h epoch, the AUC was evaluated on the validation 

set, and the model weights with the best validation AUC were saved. To alleviate class imbalance, we used a weighted 

binary cross -entropy loss (weighted inversely to the class frequency in the training set) in combination with a random 

oversampling strategy. Data augmentation was applied only during training and included random left/right and 

front/back flips, random rotations (±10°), random scaling (±10%), and intensity perturbations (Gaussian noise, 

standar d deviation, σ = 0.01). The random seed was fixed at 42 for data partitioning, data augmentation, and parameter 

initialization to improve reproducibility. 

To better integrate clinical features with imaging features, we constructed two combined model s (DL_c om and DLFM )

based on MobHy -Net. This hybrid architecture synergistically integrates Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and 

Transformer mechanisms for cross -modal semantic feature fusion. ECV images and multi -sequence MRI were 

independently processed using MobileNetV2 encoders. These encoders extract modality -specific spatial features from 

volumetric data while maintaining computational efficiency via depthwise separable convolutions and inverted 

residual blocks. The Mobil eNetV2 branch maintained its standard architecture, and a global average pooling layer 

followed by a 512 -dimensional fully connected layer was added after the backbone output to serve as the image feature ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS vector. The obtained different features are concate nated along the channel dimension. Clinical features were mapped 

to 100 dimensions through two fully connected layers, concatenated with the image features, and then input into two 

layers of multi -head self -attention (head = 8) and a two -layer MLP classifi cation head (hidden dimension 256, 

activation ReLU), with the final output being a sigmoid probability. All activation functions used ReLU, batch 

normalization (BatchNorm) was used in appropriate places during training, and L2 regularization was applied to the 

fully connected layers. The clinical features are transformed into a 100 -dimensional vector through a fully connected 

layer, and then concatenated with the extracted 512 -dimensional image features. The concatenated features are then 

input into a multi -head self -attention module. Specifically, we di vide the features into fixed -size blocks and perform 

multi -head self -attention calculation on these different blocks to obtain blocks containing location information. The 

processed blocks are classified by a classifier, that is, the fused information is decoded by a multilayer perceptron, and 

the prognosis is predicted. To avoid overfitting caused by data imbalance, a weighted oversampling method and binary 

cross -entropy loss function are used. 

Statistical an alysis 

All statistical analyses were performed using R (version 4.0.2; https://www.r -project.org) and Python (version 3.8; 

https://www.python.org). Continuous variables are expressed as mean ± standard deviation, and categorical variables 

are presented as counts (n) and percentages (%). Student ’s t -test was used to analyze normally distributed quantitative 

data, with Levene ’s test used to assess the homogeneity of variance. The Mann -Whitney U test was used to analyze 

non -normally distributed data. The chi -square test was used to compare categorical data. Statistical significance was 

set at p < 0.05. ROC curves and area under the curve (AUC) were used to evaluate classification model performance. 

DCA was performed to assess the clinical utility of the models by quantifying the net benefit at different threshold 

probabilities. 

Survival analysis 

We conducted survival analyses utilizing a Cox proportional hazards regression model, and optimal cut -off thresholds 

were determined using X -tile software. This quantita tive stratification yielded discrete high -risk and low -risk cohorts 

that were subsequently subjected to Kaplan -Meier survival estimation. And the significance of group separation was 

evaluated using a log -rank test. This comprehensive approach ensures a th orough evaluation of the predictive model ’s

effectiveness in the clinical cohort.
