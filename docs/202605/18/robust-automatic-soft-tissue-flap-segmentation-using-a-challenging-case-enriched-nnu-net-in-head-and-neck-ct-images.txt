Introduction 

Reconstructive surgery with a flap makes the definition of postoperative radiotherapy volumes challenging. It may also result in errors in automatic segmentation atlases of organ-at-risk and nodal volumes. Automating flap segmentation process could assist clinicians in planning radiotherapy and enable characterization of flap evolution over time and after radiotherapy. Flaps vary significantly in shape, volumes and associated artefacts. We therefore enriched a previously built atlas with challenging cases to obtain a more robust real-world flap segmentation. 

Methods 

Within the framework of the state-of-the-art nnU-Net deep learning architecture, we investigated whether constructing a training dataset with enhanced representation of challenging cases, often associated with poor segmentation performance or outright failures, could improve the overall accuracy and robustness of automated flap segmentation, based on Dice scores compared through paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests. Clinical trial and real-world data were selected to increase the heterogeneity and enrich the training set with rare challenging cases (such as pedicled flaps, small flaps, unusual location including maxillary flaps, bone resection, presence of dental artefacts or bite block). 

Results 

This enriched training dataset led to improved performance of the nnU-Net model, increasing the mean Dice scores from 0.66 ± 0.29 to 0.74 ± 0.20 (p<0.001), with median Dice scores rising from 0.76 to 0.80. 

Conclusion 

Robust flap segmentation was achieved without modifying the neural network architecture, loss function, or algorithmic structure, through enrichment of the training set with anatomically and visually challenging cases. This model can be used for detailed analysis of geometrical and textural flap changes over time. 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS Introduction 

The management of head and neck cancer (HNC) presents significant challenges in radiotherapy (RT) because of the proximity to anatomical structures that have vital functions (1). The efficacy and safety of RT are dependent on the accurate delineation of tumors and anatomical structures to achieve therapeutic effectiveness and preservation of healthy tissues. In the context of reconstructive surgery, the anatomy is highly modified by postoperative inflammation, edema, fibrosis, and tissues missing after tumor resection and replaced by ectopic autologous tissues, i.e., flaps. Flaps add a distinct layer of complexity to postoperative HN RT because they introduce ectopic tissue (fat, skin, fascia, muscle ± bone not originating from this part of the patient body) into a bed that is itself undergoing dynamic healing (edema, inflammation and fibrosis) and often contains non-biological material (metallic surgical staples ± fixation plates and screws) (2). In Computed Tomography (CT), this produces highly variable intensity patterns and boundary ambiguity: flap interfaces may be partially confluent with adjacent soft tissues, and the flap’s internal heterogeneity can mimic native structures or pathological tissue. From an algorithmic standpoint, these cases violate two assumptions that many commercial atlas- and deep learning (DL)-based systems exploit (3). In particular, DL-based segmentation relies on stable spatial priors (expected organ position/shape relative to landmarks) and consistent appearance priors (predictable Hounsfield Unit texture for a given structure). As a result, the presence of a flap can propagate errors beyond the flap itself, including mis-segmentation of nodal levels (as encountered in some commercial systems, data not shown for confidentiality issues), because postoperative topology differs substantially from “standard anatomy”. In practice, this can break generalization of commercial auto-contouring, including for nodal regions, underscoring that performance validated on “typical” planning CTs may not transfer to the heterogeneous postoperative reality without targeted data coverage. Accurate flap segmentation is therefore clinically and scientifically consequential. It is also critical to enable longitudinal quantification of flap volume change (spontaneous remodeling and radiation-associated shrinkage), which is increasingly relevant for functional outcomes and reconstructive endpoints. In the dedicated postoperative flap auto-segmentation tasks, where artefacts and rare/anatomically complex situations are inevitable (e.g., pedicled flaps, certain primary sites, complex reconstruction scenarii) (4), training sets must deliberately capture the “hard tail” of postoperative heterogeneity rather than only the common configurations of “clean” cases which introduce systematic biases and limit clinical applicability. 

Traditional segmentation methodologies have demonstrated limitations in addressing the complex morphological variations inherent to flaps (5,6), necessitating the exploration of advanced computational approaches, particularly those leveraging DL frameworks. DL techniques have gained significant traction in medical imaging applications, especially in RT planning (7–10). Flap delineation in three-dimensional (3D) Computed Tomography (CT) images indeed remains challenging and very time-consuming for radiation 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS oncologists, even with a high expertise (4). While segmentation models have been widely applied to medical imaging including CT (11), their primary focus has been on lesions detection and delineation or multi-organs segmentation (12) rather than the specific challenges posed by flaps. Despite numerous attempts to propose new network architectures like transformers that come at the cost of increased computation time and potentially larger model size, hence requiring larger datasets for training (13), the nnU-Net (14) remains a very reliable approach that can achieve state-of-the-art performance especially in the context of limited datasets size (15,16). We therefore chose not to try developing new or alternative architectures but, within the context of the nnU-Net framework, to rather focus on improving the training data by including more diverse and challenging cases, to obtain more robust and accurate segmentation, which could in turn translate into more generalizable results. Our aim was to enhance nnU-Net automated segmentation for flap analysis in the context of RT by optimizing the choice of the training samples with rare challenging cases to represent real-world heterogeneity. 

Materials and Methods 

Datasets 

Four distinct datasets containing CT scans and RT structure sets, including flaps volumes and OAR information, served both for model training and testing of the nnU-Net model. The first, sourced from a prospective multicentric trial by GORTEC (Groupe d’oncologie radiothérapie tête et cou), comprised DICOM-RT data from 145 patients recruited across 33 centers between 2019 and 2021. This dataset (denoted “Multi” from here onwards) captured a wide range of flaps in the inherently controlled patient selection context of a clinical trial, where flap delineation was not an aim and reconstructive surgery modalities were left to surgeons’ appreciation based on tumor and patient’s morbidity. Planning CTs were performed according to standard practice with 1-3 mm thick slices, contrast enhancement and use of artefact minimization algorithms encouraged. To improve model robustness to challenging and rare cases encountered in routine, three real-world datasets of University Hospitals and Cancer centers denoted A (70 patients), C (46 patients), and R (20 patients) were added to the Multi dataset. They provided a valuable contrast to the more controlled data from the trial and aimed to enhance the clinical heterogeneity and flap segmentation robustness and accuracy (Figure 1). Ground truth flap contours were delineated across all datasets according to established guidelines and interobserver training for calibration (17). The contouring process was conducted independently by two expert radiation oncologists who were blinded to patient characteristics and outcomes, ensuring unbiased annotations. Each expert reviewed the other’s delineation and corrections were made if deemed necessary. Collegial discussions with head and neck surgeons and a radiologist were also undertaken in difficult cases. This rigorous methodology minimized inter-observer variability and ensured consistency. Inclusion of trial and routine data aimed to enhance generalization capability of the segmentation model.

nnU-Net segmentation model and evaluation framework 

The nnU-Net framework was first employed to train a pre-configured generic U-Net on the multi dataset (18), adopting random partitioning into training (80%, 117 patients) and testing (20%, 28 patients) subsets. Images were uniformly interpolated to voxel size of 1.17×1.17×2 mm 3 (i.e. median voxel dimensions of the dataset) using third-order spline interpolation for image data and nearest-neighbor interpolation for the ground truth segmentation masks. To improve model generalization and mitigate overfitting, data augmentation techniques 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS from the nnU-Net framework were applied. These included random flips, affine transformations (rotation, translation, and scaling), elastic deformations (grid-based warping) applied to both CT scans and segmentation maps, brightness and contrast adjustments, and controlled image quality degradation to simulate real-world variability. All experiments adhered to the standard parameter settings of the nnU-Net framework (supplementary table 1). For evaluation of segmentation performance, Dice score (Dsc) and Hausdorff distance (HD) (15,19), mean, standard deviation and median values were used. Note that for HD, cases with complete failures where no contour was produced (hence no measurement can be made) were assigned the largest value observed in the dataset, as a value of 0 would be considered a perfect segmentation. Statistical comparison of Dsc between models was carried out using paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests, as distributions were not normally distributed. No adjustment for multiple comparisons was considered. Since HD showed similar trends as Dsc, we mostly focused on Dsc (HD are provided in supplemental materials but are still described and discussed in the paper). Inter-observer variability being in the 0.70 Dsc range (17,20), model performance with Dsc≥0.70 can be considered an accepted target value and clinically satisfactory, especially when it comes to the postoperative setting, and a substantial improvement when it comes to exotic structures that are becoming standard practice. The evaluation of the nnU-Net model trained on the trial dataset, and three real-life datasets testing subsets (half of the patients) highlighted that it performed with lower accuracy or even led to complete failures (Dsc of 0) in some cases (see results). These included situations considered as anatomy-changing outliers, such as pedicled flaps, small flaps, unusual location including maxillary flaps, bone resection left unreconstructed, presence of dental artefacts that can be present due to metallic dental materials or plaques (they represent up to 75% of patients in daily care, with use of artefact minimization algorithms left to physician’s appreciation) or tongue displacement devices (bite-blocks). We therefore implemented an alternative strategy to build a training dataset to enhance the final model performance. 

Building a new training set 

We created a new training dataset with more cases presenting with challenging characteristics to expose the nnU-Net to outliers, leading to a hopefully more accurate and robust model. The selection of patients for this new training set was guided by clinical and imaging criteria identified in the initial model (15) as being associated with lower Dsc. Patients from each dataset were set aside for the testing phase in order to compare both models in the same testing set of samples never used for training either the initial model or the enriched one. We categorized Dsc into low (0.00–0.40), intermediate (0.41–0.70), and high (0.71–1.00) performance. Similarly, the segmented flap volume (in the manual ground-truth) was classified into small (0.00–50.00 cm 3), medium (50.01–

Discussion 

More accurate and robust flap segmentation was obtained using an enriched nnU-net model built on a previously built soft tissue flap segmentation atlas. Its originality lied in the incorporation of selected challenging cases (such as pedicled flaps, small flaps, unusual location including maxillary flaps, bone resection, presence of dental artefacts or bite block) in the training dataset to enhance heterogeneity and representativity of real-world data. It should be underscored that such an increase of both accuracy (i.e., average of Dsc across cases) and the associated reliability (i.e., standard deviation of Dsc across cases), from mean 0.66 ± 0.29 to 0.74 ± 0.20 (median from 0.76 to 0.80) (p<.001), cannot be considered negligible. Indeed, in segmentation challenges, the differences across the best-ranked challengers are often found within 0.1 of Dsc or less. For example, in the HECKTOR challenge, the task was to segment primary tumors and lymph nodes in HNC patients: the winning team got an average Dsc of 0.788 while the team ranked 5 th reached 0.772 (21). Compared to a task like tumor delineation in PET/CT for HECKTOR, the postoperative context for segmenting flaps in the present work is more challenging, in particular in the context of reconstructive surgery with new tissues, materials and artefacts. While segmentation of a definitive primary tumor has often a high Dsc, postoperative tumor bed segmentation usually shows lower performance 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS due to distorted anatomy, fibrosis and surgical changes and absence of well-defined mass effect. A Dsc≥0.70 is interesting for guidance in the case of new, previously ignored, structures, such as flaps. It translates into a reduction in contour correction time and decreased geometric variability, particularly at structure boundaries that most influence dose–volume metrics. Importantly, it serves in this exploratory phase of defining dose-volume relationships for previously understudied structures. It is important to emphasize that the initial flap segmentation model had been trained on a multicentric dataset from a clinical trial. It seemed it was however insufficient to cover the variety of challenging cases, as the model failed to segment numerous cases in the three other real-life datasets. Patients included in clinical trials may not be representative enough of the heterogeneity present in real-world patients. In-trial patients are usually fit, and have less comorbidities (therefore more likely to have free flaps than pedicled flaps) and complications. In this study, this hypothesis was reinforced by the fact that in-trial patients more frequently had pedicled flaps and bone resection without bone reconstruction, situations that are usually driven by contraindications to prolonged sophisticated reconstructive surgery. Building an improved training set by selecting representative cases of challenging situations for the segmentation, improvements in performance were observed, both in terms of the level of accuracy (higher mean and median values) and reliability (smaller associated standard deviation) across most centers/datasets included in the present study and across patients cases, showing that the enriched model is more reliable across different samples and contexts. We nonetheless recognize a potential selection bias introduced by our enrichment strategy, which consisted in building the training set for T2 explicitly based on characteristics associated with poor performance of T1. This process was thus informed by prior model behavior, which introduces a form of model-guided selection bias. The observed improvements were demonstrated for identified failure modes and cannot constitute a guaranteed generalization to new unseen cases (e.g., different anatomical characteristics or artifact distributions). T2 was also trained on 127 samples instead of 117 for T1, about 9% more training samples, but this is unlikely to drive a performance difference. Nonetheless, T2 still completely failed or provided limited performance in several cases, hence its generalizability should not be inferred beyond the included datasets. Further external validation at a larger scale and potentially additional methodological improvements in the model design and/or enrichment through the use of additional labeled data for especially challenging or complex cases not considered here (e.g., bite blocks) are thus warranted before considering it for potential broader applicability. Sub-analysis by dataset is underpowered due to small numbers of patients and should be considered exploratory, our main conclusions being drawn from the pooled data. We found the best improvements in the datasets A and C, whereas they were less striking in Multi and R. This was observed using both performance metrics Dsc and HD. However, even if the results were not 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS strikingly improved using T2 in Multi and R, they were not decreased either. In the case of Multi, it is especially important to emphasize on the fact that despite being trained using only half the number of samples of the Multi dataset (n=59 instead of 117), the T2 model still achieved slightly improved performance in that specific dataset testing set (n=28), with average Dsc increasing from 0.74 to 0.80 and the associated standard deviation being halved (from ± 0.31 to ± 0.15), despite a small decrease of the median Dsc (0.88 to 0.86) and a resulting p-value of 0.653. This is explained by the fact that there were cases with complete failures with T1 that got moderate or good results with T2 (see right part of the distribution in Figure 2), however this was partly compensated by slightly less high Dsc values obtained by T2 in cases that were well delineated by T1 (see left part of the distribution). However, it can be considered that these small decreases in performance still lead to good results (most have a score that remains above 0.8) and it is more important to get partial or good segmentation in complete failures, or improvements of insufficient Dsc values. In the case of R, there were only 10 cases to evaluate and no complete failure for T1, therefore some small decrease in performance for the best results compensated for the improvements seen in the worst ones. The analysis of Dsc by localization, magnitude of artefacts, volume of flap, as well as the decrease of the levels of correlation between Dsc and each factor, all support the same trend regarding the consistent improvement of performance through the training of enriched model T2. T2 improved the performances in the presence of artefacts. As artefacts are frequent in HNC patients and often significantly hinder manual delineation, this automatic segmentation tool, able to overcome some limitations due to artefacts for clinicians un- trained to manual flap delineation, could provide a welcome assistance for flap segmentation (22). Artefacts in this study were consistently correlated with tumor sites. Oral cavity tumors with a flap in the oral cavity are often associated with bone resection and an edentulous bone flap. Bone resection removes some teeth and their associated dental fillings and crowns, and sub- sequent artefacts on imaging. In contrast, oropharyngeal tumors with a flap in the oropharynx are less frequently associated with bone resection. As conservative dental care is the rule, the teeth are more often preserved with their associated artefacts. laryngeal and hypopharyngeal tumors being below the jaws are much less affected by artefacts when considering flap delineation. Given the previously established inter-expert variability with Dsc around 0.7 (17,20), the absolute performance in 75 cases out of 96 in the testing set can be considered consistent with clinical requirements, compared to 64 cases with T1. T2 could be implemented in routine practice through interfacing with radiology and radiotherapy software, drastically shortening the time-consuming task of flap delineation and enhancing the robustness of downstream dosimetry parameters (e.g., Dmax, Dmean, Vx). It is being used as an ancillary study in the OPTIFLAP phase III trial (23). When these automatic contours serve to prescribe the dose and optimize the dose distribution, they must always be verified, and modified accordingly, by the radiation oncologists. This remains true for all automatically contoured structures. Therefore, keeping the experts 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS in the loop and allowing them to validate and potentially edit/correct the result from the automated segmentation remains of the utmost importance, especially since T2 still partly or completely failed in some cases (10 cases with Dsc between 0.32 and 0.59, and 4 cases with Dsc of 0). Using T2, the time radiation oncologists spend correcting automated contours could be concentrated on a smaller number of cases compared with T1. The limitations of our work also include the retrospective nature of the datasets, the limited number of patients (especially for the sub-analysis of each dataset), and the limited reliability of manual contours to generate the ground-truth in such a complex task. Interobserver reliability in artifact grading was not explicitly assessed during the process and cannot therefore be reported quantitatively here, although the number of cases with different grading amongst the three clinicians was small. Finally, only Dsc and HD metrics were explored. Future investigations could also consider additional metrics, e.g., boundary-focused metrics such as 95 th percentile HD. We will next evaluate further this improved model on yet another database with prospectively recruited patients (OPTIFLAP phase III trial) (23). Then, as our goal is to characterize the flaps during RT, our future work will focus on the segmentation of flaps in sequential CT scans during and after RT using the improved T2 model. Using the segmented flaps, we then intend to characterize the evolution of their properties (volume, geometrical shape and textural/intensity information) during RT, which will also be analyzed in regard to the planned dose map. 

Conclusion 

A more robust flap segmentation model was built by enrichment of a previous trial-based model with rare and challenging flaps obtained from a real-world database framework. This improvement was achieved without modification to the neural network nnU-Net architecture, loss function, or algorithmic pipeline, demonstrating potential clinical applicability and higher robustness for faster flap segmentation. However, clinical validation and edits by experts remain crucial given the small number of unsatisfactory or failed segmentations.
