## Abstract 

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) tra-ditionally relies on a sparse, outcome-based signal. Recent work shows that providing a fine-grained, model-intrinsic signal—rewarding the confidence growth in the ground-truth answer—effectively improves language reasoning training by providing step-level guidance without costly external mod-els. While effective for unimodal text, we find that naively applying this global reward to vision-language (V-L) reason-ing is a suboptimal strategy, as the task is a heterogeneous mix of sparse visual perception and dense textual reason-ing. This global normalization creates mixture-induced sig-nal degradation, where the training signal for visual steps is statistically distorted by the predominant textual steps. We propose Perception-Decomposed Confidence Reward (PDCR) , a framework that solves this by aligning the reward structure with the task’s heterogeneous nature. PDCR first performs an unsupervised skill decomposition, introducing a model-internal Visual Dependence Score to quantify visual reliance and applying a clustering algorithm to separate per-ception and reasoning steps. Based on this, PDCR computes a decomposed advantage by normalizing confidence gains within each skill cluster. This intra-cluster normalization provides a stable, correctly-scaled signal for both perception and reasoning. We demonstrate that PDCR outperforms the naive, global-reward formulation and sparse-reward base-lines on key V-L reasoning benchmarks. The code is publicly accessible at https://github.com/hee-suk-yoon/PDCR.

## 2. Related Works 

2.1. Reasoning in Multimodal Models 

Improving the reasoning capabilities of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) is an active area of research [ 4, 7, 10 – 12 , 19 , 22 , 26 , 27 , 35 , 37 , 43 , 45 ]. A common paradigm is to fine-tune MLLMs using Reinforcement Learn-ing (RL) with verifiable rewards [ 9, 28 , 38 ]. However, this approach typically relies on a sparse, outcome-based reward (i.e., whether the final answer is correct), which is inefficient for training complex, multi-step tasks. To overcome this, several works have introduced external Process Reward Models (PRMs) [ 3 , 21 , 23 , 31 ]. PRMs pro-vide a dense, step-level training signal, but are notoriously costly to train, often requiring extensive and difficult dataset curation. This cost has motivated a recent shift towards find-ing reward signals that are model-intrinsic or self-generated. 

2.2. Model-Intrinsic and Self-Rewarding Methods 

To address the costs of external PRMs, an alternative direc-tion is self-rewarding reinforcement learning, which sources rewards from the model’s own internal signals [ 44 , 47 , 54 ]. These signals often include logit-based self-certainty [ 53 ], self-confidence [14], or majority-voting [55]. A particularly relevant framework in the language do-main is the Progressively Ascending Confidence Reward (PACR) [ 42 ]. PACR provides a dense, step-level reward by calculating the increase in the model’s log-probability of the ground-truth answer, guided by the inductive bias that confi-dence should ascend during a correct reasoning trace. This approach has proven effective for text-only reasoning but has not been sufficiently adapted for the unique challenges of multimodal tasks. 

2.3. Decomposed Reward Modeling 

A parallel line of research focuses on decomposing complex reward signals. In text-only LLMs, this is often done by evaluating a response from various perspectives, such as helpfulness, safety, or correctness [ 5, 33 , 51 ]. This concept is also being explored in MLLMs, where the reward signal is often denser due to the multimodal inputs [25, 35]. However, existing work on vision-language decompo-sition primarily focuses on generating explicit textual de-scriptions (e.g., image captions) to serve as the basis for the reward [ 6 , 15 , 19 ]. Our work diverges from this approach by decomposing the original reasoning steps directly via an internal, process-level signal, rather than relying on the generation of additional, explicit text like an image caption. We argue that visual and linguistic capabilities are hetero-geneous skills with distinct distributions, and therefore ben-efit from independent normalization. Analogous concepts have been explored in concurrent works in other domains, such as multi-reward [17] or tool-use agentic tasks [56]. Figure 2. The baseline dense reward pipeline. ## 4 . The Challenge of Heterogeneous Skills in Vision-Language Reasoning 

While the dense reward signal from ground-truth confidence growth (Eq. 5) is effective for unimodal language tasks, its naive application to vision-language (V-L) reasoning is suboptimal. The core issue, which we validate in this section, is that V-L reasoning is not a homogeneous process. Instead, it is a heterogeneous mixture of two functionally distinct, high-level skills: (1) sparse, but critical, visual perception 

steps and (2) dense, predominant textual reasoning steps. 

[Observation 1] : V-L Reasoning as a Heteroge-neous Skill Mix. The two primary skills in a V-L rea-soning trajectory serve different purposes. As illustrated in the example in Figure 3-(a), visual perception involves extracting evidence from the visual input I and translating it into textual observations. This includes identifying objects, their attributes, or their spatial relationships (e.g., “The im-age shows a person standing at a counter, actively using a POS terminal.” ). In contrast, textual reasoning involves logical deduction, calculation, or conclusion-drawing based on previously established facts (e.g., “The act of operating a POS terminal in a commercial setting is the primary role of a cashier.” ). To empirically validate this functional distinction, we analyze the model’s internal attention patterns. We first annotated a representative set of reasoning trajectories, cat-egorizing each step as either visual perception or textual reasoning (see Appendix 15 for full annotation details). We then measured the average attention each step paid to the 

input visual tokens . As shown in the plot in Figure 3-(b), the two skills exhibit distinct attention profiles. Visual percep-tion steps exhibit high attention to the input visual tokens, as they are responsible for converting visual information into a textual format. Conversely, textual reasoning steps attend almost exclusively to the preceding text tokens, using the information established by prior steps to perform logical inference. This shows that a V-L reasoning trajectory is not a homogeneous process, but a heterogeneous mixture of these two distinct capabilities. 

[Observation 2] : Statistical Imbalance of Skills. 

Beyond their functional differences, we find a statistical im-balance between the two skills. Analyzing the same ground-truth annotated set from [Observation 1] , we observe that visual perception steps are inherently sparse, account-ing for approximately 30% of the total reasoning steps, while the textual reasoning steps form the dense majority (approx. 70%), as shown in Figure 3-(c). This sparsity is a key driver of the normalization problem we describe next. 

[Observation 3] : Mixture-Induced Signal Degra-dation from Skill Imbalance. This statistical imbalance poses a significant challenge for the global normalization in Eq. 5. We term this issue mixture-induced signal degrada-tion, as the global statistics ( min , max ) are computed from a pool overwhelmingly dominated by the returns of textual 

steps. This global baseline is, therefore, unrepresentative of the sparse perception steps. Figure 3-(d) ( left ) illustrates this effect: the naive global normalization compresses and misaligns the perception ad-vantage distribution, which could lead to ill-scaled credit for these critical actions. ## 5 . Method: Perception-Decomposed Confi-dence Reward (PDCR) 

Our method, Perception-Decomposed Confidence Reward (PDCR), directly addresses the core problem identified in Section 4: that a single, homogeneous reward is suboptimal for heterogeneous V-L skills. As illustrated in Figure 4, PDCR operates in two stages. Specifically, it (i) performs 

unsupervised skill decomposition to automatically cluster steps into visual perception and textual reasoning groups, and (ii) computes a decomposed advantage by normalizing confidence gains within each respective cluster, ensuring a more stable, intra-cluster comparison.

Hallucination Diagnosis. We use HallusionBench [ 8 ], an image-context reasoning benchmark specifically designed to test a model’s hallucination and visual illusion errors. 

6.3. Baselines 

We compare our proposed PDCR against several key base-lines on both model backbones. For a direct and fair compar-ison, we train using identical system prompts and hyperpa-rameters (details in Appendix 16): GRPO [ 28 ]; DAPO [ 46 ], which solves the vanishing advantage problem of GRPO (for this baseline, we add only dynamic sampling to stan-dard GRPO, without incorporating other improvements from DAPO such as a higher clip rate.); and PACR [ 42 ], which applies a naive, globally-normalized ground-truth confidence growth process reward.

## 8. Conclusion 

We introduced PDCR to address the challenge of hetero-geneous skills in Vision-Language reasoning. By cluster-ing steps via a model-internal Visual Dependence Score, PDCR decomposes the confidence growth reward in an un-supervised manner. This intra-cluster normalization resolves mixture-induced signal degradation, providing stable advan-tages for both perception and reasoning. ## 14. Segmentation Detail 

A prerequisite for a process-based reward framework is the segmentation of the reasoning trajectory τ (i) into a discrete sequence of steps {h(i) 

> k

}Ki

> k=1

. The step is the fundamental unit to which a reward or advantage is assigned. Previous work in process-reward modeling has adopted several strategies to define this unit: • Supervised Formatting via SFT: Some approaches utilize Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) to enforce a highly structured output format, such as training the model to generate explicit step delimiters (e.g., “Step 1:”, “Step 2:”). While this simplifies segmentation, it necessitates an SFT stage to instill these formatting constraints. As detailed in our implementation (Appendix 16), our training adopts a R1-style pipeline [ 9 ] that bypasses SFT to initiate reasoning training directly via RLVR. Consequently, strategies relying on pre-learned formatting are not applicable to our setting. • Confidence-based Segmentation: Some approaches leverage intrinsic model signals to dynamically segment reasoning trajectories. For instance, Liu et al. [18] proposes, a method that divides reasoning steps based on the model’s confidence in predicting the next token. This approach aims to identify true decision points by observing how the model’s uncertainty changes, avoiding arbitrary segmentation based on fixed lengths or rule-based heuristics that may not align with the model’s internal reasoning flow. • Rule-Based Segmentation: A more common, lightweight, and model-agnostic approach is to use simple heuristics to parse the generated text. This is the strategy adopted by the original PACR framework [ 42 ] and other process-reward based methods [ 13 , 40 , 50 ]. The most common heuristic is to define a new step at each newline character ( \n), or after sentence-terminating punctuation (e.g., a period followed by a space). In this work, we adopt this strategy due to its simplicity and to ensure a fair comparison with PACR [42]. 15. Label Acquisition for Skill Analysis 

To empirically validate the heterogeneous nature of V-L reasoning ( [Observation 1] in Section 4) and the effectiveness of our unsupervised skill decomposition (Section 5.1), we required a set of ground truth skill labels. Since no existing dataset provides step-level distinctions between perception and reasoning, we constructed a high-quality annotated set using a state-of-the-art Large Language Model as a programmatic annotator. 

15.1. Annotation Setup 

We sampled 10,000 reasoning trajectories generated by the Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct model from our valida-tion set. Each trajectory was first segmented into discrete steps following Appendix 14. Subsequently, we utilized 

gpt-5-2025-08-07 to classify every individual step within these trajectories. To ensure the labels captured true vi-sual dependency rather than just semantic relevance, we designed a rigorous prompt with a specific exclusion criterion. The model was provided with the full context (Image, Question, Full Reasoning Trace) and the specific Candidate Step, and instructed to output a boolean classification in strict JSON format. The exact system instruction provided to the annotator is as follows: System Instruction for Skill Annotation              

> Role: You are an expert annotator. Decide whether a SPECIFIC step in a chain-of-thought is a “visual perception” step.
> Definition: A visual perception step should primarily involve reading or describing visual content from the provided image (e.g., recognizing objects, numbers, colors, relative positions, text in the image, bars in a chart, etc.). Steps that perform logical deductions, arithmetic, or generic reasoning without referencing observed visual features are NOT visual-perception steps.
> Exclusion Criterion: If a step refers to visual details that could already be inferred from earlier reasoning (i.e., it does not require directly inspecting the image again), classify that step as NOT visual perception.
> Output Format: Return STRICT JSON with keys exactly: •is visual perception (boolean) •reason (string) Do not include extra keys or text outside JSON.
> Input Template:
> Question: {question }
> Full thinking process (all steps concatenated): {full process }
> Candidate step to classify: {step text }
> Now output JSON only.

Distinguishing Perception from Contextual Recall. A critical challenge in defining these labels is separating active perception from visual memory . As noted in the Exclusion Criterion above, a reasoning step might reference a visual object without actually looking at the image, relying instead on facts established in previous steps. We explicitly instructed the model to classify such instances as Textual Reasoning , ensuring that the Visual Perception label is reserved strictly for steps that extract new information from the raw visual signal.

16.1. Training Framework and Hyperparameters 

We perform all experiments using the EasyR1 framework. Consistent with the R1-Zero style training [ 9], we apply Reinforce-ment Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) directly on the base model, bypassing any Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) stage. This ensures that the reasoning behaviors we observe are genuine results of the reinforcement learning process, rather than artifacts inherited from supervised fine-tuning. All models (including baselines) are trained on the Vision-SR1-47K dataset for 1 epoch. To ensure a strictly fair comparison, we maintain a consistent configuration across all methods (GRPO [ 28 ], DAPO [ 46 ], PACR [ 42 ], and our PDCR). Common hyperparameters for the optimizer, RL process, and evaluation are detailed in Table 6. All models were trained on a single node with 8 × NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs. 

Format reward. Given that our training pipeline bypasses SFT, the model does not inherently possess the structural priors to format its reasoning steps. To address this, we incorporate a format reward alongside the outcome accuracy reward. A trajectory is assigned a format score Rfmt based on the valid enclosure of the reasoning process within <think> and </think> tags, and the final answer within \boxed {} . Specifically, we augment the outcome reward R(i) (Eq. 1) with a fixed bonus of 

Rfmt = 0 .1 for compliant trajectories, while assigning Rfmt = 0 to invalid ones. 

Details on DAPO baseline. For the DAPO baseline [ 46 ], we adopted a specific configuration to isolate the impact of reward stability. The full DAPO framework originally introduces three modifications to GRPO: (1) Clipping to promote diversity and prevent entropy collapse, (2) Dynamic Sampling to solve the vanishing advantage problem (where a batch has 0 variance in rewards), and (3) a Token-Level Policy Gradient loss to mitigate length bias. As discussed in Section 7, our primary goal in comparing with DAPO is to determine if PDCR’s performance gains are merely a result of stabilizing the training signal against the vanishing advantage problem. Therefore, our implementation of the DAPO baseline specifically applies only Dynamic Sampling on top of the standard GRPO framework. This controlled configuration allows for a direct, isolated comparison regarding reward signal stability.

Limitations and Future Works 

Our work presents a promising direction for self-rewarding V-L models, but has limitations that offer avenues for future research. 

Computational Cost. The primary limitation of our framework is the computational overhead during the training phase. As shown in Figure 6-(b) of main paper, both PDCR and the dense-reward baseline (PACR) [ 42 ] require additional computation to estimate the step-level confidence gains (and in our case, an additional visual dependence scores). This results in a higher per-step training cost compared to standard sparse-reward methods like GRPO [ 28 ]. However, since our decomposition mechanism is lightweight, the cost of PDCR is comparable to that of PACR. Crucially, we argued that this training overhead is a favorable trade-off for inference efficiency. As shown in Figure 6-(c)&(d), PDCR induces significantly more concise reasoning traces compared to GRPO. Consequently, while PDCR requires more GPU-hours to train, it yields a more efficient model for deployment. Future work could explore internal activation-based approximations to obtain these confidence scores without requiring full additional forward passes, potentially bridging the training cost gap. 

Binary Skill Decomposition. Our method makes a simplifying assumption that V-L reasoning steps are a heterogeneous mixture of two distinct and separable skills. We then use Otsu’s method to enforce a single, hard threshold c∗ between them. In reality, some steps may be hybrid (e.g., complex spatial reasoning that is both perceptual and logical). Our binary decomposition forces these hybrid steps into one of the two clusters, which may not be optimal. Future research could explore soft-clustering assignments or the identification of three or more distinct skill types.
