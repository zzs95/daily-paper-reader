ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS Abstract 

Chronic low back pain (CLBP) is a prevalent condition with unclear pathophysiology and substantial socioeconomic burden. Cerebral blood flow (CBF) alterations have been implicated in CLBP, yet previous arterial spin labeling (ASL) studies using single post-labeling delay (PLD) have yielded inconsistent results. In this study, multi-PLD ASL was combined with machine learning to characterize CBF alterations in CLBP and to explore their classification feasibility. Seventy-eight patients with CLBP and seventy-eight age- and sex-matched healthy controls underwent multi-PLD ASL scanning. Voxel-wise comparisons of normalized CBF were performed, followed by correlation analyses with clinical measures. Radiomics features extracted from brain regions showing significant CBF differences were used to construct machine learning classification models via a rigorous nested cross-validation and LASSO feature selection framework. Compared with healthy controls, patients with CLBP exhibited significant hyperperfusion in the right lingual gyrus and right thalamus. CBF values in the right lingual gyrus were positively correlated with Oswestry Disability Index scores, while thalamic CBF was positively correlated with pain intensity. Among the evaluated models, the XGBoost classifier achieved the best performance, with an area under the curve of 0.842 (95% CI: 0.774– 0.901). These findings indicate that region-specific CBF alterations

Keywords: chronic low back pain; arterial spin labeling; post-labeling delay; cerebral blood flow; machine learning 

Introduction 

Chronic low back pain (CLBP) is defined as low back pain persisting for over 3 months, which is a major global health concern (1, 2). Most individuals will encounter either acute or chronic low back pain at some point in their lives, with a few progressing to a chronic state that impacts their quality of life (3-5). In China, CLBP is the second leading cause of disability, imposing significant social and economic burdens (6, 7). Despite its high prevalence, the exact pathogenesis of CLBP remains unclear, hindering advancements in diagnosis and treatment. Recent neuroimaging studies have identified changes in central nervous system function in individuals with CLBP, which are increasingly recognized as key neural correlates of chronic pain (8-16). Among these studies, alterations in cerebral blood flow (CBF) have been shown to be associated with the aggravation and relief of CLBP (17, 18). However, only a few studies have examined the 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS changes in CBF in the resting state of patients with CLBP, and the results are inconsistent. For example, while one study reported reduced perfusion in brain regions such as the motor cortex, supplementary motor area, and cingulate cortices in patients with CLBP compared to healthy controls (HCs) (19), another study found no significant CBF differences between these groups (18). The divergent results between these studies may be partially explained by their reliance on single post-labeling delay (PLD) arterial spin labeling (ASL) sequences. Single-PLD ASL is highly sensitive to physiological variations in arterial transit time (ATT), which can lead to significant CBF quantification errors (22-24). ASL is a non-invasive perfusion imaging technique that measures CBF using magnetically labeled arterial blood water as an endogenous tracer, eliminating the need for contrast agents and radiation (20, 21). Accurate CBF quantification heavily depends on the PLD—the timing between blood labeling and image acquisition— which must adequately account for the ATT (22, 23). While ASL typically uses a single PLD, this may not be sufficient when the ATT distribution is wide (24). Using multiple PLDs (multi-PLD) ASL can effectively account for ATT variations, thereby providing more accurate CBF quantification compared to single-delay methods (25-27). 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS In current research, machine learning methods enable researchers to better identify brain regions closely associated with CLBP, which can reveal potential functional changes that are often overlooked in traditional analyses (28, 29). For example, previous studies have applied machine learning techniques to differentiate patients with CLBP from HC using cortical thickness and functional connectivity values (30). However, structural neuroimaging biomarkers often manifest after functional alterations, and clinical scales rely on subjective reporting. This highlights the need for sensitive functional biomarkers to characterize CLBP pathology (31, 32). Therefore, in this study, we utilized radiomics features of brain regions with differences in CBF between two groups to evaluate their potential discriminative performance in identifying patients with CLBP. Commonly used machine learning models for classification based on neuroimaging data include Support Vector Classifier (SVC), Random Forest (RF), Logistic Regression (LR), Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost), and Light Gradient Boosting (LightGBM) (33-35). By leveraging these diverse algorithmic approaches within a strictly cross-validated framework, we aimed to explore the classification feasibility of multi-PLD ASL radiomics as a supplementary functional indicator for CLBP. 

Consequently, in this study, we aimed to utilize multi-PLD ASL 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS techniques to assess CBF alterations in patients with CLBP. This approach enables a more precise quantification of perfusion, thus enhancing our understanding of the role of cerebral hemodynamics in CLBP pathophysiology. Furthermore, by combining rigorous machine learning methodologies with CBF radiomic features derived from these altered brain regions, we aimed to explore the classification feasibility and potential discriminative performance of these functional biomarkers in identifying patients with CLBP.

We recruited 78 patients with CLBP [39 men and 39 women; median (25-75 percentile): 54.58 (47.25-65.75)] from January 2021 to June 2022, as well as 78 HCs [41 men and 37 women; median (25-75 percentile): 57.00 (47.00-65.25)] matched for age and sex. The trial was approved by the local institutional review board. All participants were required to sign informed consent prior to the research. This study was registered at the Chinese Clinical Trial Registry (Registration number: ChiCTR2100053089; Registration date: 2021-11-10). 

The inclusion criteria for patients with CLBP were as follows: (1) aged between 18 and 75 years; (2) pain that had continued or fluctuated over a period of three months or more (36), Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) scores of at least 3 points (37); (3) no prior history of neurological or psychiatric conditions, major systemic illnesses, head trauma, or comas, as well as other chronic pain disorders; (4) right-handedness. 

The exclusion criteria for patients with CLBP were: (1) poor cooperation during MRI scan and motion artifacts in images; (2) MRI plain scan detected brain tumors, brain atrophy, previous intracranial hemorrhage, subarachnoid hemorrhage, or other 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS disorders affecting CBF measurement; (3) presence of diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia . All patients regularly took analgesic medications and underwent MRI scans 12 hours after discontinuing the medication. Recruitment of HCs was based on the following inclusion criteria: (1) aged between 18 and 75 years; (2) previous physical health, no history of neurological diseases, no diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, no mental illness, no long-term smoking, no alcohol abuse, no hormonal drugs, no acute or chronic pain; (3) right-handedness. The exclusion criteria for HCs were: (1) poor cooperation during MRI scan and motion artifacts in images; (2) MRI plain scan detected brain tumors, brain atrophy, previous intracranial hemorrhage, subarachnoid hemorrhage, or other disorders affecting CBF measurement. 

Clinical characteristics 

Demographic information for all subjects was collected before they underwent MRI scanning. Before undergoing MRI scans, we also collected the participants' BMI. Additionally, all participants completed the VAS and Oswestry Disability Index (ODI) scales. 

VAS. This scale is used to evaluate the pain level of patients with CLBP, using a 10cm line or ruler. The participant marks the line

MRI data acquisition 

The MRI data were acquired from a 3.0 Tesla scanner (Discovery 750w, GE, USA), using a 24-channel head coil. Participants were asked to refrain from taking painkillers, drinking alcohol, or consuming caffeine on the day of the examination. During the examination, the subjects were required to keep their heads as still as possible and remain relaxed and awake. The multi-PLD ASL data were acquired using a 3D pseudo-Continuous Arterial Spin Labeling (pCASL) sequence with a 3D spiral fast spin-echo readout. Crucially, background suppression was inherently integrated and applied within this optimized manufacturer protocol to effectively minimize static tissue artifacts and enhance the perfusion signal-to-noise ratio. The specific scan parameters were as follows: Repetition time (TR) = 5978ms, Echo time (TE) = 11.5ms, Field of View (FOV) = 22cm x 22cm, Slice 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS thickness = 4.5mm, Number of slices = 106, Resolution = 4.67mm x 4.67mm, Number of Excitations (NEX) = 1, Total examination time = 8 minutes, 7 PLD times = 1.0s, 1.22s, 1.48s, 1.78s, 2.15s, 2.63s, 3.32s, and Effective label durations = 0.22s, 0.26s, 0.30s, 0.37s, 0.48s, 0.68s, 1.18s. In addition, axial T 2-weighted imaging (T 2WI, TR = 4357ms, TE = 1000ms), T 2-weighted fluid attenuated inversion recovery (T 2WI-FLAIR, TR = 9000ms, TE = 140ms), T 1-weighted fluid attenuated inversion recovery (T 1WI-FLAIR, TR = 2213.1ms, TE = 1000ms), and axial diffusion weighted imaging (DWI, TR =3586ms, TE = 77.2ms) were also acquired for radiological assessment. 

CBF preprocessing 

Imaging data preprocessing was performed in Statistical Parametric Mapping (SPM, v12, https://www.fil.ion.ucl.ac.uk/spm/) and Data Processing & Analysis for Brain Imaging (DPABI, v8.2, http://rfmri.org/DPABI) (40), which were based on MATLAB R2020b (https://www.mathworks.com/products/matlab). First, CBF images of all subjects were nonlinearly aligned to the Montreal Neurological Institute (MNI) space via the PET-perfusion template of SPM 12, and the voxel size was resampled to 2 mm × 2 mm × 2 mm. Then, the normalized CBF maps were standardized by mean division using DPABI. Finally, the standardized CBF images were smoothed with 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS an 8 mm Gaussian smoothing kernel to increase the signal to noise ratio (SNR) and improve their statistical analysis. 

Group classification with Machine Learning 

We employed PyRadiomics 3.1.0 software (https://pyradiomics.readthedocs.io) to extract a comprehensive set of 1032 CBF radiomics features from the masks of brain regions with differences between CLBP and HC groups (41, 42). These features encompassed 14 shape-based features, 18 first-order statistical features, 68 second-order texture features, 688 wavelet transform features, and 244 Laplacian of Gaussian filter features. Crucially, to rigorously prevent any potential data leakage, feature normalization was not performed at this global extraction stage. Instead, Z-score standardization and robust feature dimensionality reduction were strictly embedded within the downstream nested cross-validation machine learning pipeline. In this study, to evaluate the diagnostic value of CBF radiomic features in identifying CLBP, a rigorous machine learning pipeline was constructed. Given the limited sample size, a Nested Cross-Validation (Nested CV) framework was implemented in place of the conventional train-test split approach, incorporating an outer Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOOCV) for robust model evaluation and an inner 10-fold CV for hyperparameter tuning (43-45). 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS In the data preprocessing phase, feature normalization was executed strictly within the cross-validation loop to avoid information leakage. Specifically, during each iteration of the outer LOOCV, Z-score standardization was exclusively fitted on the training fold, and the generated scaling parameters were subsequently applied to transform the test fold. Given the perfectly balanced nature of our finalized cohort, oversampling techniques were not required. 

To address the risk of overfitting associated with extracting 1032 radiomic features, the Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (LASSO) regression was initially applied for feature dimensionality reduction. Within the training set of each LOOCV iteration, an inner 10-fold CV was utilized to optimize the regularization hyperparameter of the LASSO model (46). Through this dynamic and strict regularization process, LASSO effectively penalized redundant variables, retaining an average of 61.4 essential non-zero features across the 144 independent iterations. 

The selected robust features were then fed into five distinct machine learning algorithms—LR, SVC, RF, XGBoost, and LightGBM—to develop predictive models. To ensure maximum stability and generalizability on this specific sample size, these models were trained utilizing their robust default hyperparameter 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS configurations. Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves were generated for each model to evaluate their discriminative performance. Key metrics, including the area under the curve (AUC), sensitivity, specificity, accuracy, and F1 score, along with the Balanced Accuracy and Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC), were computed to comprehensively assess model effectiveness (47). To robustly estimate the variance, the 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for AUC, sensitivity, and specificity were calculated utilizing 1,000 bootstrap resamples. To enhance model interpretability, Shapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) were utilized to quantify the importance and directional impact of features in the optimal global model (48). The model construction and statistical evaluations were implemented using Python 3.11 software (https://www.python.org) (49). Feature selection and machine learning classifications were conducted using the “scikit-learn” and “xgboost” packages, while the “matplotlib” package was employed to generate ROC curves. Additionally, the “shap” package was utilized to calculate and visualize the SHAP values of the features.

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS Discussion 

In the study, we characterized significant resting-state CBF alterations in patients with CLBP. Specifically, we observed marked hyperperfusion within the LING.R and the THA.R. Building upon these findings, we leveraged a rigorous nested machine learning framework utilizing multi-PLD ASL-derived radiomic features. Through this robust approach, we successfully demonstrated the classification feasibility and potential discriminative performance of these regional perfusion biomarkers in identifying CLBP. Furthermore, these localized hyperperfusion patterns were found to be positively correlated with clinical severity measures. 

In the context of existing literature, our observation of regional baseline hyperperfusion offers novel insights that contrast with some previous ASL studies in CLBP. For instance, earlier investigations utilizing traditional single-delay pulsed ASL (pASL) reported no significant baseline rCBF differences between CLBP patients and healthy controls, observing perfusion changes only during acute experimental pain exacerbations (50, 51). These discrepancies are likely attributable to the inherent methodological limitations of earlier perfusion imaging. Traditional single-PLD ASL is highly susceptible to macrovascular transit artifacts and arterial transit time (ATT) variations. Chronic pain can alter sympathetic tone and 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS regional hemodynamics, potentially prolonging ATT, which causes single-delay methods to underestimate true capillary-level tissue perfusion, leading to null findings at baseline. By utilizing a multi-PLD pCASL sequence, our study mathematically corrected for these physiological ATT delays. Beyond precise ATT estimation, this multi-delay approach offers the additional advantages of enhancing the overall signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and minimizing signal contamination from macrovascular compartments. Therefore, it provides a more sensitive and reliable mapping of absolute CBF. Consequently, the baseline hyperperfusion detected in our study likely reflects a more accurate neurovascular signature of the chronic pain state that was previously obscured. Indeed, recent applications of advanced pCASL have successfully captured profound CBF modulations in response to treatments like tDCS and acupuncture in CLBP, further highlighting the exceptional sensitivity of optimized ASL sequences to pain-related hemodynamic shifts (52). Compared to HCs, patients with CLBP exhibited higher CBF in the LING.R. The LING.R, located in the occipital lobe, plays a role in pain modulation and visuospatial processing; however, emerging evidence highlights its complex involvement in pain modulation and cognitive-emotional processing (53-55). Previous studies have shown that the LING.R exhibits widespread functional abnormalities across 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS various chronic pain conditions, including enhanced or diminished spontaneous brain activity and significant associations with emotional and pain-related scores (15, 56-59). For example, studies on chronic migraines have shown that the LING.R is involved in visual and emotional processing related to pain perception; specifically, it exhibits increased connectivity with brain regions involved in the reward system, suggesting a role in modulating pain perception and emotional responses (60). Interestingly, one study reported reduced activity in the LING.R in patients with CLBP (61). This apparent discrepancy may stem from the different neuroimaging modalities employed. The previous study utilized resting-state functional MRI, which measures spontaneous neuronal activity, whereas our research used ASL perfusion imaging, which reflects the metabolic demands of brain tissue. Therefore, the hyperperfusion we observed may represent a compensatory perfusion response to underlying neuronal abnormalities—whether hyperactivity or hypoactivity—aimed at maintaining tissue homeostasis. In this study, increased blood flow in the LING.R of CLBP patients, further highlights the involvement of the LING.R in chronic pain development and progression (62). Although the LING.R is not a traditional core region in the pain nociceptive conduction pathway, its hyperactivation in individuals with long-

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS term chronic pain likely extends beyond the simple processing of pain-related visual cues. Emerging perspectives suggest that these neurofunctional changes might be driven by more complex cognitive and network-level mechanisms. For instance, the observed hyperperfusion could reflect the continuous visual imagery of pain, or it may indicate the LING.R’s intricate interactions with the Default Mode Network (DMN) and attention modulation systems in response to chronic nociceptive input (63). Such multidimensional neurofunctional changes may be associated with the development and maintenance of chronic pain. Moreover, this study points out that hyperperfusion of the LING.R is significantly correlated with ODI scores. This suggests that the LING.R is particularly sensitive to pain-related processing and may contribute to the enhanced sensory experiences associated with chronic pain (64). In the present study, we also found that CBF in the THA.R was significantly increased in patients with CLBP compared to HCs, revealing a state of potential functional hyperactivity. However, in previous studies, numerous researchers have reported thalamic structural atrophy in different types of chronic pain (65). For example, decreased gray matter density (GMD) has been observed in the thalamus of CLBP patients (66), reduced gray matter volume (GMV) in the medial thalamus of patients with Complex Regional 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS Pain Syndrome (CRPS) (67), and similar GMV reductions have been found in patients with cluster headache and primary trigeminal neuralgia (68, 69). Furthermore, evidence from neurochemical studies supports this structural change. In chronic migraineurs, reduced N-acetylaspartate (NAA), a marker of neuronal integrity, may even be linked to genetic predispositions. Specific genetic variants are associated with both reduced thalamic volume and chronic pain risk (70, 71). However, thalamic structural plasticity is not a simple linear degradation. In the acute phase of pain, such as herpes zoster, studies have conversely observed an increase in thalamic GMV, which is positively correlated with pain intensity (72). This suggests that structural changes in the thalamus are a dynamic process, potentially showing opposing trends at different stages of pain. Despite this complex background of structural remodeling, our observation of thalamic hyperperfusion is strongly corroborated by multimodal imaging evidence of its sustained functional overactivity. At the local functional level, patients with trigeminal neuralgia show enhanced Regional Homogeneity (ReHo) in the THA.R, which positively correlates with VAS scores (73). This is particularly evident at the network functional level. A study specifically on CLBP found that functional connectivity (FC) between the THA.R and the 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS sensorimotor pathway was significantly enhanced, and this enhancement correlated with pain intensity (74). Similar thalamocortical connectivity abnormalities are a hallmark of chronic migraine (75), and in psoriatic arthritis, connectivity strength between the thalamus and the posterior insula correlates with nociceptive pain scores (76). This hyperactivity also has aneurochemical basis, with studies reporting elevated levels of the excitatory neurotransmitter glutamate in the thalamus of fibromyalgia patients (77). Beyond these correlational neuroimaging findings in humans, fundamental animal research offers valuable mechanistic insights into the functional significance of thalamic overactivity in chronic pain. Experimental studies have demonstrated that selectively modulating specific thalamic nuclei in healthy mice using optogenetic or chemogenetic methods can significantly influence hyperalgesic states; conversely, inhibiting neural activity within these regions in chronic pain models effectively alleviates nociceptive behaviors (78). Drawing upon these translational findings, the sustained thalamic hyperperfusion observed in our CLBP cohort is unlikely to be merely an epiphenomenon of pain perception. Instead, these convergent data suggest that such regional functional hyperactivity is intricately associated with the 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ongoing modulation and maintenance of the chronic pain pathology. The significant increase in right thalamic CBF observed in this study is likely closely linked to the persistent hyperexcitability of local neuronal populations. Fundamental animal models reveal that neuronal overactivity, continuously sustained by chronic nociceptive input, is a key neurobiological component associated with the maintenance of the pain state (78). This sustained neuronal firing imposes substantial demands on regional energy metabolism, which subsequently triggers neurovascular coupling mechanisms to increase local CBF in order to support this high-metabolic state. This interpretation aligns with functional observations in healthy individuals, where attending to a painful stimulus is sufficient to elicit increased thalamic blood flow (79). In patients with chronic pain, this metabolic demand might be further amplified, as emerging evidence suggests that their "right hemisphere-dependent energy homeostasis" is already profoundly disrupted (80). Ultimately, this functional hyperperfusion may initially emerge as a compensatory neurovascular response to structural deficits or neuronal damage previously documented in these regions (66, 81). Furthermore, machine learning classification methods based on CBF radiomic features derived exclusively from the LING.R and the THA.R demonstrated promising classification feasibility and 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS potential discriminative performance in identifying CLBP patients from HCs. Among the five evaluated algorithms, the tree-based XGBoost model achieved the optimal predictive performance. This superiority indicates that its intrinsic ensemble learning architecture, combined with gradient boosting and built-in L1/L2 regularization techniques, enables highly effective handling of high-dimensional radiomic data, mitigates the risk of overfitting, and robustly captures complex nonlinear neurovascular relationships (82, 83). Conversely, the SVC model exhibited relatively modest performance, which is likely attributable to its inherent computational limitations in scaling to high-dimensional feature spaces and its high sensitivity to hyperparameter optimization (84). While previous studies have successfully applied machine learning pipelines to classify CLBP patients utilizing macro-structural metrics such as cortical thickness (CT) or gray matter density (GMD) (29, 30), the current study advances this approach by extracting deeper, sub-visual perfusion heterogeneity information to uncover novel functional biomarkers. Crucially, the model interpretability provided by the SHAP value analysis explicitly revealed that high-order texture features (particularly those derived from wavelet transforms) and first-order statistics from the LING.R and THA.R contributed the most substantial weight to the classifier's 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS decisions. This data-driven insight underscores the critical diagnostic value of complex spatial CBF distribution patterns in chronic pain. Despite the promising findings, several limitations of this study must be acknowledged. First, the moderate sample size may limit the statistical power for high-dimensional radiomics analysis. Although we implemented a rigorous nested cross-validation framework and LASSO regularization to strictly mitigate overfitting, the lack of an independent, multicenter external test cohort remains a significant limitation. Therefore, while our models demonstrate classification feasibility, their generalizability requires further validation in larger, diverse populations. Second, while age and sex were rigorously controlled in the voxel-wise analyses, our machine learning models intentionally evaluated the pure, independent discriminative power of CBF radiomics without incorporating clinical covariates. Consequently, we could not completely rule out the potential confounding neurovascular effects of critical factors such as long-term analgesic use, sleep disturbances, anxiety, depression, pain duration variability and physical activity levels. Future multimodal models integrating these comprehensive clinical variables are warranted. Finally, due to the cross-sectional design of this study, we cannot determine any causal relationships between 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS regional CBF alterations and the chronification of CLBP. Future longitudinal studies tracking patients from the acute to chronic phase are essential to elucidate whether these regional hyperperfusion patterns are predisposing neurobiological factors or downstream consequences of chronic pain. 

Conclusions 

In conclusion, the resting-state perfusion alterations observed in our study suggest that hyperperfusion in the LING.R and THA.R is closely associated with the severity of pain and functional disability in patients with CLBP. Furthermore, utilizing rigorous machine learning frameworks to analyze these multi-PLD ASL radiomic features demonstrates strong classification feasibility and potential discriminative performance, highlighting their future value as supplementary functional biomarkers for CLBP.
