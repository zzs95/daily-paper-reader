## Abstract:

Computational pathology has recently advanced through the use of multimodal large language models (MLLMs) for visual question answering (VQA). However, most existing approaches rely on single-resolution analysis of pathology images, limiting their ability to capture both global and fine-grained morphological cues. In this work, we introduce QPath, a novel MLLM that integrates multi-resolution analysis via question-aware patch retrieval. For region-of-interest (ROI) images, QPath crops them into patches and fuses the top-k question-relevant patches with the original view to enhance localized reasoning. For whole-slide images (WSIs), we propose a dynamic sampling strategy that balances spatial coverage with semantic relevance, enabling efficient yet comprehensive slide-level understanding. Extensive experiments show that QPath achieves state-of-the-art performance on ROI-level VQA benchmarks and exhibits strong zero-shot generalization to WSI-level tasks.

SECTION 1.

## INTRODUCTION

Pathology has long been regarded as the gold standard for disease diagnosis. Traditionally, histopathological evaluation requires pathologists to meticulously examine tissue samples under a microscope to identify abnormalities. However, advancements in digital scanning technology have transformed this process, making it possible to generate high-resolution images of tissue samples. These innovations not only offer a more convenient and efficient method [1] for pathologists to analyze samples but also pave the way for enhanced diagnostic precision. Building on these breakthroughs, computational pathology (CPath) has emerged as a transformative paradigm, harnessing the power of machine learning and computer vision to enable large-scale, automated analysis of histopathological images [2].

The emergence of vision-language models (VLMs)[3], [4], such as CLIP [5], which consists of the aligned visual encoder and text encoder, has significantly boosted the progress of computational pathology, enabling zero-shot learning through foundation models [6]. CLIP-like models such as CONCH [7] and PathGen-CLIP [8] have shown remarkable performance in pathology classification tasks, leveraging contrastive learning on large-scale paired image–text datasets. Despite these successes, these models remain constrained to classification tasks and lack the generative capabilities required for more practical and question-driven applications, such as visual question answering (VQA). To overcome these limitations, multimodal large language models (MLLMs)[9], [10], [11], [12] have emerged as an effective solution. Unlike foundation models, which primarily depend on contrastive learning and are restricted to classification tasks, MLLMs combine a pre-trained visual encoder with a large language model (LLM) via a projector. This projector aligns visual embeddings with the textual embedding space of the LLM, enabling an integration between visual and textual modalities. Models such as Quilt-LLaVA [13] and SlideChat [14] exemplify this approach. With supervised fine-tuning (SFT), MLLMs not only excel in traditional image classification but also support more flexible and question-driven applications, including VQA. This development marks a significant leap forward in the evolution of computational pathology, expanding its capabilities to address more complex and interactive diagnostic tasks.

Despite recent progress, most approaches in computational pathology analyze images at a single resolution. In contrast, clinical practice involves examining WSIs across multiple resolutions to capture both global architecture and fine-grained details, a multi-scale perspective essential for accurate cancer subtype identification and diagnostic decision-making [15]. Recently, MR-PLIP [16] has introduced multi-resolution vision–language alignment, enabling foundation models to learn enhanced visual representations through multi-scale pretraining. While this approach highlights the advantages of incorporating information across resolutions, its current applications are predominantly limited to classification tasks and overlooks the potential to fully leverage the rich semantic information in question-driven tasks.

Inspired by these, we introduce **QPath,** a novel architecture for MLLM designed to enhance pathology VQA tasks. QPath addresses the limitations of current pathology MLLMs by leveraging question-aware image–text retrieval to enable multi-resolution integration. This innovation improves the ability of MLLMs to analyze and interpret pathology images, surpassing the constraints of single-resolution approaches. Specifically, for ROI images, we apply the AnyRes strategy, which involves dividing the image into multiple patches. We compute similarity scores between each patch and the question, selecting the top-_k_ patches to combine with the original image as input for the MLLM. For WSIs, we address the inherent challenges posed by their vast number of patches and the need to boost zero-shot performance at the WSI level. Rather than simply selecting the top-scoring patches based on similarity, we construct a similarity-based heatmap and implement a dynamic sampling strategy. This ensures that the sampling process not only identifies the most relevant patches but also maximizes spatial coverage across various regions of the WSI, resulting in a more comprehensive and holistic analysis. Our contributions are summarized as follows:

*   We introduce **QPath,** the first multi-resolution multimodal large language model (MLLM) specifically designed for pathology VQA tasks.

*   QPath incorporates a question-aware patch retrieval mechanism that enhances the ability of MLLMs to perform fine-grained analysis through questions on high-resolution histopathology images.

*   QPath employs a multi-resolution model design that is trained solely on ROI images, yet it achieves strong resolution extrapolation, attaining state-of-the-art performance on ROI-level benchmarks and demonstrating robust zero-shot generalization to WSI-level tasks.

## METHOD

In this section, we introduce QPath, a multi-resolution multimodal large language model (MLLM) specifically designed for pathology visual question answering (VQA), as shown in [Fig 1](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/). The primary goal of QPath is to enhance the model’s capacity to address fine-grained questions on high-resolution histopathology images by integrating question-aware retrieval with a dynamic multi-resolution sampling strategy. In [Section 2.1](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/), we describe a question-aware image–text retrieval mechanism that leverages a CLIP-like foundation model to compute similarity scores between the question and various image patches. Building upon this, we develop a dynamic multi-resolution strategy tailored for both ROI image and WSI settings, as detailed in [Section 2.2.1](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/) and [Section 2.2.2](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/), respectively. Specifically, for ROI images, the top-_k_ most relevant patches are selected based on their similarity scores. For WSIs, we extend this approach by incorporating a dynamic method designed to ensure adequate spatial coverage while assigning the remaining computational budget to the globally most relevant patches. These designs enable QPath to effectively combine semantic relevance with spatial diversity, resulting in a more comprehensive and effective multi-resolution representation for pathology VQA tasks.

### 2.1. Question-aware image-text retrieval

To better answer fine-grained questions on high-resolution pathology images, identifying the most question-relevant regions via image-text retrieval is crucial. We first apply the AnyRes strategy from LLaVA-OneVision [17] to crop the original image. Specifically, AnyRes dynamically selects an appropriate resolution or grid partition based on the original size of the input image, dividing the image into multiple patches. Which means, for the original image I, we partition it into multiple patches P={Patche s i}G i=1⊂I, where _G_ represents the total number of grids in the optimal grid partitioning computed by AnyRes.

We employ a CLIP-like foundation model to perform cross-modal retrieval between the patches and the question. Such foundation models are pretrained on large-scale pathology image–text pairs, endowing them with reliable zero-shot image–text retrieval capabilities. For a given question and the multiple patches, their embeddings are obtained by feeding them into the text encoder and vision encoder of the foundation model, respectively:

#### 2.2.2. Multi-resolution strategy for WSI

WSIs typically contain up to 100,000 _×_ 100,000 pixels, which poses significant challenges for multimodal large language models (MLLMs). To address this issue, we design a dynamic sampling strategy that ensures both the preservation of the most relevant patches and sufficient spatial coverage across different regions of the WSI.

Formally, a WSI is partitioned into a set of patches P={Patc h i}W i=1. Using the method described in [Section 2.1](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/), we compute the similarity scores _S_ between each patch and the given question. Based on the spatial positions of the patches, we further construct a heatmap _H_ ∈ ℝ _h×w_, where each entry corresponds to the similarity score of a patch at its corresponding location. Let _B_ denote the total sampling budget. We divide the overall budget _B_ into two parts, namely _B_ peak and _B_ top _−k_, where _B_ peak corresponds to the peak-based sampling strategy and _B_ top _−k_ corresponds to the rank-based sampling strategy. To ensure spatial coverage, we apply a maximum filtering operator Maximum(•) on the heatmap _H_, which identifies local peaks of similarity:

SECTION 3.

## EXPERIMENT

### 3.1. Training Setup

To improve the image understanding ability of the model in pathology domain, and facilitate multi-image understanding across different resolutions, we construct a large-scale multimodal instruction-tuning dataset comprising 18,151 high-resolution image–caption pairs and 80k VQA samples about pathology. During training, each image is first partitioned into multiple sub-images. We then compute the similarity between each sub-image and the corresponding caption, and select the most relevant sub-images, which are subsequently combined with the original image to form the input of MLLMs. This data construction strategy enhances the ability of MLLMs to capture multi-resolution visual information and improves their generalization in pathology-related tasks. Furthermore, we also incorporate data from existing instruction-tuning datasets, including PathInstruct [18], PathGen-Instruct [8], and Quilt-LLaVA-Instruct [13], to further enrich the training set. QPath is trained based on Qwen2.5-VL-7B [19] through two stages:

**Stage 1:** In the first stage, our goal is to enable the vision encoder of the general-purpose multimodal large model to learn robust visual embeddings for pathology images and to achieve effective alignment with the textual representations in the large language model. To this end, we only unfroze the visual encoder and the projection layer, and trained the model using caption data from the dataset.

**Stage 2:** In the second stage, in order to equip the model with the ability to handle more complex pathology tasks and to enhance its capacity for multi-image understanding under multiple resolutions, we further trained the model with instruction-tuning data. This includes multi-turn dialogue, caption generation, open-ended VQA, and close-ended VQA tasks.

### 3.2. Benchmark Description

We conduct evaluations on two complementary benchmarks. First, we assess the VQA performance of our model on ROI images using the PathMMU benchmark. Second, to evaluate its resolution extrapolation capability on WSIs, we further assess the model’s zero-shot VQA performance on the SlideBench-VQA (TCGA)[14]. A brief description of these two benchmarks is provided below:

**PathMMU**[20], [21] is a large-scale and expert-validated pathology benchmark for LMMs. It consists of three splits: _test-tiny_, _test_, and _val._ We used four subsets of PathMMU—_Atlas_, _PubMed_, _SocialPath_, and _EduContent_—which are derived from distinct sources. The benchmark was constructed using GPT-4V to generate enriched captions and question–answer pairs, and was further validated by seven pathologists to ensure reliability.

**SlideBench-VQA (TCGA)**[14] consists of 734 WSIs from TCGA, paired with 1,494 close-ended VQA samples. The benchmark covers three task types—microscopy, diagnosis, and clinical—as well as ten tumor types: BRCA, SKCM, READ, BLCA, LUAD, LGG, HNSC, COAD, GBM, and LUSC. To ensure data quality, the dataset underwent a two-stage filtering process involving large language models (LLMs) followed by expert pathologist validation.

### 3.3. Main Results

We compare the performance of models on PathMMU between general LMMs and pathology-specific LMMs. The general LMMs include LLaVA-v1.5-7B, LLaVA-OneVision-7B, and Qwen2.5-VL-7B, while the pathology-specific LMMs include LLaVA-Med, Quilt-LLaVA, and PathGen-LLaVA-13B. As shown in [Table 1](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/), our model outperforms all general LMMs and achieves state-of-the-art performance among pathology-specific LMMs. Compared with PathGen-LLaVA-13B, our model improves accuracy by 17% on the best-performing subset and achieves an overall gain of approximately 5%. This indicates that QPath can capture more fine-grained pathological information, thereby improving the model’s performance.

To verify the resolution extrapolation capability of QPath, we evaluate the zero-shot performance of pathology-specific LMMs on WSIs using the SlideBench-VQA (TCGA) benchmark. For WSI processing, each slide is divided into patches at a resolution of 1024p, while the corresponding thumbnail is also resized to 1024p. For LLaVA-Med, Quilt-LLaVA, and PathGen-LLaVA-13B, the thumbnail is used as input, whereas QPath adopts the method described in [Section 2.2.2](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/). In our setting, the budget _B_ is fixed at 32, with _H_, _M_, and _L_ assigned as 4, 8, and 20, respectively. The patches P WSI are then concatenated with the thumbnail to form the visual input. As shown in [Table 2](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/), QPath achieves state-of-the-art zero-shot performance at WSI-level among pathology-specific LMMs, with an overall accuracy of 62.31% and a clinical-task accuracy of 76.24%. The results suggest that QPath achieves strong resolution extrapolation capability and attains robust zero-shot performance on WSI tasks, even without explicit training on WSI datasets.

SECTION 4.

## CONCLUSION

In this work, we introduced QPath, an MLLM framework that incorporates multi-resolution analysis through question-aware patch retrieval. QPath exhibits superior resolution extrapolation capability, establishes state-of-the-art performance on ROI-level VQA benchmarks, and demonstrates strong zero-shot generalization to WSI-level tasks, even without explicit WSI training. These findings highlight the effectiveness of our design and provide a promising direction for future research on MLLMs in computational pathology.
