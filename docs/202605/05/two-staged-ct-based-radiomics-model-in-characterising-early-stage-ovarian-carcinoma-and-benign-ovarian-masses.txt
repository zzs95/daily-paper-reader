Title: Two-staged CT-based radiomics model in characterising early-stage ovarian carcinoma and benign ovarian masses

URL Source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0

Markdown Content:
## Abstract

### Background

Characterisation of CT detected ovarian masses is challenging with overlapping imaging features, unreliable biomarker or clinical presentation. We proposed a two-staged CT-based radiomics model to identify early-stage ovarian carcinoma (ES-OC) and sub-classify different types of benign ovarian masses (BOM).

### Methods

Patients with histologically confirmed BOM or ES-OC (FIGO I-II) were retrospectively recruited from 5 centres. Radiomics features were derived from CT images using PyRadiomics (v3.0.1), which intrinsically resampled volumes to isotropic 1 mm³ voxels. To reduce feature redundancy, features with high correlation (Spearman’s ρ ≥ 0.85) were excluded. Two-staged feature selection was applied. First, elastic-net regression with repeated 5-fold stratified cross-validation (100 iterations) was performed to identify highly repeatable features, followed by Mann-Whitney U testing for statistical significance. Second, Boruta algorithm with Random Forest (RF) estimator was employed over 500 iterations to robustly select features by comparing their importance to randomized shadow features. Several machine learning (ML) classifiers were evaluated using stratified 10‑fold GridSearch cross-validation with area under the curve (AUC) as tuning metric. The optimal model from each stage with highest cross-validated AUC was then evaluated on the respective test set. The AUC, calibration plot, and decision curve analysis (DCA) were employed to assess the performance and clinical utility of models.

### Results

The study enrolled 483 patients with 529 lesions (ES-OC: 192 patients, 192 lesions; BOM: 291 patients, 337 lesions). In the first-stage, logistic regression (LR) algorithm was selected with high sensitivity (0.870), moderate specificity (0.719) and high AUC (0.859) in the test set. In the second-stage, support vector machines (SVM) had high diagnostic accuracy with sensitivity 0.750, specificity 0.839 and AUC 0.918. DCA identified the highest benefit at 0.20 risk threshold probability in determining ES-OC.

### Conclusion

The two-staged CT-based radiomics model incorporating LR and SVM algorithms had high diagnostic efficiency in characterising ES-OC and BOM, potentially in triaging disease and personalising care.

## Introduction

With the extensive use of computed tomography (CT), ovarian masses are frequently identified incidentally on abdominopelvic and pelvic CT that are done for other clinical indications. Characterization of these ovarian masses is challenging, often lacking in clinical symptoms, especially in differentiating between early-stage ovarian carcinoma (ES-OC) and benign ovarian masses (BOM). Furthermore, the imaging features of both entities overlap, resulting in diagnostic dilemma [[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR1 "Elsherif SB, Agely A, Gopireddy DR, Ganeshan D, Hew KE, Sharma S, et al. Mimics and Pitfalls of Primary Ovarian Malignancy Imaging. Tomography. 2022;8(1):100–19.")]. Tumor marker, cancer antigen 125 (CA125) is commonly elevated in OC but is not specific with false-positive rate of 40% in BOM [[2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR2 "Chen DX, Schwartz PE, Li XG, Yang Z. Evaluation of CA 125 levels in differentiating malignant from benign tumors in patients with pelvic masses. Obstet Gynecol. 1988;72(1):23–7.")]. Therefore, relying solely on clinical symptoms, tumor marker and traditional CT imaging features may lack sensitivity and specificity, often resulting in inaccurate tumor characterisation.

BOM comprises many different pathologic entities, including fibroma, endometriotic cysts and cystadenomas, to name a few. Management strategies are varied for different BOM. Ovarian fibroma being the most common benign solid ovarian neoplasm is recommended to be surgically removed [[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR3 "Son CE, Choi JS, Lee JH, Jeon SW, Hong JH, Bae JW. Laparoscopic surgical management and clinical characteristics of ovarian fibromas. Jsls. 2011;15(1):16–20.")]. Endometriotic cysts primarily affect women of reproductive age. Women with endometriotic cysts often experience a decline in ovarian reserve, resulting in female infertility [[4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR4 "Samartzis K, Kathopoulis N, Loutradis D, Protopapas A. Do techniques of surgical management of ovarian endometrioma affect ovarian reserve? A narrative review. J Obstet Gynaecol. 2022;42(5):778–84.")]. Endometriotic cysts can be medically or surgically managed depending on patient’s age [[5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR5 "Mandai M, Suzuki A, Matsumura N, Baba T, Yamaguchi K, Hamanishi J, et al. Clinical Management of Ovarian Endometriotic Cyst (Chocolate Cyst): Diagnosis, Medical Treatment, and Minimally Invasive Surgery. Curr Obstet Gynecol Rep. 2012;1(1):16–24.")]. Ovarian cystadenoma is the most prevalent benign tumor with favourable prognosis [[6](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR6 "Limaiem F, Mlika M. Ovarian Cystadenoma. StatPearls. Treasure Island (FL): StatPearls Publishing Copyright © 2025. StatPearls Publishing LLC.; 2025.")]. Conservative management is recommended for asymptomatic tumor smaller than 5 cm in premenopausal women [[7](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR7 "Lems E, Koch AH, Armbrust S, Leemans JC, Bongers MY, Leon-Castillo A, et al. Do we more often opt for conservative management of ovarian tumors after changing the Dutch national guideline on enlarged ovaries? A nationwide cohort study. Acta Obstet Gynecol Scand. 2024;103(11):2183–92.")]; whereas, unilateral salpingo-oophorectomy or ovarian cystectomy is appropriate in postmenopausal women [[6](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR6 "Limaiem F, Mlika M. Ovarian Cystadenoma. StatPearls. Treasure Island (FL): StatPearls Publishing Copyright © 2025. StatPearls Publishing LLC.; 2025.")]. Therefore, it is desirable to be able to differentiate the different BOM upfront to determine the optimal treatment, particularly for women of reproductive age, aiming to preserve fertility [[8](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR8 "Practice Bulletin No. 174: Evaluation and Management of Adnexal Masses. Obstet Gynecol. 2016;128(5):e210–26.")].

Both Risk of Ovarian Malignancy Algorithm (ROMA) score and International Ovarian Tumor Analysis (IOTA) are able to predict the likelihood of malignancy of ovarian masses but require additional tests, for example CA125 and ultrasound. Similarly, the Ovarian-Adnexal Reporting and Data System (O-RADS) for ultrasound and MRI, although can assign these masses into risk categories, will require additional imaging tests.

Radiomics involve extracting a wide range of quantitative features from medical images. This approach provides a more comprehensive analysis of tumor characteristics compared to traditional imaging assessments. Radiomics features can capture information about tumor shape, size, texture, and intensity, revealing underlying biological and physiological properties of the tissue. Radiomics have the potential to serve as clinical diagnostic tool for patients with ovarian masses, enabling improved lesion stratification and personalizing patient care in the future [[9](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR9 "Adusumilli P, Ravikumar N, Hall G, Swift S, Orsi N, Scarsbrook A. Radiomics in the evaluation of ovarian masses - a systematic review. Insights Imaging. 2023;14(1):165.")]. CT-based radiomics for differentiating benign and malignant lesions have been extensively studied in other tumor types with promising results [[10](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR10 "Liu N, Huang Z, Chen J, Yang Y, Li Z, Liu Y, et al. Radiomics analysis of dual-energy CT-derived iodine maps for differentiating malignant from benign thyroid nodules. Med Phys. 2025;52(2):826–36."),[11](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR11 "Zheng Y, Zhou D, Liu H, Wen M. CT-based radiomics analysis of different machine learning models for differentiating benign and malignant parotid tumors. Eur Radiol. 2022;32(10):6953–64."),[12](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR12 "Zheng YM, Xu WJ, Hao DP, Liu XJ, Gao CP, Tang GZ, et al. A CT-based radiomics nomogram for differentiation of lympho-associated benign and malignant lesions of the parotid gland. Eur Radiol. 2021;31(5):2886–95.")].

In this study, we aimed to establish a two-staged CT-based radiomics approach to differentiate ES-OC from BOM, and to discriminate the different types of BOM.

## Methods

### Patients

This was a retrospective study with patients recruited between February 2009 and October 2019 from 5 different centres. Approvals were obtained from the local institutional review boards (Institutional Review Board of The University of Hong Kong/Hospital Authority Hong Kong West Cluster UW20-251, Hong Kong East Cluster Research Ethics Committee HKECREC-2020-040, Kowloon Central and Kowloon East Research Ethics Committee KC/KE-23-0068/ER-2, Sun Yat-Sen University Cancer Center Institutional Review Board YB2018-52) and informed consent was waived. All patients included in the study met the following inclusion and exclusion criteria. Inclusion criteria: (1) histologically confirmed BOM or ES-OC (FIGO I-II); (2) underwent contrast-enhanced CT examination before surgery or treatment. Exclusion criteria: (1) did not undergo surgery or tissue diagnosis; (2) simple functional ovarian cysts or teratoma; (3) patients with borderline ovarian tumors (4) significant artefacts on CT images precluding radiomics analysis; (5) small lesion with volume less than 1 cm 3.

In the first-stage in differentiating between BOM and ES-OC, the patients from centres A-D were divided into training and internal validation cohorts with an 8:2 stratified split and the patients from centre E acted as test set. In the second-stage for sub-classifying different BOM, the patients with BOM were separated into training and test cohorts with an 8:2 stratified split; and then randomly selected 20% cases from the training cohort acted as internal validation cohort. To prevent data leakage, all data splits (training, internal validation, and test cohorts) were performed at the patient level. For patients with bilateral lesions, each side was treated as a separate case due to tumor heterogeneity; however, both sides were assigned to the same subset to maintain patient-level separation.

### CT data acquisition and tumor segmentation

All patients underwent contrast-enhanced CT (portovenous phase) covering the abdomen and pelvis before surgery. A post-graduate student with prior training in medical image analysis was trained in tumor segmentation and manually segmented all the tumors on CT images. The volume of interest (VOI) was placed to cover the entire lesion using ITK-SNAP software (Version 4.0.1). These VOIs were subsequently verified by board-certified radiologist with more than 15 years of experience in pelvic imaging and special interest in gynae-oncological imaging. Both were blinded to the clinicopathological outcomes.

### Radiomics feature extraction

All CT examinations and their corresponding segmentation masks were processed using the open-source PyRadiomics package (v3.0.1; [http://www.radiomics.io/pyradiomics.html](http://www.radiomics.io/pyradiomics.html)) with a fixed intensity bin width of 25, in strict adherence to the Imaging Biomarker Standardization Initiative guidelines [[13](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR13 "Zwanenburg A, Vallières M, Abdalah MA, Aerts H, Andrearczyk V, Apte A, et al. The Image Biomarker Standardization Initiative: Standardized Quantitative Radiomics for High-Throughput Image-based Phenotyping. Radiology. 2020;295(2):328–38.")]. Image intensities were normalized within the PyRadiomics using YAML configuration to standardize the intensity distribution across scanners. To harmonize spatial resolution across the cohort, volumes were resampled to an isotropic voxel grid of 1 × 1 × 1 mm³ via B-spline interpolation. From each lesion, 1143 quantitative features encompassing shape descriptors (e.g., sphericity, compactness), first-order histogram statistics, and higher-order texture metrics derived from gray-level co-occurrence (GLCM), run-length (GLRLM), size-zone (GLSZM), and neighboring gray-tone difference (NGTDM) matrices were extracted. In addition, wavelet-transformed features were included to capture multiscale heterogeneity within the tumor microenvironment.

### Feature reduction and selection

Feature selection was performed separately for each stage using respective training dataset. To ensure reproducibility and prevent data leakage, all radiomics features were first standardized to zero mean and unit variance using respective training cohort for each stage; the same scaling parameters were subsequently applied to both internal validation and test cohorts. Prior to any selection, features exhibiting high redundancy (Spearman correlation coefficient ≥ 0.85) were excluded hereby reducing multicollinearity in both the BOM versus ES-OC and benign subgroup pipelines.

### Feature selection for the first stage

For the first stage, feature selection was performed applying elastic-net regression with 5-fold stratified cross-validation (SCV), repeated across 100 iterations. Features retained more than 475 times were considered for subsequent analyses. This rigorous process yielded a set of 23 candidate features. For these features, Mann–Whitney U-tests were performed, and the resulting p-values were adjusted for multiple testing using Benjamini–Hochberg procedure, with a q-value threshold of < 0.05 applied to define statistical significance, resulting in 12 retained radiomics predictors for modelling ([Supplementary Table S1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#MOESM1)). A composite radiomics score (RadScore) was calculated for each lesion to evaluate the discriminative capability ([Supplementary Section S1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#MOESM2)).

### Feature selection for the second stage

For the second stage, feature selection was performed using the Boruta algorithm with Random Forest (RF) as estimator over 500 iterations. In each of these iterations, RF computes importance scores for the actual features alongside randomized counterparts that define a baseline importance distribution; only those features whose importance consistently exceeds this baseline were considered as relevant. After all iterations, features confirmed in at least 80% times (≥ 400 of 500) were retained for modelling stage and this resulted in 9 features. We used these features for subsequent predictive model building. Following the selection of these radiomics feature sets, we incorporated patient’s age as an additional variable (feature). This was aimed at assessing whether demographic information could complement the radiomics feature set and enhance predictive capability.

### Model construction and evaluation

Machine learning (ML) classification models were employed to establish two-staged model to differentiate BOM and ES-OC, and then to sub-classify BOM. We also conducted comparative quantitative evaluation of models built with radiomics features against those integrating patient’s age along with radiomics features to estimate any incremental improvement in performance. We investigated different commonly used ML classifiers, including Extreme Gradient boosting (XGBoost), Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM), Categorical Boosting (CatBoost), Logistic Regression (LR), Decision Tree, Random Forest (RF), K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), Gaussian Naive Bayes (GNB), Support Vector Machines (SVM), Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA), AdaBoost, Gradient Boosting, Bagging, and Extra Trees. To ensure model selection process was rigorous and unbiased, we conducted a comprehensive evaluation of each classifier for both stages where each classifier underwent extensive hyperparameter tuning using stratified 10-fold cross-validation with grid search. The efficiency of the models for prediction was calculated utilizing receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves and the top 5 models based on highest mean cross-validated AUC in each stage were selected. The models’ performance was then evaluated on respective internal validation and test cohort for each stage. The predictive performances of the models in differentiating ES-OC from BOM and sub-classifying BOM were evaluated using AUC, accuracy, sensitivity and specificity. Calibration curves were evaluated using the Hosmer–Lemeshow (HL) goodness-of-fit test with a two-sided significance level of 0.05, and Bonferroni correction was applied to account for multiple comparisons to assess the agreement between predicted probabilities and actual outcomes, thereby providing insight into the goodness-of-fit and reliability of the models’ probabilistic predictions. Across both stages, there was no statistically significant evidence of miscalibration. Decision curve analysis (DCA) was performed to evaluate the net benefit by applying the selected model in differentiating ES-OC from BOM using different risk threshold probabilities. Methodological Radiomics Score (METRICS) tool was employed to ensure methodological rigor across the entire radiomics pipeline [[14](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR14 "Kocak B, Akinci D’Antonoli T, Mercaldo N, Alberich-Bayarri A, Baessler B, Ambrosini I, et al. METhodological RadiomICs Score (METRICS): a quality scoring tool for radiomics research endorsed by EuSoMII. Insights Imaging. 2024;15(1):8.")].

All analyses and model development steps were executed within a Python-based environment v3.9.22 with the following key pip dependencies: Matplotlib v3.9.4, scikit-learn v1.6.1, statsmodels v0.14.4, SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) v0.42.1, NumPy v1.26.4, SciPy v1.13.1, and pandas v2.2.3.

## Results

This study enrolled 483 eligible patients, 291 patients (45 ± 15 years old) presented with 337 BOM lesions and 192 patients (51 ± 10 years old) had 192 ES-OC lesions. Tubo-ovarian abscesses (13 patients, 19 lesions) were excluded in the second-stage due to small number, precluding meaningful analysis. Lesions were divided into training cohort (333 lesions in first-stage, 203 lesions in second-stage), internal validation (84 lesions in first-stage, 51 lesions in second-stage) and test cohort (112 lesions in first-stage, 64 lesions in second-stage).

Tree-based ensemble approaches such as RF, Gradient Boosting, Bagging, AdaBoost, Extra Tree classifiers, etc. demonstrated overfitting on training cohort, hence these were excluded from the model selection. Despite thorough hyper parameter tuning and regularizations (depth limits, feature subsampling, shrinkage, class weighting, etc.), these tree-based ensemble classifiers exhibited overfitting on the training cohort. In contrast, LR (stage 1) and SVM (stage 2) offered balanced performance over training and validation phases with substantially fewer effective parameters and lower computational burden.

In the first stage, to ensure ES-OC was predicted correctly, the LR model was selected showing high AUC while maintaining a high sensitivity with moderate specificity in the test cohort (Table[2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#Tab2)). Despite high AUCs in other models, sensitivity rates were low. The AUC analysis of LR model together with DCA and the respective selected cases of ES-OC and BOM are presented in Fig.[1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#Fig1). At threshold probability of 0.20, there was a net benefit of 8% of the LR model over treat none or treat all approach in test cohort. There was a positive net benefit of more than 1% in the test cohort for range of threshold probabilities from 0.11 to 0.36.

The inclusion of patient’s age as predictor alongside radiomics features did not yield any performance benefit. Although age ranked among the top four features by SHAP importance in the test set ([Supplementary Figure S1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#MOESM3)), it indicated a non-trivial association with the outcome. However, exclusion of age from the model did not materially alter discrimination, with training, internal validation, and test AUCs remaining effectively unchanged ([Supplementary Table S3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#MOESM1)). For some classifiers, it even resulted in deterioration of predictive performance. Therefore, given the lack of improvement, we reported performance metrics based exclusively on radiomics features.

We also evaluated the performance of developed pipeline using METRICS score and achieved overall score of 85.7% as illustrated in [Supplementary Table S4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#MOESM1).

## Discussion

In this study, a two-staged CT-based radiomics model was developed to differentiate ES-OC from BOM and discriminate different types of BOM, allowing characterisation of ovarian masses without the need of additional tests, especially when detected incidentally on CT.

CT has become indispensable and forms an integral part of medical diagnosis. Its use has seen exponential increase with 93 million CT examinations performed in the United States in 2023 [[15](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR15 "Smith-Bindman R, Chu PW, Azman Firdaus H, Stewart C, Malekhedayat M, Alber S, et al. Projected Lifetime Cancer Risks From Current Computed Tomography Imaging. JAMA Intern Med. 2025;185(6):710–9.")]. Incidental ovarian mass is prevalent on CT abdomen and pelvic, accounting for 6.6% [[16](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR16 "Boos J, Brook OR, Fang J, Brook A, Levine D. Ovarian Cancer: Prevalence in Incidental Simple Adnexal Cysts Initially Identified in CT Examinations of the Abdomen and Pelvis. Radiology. 2018;286(1):196–204.")]. As characterisation of these ovarian masses is challenging based on traditional CT features with overlapping imaging findings, often this would lead to additional tests, clinic visits and sometimes surgical tissue diagnosis, not to mention the potential psychological impact on patients with uncertain diagnosis. Radiomics for ovarian mass characterization is an emerging field with promising implications for diagnosis and management. Recent studies have demonstrated the value of CT-based radiomics models in the primary task of differentiating BOM from ES-OC [[17](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR17 "Chen J, Liu L, He Z, Su D, Liu C. CT-Based Radiomics and Machine Learning for Differentiating Benign, Borderline, and Early-Stage Malignant Ovarian Tumors. J Imaging Inf Med. 2024;37(1):180–95."),[18](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR18 "Chen J, Yang F, Liu C, Pan X, He Z, Fu D, et al. Diagnostic value of a CT-based radiomics nomogram for discrimination of benign and early stage malignant ovarian tumors. Eur J Med Res. 2023;28(1):609."),[19](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR19 "Jan YT, Tsai PS, Huang WH, Chou LY, Huang SC, Wang JZ, et al. Machine learning combined with radiomics and deep learning features extracted from CT images: a novel AI model to distinguish benign from malignant ovarian tumors. Insights Imaging. 2023;14(1):68.")]. Our first stage model’s performance is relatively similar to previous studies, such as a CT-based nomogram by Chan et al. [[18](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR18 "Chen J, Yang F, Liu C, Pan X, He Z, Fu D, et al. Diagnostic value of a CT-based radiomics nomogram for discrimination of benign and early stage malignant ovarian tumors. Eur J Med Res. 2023;28(1):609.")] and an AI radiomics model by Jan et al. [[19](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR19 "Jan YT, Tsai PS, Huang WH, Chou LY, Huang SC, Wang JZ, et al. Machine learning combined with radiomics and deep learning features extracted from CT images: a novel AI model to distinguish benign from malignant ovarian tumors. Insights Imaging. 2023;14(1):68.")]. Crucially, our model achieved this performance on a more diverse multi-center dataset and a larger cohort number, minimizing the risk of model overfitting to institution-specific imaging protocols or patient populations, thereby yielding a more robust and widely applicable model. Second, and most notably, our work introduced a novel two-staged classification framework. Beyond the initial differentiation of BOM from ES-OC, our model was uniquely designed to perform a second-stage classification to identify specific benign subtypes. This is a critical unmet need, as accurate subtyping of BOM has direct implications for clinical management, potentially avoiding unnecessary surgical interventions. The proposed two-staged model incorporated data extracted from the existing CT could provide additional information on tumor characterisation, enhancing the diagnostic accuracy of radiologists and avoid ambiguity in the radiology reports. To elucidate the translational pathway for our model, we propose a hierarchical diagnostic workflow ([Supplementary Figure S2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#MOESM3)). This paradigm first employs the first-stage model for the initial stratification of an ovarian lesion into benign or malignant categories based on CT images. Lesions classified as benign are then subjected to the second-stage model for further characterization into specific histological subtypes. With increase certainty in the diagnosis in a one-stop assessment, patients could be risk stratified based on the two-staged CT-based radiomics model for more personalised treatment strategy and management.

The two-staged model consisting of LR and SVM algorithms exhibited the best differentiation efficiency. In the first stage, LR was chosen over other models to preserve sensitivity of the prediction in detecting ES-OC as high sensitivity would ensure ES-OC would be captured and not missing the opportunity to be triaged and treated early to improve disease outlook. Understandably, this would sacrifice specificity and result in potentially over-treating BOM. DCA showed that with risk threshold probabilities between 0.11 and 0.36, there was an observable net benefit of the LR model in the test cohort, in line with the risk threshold probabilities used in other study when various clinical risk models were considered [[20](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR20 "Wynants L, Timmerman D, Verbakel JY, Testa A, Savelli L, Fischerova D, et al. Clinical Utility of Risk Models to Refer Patients with Adnexal Masses to Specialized Oncology Care: Multicenter External Validation Using Decision Curve Analysis. Clin Cancer Res. 2017;23(17):5082–90.")]. The highest net benefit was observed at 0.20 risk threshold probability, in concordance with the prevalence of ES-OC [[21](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR21 "United States Cancer Statistics: 1999–2009 Incidence and Mortality Web-based Report [Internet].                   www.cdc.gov/uscs
                  
                ")]. Although the diagnostic accuracy of the current LR algorithm was inferior when compared to existing models or algorithms, like IOTA, ROMA or O-RADS, it was able to utilise pre-existing CT data to predict the nature of ovarian masses without additional tests and costs [[22](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR22 "Fathi A, Heidari M, Rasouli J, Ghasemnejad-Berenji H. Diagnostic value of the Risk of Ovarian Malignancy Algorithm (ROMA) index in the detection of ovarian cancer in postmenopausal women: a systematic review and meta-analysis. BMC Womens Health. 2025;25(1):280."),[23](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR23 "Gareeballah A, Gameraddin M, Alshoabi SA, Alsaedi A, Elzaki M, Alsharif W, et al. The diagnostic performance of International Ovarian Tumor Analysis: Simple Rules for diagnosing ovarian tumors-a systematic review and meta-analysis. Front Oncol. 2024;14:1474930."),[24](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR24 "Zhang Q, Dai X, Li W. Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of O-RADS Ultrasound and O-RADS MRI for Risk Assessment of Ovarian and Adnexal Lesions. AJR Am J Roentgenol. 2023;221(1):21–33.")]. The LR algorithm would not replace the currently used algorithms mentioned above, but would serve as an adjunct tool if these ovarian masses were detected incidentally on CT or if CT data was available.

In the second stage, SVM could differentiate the different types of common BOM. The ability of SVM model to correctly classify BOM would allow individualised patient counselling and patient selection for observation or minimally invasive surgical interventions. We excluded simple functional ovarian cysts and teratoma in the BOM group in this study as these should not pose diagnostic dilemma with their characteristics CT findings [[25](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR25 "Outwater EK, Siegelman ES, Hunt JL. Ovarian teratomas: tumor types and imaging characteristics. Radiographics. 2001;21(2):475–90."), [26](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR26 "Wang PS, Schoeck OG, Horrow MM. Benign-appearing Incidental Adnexal Cysts at US, CT, and MRI: Putting the ACR, O-RADS, and SRU Guidelines All Together. Radiographics. 2022;42(2):609–24.")].

Only minor losses in predictive performance were observed in the test cohorts suggesting that the multi-step ensemble approach used in the feature selection process decreased the risk of overfitting and only included features with salient tumor characteristics. It was noteworthy that all the CT images were obtained from different scanners from 5 centres, exposing the model to variability in data and enhancing its generalizability. The methodological and ethical integrity of our radiomics study, evaluated by METRICS tool, reached an excellent quality category. It showed that this comprehensive process, spanning from data acquisition to model validation, aligned with best practices and ethical standards in radiomics research, delivering a high standard of scientific reliability and transparency.

We acknowledged the various constraints in the current study. Firstly, the number of patients in this multicentre retrospective study was relatively small, warranting the need for an expanded sample size and prospective multicentre recruitment to assess the generalizability of the prediction model, its reproducibility and robustness. Second, the study included histologically confirmed cases and not merely incidental ovarian masses found on CT. Nevertheless, using a highly curated dataset with confirmed pathological diagnosis, this model could serve as an adjunct tool for diagnostic refinement in challenging cases and overcome some of the potential issues encountered by these incidental ovarian masses. Third, the manual tumor segmentation was time-consuming and labour-intensive. Use of deep-learning-based automated tumor segmentation with high precision would be desirable [[27](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13048-026-02110-0#ref-CR27 "Wang Y, Wang M, Cao P, Wong EMF, Ho G, Lam TPW, et al. CT-based deep learning segmentation of ovarian cancer and the stability of the extracted radiomics features. Quant Imaging Med Surg. 2023;13(8):5218–29.")], at the same time reducing the intra-observer and inter-observer variability. Fourth, in this study, certain types of BOM were not considered in the second-stage of the model, as only limited cases were found among the enrolled patients. Fifth, we acknowledge the relatively small sample sizes for certain benign categories and that only internal validation was performed in the second stage. Future studies incorporating larger BOM cohorts and external or prospective validation will be necessary to further establish the generalizability and clinical applicability of this component. Lastly, the radiomics features were not combined with clinical and serum parameters, which could serve as future research direction and may enhance the prediction capability of the combined model.

## Conclusions

The two-staged CT radiomics model incorporating LR and SVM algorithms could differentiate ES-OC from BOM and subsequently sub-classifying BOM with high diagnostic efficiency.
