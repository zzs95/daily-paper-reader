# Auditing Frontier Vision-Language Models for Trustworthy Medical VQA: Grounding Failures, Format Collapse, and Domain Adaptation

## Abstract 

Deploying vision-language models (VLMs) in clinical settings demands auditable behavior un-der realistic failure conditions, yet the failure landscape of frontier VLMs on specialized med-ical inputs is poorly characterized. We au-dit five recent frontier and grounding-aware VLMs (Gemini 2.5 Pro, GPT-5, o3, GLM-4.5V, Qwen 2.5 VL) on Medical VQA along two trust-relevant axes. Perception : all models localize anatomical and pathological targets poorly—the best model reaches only 0.23 mean IoU and 19.1% Acc@0.5—and exhibit clinically dangerous lat-erality confusion. Pipeline integration : a self-grounding pipeline, where the same model local-izes then answers, degrades VQA accuracy for every model—driven by both inaccurate local-ization and format-compliance failures under the two-step prompt (parse failure rises to 70%–99% for Gemini and GPT-5 on VQA-RAD). Replacing predicted boxes with ground-truth annotations re-covers and improves VQA accuracy, consistent with the failure residing in the perception mod-ule rather than in the decomposition itself. These observational findings identify grounding qual-ity as a primary trustworthiness bottleneck in our SLAKE bounding-box setting. As a complemen-tary fine-tuning follow-up, supervised fine-tuning of Qwen 2.5 VL on combined Med-VQA training data attains the highest reported SLAKE open-ended recall (85.5%) among comparable methods, suggesting that the VQA-level gap is tractable with domain adaptation; whether this also closes the perception/trustworthiness bottleneck is left to future work.

## 1. Introduction 

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) are increasingly proposed as clinical decision-support tools for radiology and pathol-ogy, yet deploying them safely requires concrete evidence about where and how they fail—not only aggregate scores on benchmarks. Medical Visual Question Answering (VQA) is a clean test bed for this auditing question: it demands two equally critical skills—high-level reasoning, at which modern LLMs excel, and low-level perception, namely the precise localization of anatomical structures or pathologies in a complex medical image. A failure of perception can render even the most sophisticated reasoning useless, and produces clinically dangerous errors such as laterality con-fusion in chest radiographs. We audit the failure landscape of five frontier VLMs along two trust-relevant axes: (i) the perception axis—can a model find the relevant region in a medical image at all?—and (ii) the pipeline-integration axis—when grounding and an-swering are coupled, does the system degrade gracefully or collapse? We further test whether the medical-VQA gap re-vealed by this audit can be partially closed through targeted supervised fine-tuning of a strong open-source VLM. Our key contributions are: • Perception audit. We construct a dedicated visual grounding benchmark from SLAKE and evaluate five leading VLMs, revealing that all models exhibit remark-ably poor medical image localization (best IoU: 0.23) and systematic laterality confusion—a clinically dangerous failure mode. • Pipeline-integration audit. We demonstrate that self-grounding—where the same model localizes then answers—degrades VQA accuracy for all models, with severity tracking grounding quality and, for Gemini and GPT-5, dominated by format-compliance collapse (70%– 99% parse failure under the two-step prompt). • Oracle-grounding diagnostic. We show through an oracle GT-grounding experiment that accurate grounding 

recovers and improves VQA performance—supporting the interpretation that the failure resides in the perception module rather than the decomposition—and identifying grounding quality as a primary trustworthiness bottleneck 1

## 2. Related Work 

Large Vision-Language Models. Foundational con-trastive models such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and BLIP (Li et al., 2022) established vision–language align-ment; modern frontier VLMs (Gemini (Comanici et al., 2025), the GPT series (OpenAI, 2025; OpenAI et al., 2024), InternVL (Chen et al., 2024)) and strong open-source systems (GLM (GLM-V Team et al., 2025), Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2025), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a)) extend this paradigm with LLM-based reasoning. Their reliability in high-stakes specialized domains such as medicine, however, remains underexplored. 

Medical Visual Question Answering. VQA-RAD (Lau et al., 2018), SLAKE (Liu et al., 2021), and PathVQA (He et al., 2020) are the standard Med-VQA benchmarks; SLAKE in particular provides bounding-box annotations for organs and pathologies useful for grounding (we de-scribe all three in Section 3.2). Concurrent to our work, Liu et al. (Liu et al., 2025) benchmark medical VLMs across eight datasets, separating understanding from reasoning; our study complements theirs by examining the empirical relationship between visual grounding quality and VQA accuracy through a controlled Self- vs. GT-grounding com-parison. 

Medical VLM Fine-Tuning. Because zero-shot gener-alists trail in-domain specialists on medical VQA, sev-eral works adapt VLMs to medicine via supervised fine-tuning. LLaVA-Med (Li et al., 2023) trains LLaVA in two stages using PubMed figure–caption pairs and downstream Med-VQA instruction data. Our fine-tuning study comple-ments this line of work by (i) using a more recent base (Qwen 2.5 VL 7B) and (ii) relying purely on the standard train splits of the three benchmarks as a transparent and reproducible recipe. 

Visual Grounding in VLMs. Specialized models such as Grounding DINO (Liu et al., 2024) and SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) achieve strong open-set localization and segmentation. Some generalist VLMs (e.g., GLM-4.5V) advertise built-in grounding, but their precise-localization ability in special-ized domains like medicine has not been systematically benchmarked against their reasoning performance—the gap our work aims to fill. 

## 3. Experiments 

To audit the failure landscape of current leading Vision-Language Models (VLMs) along the two trust-relevant axes (perception and pipeline integration), we evaluate them on two coupled tasks: a standard Medical VQA assessment (the integration axis) and a precise visual grounding assess-ment (the perception axis). This section details the models, datasets, evaluation metrics, and audit setup used in our study. 

3.1. Models 

We evaluate five recent VLMs spanning proprietary and open-source frontiers: Gemini 2.5 Pro (Comanici et al., 2025), GPT-5 (OpenAI, 2025), o3 (OpenAI, accessed 2026), GLM-4.5V (GLM-V Team et al., 2025), and Qwen 2.5 VL (Bai et al., 2025). Among the five, GLM-4.5V and Qwen 2.5 VL both explicitly advertise built-in grounding/localization capabilities; we therefore highlight them as the grounding-aware frontier models in our audit. 

3.2. Datasets 

We use three standard Med-VQA benchmarks, covering both radiology and pathology. Across all three we follow the common convention of categorizing questions as either 

closed-ended (yes/no or single-word, e.g., “are regions of the brain infarcted?” → “yes”) or open-ended (descriptive, e.g., “what abnormality is seen?” → “blind-ending loop of bowel arising from the cecum”). 

VQA-RAD (Lau et al., 2018) contains 315 radiology im-ages and 3,515 clinician-curated QA pairs. We evaluate on the 451-QA test split following the convention adopted by LLaVA-Med (Li et al., 2023). VQA-RAD does not pro-vide bounding-box annotations, so it is excluded from the SLAKE-derived grounding benchmark (Table 2) and from the GT-Grounding VQA condition; it is, however, included in Direct VQA and Self-Grounding VQA, since those do not require bounding-box ground truth. 

SLAKE (Liu et al., 2021) is a semantically labeled, knowledge-enhanced radiology dataset with 642 images, over 14,000 QA pairs, and rich spatial annotations (segmen-tation masks and bounding boxes for organs and patholo-gies). We use SLAKE twice : (i) its 1,061-QA test split for VQA evaluation, and (ii) a grounding split that we program-matically derive from SLAKE’s bounding-box annotations by generating an explicit grounding query (e.g., “Locate the liver.” or “Locate the lung cancer.”) for each annotated target, yielding 418 image–query–bbox triples that span both anatomical structures (e.g., liver, kidneys, spleen) and 2Auditing Frontier VLMs for Trustworthy Medical VQA

PathVQA (He et al., 2020) is a pathology Med-VQA dataset derived from medical textbooks, containing 4,998 images and 32,799 QA pairs. We evaluate on its 6,761-QA test split. PathVQA does not provide bounding-box annotations and is used only for the VQA task. 

3.3. Evaluation Metrics VQA. We report closed-ended accuracy and open-ended recall. Ground-truth answers are routed into the two types by a keyword vocabulary, then matched with a rule-based pipeline tailored to medical language: closed-ended answers are first screened for direct contradictions (e.g., “left” vs. “right”) against a curated dictionary, then matched through a synonym map; open-ended answers use synonym-expanded token overlap with a dynamic threshold (50% for answers 

≤3 words, 30% otherwise) and a laterality contradiction penalty. Because this matching is heuristic rather than human-validated, we discuss its limitations in Section 5. 

Grounding. We report the mean Intersection over Union (IoU), the Dice coefficient, and Accuracy@0.5 (the fraction of predictions with IoU ≥0.5) between the predicted and ground-truth bounding boxes. 

3.4. Experimental Setup 

Our evaluation methodology is centered around a two-step pipeline (Figure 1), applied consistently across all models. Proprietary models are accessed via their official APIs; open-source models are run in a controlled local environment. 1. Step 1 – Grounding. The model is prompted as an “expert radiologist AI” to return the bounding box of the object referenced in the question, in a strict [x1, y1, x2, y2] format, with the original image width and height included in the prompt. A regular expression extracts the first tuple from the response; unparseable outputs are assigned a sentinel box [0 , 0, 0, 0] and kept in the final score so that reported numbers reflect real-deployment robustness. 2. Step 2 – Question Answering. The model, re-prompted 3Auditing Frontier VLMs for Trustworthy Medical VQA 

as a “clinical Q&A assistant,” answers the original ques-tion using the visual input from Step 1: either the cropped region (if grounding was used) or the full image (otherwise). The prompt asks for brief answers to closed questions and descriptive answers to open ones. Prompt, parsing, and scoring are identical across all models, where applicable to each condition. 

3.5. Supervised Fine-Tuning Setup 

To test whether VQA performance can be substantially raised by domain-specific supervised fine-tuning, we fully fine-tune Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct on the combined train splits of VQA-RAD, SLAKE, and PathVQA, with no exter-nal instruction data. We train all parameters (vision encoder and language model) for 3 epochs with effective batch size 32 (per-device batch 4, gradient accumulation 8), AdamW with learning rate 1e-5 , cosine decay, and warmup ratio 0.03. Training runs on a single node with 8× NVIDIA A100 80GB , taking approximately 8 hours. We evaluate the fine-tuned model on the same test splits used for zero-shot evaluation and report closed-ended accuracy and open-ended recall. 

## 4. Comparative Analysis of Model Performance 

4.1. Zero-shot Evaluation of Open-Source and Proprietary VLMs 

As a baseline reference for the audit, Table 1 reports zero-shot Medical VQA performance across VQA-RAD, SLAKE, and PathVQA, situating the five audited frontier VLMs against open-source and supervised baselines from the literature. These numbers establish the integration-axis baseline against which self-grounding and GT-grounding behaviors (Section 4.3) will be compared. Two patterns emerge. (i) Even the strongest zero-shot VLMs trail supervised specialists—LLaVA-Med (84.19% VQA-RAD closed), BiomedCLIP (89.70% SLAKE closed), Q2ATransformer (79.19% VQA-RAD open), Prefix Tun-ing Medical LM (84.30% SLAKE open)—confirming the persistent gap to in-domain supervision. (ii) Among our zero-shot frontier models, Qwen 2.5 VL is clearly strongest (51.2/66.9% SLAKE open/closed) and Gemini 2.5 Pro the weakest (23.5/39.0%). 

4.2. Grounding Accuracy of VLMs 

To evaluate the perceptual capabilities of current VLMs, we benchmarked all five models on our SLAKE-derived ground-ing split. Table 2 reports the mean IoU, Dice coefficient, and Accuracy@0.5 for each model. All evaluated VLMs exhibit a stark perceptual gap. Even the best model, Qwen 2.5 VL, reaches only 0.23 mean IoU and 19.1% Acc@0.5—fewer than one in five predicted boxes overlap the ground truth by at least half. GPT-5 and o3 attain near-identical IoU 0.16 (Acc@0.5 7.9–9.1%); Gem-ini 2.5 Pro trails at IoU 0.09. Notably, GLM-4.5V—one of the two models in our audit that explicitly advertise built-in grounding—underperforms GPT-5 and o3 (IoU 0.13), indicating that general-purpose grounding features do not transfer to the fine-grained spatial demands of medical imag-ing. Figure 2 shows the same pattern qualitatively: small targets like lung cancer lesions yield IoU 0, while larger structures like cardiomegaly only get partial overlap with oversized boxes. We observe two systematic failure modes across models. 

(i) Laterality confusion : models frequently confuse left and right in chest radiographs (which are conventionally mirrored), producing bounding boxes on the anatomically incorrect side—a clinically dangerous error. (ii) Scale mis-match : for small targets such as lesions or nodules, models default to large bounding boxes encompassing the entire organ or image quadrant, suggesting an inability to perform fine-grained localization at the pathology level. As Fig-ure 2 (top row) shows, all models fail completely on the small “Lung Cancer” lesion. These patterns indicate that the grounding gap reflects fundamental limitations in spa-tial reasoning when models must produce precise numeric coordinates rather than natural language. 

Format compliance is not the grounding-task bottleneck. 

One might worry that low IoU merely reflects failure to emit a parseable bounding box. On the dedicated grounding task only Gemini 2.5 Pro shows substantial parse failure (61/418 samples, 14.6%); GLM-4.5V, GPT-5, and Qwen 2.5 VL achieve 0% and o3 only 0.5%. Restricting Gemini to parseable outputs barely shifts its IoU (0.09 →0.11), so the reported scores reflect genuine localization errors rather than formatting artifacts.

4.4. Closing the Gap via Fine-Tuning 

As a secondary follow-up to the audit—and to test whether the medical-VQA gap identified above is at all tractable through domain adaptation—we fine-tune Qwen 2.5 VL 7B on the combined train splits of VQA-RAD, SLAKE, and PathVQA (protocol in Section 3.5). The SFT block of Ta-ble 1 shows that the fine-tuned model attains the highest SLAKE open-ended recall in Table 1 ( 85.5% , vs. 84.30% from Prefix Tuning Medical LM (van Sonsbeek et al., 2023)) and matches or exceeds LLaVA-Med-reported numbers on four of six dataset–metric combinations (VQA-RAD Open +4.4 pp, SLAKE Open +2.4 pp, SLAKE Closed +1.1 pp, PathVQA Open +2.7 pp), remaining within 1 pp on the other two (VQA-RAD Closed −1.0 pp, PathVQA Closed 

−0.9 pp). Cross-paper comparisons should be interpreted with caution as evaluator harnesses may differ; the within-harness gain over zero-shot Qwen 2.5 VL is, however, large and unambiguous (e.g., SLAKE open-ended recall improves from 51.2% to 85.5%, +34 .3 pp). Crucially, these are VQA-level improvements: we evaluate the SFT model only on VQA metrics, so this result shows the medical-VQA gap is tractable but does not establish that SFT specifically repairs the perception/grounding bottleneck identified by the audit. Whether domain adaptation closes the trustworthiness gap (or only the answer-prior gap) is left to future work. 

## 5. Limitations 

We note five limitations. Evaluation methodology. Our VQA scoring relies on heuristic rules (synonym matching, contradiction detection, dynamic thresholds) that have not been validated against human judgment or reference-based metrics such as BERTScore or RadGraph F1, and may be sensitive to prompt style and answer verbosity; this caveat applies to both our zero-shot and SFT numbers. Grounding task design. We use SLAKE bounding boxes (organs and discrete pathological findings) with template queries, which does not capture diffuse pathologies, irregular lesion shapes, or ambiguous boundaries where masks or point ground-ing would be more appropriate. Dataset scope. Zero-shot and grounding evaluation cover VQA-RAD, SLAKE, and PathVQA (with grounding restricted to SLAKE); generaliza-tion to other modalities (dermatology, ophthalmology) and larger-scale benchmarks remains unverified. Fine-tuning scope. Our SFT study is confined to a single base model (Qwen 2.5 VL 7B) and a single configuration (full fine-tuning, three datasets’ train splits, 3 epochs); we do not ablate PEFT methods (LoRA/QLoRA), data-mix composi-tions, or training duration, which limits attribution of the observed gains to specific design choices. We also eval-uate the SFT model only on VQA metrics, so we cannot disentangle whether the gains reflect improved perception, better answer priors, or both. Causal interpretation. The IoU-vs-VQA pattern is observational across five VLMs and partly confounded by format-compliance failure (notably 70.3% parse failure for GPT-5 and 98.9% for Gemini on VQA-RAD self-grounding); we therefore frame “grounding quality is the bottleneck” as a hypothesis well supported by our data rather than a tested causal claim. 

## 6. Conclusion and Future Work 

In this work, we presented a diagnostic benchmark that sep-arately evaluates the visual question answering (reasoning) and visual grounding (perception) capabilities of five vision-language models on medical imaging data. By constructing a dedicated grounding evaluation split from the SLAKE dataset and comparing direct VQA, self-grounding, and GT-grounding pipelines, we provide empirical evidence on the 7Auditing Frontier VLMs for Trustworthy Medical VQA 

relationship between perception and reasoning in medical VQA. Our results reveal four key findings. First, all evaluated VLMs—including those with explicitly advertised ground-ing features—exhibit remarkably poor localization in the medical domain, with the best model (Qwen 2.5 VL) achieving only 0.23 mean IoU and 19.1% Acc@0.5 on our SLAKE-derived grounding split. Second, self-grounding degrades VQA accuracy for all models, with severity track-ing grounding quality (and, for some models, format-compliance failure under the two-step prompt). Third, re-placing predicted bounding boxes with ground-truth annota-tions recovers and improves VQA accuracy beyond direct VQA baselines, indicating that grounding is genuinely ben-eficial when accurate. Fourth, as a secondary follow-up, supervised fine-tuning of Qwen 2.5 VL 7B on the com-bined train splits attains the highest SLAKE open-ended recall in Table 1 (85.5%) and matches or exceeds LLaVA-Med-reported numbers on four of six dataset–metric com-binations while remaining within ∼1 pp on the other two, showing that the VQA-level gap is tractable via domain adaptation; whether this closes the trust-relevant perception bottleneck is left to future work, as we evaluate only VQA metrics on the SFT model. These results point to visual grounding quality as a primary bottleneck in the SLAKE bounding-box setting we evaluate. The grounding-enhanced pipeline is not inherently flawed; rather, current VLMs lack the perceptual precision needed for grounding to serve as a reliable intermediate step in this setting. Future work should prioritize three directions. First, hybrid architectures that decouple perception from reasoning— using specialized, domain-adapted grounding modules (e.g., medically fine-tuned variants of Grounding DINO (Liu et al., 2024) or SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023)) to provide reliable spatial inputs to VLM reasoning—should be evaluated as a practical path to grounding-enhanced medical VQA. Sec-ond, fine-grained failure analysis , stratified by organ type, lesion scale, imaging modality, and anatomical laterality, is needed to build a systematic taxonomy of grounding fail-ures and guide targeted improvements. Third, our evaluation framework should be extended with human validation of the automatic VQA metrics, segmentation-level ground-ing evaluation, and broader dataset coverage to establish a robust, community-reusable benchmark. Finally, recent ad-vances in test-time reinforcement learning for VLMs (Singh et al., 2025) suggest that inference-time adaptation could lift VLM behavior on specialized tasks without additional training data, and exploring whether such methods transfer to medical perception/grounding is a promising direction.
