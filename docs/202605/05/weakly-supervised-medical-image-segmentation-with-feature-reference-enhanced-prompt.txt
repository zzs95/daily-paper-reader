## Abstract:

Weakly supervised learning often relies on pseudo-labeling techniques to provide supervision, but the generated pseudo labels are inherently noisy and unreliable. Although the Segment Anything Model (SAM) is capable of producing high-quality pseudo labels, most existing studies primarily focus on its fine-tuning paradigms, while its core prompt-driven mechanism remains underexplored in the context of weakly supervised learning. To fully exploit the prompt capability of SAM, we propose a Feature Reference Enhanced Prompt strategy that integrates multi-granularity prompts to generate reliable pseudo labels. Specifically, we design a Feature Reference Prompt Adapter that extracts salient target information as references to enhance image features. We introduce learnable queries to aggregate dense prompts from these references. These learned dense prompts, together with sparse prompts from scribbles, collaborate to generate high-quality pseudo labels, facilitating multi-granularity representations via attention mechanism. Moreover, a Bilateral Fusion Module is employed to align global and local features, thereby reinforcing cross-branch representational consistency. Extensive experiments on two public datasets demonstrate that our model outperforms state-of-the-art (SOTA) scribble-supervised methods.

SECTION 1.

## INTRODUCTION

Weakly supervised medical image segmentation has emerged as a prominent research area, as it allows accurate segmentation of anatomical structures with only a limited amount of labeled data, thereby significantly alleviating the manual labeling burden. A common strategy involves generating pseudo labels [1] [2] [3] to expand sparse annotations, such as scribbles, and guide model training.

However, the effectiveness of these methods largely depends on the quality of the generated pseudo labels. In practice, model-generated pseudo labels inevitably contain noise, which necessitates complex constraints or regularization strategies to ensure reliable supervision [4] [5].

The Segment Anything Model (SAM) [6], which has been pretrained on large-scale natural image datasets, can provide more reliable pseudo labels due to its powerful generalization ability. Distinguished by its interactive design, SAM enables segmentation based on sparse prompts, such as points or bounding boxes, as well as dense prompts like coarse masks [7]. This prompt-driven mechanism offers a natural and efficient way to support weakly supervised segmentation and pseudo-label generation.

In spite of this, current weakly supervised approaches have not fully exploited the potential of the prompt mechanism. A recent work [8] feeds scribbles as prompts for SAM to obtain coarse masks, followed by a series of complicated post-processing steps to refine their quality. However, such a strategy suffers from false positives in pseudo masks and strong sensitivity to hyperparameters, resulting in heavy dependence on pseudo-label quality and limited generalization. Another effort attempts to exploit prompts in a more sophisticated manner. For instance, CPC-SAM [9] adopts a cross-prompting strategy that generates fixed sparse point prompts from predicted feature maps. Consequently, it struggles to capture fine-grained object details because of the lack of dense prompt information.

To fully exploit dense prompt information and generate reliable pseudo labels under sparse scribble annotations, we propose a Feature Reference Prompt Adapter (FRP-Adapter) integrated into a dual-branch SAM framework. Specifically, the FRP-Adapter extracts salient information from target objects as references to enhance image feature representations. Furthermore, a set of learnable tokens is introduced to effectively fuse convolutional and attentive features, enabling the construction of robust dense image representations. By incorporating prompt learning, we integrate dense image features with sparse features guided by interactive inputs to generate high-quality pseudo labels. This design enhances multi-granularity representations of target regions and facilitates fine-grained segmentation performance. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows: (1) We propose a weakly supervised segmentation model which utilizes Feature Reference Enhanced Prompt (FREP) strategy by constructing image features as dense prompts to enhance pseudo-label reliability and alleviate the annotation burden. (2) We design a FRP-Adapter to extract reference features and employ prompt learning to fuse dense prompts from reference features and sparse prompts from scribbles, thereby enabling multi-granularity representations and improving fine-grained segmentation accuracy. (3) We introduce a Bilateral Fusion Module (BFM) to align global features with local features, reinforcing cross-branch representational consistency. By incorporating consistency learning with pseudo labels, we achieve SOTA performance on ACDC and MSCMR datasets.

SECTION 2.

## METHOD

### 2.1. Overview

[Fig. 1](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/) shows our FREP framework designed for weakly supervised segmentation. SAM consists of three main components: an image encoder, a prompt encoder, and a mask decoder. The image encoder is ViT-B fine-tuned using Low Rank Adaptation (LoRA) to extract task-adaptive image embeddings. The prompt encoder embeds sparse prompts from scribbles and aligns them with the image features. The mask decoder employs two-way cross-attention to generate the target segmentation mask. We design a FRP-Adapter to extract spatial and channel reference features and then employ an attention enhancement mechanism to generate dense prompts. By refining the mask decoder at both sparse and dense levels, it produces high-quality pseudo-labels. We employ a lightweight U-Net as an auxiliary branch to provide complementary segmentation cues. Moreover, in the encoding stage, the BFM is introduced to couple global and local features to reinforce cross-branch representational consistency.

### 2.2. Feature Reference Prompt Encoder

We propose a training-efficient FRP-Adapter, composed of a Reference Feature Extractor (RFE) and a Prompt Adapter (PA). The RFE employs channel and spatial attention mechanisms to enhance features from different branches, producing abundant reference representations. RFE takes two feature maps as inputs: semantic representations _F trans_ from SAM encoder and structural features _F cnn_ from the U-Net encoder. Channel attention evaluates the importance of each channel by applying average pooling on _F trans_, followed by an MLP to generate the channel attention map _M c_ ∈ **R**_C_×1×1. Spatial attention focuses on capturing the importance of spatial locations within the structural features. We use a 1 × 1 convolutional layer on _F cnn_ to generate the spatial attention map _M s_ ∈ **R**1×_H_×_W_. An element-wise selective mask is fused by 
M=σ(M c+M s)M∈R C×H×W(1)

⊙ denotes element-wise multiplication. By dynamically allocating the contributions across spatial and channel dimensions, this strategy enables the extraction of reference features while achieving complementary feature integration.

Subsequently, the Prompt Adapter leverages the reference features to construct category-specific dense prompt embeddings. Specifically, a set of learnable queries _Q_ is introduced to interact with reference features through cross-attention and self-attention operations.

In the first stage, queries interact with _F c_, enriching the queries with semantic context from transformer features. 
Q c=Self−A t t n 1(Cross−A t t n 1(Q,F c))(3)

In the second stage, queries attend to _F s_ to capture fine-grained spatial cues and structural patterns inherent in convolutional features. 
Q s=Self−A t t n 2(Cross−A t t n 2(Q c,F s))(4)

This two-step interaction progressively enriches the queries with referenced semantic and spatial information. A convolutional projection then transforms the queries _Q s_ into dense mask prompts _P d_.

Our prompt encoder transforms the dense prompts _P d_ and sparse point prompts _P s_ derived from scribble annotations into a unified embedding space. By encoding these multi-granularity prompts into complementary representations, we can effectively bridge the gap between coarse scribbles and fine-grained target boundaries. These integrated embeddings are subsequently fed into the SAM decoder, alongside the image features, to generate high-quality pseudo labels _y pl_.

### 2.3. Cross-branch Consistency Supervision

Consistency learning has been widely adopted in semi-supervised and weakly supervised segmentation, aiming to enforce consistent predictions across models or input perturbations. Between the dual-branch encoders, a Bilateral Fusion Module [4] that aligns global and local features is introduced to reinforce cross-branch representational consistency.

To strengthen feature alignment in the dual-branch architecture, we employ consistency learning, where pseudo labels _y pl_ serve as weak supervision to guide a cross-branch consistency loss. We apply the partial cross-entropy function and dice function for both supervised learning. The pseudo label consistency loss is formulated as a weighted combination of dice loss and cross-entropy loss: 
L c(y,y p l)=L d i c e(y,y p l)+0.5⋅L c e(y,y p l)(5)

Our training objective combines supervised loss with consistency loss to effectively exploit weak annotations while enforcing cross-branch consistency. The loss for scribble supervision is formulated as: 
L s(y,s)=0.8⋅L d i c e(y,s)+0.2⋅L c e(y,s)(6)

Finally, the training objective _L_ combines the losses of the dual-branch outputs (_y_ 1, _y_ 2) and the annotations. Here _y_ 1 and _y_ 2 are the mask predictions from SAM decoder and U-Net decoder without prompt guidance, and the annotations include both pseudo labels _y pl_ and scribble annotations _s_. 
L=λ 1⋅(L s(y 1,s)+L s(y 2,s))+λ 2⋅(L c(y 1,y p l)+L c(y 2,y p l))(7)

SECTION 3.

## EXPERIMENTS

### 3.1. Implementation Details and Datasets

We trained the model using the AdamW optimizer for 1000 epochs, with a learning rate of 0.001 and a warm-up period of 2000 iterations. Our experiments strictly follow the data splits: 70/15/15 scans for training, validation, and testing on the ACDC scribble annotation dataset [10] and 25/5/15 scans on the MSCMR scribble annotation dataset [11]. The input images are resized to 256×256, and the batch size is set to 12 for both datasets. During evaluation stage, Dice coefficient (Dice) and 95% Hausdorff Distance (HD95) are used to evaluate the predictions generated by the mask decoder.

SECTION 4.

## CONCLUSION

We propose a weakly supervised segmentation model with feature reference enhanced prompt strategy. Our method extracts multi-granularity features from scribble labels and images as prompts, leveraging prompt learning to enhance the representation of target segmentation regions. Experiments on two benchmarks show that our method surpasses scribble-supervised and SAM-based methods while significantly reducing annotation costs.
