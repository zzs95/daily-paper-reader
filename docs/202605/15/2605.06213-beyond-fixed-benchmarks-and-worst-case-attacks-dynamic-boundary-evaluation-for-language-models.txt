## Abstract 

Evaluating large language models (LLMs) today rests on fixed benchmarks that apply the same set of items to any model, producing ceiling and floor effects that mask capability gaps. We argue that the most informative evaluation signal lies at the boundary , where the per-prompt pass probability is near 0.5 under random-sampling decoding strategy, and propose Dynamic Boundary Evaluation (DBE) , which actively locates each model’s boundary and places it on a globally comparable difficulty scale. DBE delivers three artifacts: (i) a calibrated item bank covering safety, capability, and truthfulness, with per-item difficulty labels validated across 9 reference LLMs; (ii) Skill-Guided Boundary Search (SGBS), a search algorithm that finds boundary items for a given target LLM using only API-level query access; and (iii) an evaluation protocol that places a new LLM on a unified ability scale and grows the evaluation set adaptively when the target falls outside the bank’s coverage. We instantiate DBE on four categories spanning safety (harmful request refusal, over-refusal), capability (constrained instruction following), and truthfulness (multi-turn sycophancy resistance). The resulting evaluation covers a broader model spectrum without saturation while remaining compatible with existing datasets. 

## 1 Introduction 

Fixed benchmarks , such as JailbreakBench [Chao et al., 2024] and MMLU [Hendrycks et al., 2021a], dominate large language model (LLM) evaluation, and all share one design: apply the same set of items to any model. When a benchmark is easier than the model’s capability, pass rate saturates near 1 (ceiling effect ); when it is harder, pass rate floors near 0. In both regimes, models with substantially different capabilities produce indistinguishable numbers. For instance, on the standard GPQA benchmark [Rein et al., 2023], high-performing models such as Claude Opus and Qwen Plus achieve nearly indistinguishable, saturated scores (91.3% vs. 90.4%) [BenchLM.ai, 2026a]. Yet, when evaluated on the more challenging SuperGPQA [Du et al., 2025], a capability gap of over 20% emerges between the exact same models [BenchLM.ai, 2026b]. This phenomenon, where fixed benchmarks rapidly lose their ability to provide meaningful signals, is widespread, driving the continuous transition from original benchmarks to harder variants (e.g., from MMLU [Hendrycks et al., 2020] to MMLU-Pro [Wang et al., 2024a], and MMMU [Yue et al., 2024] to MMMU-Pro [Yue et al., 2025]) simply to restore discriminative power among state-of-the-art systems.

We argue that the most useful evaluation signal comes from the boundary : prompts on which the target model’s pass probability is close to 0.5 under random-sampling decoding strategy. Prompts that are almost always passed or almost always failed mainly tell us that the model is capable of answering those particular items; they provide limited information about the precise location of the model’s capability. Boundary items, in contrast, are matched to the target model’s current ability and are therefore most informative for localizing the model on a difficulty scale. This view changes the goal of evaluation from measuring pass rate on a fixed item set to estimating where each model lies on a shared difficulty axis. A principled boundary-aware evaluation therefore requires three ingredients: (i) a way to localize each target model’s boundary, (ii) a shared difficulty scale for comparing boundary locations across models, and (iii) a category-agnostic item format so the same machinery can be applied across safety, capability, and truthfulness categories. None of the existing evaluation protocols address these requirements all at once. Fixed benchmarks provide a common item set for comparison, but they do not adapt to the boundary of a particular target model. Adversarial attacks and dynamic red-teaming methods, such as Rainbow Teaming [Samvelyan et al., 2024] and h4rm3l [Doumbouya et al., 2025], search for failures rather than boundary items; their outputs are often target-specific, difficult to place on a shared scale, and tied to domain-specific failure modes. Item-response theory (IRT), especially the Rasch model [Rasch, 1993], provides a shared difficulty scale for models and questions through the specific objectivity property. Recent IRT-for-LLM work has applied this idea to static benchmark subset selection [Polo et al., 2024, Kipnis et al., 2025], per-model adaptive item selection [Li et al., 2025, Hofmann et al., 2025], and difficulty-conditioned generation [Truong et al., 2025]. However, these methods typically require either a pre-calibrated item pool or a large response corpus, e.g., Hofmann et al. [2025] uses 102 

LMs from the Open LLM Leaderboard, and often focus on a single general-capability dimension. This makes them difficult to deploy in categories where no calibrated pool exists, such as multi-turn sycophancy resistance or over-refusal. 

Our approach. We propose Dynamic Boundary Evaluation (DBE) , a framework that turns boundary-aware evaluation into a calibrated measurement procedure. Instead of relying on a fixed item set for every model, DBE first builds a reusable Rasch difficulty scale from a reference panel, then evaluates each new model on this scale, and expands the scale only when the model falls outside the covered range. We instantiate DBE on four categories: harmful request refusal, over-refusal [Röttger et al., 2024, Cui et al., 2024], constrained instruction following, and multi-turn sycophancy resistance [Sharma et al., 2023]. DBE consists of three components: 1. A calibrated anchor bank. For each category, DBE constructs an anchor bank whose item difficulties are calibrated from responses of 9 reference LLMs. The resulting item difficulties define a category-specific Rasch logit scale and are held fixed when evaluating new models. This gives a reusable evaluation resource rather than a per-target benchmark. 22. A Skill-Guided Boundary Search (SGBS) Algorithm. Given a target LLM, SGBS searches the compositional item space by combining bare requests with difficulty-increasing skills. Its objective is not to find maximally failing prompts, but to find boundary items whose pass probability is close to 0.5. SGBS requires only API-level access to the target model and uses the same high-level procedure across all four categories. 3. An adaptive evaluation protocol. When the anchor bank provides sufficient coverage around a target model’s ability, DBE estimates the model’s ability from anchor items alone. When the target falls outside the anchor bank’s covered range, DBE invokes SGBS to generate new boundary items and calibrates them onto the existing scale using the reference panel. In this way, the evaluation set grows only when needed, extending the difficulty scale rather than reporting saturated anchor responses. 

## 2 Background and Related Work 

2.1 Item Response Theory 

Item Response Theory (IRT) assigns each model m a latent ability θm and each item x a latent difficulty βx. Under the 1-parameter logistic Rasch model, 

Pr(pass | m, x ) = σ(θm − βx),

so performance depends only on the difference between model ability and item difficulty. The Rasch model also provides specific objectivity [Rasch, 1993]: item difficulties are comparable across suitable respondent sets up to a constant shift, enabling shared-scale model comparison. We use 1PL because this property is weakened in more parameterized 2PL/3PL models; held-out comparisons with 2PL and 3PL are reported in Appendix D.4. IRT was introduced to NLP evaluation by Lalor et al. [2016]. 

2.2 Closely related benchmarks and evaluation methods Adversarial attack methods. Adversarial methods maximize attack success rate (ASR) on in-dividual targets, spanning gradient-guided suffix search [Zou et al., 2023], attacker-LLM iterative refinement [Chao et al., 2023], and quality-diversity coverage of failure modes [Samvelyan et al., 2024]. The closest analog to SGBS is h4rm3l [Doumbouya et al., 2025], which uses a bandit to com-pose jailbreak primitives; SGBS shares this compositional search pattern but replaces the max-ASR objective with the boundary-seeking reward of §3.3 and extends beyond safety. 

IRT-based LLM evaluation. tinyBenchmarks [Polo et al., 2024] and metabench [Kipnis et al., 2025] use IRT for static subset selection, compressing benchmarks by 100 × or more. PSN-IRT [Zhou et al., 2026] fits rich item parameterizations to diagnose benchmark quality. ATLAS [Li et al., 2025] and Fluid Benchmarking [Hofmann et al., 2025] perform per-model adaptive testing via Fisher-information-greedy selection from pre-calibrated pools; Fluid Benchmarking calibrates a 2PL model on 102 Open-LLM-Leaderboard models, achieving 50 × item reduction on MMLU. Habba et al. [2026] use multidimensional IRT with fixed anchor parameters so new datasets/models can be added without recalibration—a direct precursor to our Claim 3. Closest in spirit, Truong et al. [2025] combine IRT with active generation, training a neural generator conditioned on target difficulty. DBE differs from these works along the three design choices in §1. 

## 3 The Pipeline of Dynamic Boundary Evaluation (DBE) 

At a high level, DBE can be viewed as a procedure for building, calibrating, and extending a measurement instrument: a difficulty “ruler” on which both items and models are positioned. The Rasch model (§2.1) defines the ruler as a single logit axis where item difficulty βx and model ability θm are directly comparable. Under the specific objectivity property of the Rasch model, item difficulties are stable up to a global translation across suitable respondent panels. The anchor set (§3.2) instantiates this ruler. It is a curated bank of items whose difficulties {βx} are jointly estimated on a fixed reference panel, so that a new model can be placed on the same scale by estimating only its ability θ. SGBS (§3.3) extends the ruler when it is too short: if a target model lies outside the panel-covered range, few or no anchor items fall near its boundary, where p ≈ 0.5, and 3the local resolution of the anchor set degenerates. SGBS therefore generates new items at the target model’s boundary; these items are then calibrated onto the existing scale through the reference panel. At evaluation time, however, the components are invoked adaptively: the protocol first scores the target model using the fixed anchor set, and invokes SGBS only as a fallback when the anchor set no longer provides sufficient boundary coverage (§3.4).

Assumptions. The anchor set is intended to localize target models whose abilities lie within the range covered by the reference panel. This is the only structural coverage assumption :(A1) The panel θ-range covers the ability range of the target LLM population. The filtering step additionally relies on the following operational assumption :(A2) Items with panel-mean pass rate ¯px ≈ 0 or ¯px ≈ 1 carry negligible per-cell Fisher informa-tion at panel ability and can be removed without changing in-panel evaluation outcomes, such as model rankings and the point estimates of θ and β, beyond tolerance. 5A2 is validated by the four-axis ablation in Appendices C.1 and C.2. A1, however, is not guaranteed for future models. When a target model falls outside the panel-covered range, the anchor set may contain too few items near that model’s boundary, and ability estimation becomes poorly localized. This is precisely the role of SGBS: rather than requiring the anchor set to cover all future models in advance, the adaptive evaluation protocol invokes SGBS on demand to generate new boundary items and extend the calibrated scale (§3.3; §3.4). 

3.3 Skill-Guided Boundary Search 

Given a target model m and an inference budget B, Skill-Guided Boundary Search (SGBS) searches the compositional item space for prompts on which m is close to its decision boundary, i.e., prompts whose pass probability is near 0.5. Each item is represented as x = Compose( q, s), where q ∈ Q is a bare request and s ⊆ S is a subset of skills. SGBS treats boundary discovery as a bandit problem over three coupled choices: which bare request to try, how many skills to apply, and which compatible skills to compose. In each round, SGBS first selects a bare request q by Thompson sampling. It then samples a skill count k ∈ { 0, . . . , k max } from the current skill-count distribution and selects a compatible skill subset 

s ⊆ S with |s| = k. A category-specific composer produces the candidate item x = Compose( q, s).If the pair (q, s) has already been attempted, the round is skipped. Otherwise, SGBS evaluates x on the target model m and obtains a pass-rate estimate ˆp. The item is assigned the boundary reward 

r(ˆ p) = 1 − 2|ˆp − 0.5|,

which is maximized at ˆp = 0 .5 and decreases toward zero as item becomes saturated. If ˆp lies in the boundary band, the item is added to the discovered boundary set E. The observed reward is then used to update the bare-request, skill-count, and skill-selection statistics. The key components of SGBS are as follows. Bare requests are selected by Thompson sampling, which allocates more trials to requests that have previously yielded near-boundary items. Skill counts 

are sampled from an adaptive distribution updated by directional feedback: easy outcomes shift mass toward larger skill counts, hard outcomes shift mass toward smaller counts, and near-boundary outcomes reinforce the current count. Skills are selected by Thompson sampling under compatibility constraints, with aggregate feedback assigned to the selected subset. The selected bare request and skills are composed into an item x = Compose( q, s) using a category-specific composer: an external API model for natural-language rewriting categories and a deterministic template for verifier-compatible constrained-instruction items. Reward is defined as 

r(ˆ p) = 1 − 2|ˆp − 0.5|, which favors items that are neither consistently passed nor consistently failed. 

Deduplication and termination are handled by skipping repeated (q, s) combinations and stopping when the budget B is exhausted or when no new boundary item is found in R consecutive rounds. Algorithm 1 in Appendix E gives the full implementation details, including priors, sampling truncation, compatibility backtracking, progressive evaluation, hyperparameters, and stopping criteria. 

3.4 Adaptive evaluation of new models 

Given a new target model mnew , the goal is to estimate its ability θnew on the calibrated category-specific logit scale. The item difficulties ˆβ of the anchor set are held fixed throughout the initial evaluation, so only θnew is updated. The adaptive protocol has two steps. First, we run Fisher-information computerized adaptive testing (CAT) on the anchor set [Hofmann et al., 2025, Li et al., 2025]: at each round, the next item is selected to maximize Rasch information at the current estimate of θnew , and the estimate is updated by an Expected A Posteriori (EAP) inference [Li et al., 2025]. This step efficiently localizes models whose abilities lie within the panel-covered range. If the anchor set no longer provides sufficient boundary coverage—for example, when ˆθnew lies outside the reference panel’s θ-range—the protocol invokes SGBS as a fallback. SGBS generates new items near the target model’s boundary. These items are then evaluated on the reference panel and calibrated onto the existing scale, extending the effective range of the ruler rather than requiring a full panel recalibration from scratch. 6Therefore, Step 1 scores in-range models using only the fixed anchor set, while Step 2 expands the calibrated item bank only when needed. This on-demand extension mechanism is the basis of Claim 3 (§4.3). The full procedure, including trigger guards, is given in Algorithm 2 in Appendix E. 

## 4 Evaluation of The DBE Protocol 

We evaluate the DBE protocol against three claims as follows. The shared experimental setup (categories, bare request pools, reference panel, anchor pool, composer and judges) is consolidated in Appendix A; per-claim methodology is introduced where each claim is reported.

## 5 Limitations and Future Work 

There are several limitations remaining. First, several components depend on API LLMs, especially the composer and judge for A, A ′, and C. Repeated trials reduce sampling noise, but they do not eliminate systematic judge or composer biases. Second, the searchable item space is limited by the curated bare-request pools, skill dictionaries, and fixed multi-turn scripts used in this work. Expanding these resources may reveal boundary regions not covered by the current implementation. ## A Experiment setup 

This appendix consolidates the experimental setup shared across Claims 1–3 (§4). Per-claim method-ology and metrics are introduced where each claim is reported. 

Categories. We instantiate the protocol on the four categories of Table 1: harmful request refusal (A), over-refusal (A ′), constrained instruction following (B), and sycophancy resistance (C). All four share the protocol and infrastructure (§3.1–§3.2); only the judge (rule-based vs. LLM) and composition template are category-specific. 

Bare request pools. Per-category bare request pools Q are assembled from canonical published benchmarks: A combines HarmBench [Mazeika et al., 2024], AdvBench [Zou et al., 2023], and StrongREJECT [Souly et al., 2024]; B combines IFEval [Zhou et al., 2023] task descriptions (with original constraints stripped so SGBS can apply its own) and InFoBench [Qin et al., 2024] multi-domain prompts; C combines TruthfulQA [Lin et al., 2022], MMLU [Hendrycks et al., 2021a], and ARC-Challenge [Clark et al., 2018] factual QA items; A ′ combines XSTest safe prompts [Röttger et al., 2024] and OR-Bench-Hard [Cui et al., 2024]. Sourcing principles, minor sources, and per-category provenance are in Appendix B. 

Reference model panel. The reference panel is fixed at M = 9 instruction-tuned models, listed in Table 2. The panel spans four families (Qwen, Gemma, Mistral, DeepSeek-Distill) and parameter sizes from 4B to 72B. Family balance is enforced to limit shared-pretraining leakage of difficulty signal; the selection rationale, excluded candidates, and panel-LOO stability of ˆβ are detailed in Appendix D. 13 Anchor pool and trials. For each category we generate a random-composition pool of K = 500 

items (§3.1) under a skill-count distribution roughly uniform on k ∈ { 0, 1, 2, 3}, so the pool spans the panel’s empirical difficulty range rather than concentrating at one complexity level. Each (panel-model, item) cell receives N = 40 trials under the two-stage protocol of Appendix C.4, yielding a 

9 × 500 Binomial response matrix per category; the AVG [0 .05 , 0.95] filter (§3.2) extracts the anchor set. Anchor-set quality is validated against 14 alternatives on a four-axis framework in Appendix C.2, and the anchor set’s measurement resolution is audited against published fixed benchmarks at matched item budget in Appendix G. 

Composer and judges. The composer for A, A ′, C is an external API LLM with a category-specific prompt template; B uses a deterministic template that wires each selected IFEval/IFBench skill into a verifier-compatible prompt. The judge is rule-based for B (the upstream IFEval/IFBench Python verifiers [Zhou et al., 2023, Pyatkin et al., 2025]) and an external API LLM with category-specific rubric for A, A ′, C. Prompt templates are in Appendix F; API models and judge-validation accuracy against human annotation are in Appendix B.

Sourcing principles. Every category follows the same three-pillar sourcing template (bare-request pool Q, skill dictionary S, held-out validation set), governed by uniform principles. Stage A–B–C decision. With four axes and four category-specific filter rankings, we adopt a three-stage funnel that strictly dominates per-category argmin-bSE (which selects different filters per category at low FIR) and FIR-threshold variants (no single threshold satisfies all four categories simultaneously, see §C.2). The pattern is consistent with how SGBS items are constructed: each EC item is anchored to a specific target model’s boundary, so the panel responses on EC( m) are not generated by a single shared difficulty acting on different abilities, but by a per-item pattern that single-trait IRT cannot reproduce. Richer models fit this per-cell variation more flexibly on visible cells, but the additional parameters they spend doing so do not predict the held-out target’s behavior. Adding a second or third latent dimension worsens generalization further: at M = 9 each new dimension is identified by only 9 panel anchor points, well below the panel size at which MIRT becomes statistically reliable, consistent with our M ≥ 20 scaling caveat in §5. 

Conclusion. 1PL is simultaneously (i) the only family with the specific objectivity property that supports cross-panel difficulty transfer in Claim 3 (§4.3), and (ii) the held-out optimum among the families compared on every category. Step 2 calibration protocol. The boundary items Enew that SGBS returns carry only mnew ’s responses, and mnew ’s ability is itself not yet calibrated to the panel scale. From outlier responses alone, β and θ are unidentifiable up to a shared translation. We restore identifiability by evaluating 

Enew on the full panel and then performing the calibration in two principled steps. (i) With the panel abilities ˆθpanel held fixed at their anchor-set values, each new item’s ˆβx is the 1-D MLE solution from M panel cells, independent of any mnew response — the panel pins the new items onto the existing logit scale. (ii) With ˆβ now defined on Xanchor ∪ E new , ˆθnew is updated by EAP over the union, with ˆβanchor also frozen so the existing scale is preserved across the merge. The two steps can equivalently be folded into a single joint optimization with the appropriate parameter freezing. The two State lines following the SGBS call in Algorithm 2 are exactly this two-step calibration; the loop then returns the augmented-set EAP estimate as ˆθnew .

Step 2 cost. Each Step 2 trigger incurs M · K additional panel cell evaluations (each cell costs 

N trials), where K = |E new | is the SGBS target count. Note that pure SGBS would only cost K

outlier-side cells; Step 2 anchoring requires the panel side as well, since without panel responses on Enew the new ˆβ are unidentifiable. We minimize triggering frequency through the conservative trigger of the next paragraph and through panel selection that pre-empts the most common outlier modes (Appendix D); at M = 9 and K = 50 , a single trigger costs 450 N inferences which we treat as the per-outlier amortization cost of placing mnew on the calibrated scale. 

Trigger robustness. The coverage condition ˆθ(t−1) /∈ [θlo , θ hi ] is a point-estimate test on the latest 

ˆθnew , not a statement about which anchor item is closest. This decouples the trigger from the local geometry of the ˆβ grid: even if every remaining anchor is far from ˆθ(t)new , the trigger does not fire 30 as long as ˆθ(t−1) stays within the panel coverage interval. What it does not filter is early-iteration noise — a small pilot can produce ˆθ(0) that lies far from the model’s true θ, drifting briefly outside 

[θlo , θ hi ] until subsequent CAT iterations re-anchor it. Algorithm 2 therefore adds (i) an iteration guard t ≥ tmin before any trigger is allowed and (ii) a hysteresis counter c requiring L consecutive iterations of coverage failure before SGBS is invoked, with c reset on any iteration where coverage holds. Defaults are tmin = 2 , L = 2 , n0 = 5 pilot items spread across the ˆβ range; the pad τpad = 0 .5

tolerates inherent estimation noise of ˆθ(t) on a finite cell budget (Appendix I.2). A more principled posterior-based alternative — triggering on Pr( ˆθnew /∈ [min m ˆθm, max m ˆθm] | data ) > p trig using the EAP posterior directly — is left as a Phase-9 refinement; the point-estimate trigger with guards is sufficient for our M = 9 panel. The stability tolerance ϵstab , consecutive count Kstab , and pad τpad 

are deferred to the experimental setup of Claim 3 (§4.3).

## J Extended limitations and future work 

This appendix expands on the limitations summarized in §5. 

1PL mis-specification at finite panel. At M = 9 , observed leave-one-model-out |∆ ˆβx| exceed the 1PL parametric-bootstrap null (Appendix D.2), suggesting residual multi-dimensional structure. We therefore report panel-LOO stability of ˆβ as “ordering preserved” rather than the strict large-M

specific-objectivity threshold; protocol uses of ˆβ are monotone-invariant and unaffected. A stabilized MIRT-2D fit requires M ≥ 20 and is left as future work. 

Filter validity assumptions A1, A2. The compute-only filter (§3.2) is sound under (A1) panel coverage of the target θ range and (A2) negligible Fisher information at panel-mean pass rates near saturation. Out-of-range targets are routed to on-demand SGBS (§3.4); A2 is validated by the four-axis ablation and Rasch residual-PCA (Appendices C.1, C.2). 

Cross-category unification deferred. We report per-category Rasch fits rather than a unified logit scale; Hofmann et al. [2025] document construct-validity loss in unified IRT, and our four categories do not yet support such a fit. None of Claims 1–3 depend on unification. 

External LLM dependence in A, A ′, C. These categories use an API LLM judge and an API composer (for SGBS). Trial-level stochasticity is absorbed by the N -trial Binomial; systematic judge bias is not, propagating as a category-wide logit constant—mitigated by per-category judge– human accuracy reporting (Appendix B). Composer choice changes the realized item distribution and item-level ˆβ, but Claim 2’s ˆθm– ¯β(EC( m)) relation is invariant, since it is derived per-item from panel-evaluated ˆpm,x .

Item-space scope. Multi-turn pressure in C is a pre-generated script fixed across panel models— required for IRT model-independence of ˆβ but does not exercise adaptive multi-turn dynamics. Skill dictionaries are curated from fixed source taxonomies (Table 1, Appendix B); novel surfaces require dictionary updates, supported by SGBS’s cold-start logic but bounded by current expressivity. Adaptive multi-turn red-teaming and dictionary expansion are future work. 42
