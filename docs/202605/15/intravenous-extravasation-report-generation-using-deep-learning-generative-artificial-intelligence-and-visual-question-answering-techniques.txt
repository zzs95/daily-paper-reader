## 2 Introduction 44 

2.1 Background 45 

Intravenous (IV) therapy is a fundamental component of medical treatment, 46 

frequently utilizing peripheral intravenous catheters to establish vascular access 47 

[1]. This therapy delivers substances directly into the bloodstream, providing 48 

an essential route for administering medications unsuitable for oral intake. Such 49 

direct delivery is particularly crucial for certain agents, including chemotherapy 50 

and cytotoxic drugs, which require precise and controlled administration for 51 

optimal efficacy and safety [2]. Despite its advantages, IV therapy is associated 52 

with several complications, among which extravasation represents a significant 53 

concern [1]. 54 

Extravasation occurs when vesicant agents such as vasopressors (e.g., dobu-55 

tamine, dopamine, epinephrine, and norepinephrine) leak from the intended 56 

vascular compartment into the surrounding tissue, potentially causing vesi-57 

cant damage [3]. Other therapeutic agents, including phenytoin, promethazine, 58 

and various chemotherapeutic agents, also pose a significant risk. When ex-59 

travasated, these substances can induce tissue damage through mechanisms 60 

such as localized vasoconstriction, osmotic imbalances, pH alterations, and di-61 

rect cytotoxic effects [4]. The overall incidence of extravasation among patients 62 

receiving IV therapy across all age groups is estimated at approximately 6% [5]. 63 

However, this rate is notably elevated in specific pediatric populations: chil-64 

dren exhibit a higher incidence, potentially up to 16%, which may be partly 65 

attributed to their smaller vessel caliber, while neonates are particularly vul-66 

nerable, with reported incidence rates reaching as high as 46% [6]. 67 

2.2 Complications and Symptoms 68 

Immediate care following extravasation is essential, particularly when leakage 69 

occurs near nerves, ligaments, and joints. Delayed treatment can result in seri-70 

ous complications, including scarring, cellulitis, neurovascular injury, compart-71 

ment syndrome, hemorrhage, thrombosis, and phlebitis [1, 7, 8]. Injury severity 72 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS is clinically reflected in the overlying skin: mildly affected areas may appear 73 

blanched, cold, or edematous, whereas severe involvement produces leakage, 74 

discoloration, and signs of circulatory impairment [4, 9]. Left untreated, severe 75 

cases progress to tissue necrosis and ulceration requiring aggressive debride-76 

ment or, in the most extreme instances, surgical reconstruction or amputation 77 

[9, 10, 11]. 78 

Extravasation is often underreported, partly due to gaps in nursing knowl-79 

edge [11, 12, 13]. Nurses frequently have limited experience managing such in-80 

juries [12], and some oncology nurses report inadequate training in extravasation 81 

prevention despite years of clinical practice [11]. These knowledge gaps directly 82 

impede timely treatment [11, 12, 13], and while targeted training programmes 83 

can help, they demand considerable time and resources [12, 13]. Technological 84 

solutions have therefore been developed to supplement clinical knowledge and 85 

support decision-making. 86 

In addition to the issues mentioned, it is essential to consider that Thai 87 

nurses have a high turnover rate, often driven by dissatisfaction with job de-88 

mand, salary, benefits, recognition, and other factors [14, 15]. When basic 89 

material and economic needs are not met, nurses may experience reduced mo-90 

tivation for professional development and feel incentivized to seek positions in 91 

organization offering better work environment, compensation, or proximity to 92 

home [14, 16]. Such workforce instability makes it difficult to ensure sustained, 93 

specialized knowledge across the nursing team for managing conditions like ex-94 

travasation [11, 12, 13]. As a result, the resources allocated to training and 95 

education may not achieve their intended long-term impact efficiently. 96 

When nurses lack the necessary expertise to effectively screen and man-97 

age extravasations, dermatologists are likely to take the initiative [11, 12, 13]. 98 

Nonetheless, Thailand faces a documented shortage of dermatologists, resulting 99 

in poverty of adequate delivery of treatment to extravasation cases. As of 2022, 100 

the average number of newly certified dermatologists entering the workforce an-101 

nually was only 28 [17]. Furthermore, a significant geographic maldistribution 102 

exists, with approximately 75% of Thai dermatologists practicing in the capital 103 

city, Bangkok, while the remaining 25% serve the other 77 provinces [18]. This 104 

disparity creates significant barriers to accessing specialist dermatological care, 105 

particularly in rural and non-metropolitan regions. To address these gaps, there 106 

is a compelling need for advanced programs or technologies that can assist both 107 

nurses and dermatologists by providing a holistic evaluation and management 108 

of extravasations. 109 

2.3 Sensor Technologies and Their Limitations 110 

Current extravasation detection technologies often rely on unimodal sensors, 111 

which measure changes in specific parameters during extravasation events. For 112 

instance, impedance sensors with attached electrodes can detect changes in fluid 113 

accumulation at a certain location, indicating potential extravasation [19]. Ra-114 

dio frequency signals can also be used to measure changes in electrical permit-115 

tivity of soft tissue during extravasation [20]. Additionally, temperature sensors 116 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS and infrared thermography can detect extravasation by sensing temperature 117 

variations around the IV site caused by the extravasated fluid [21]. 118 

Jambulingham et al. developed a non-invasive multimodal sensor that de-119 

tects skin stretch due to the swelling of soft tissue and bioelectrical impedance, 120 

which confirms the change in skin firmness due to vesicant leakage [22]. Other 121 

investigated methods include optical sensors, ultrasound sensors, pressure sen-122 

sors, gamma scintillation sensors, and devices designed to measure fluid pressure 123 

and resistance during infusion [23]. While many sensor-based extravasation de-124 

tection products are commercially available, several limitations exist. Many 125 

products require close contact with the skin at the monitoring site, which car-126 

ries a risk of irritation. Furthermore, different types of sensors possess varying 127 

sensitivity levels, leading to inconsistencies in extravasation screening potential 128 

[23]. 129 

Administering these sensor-based technologies may require additional staff 130 

training, which can be challenging to deliver consistently and effectively across 131 

all nursing staff, especially when considering rotating hospital shifts. Further-132 

more, while these technologies are diverse, they primarily indicate the presence 133 

and severity of extravasation event without providing comprehensive screening 134 

information. The acquisition and maintenance costs of these devices can also 135 

be significant, contributing to overall healthcare expenditures. Regular mainte-136 

nance and calibration are essential to ensure accuracy and reliability, potentially 137 

increasing the workload for clinical staff and may necessitate support from spe-138 

cialized technicians [1, 23]. 139 

2.4 Advances in Image Processing 140 

Image processing technologies have demonstrated potential in various medical 141 

fields for tasks such as image classification, segmentation, recognition, and ob-142 

ject detection [24]. Although there are few instances where image processing 143 

has been applied specifically to screen for extravasations, this technology has 144 

shown potential in adjacent fields. The closest study involved detecting plasma 145 

extravasation in animal models [25]. In this study, Evans Blue dye was in-146 

fused intravenously to color the plasma, resulting in visible darkening of the 147 

skin. To observe the course of extravasation, a monochromatic charge-coupled 148 

device camera was used to monitor skin color changes. Image processing algo-149 

rithms measured changes in pixel intensity within the digital images, revealing 150 

the course and extent of the extravasation on the skin surface. However, this 151 

surface-monitoring method was unable to detect deeper extravasations, poten-152 

tially delaying treatment or missing necessary intervention in such cases [25]. 153 

Another relevant study involved using Tc-99m Hydroxymethylene Diphos-154 

phonate (HMDP) and an image processing software to detect and assess radio-155 

pharmaceutical extravasation during bone scintigraphy [26]. Although results 156 

successfully concluded that the prevalence of Tc-99m HMDP extravasation was 157 

26.5%, seven times higher in inpatient settings, the image processing software 158 

was primarily limited to classifying the severity of extravasation events after 159 

they occurred. Given that clinical management of extravasation necessitates 160 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS immediate detection and intervention, technologies that provide only post-hoc 161 

severity classification may be insufficient to effectively assist clinicians or derma-162 

tologists. Furthermore, the reliability of any image processing approach is highly 163 

dependent on the selection of appropriate image features. If the suboptimal fea-164 

ture is chosen, results can lead to skewed or inaccurate results, underscoring the 165 

need for robust and validated algorithms for clinical applications [24]. 166 

2.5 Advances in Machine Learning 167 

Unlike standard image processing, machine learning offers enhanced pattern 168 

recognition capabilities. This technology has been implemented in various med-169 

ical applications. For example, a study by Kostrikov et al. utilized machine 170 

learning to precisely characterize the extent of compound extravasation within 171 

brain tumors [27]. They employed a semi-automated machine learning-based 172 

method for vascular tree analysis in large 3D volumes [27]. The resulting de-173 

tailed generation of vascular maps and evaluation of extravasations promotes 174 

the understanding of tumor microenvironments and their role in tumor growth, 175 

thus streamlining oncologists’ diagnostic capabilities and informing treatment 176 

strategies. 177 

Another study by Nesbit et al. used a machine learning approach to quan-178 

titatively analyze blood-brain-barrier permeability. Their model was designed 179 

to detect extravasation of plasma-derived proteins during blood-brain-barrier 180 

dysfunction to prevent neurodegeneration [28]. Methodologically, the study 181 

involved extracting mouse brains, performing laminin-α4 staining on brain sec-182 

tions, and then utilizing the Zen Intellesis software for image analysis. This 183 

machine learning tool classified the extent of IgG extravasation into distinct 184 

categories: no leakage, mild leakage, or severe leakage [28]. Through these two 185 

programs, researchers were able to eliminate the subjective bias inherent in 186 

manual interpretation of leakage severity. 187 

Although machine learning surpasses traditional methods in predictive per-188 

formance on complex datasets, it possesses inherent limitations. For example, 189 

machine learning models are heavily dependent on the quality and representa-190 

tiveness of their training data [29]. If the dataset is biased, the model’s per-191 

formance can be significantly impaired. Moreover, machine learning models 192 

can exhibit limitations in contextual understanding or causal reasoning, poten-193 

tially leading to spurious conclusions or poor generalization to data distributions 194 

not encountered during training [29, 30]. Deep learning, a subfield of machine 195 

learning characterized by the use of deep neural networks, offers significant ad-196 

vantages in specific areas, with a key strength being the automation of feature 197 

representation. Deep learning models can learn hierarchical features directly 198 

from raw data, thereby reducing or eliminating the need for the laborious, time-199 

consuming, and often domain-expertise-dependent process of manual feature 200 

engineering [30]. 201 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 2.6 Extravasation Screening Using Deep Learning 202 

Munthuli et al. have developed the Thammasat University Extravasation As-203 

sessment Tool (TUXAT), a system based on deep learning methodologies for the 204 

accurate screening of intravenous extravasations [31]. TUXAT is implemented 205 

as a smartphone application capable of offline operation, making it well-suited 206 

for use in remote clinical settings. The 2022 iteration of the TUXAT model 207 

utilized specific Deep Neural Network architectures: DenseNet-121 and U-Net 208 

[32, 33]. Two distinct U-Net models were employed to perform semantic seg-209 

mentation of the skin and lesion regions in input images, as a preprocessing 210 

step. Consequently, the DenseNet-121 model was responsible for classifying the 211 

severity of detected extravasations [32, 33]. This multi-stage approach classified 212 

extravasation severity into four levels (Asymptomatic, Mild, Moderate, and Se-213 

vere extravasation), achieving an overall F1-score of 78.83% [31]. Performance 214 

evaluation revealed that this iteration demonstrated the highest accuracy for 215 

Severe cases, followed by Mild and Asymptomatic instances, but showed lower 216 

performance in identifying Moderate extravasations [31]. 217 

While the first iteration of the TUXAT model [31] was developed using 1,084 218 

images, advancements in Vision Transformers (ViT) and Contrastive Language-219 

Image Pre-training (CLIP) enabled the second iteration [34] to achieve superior 220 

classification performance using significantly less data only 64 images per class 221 

[35, 36, 37, 34]. A key innovation of the 2024 TUXAT model was its applica-222 

tion of zero-shot transfer from a ViT-CLIP/14 @336px model via linear probing 223 

[37]. ViT-CLIP/14 @336px is a specific AI model combining ViT and CLIP, 224 

optimized for 336 ×336 pixel images and using 14 ×14 pixel internal patches 225 

[37]. By integrating technologies like GroundingDINO for skin segmentation, 226 

this iteration achieved state-of-the-art classification of extravasation severity. 227 

While the model demonstrated impressive capabilities, there were still chal-228 

lenges to overcome, particularly with the classification of Mild severity cases 229 

[34, 38]. Mild severity cases can share similar features with Asymptomatic skin 230 

and other lesion classes, as well as being conflated with the uncertain image 231 

class [34]. Overall, the 2024 iteration obtained a maximum macro F1-score of 232 

81.34% compared to 78.83% for the 2022 iteration [31, 34]. 233 

2.7 Transition to the Visual Question Answering Era for 234 

Extravasation Screening 235 

While the TUXAT mobile application streamlines extravasation assessment 236 

through classification, its current functionality is restricted to this classifica-237 

tion task [31, 34]. To overcome this limitation, we propose enhancing TUXAT 238 

by integrating a Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) approach, specifically 239 

utilizing a Visual Question Answering (VQA) model to generate a holistic report 240 

of the extravasation in response to an image [39]. Indeed, this enhanced version 241 

of the TUXAT application intends to facilitate earlier detection and potentially 242 

contribute to the prevention of severe extravasation events. Furthermore, the 243 

generated report could serve as a valuable decision support and educational 244 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS resource, particularly guiding nurses less experienced with extravasation man-245 

agement towards providing timely and effective care. 246 

Implementing VQA technology in the TUXAT model would be particularly 247 

beneficial for dermatologists practicing in low resource setting countries, where 248 

the patient-to-doctor ratio is high or in understaffed hospitals [18, 40]. The en-249 

hanced TUXAT could support in the dermatologist’s decision-making and pre-250 

pare adequate medical treatment plans for both the dermatologist and the nurse 251 

to execute. For nurses requiring guidance on managing extravasation, the com-252 

prehensive information generated by the VQA-enhanced TUXAT could serve 253 

as a valuable reference. Increased access to pertinent medical information and 254 

instructions can decrease the incidence of delayed treatment, underreporting of 255 

extravasation, and severe cases. Additionally, early detection and preventative 256 

measures achieved by this technology can contribute to reducing the significant 257 

costs associated with emergency surgical interventions often required for severe 258 

extravasation [41]. 259 

The potential benefits of the proposed TUXAT model by integrating ex-260 

travasation screening with AI-generated reporting, are particularly relevant for 261 

healthcare delivery in settings such as Thailand’s rural hospitals. The compre-262 

hensive report, offering a detailed evaluation of the lesion alongside treatment 263 

considerations, represents a valuable resource for all healthcare practitioners 264 

managing extravasation. This enhanced TUXAT model can serve as a sig-265 

nificant asset, supporting nurses who may require guidance on extravasation 266 

management to provide timely and effective care. 267 

Compared with general-purpose medical VQA systems such as Med-PaLM 2 268 

[42], LLaVA-Med [43], and VQA-RAD [44], which target broad diagnostic tasks 269 

across diverse medical domains, the proposed VQA-enhanced TUXAT is purpose-270 

built for peripheral IV extravasation assessment. In terms of methodology, Med-271 

PaLM 2 and LLaVA-Med employ large-scale pre-training on general biomedical 272 

corpora and are evaluated on open-ended medical question answering, while 273 

VQA-RAD relies on a curated radiology-specific dataset. In contrast, the VQA-274 

enhanced TUXAT integrates a domain-specific clinical ontology – skin discol-275 

oration, integrity, edema, and severity grading – with structured treatment plan 276 

generation based on extravasation clinical guidelines [9]. Regarding clinical 277 

workflow integration, existing medical VQA systems operate primarily as re-278 

search benchmarks or general-purpose assistants without direct coupling to a 279 

specific clinical tool or guideline set. The VQA-enhanced TUXAT, by contrast, 280 

is embedded within an existing validated smartphone-based application [31, 34], 281 

enabling point-of-care use by frontline nursing staff. In terms of practical im-282 

pact, the system employs a modular plug-and-play architecture that enables 283 

component-level updates without full system retraining. The growing interest 284 

in structured, domain-specific report generation, such as the self-boosting mul-285 

timodal alignment framework of Mir et al. further motivate the development of 286 

domain-specific VQA-based reporting for specialized clinical tasks [45]. 287 

Therefore, the contributions of this paper are as follows: 288 

1. To utilize VQA technology to generate a tailored report on extravasa-289 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS tion indicators, including skin discoloration, skin integrity, skin edema, 290 

and presence of IV catheter, delivering a comprehensive evaluation to aid 291 

healthcare providers, including nurses and dermatologists. 292 

2. To create an AI-generated report that presents a thorough treatment plan 293 

for healthcare professionals based on identified extravasation indicators 294 

and severity. 295 

3. To design the workflow of a series of models and necessary resources using 296 

a modular (plug-and-play) architecture, ensuring updates can be imple-297 

mented easily due to its flexible, self-contained components. 298 

4. To leverage the extravasation severity classification functionalities of the 299 

latest versions of the 2024 TUXAT model as a basis for the VQA compo-300 

nent to generate detailed reports. 301 

It is important to acknowledge the inherent limitations of an assessment 302 

tool relying solely on visual data. While the model analyzes photographic in-303 

put, certain crucial clinical signs relevant to extravasation assessment cannot 304 

be captured by a standard camera. Examples include localized skin temper-305 

ature changes, the presence of systemic fever, and the degree of affected limb 306 

mobility [9]. Therefore, these non-visual parameters will not be included in the 307 

AI-generated report. 308

## 3 Literature on Visual Question Answering model 315 

## for Medical Image Analysis 316 

Visual Question Answering (VQA) is an interdisciplinary field merging computer 317 

vision and natural language processing [39]. The objective is to create models 318 

capable of accurately answering questions about visual inputs based on context. 319 

These models leverage deep learning techniques, such as transformers, to ana-320 

lyze images and comprehend accompanying textual queries, enabling them to 321 

generate precise responses [35, 36, 39]. VQA systems integrate multiple layers 322 

of image processing and language understanding, facilitating advancements in 323 

various applications such as assistive technologies for the visually impaired, in-324 

teractive AI systems, and medicine [46, 47]. By bridging the gap between visual 325 

data interpretation and linguistic expression, VQA models represent a signifi-326 

cant stride towards more intuitive and intelligent human-computer interactions. 327 

Certainly, VQA technology holds significant potential to aid dermatologists 328 

and nurses in managing extravasation. However, it is equally important to rec-329 

ognize that most VQA models are currently at an early stage of development 330 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS and should not be considered substitutes for the clinical judgment of medical 331 

professionals [46, 47]. VQA technology often utilizes or falls under the umbrella 332 

of GenAI. GenAI itself represents a novel category of tools in medicine, with 333 

potential applications including streamlining screening processes, addressing pa-334 

tient inquiries, and automating laborious tasks like clinical note documentation. 335 

Therefore, VQA technology, along with other GenAI tools, should primarily be 336 

viewed as an instrument for assisting medical professionals to minimize costs 337 

and address time poverty. 338 

Several GenAI models are currently utilized in healthcare, although none 339 

are specifically designed to address extravasations. One well-known GenAI 340 

in this field is Google’s Med-PaLM 2, which can answer personalized medi-341 

cal prompts, analyze large volumes of data, and assist in telemedicine [42]. 342 

IRENE, developed by Zhou et al. , is a transformer-based model designed to 343 

process multimodal inputs such as images, unstructured text, and structured 344 

text. Unlike other models that focus on modality-specific features, IRENE 345 

aims to integrate various forms of information to aid in diagnosis [48]. Signifi-346 

cant progress is also being made with the Large Language and Vision Assistant 347 

for BioMedicine (LLaVA-Med). This model utilizes figure-caption datasets ex-348 

tracted from PubMed Central ® and using OpenAI’s Generative Pre-Trained 349 

Transformer version 4.0 (GPT-4) generated instructions, learns to facilitate 350 

open-ended conversations and answer questions related to biomedical images 351 

[43]. 352 

A primary application of VQA within medicine is in radiology, particularly 353 

with chest X-rays. For example, Contrastive X-Ray REport Match (X-REM), 354 

developed by Jeong et al. , employs an image-text matching score to generate 355 

retrieval-based reports for chest X-rays [49]. Another VQA application in radiol-356 

ogy is Electronic Health Records chest X-ray Question Answering (EHRXQA), 357 

which answers questions by combining relevant information from Electronic 358 

Health Records (EHR) with chest X-ray images [50]. The Visual Question 359 

Answering in Radiology (VQA-RAD) model is trained on a validated dataset 360 

manually generated by radiologists and medical students who have completed 361 

their rotations [44]. Beyond radiology, VQA applications are also emerging 362 

in pathology, with the goal of passing the board-certified examinations of the 363 

American Board of Pathology [51]. Furthermore, the Med-Gemini model is an-364 

other AI capable of performing VQA tasks on diverse datasets, encompassing 365 

both 2D and 3D radiology images, histopathology, ophthalmology, dermatology, 366 

and genomic data [52, 53]. 367 

Continuous development efforts are focused on improving VQA performance. 368 

One such advancement is the Medical Knowledge-based VQA Network (MKBN), 369 

which integrates images, questions, and knowledge graphs to aid diagnostic pro-370 

cesses and enhance disease understanding [54]. The Transformer-based frame-371 

work for medical VQA (2ATransformer) is a VQA model equipped with an 372 

additional Transformer decoder, featuring candidate answer embeddings that 373 

interact with the fused features of the image-question pair to make decisions 374 

[55]. Medical Visual Question Answering (Med-VQA) is another model pre-375 

trained using large-scale medical image-text pairs by multi-modal masked au-376 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS toencoders (M3AE), which incorporate masking ratios, representation selection 377 

for reconstruction, and decoder designs [56]. Enhancements to the VQA-Med 378 

model’s question-answering capabilities have been achieved using techniques like 379 

the Question-guided Feature Pyramid Network (QFPN), demonstrably surpass-380 

ing previous performance benchmarks [57]. Additionally, the implementation of 381 

self-supervised Vision Language Pre-training (VLP) has improved performance. 382 

This method uses image and text modeling with Unimodal and Multimodal 383 

Contrastive losses (MUMC), achieving improved performance on public VQA 384 

benchmark datasets [58]. 385 

Despite advancements in AI capabilities including multimodal task per-386 

formance, susceptibility to hallucinations remains a significant challenge for 387 

GenAI[59]. AI hallucination occurs when the AI, usually an LLM, provides in-388 

correct information in response to a given prompt. This limitation significantly 389 

reduces the efficacy and reliability of AI in scientific inquiries and healthcare 390 

[60]. To address this issue, the TUXAT model incorporates a mechanism de-391 

signed to limit hallucinations by constraining its outputs based on its predefined, 392 

validated classification results from the 2024 TUXAT model [34]. The TUXAT 393 

model’s classification system has been validated through a train-validate-test 394 

design with consensus from three expert dermatologists [31]. Furthermore, it 395 

adheres to extravasation guidelines created by Thammasat University Hospital 396 

and Infusion Nurse Network of Thailand [9]. 397 

## 4 Methods 398 

This section outlines the methodology employed for intravenous extravasation 399 

report generation, encompassing the key stages of data collection, preprocess-400 

ing and feature extraction, model selection, extravasation classification, VQA 401 

prompting for assessment, and report generation. 402 

4.1 Data Collection 403 

The study utilized a dataset of 1,085 images characterized by substantial di-404 

versity across multiple factors. The images varied in size, source smartphone 405 

camera, anatomical location captured (hand, arm, leg, and foot), image quality, 406 

rotation, and lighting conditions. To preserve data authenticity and reflect real-407 

world clinical variability, they were not subjected to editing or standardization. 408 

Data collection involved 135 patients across 19 hospitals in Thailand. Of these 409 

institutions, nine were managed by the Ministry of Public Health, six were under 410 

the jurisdiction of Bangkok city, and four were university hospitals. The study 411 

was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki, and all methods 412 

were carried out in accordance with relevant guidelines and regulations. The 413 

protocol was approved by the Institutional Review Board (IRB) of Thammasat 414 

University Hospital, Pathum Thani, Thailand (approval no. 039/2019), and 415

5.1.1 Inter-rater Reliability Assessment 577 

To assess the consistency of the three annotators’ ratings on the ordinal 1–5 578 

Likert scale, inter-rater reliability (IRR) was evaluated using multiple metrics. 579 

Weighted Cohen’s Kappa (quadratic weights) was considered for its suitability in 580 

measuring agreement on ordinal data, appropriately penalizing larger disagree-581 

ments more heavily [68]. Krippendorff’s Alpha was also calculated, offering a 582 

flexible measure suitable for multiple raters and various levels of measurement 583 

[69]. Additionally, the Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) was computed to 584 

determine the degree of agreement, specifically using a two-way random effects 585 

model assessing the absolute agreement for average rater scores (ICC(2 , k ) and 586 

ICC(3 , k )), treating ratings as reflecting an underlying continuous construct of 587 

perceived report quality [70]. 588 

Standard interpretation benchmarks were used. For Cohen’s Kappa and 589 

Krippendorff’s Alpha, values above 0.80 suggest almost perfect agreement, 0.61– 590 

0.80 substantial agreement, 0.41–0.60 moderate agreement, 0.21–0.40 fair agree-591 

ment, and values below 0.20 slight or poor agreement [68]. For the ICC (average 592 

measures), values above 0.90 typically indicate excellent reliability, 0.75–0.90 593 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS good, 0.50–0.75 moderate, and below 0.50 poor reliability [70]. Percent agree-594 

ment was also calculated as a fundamental measure of concordance. 595 

5.2 Multivariate Analysis of Variance (MANOVA) 596 

To systematically evaluate the factors influencing the human assessment ratings 597 

across multiple key criteria (Discoloration, Integrity, and Edema), a three-way 598 

Multivariate Analysis of Variance (MANOVA) was conducted. This statistical 599 

test was selected because the assessment involved multiple, potentially corre-600 

lated dependent variables (the ordinal 5-point Likert scale ratings for Discol-601 

oration, Integrity, and Edema) [71]. MANOVA allowed for the simultaneous 602 

examination of group differences across this set of related outcome measures, 603 

accounting for their inter-correlations and controlling the overall Type I error 604 

rate, which would be inflated by running separate ANOVAs for each criterion 605 

[72]. The analysis examined the main effects and potential interactions of three 606 

key independent variables, each chosen for specific reasons: 607 

• Model: The comparison between the selected VQA models (Model A: 608 

Qwen-VL-72b-AWQ vs. Model B: Mixture including Qwen-VL-72b-AWQ, 609 

Ovis1.6-Gemma2-9, and MiniCPM-V-2.6) represented the central research 610 

question. This factor allowed for determining whether the distinct under-611 

lying VQA architectures resulted in statistically significant differences in 612 

the perceived quality and accuracy of the generated assessments, consid-613 

ering the combined profile of Discoloration, Integrity, and Edema ratings. 614 

• Severity: Incorporating the predefined skin condition severity levels (Asymp-615 

tomatic, Mild, Moderate, and Severe extravasation) as a factor served a 616 

dual purpose. Primarily, it functioned as a manipulation check, as ratings 617 

were expected to align with known clinical distinctions (e.g., clearer vi-618 

sual signs in Asymptomatic/Severe cases compared to potentially overlap-619 

ping Mild/Moderate presentations). Additionally, it allowed for exploring 620 

whether the performance of the models or the consistency of annotator 621 

ratings varied across this spectrum of clinical case difficulty. 622 

• Annotator: Including the individual human annotators (Annotator 1, 2, 623 

and 3) as a factor was essential for quantifying the inherent subjectivity 624 

and potential inter-rater variability associated with the human assessment 625 

process. Identifying systematic differences in scoring patterns among an-626 

notators provides critical context for interpreting the significance of any 627 

observed effects related to the VQA models. 628 

The primary objectives of the three-way MANOVA were to determine whether 629 

the choice of VQA model, the assessed severity level, or the identity of the an-630 

notator significantly impacted the combined profile (multivariate mean) of the 631 

Discoloration, Integrity, and Edema ratings. Furthermore, the analysis aimed 632 

to identify significant interactions, with particular interest in: 633 

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS • Model × Severity: Investigating if any performance difference between 634 

Model A and Model B remained consistent across all severity levels. 635 

• Severity × Annotator: Assessing the consistency of rating application by 636 

the annotators across the different severity categories. A non-significant 637 

interaction here, despite potential main effects of Annotator (indicating 638 

overall differences in strictness), would lend support to the reliability of 639 

the human judgments across the varying levels of case complexity. 640 

A significance level of α = 0 .05 was used to determine statistical significance 641 

for all main effects and interaction terms based on the Wilks’ Lambda statistic 642 

[72, 73]. Significant multivariate effects were further examined using post-hoc 643 

univariate ANOVAs for each dependent variable (Discoloration, Integrity, and 644 

Edema) and visual inspection of estimated marginal means plots to understand 645 

the nature of the differences. 646
