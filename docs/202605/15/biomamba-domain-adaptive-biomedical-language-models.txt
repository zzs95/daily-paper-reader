Abstract Background. Biomedical language models should improve performance on biomedical text while retaining general-language-modeling fluency. For Mamba-based models, this trade-off has not been systematically studied across biomedical literature and clinical text. 

Methods. We developed BioMamba, a family of biomedical Mamba2 models at five scales (130M, 370M, 780M, 1.3B, and 2.7B) obtained by continued pretraining of released public Mamba2 checkpoints on a balanced 80%/10%/10% mixture of PubMed abstracts, the Colossal Clean Crawled Corpus (C4), and Wikipedia. The architecture is unchanged from Mamba2; the contribution is the adaptation recipe and the accompanying open-weight checkpoints. We evaluated internal language modeling on fixed held-out sets, out-of-domain multiple-choice benchmarks, and three downstream tasks across multiple model scales: clinical note completion and discharge summary generation on MIMIC-IV-Note, and biomedical yes/no question answering on BioASQ and PubMedQA. 

Results. Across five scales, BioMamba consistently lowered PubMed perplexity, improved Wikipedia-style held-out perplexity by 1.46–4.72 PPL, and left C4 perplexity essentially un-changed ( |∆PPL | ≤ 0.17 ). On six out-of-domain multiple-choice benchmarks, BioMamba stayed within ±3 percentage points of Mamba2 with no systematic regression. After supervised fine-tuning, BioMamba+SFT matched or exceeded Mamba2+SFT on MIMIC-IV note completion 

1Downloaded from https://spj.science.org on May 15, 2026 and discharge summary generation at every evaluated scale (paired-bootstrap P (∆ > 0) ≥ 0.997   

> at 2.7B), and improved PubMedQA at every scale. The strongest model (BioMamba-2.7B) reached a PubMed perplexity of 5.28 and accuracies of 90.24% and 73.00% on BioASQ and PubMedQA, respectively.
> Conclusions. A balanced domain-adaptive continued pretraining recipe strengthens Mamba2 language models on biomedical literature and clinical text while preserving general-language-modeling fluency.

Keywords: biomedical natural language processing; Mamba language model; domain-adaptive pretraining; question answering; health data science 

# 1 Introduction 1

Biomedical research and health data science increasingly rely on unstructured text, including PubMed 2

abstracts, full-text articles, clinical guidelines, trial reports, and clinical documentation. Domain-3

adapted language models are therefore important for biomedical information extraction, evidence 4

synthesis, summarization, completion, and question answering [1]. Transformer-based biomedical 5

models such as BioBERT [2], SciBERT [3], PubMedBERT [4], BioGPT [5], SciFive [6], and BioBART 6

[7] have shown that adapting general-purpose language models to biomedical corpora can yield 7

strong performance. In clinical natural language processing, ClinicalBERT [8], GatorTron [9], and 8

ClinicalT5 [10] further demonstrate the value of domain adaptation for clinical text. Recent work in 9

health data science and public-health artificial intelligence has also emphasized that models used in 10 

biomedical workflows should be accurate, transparent, and practically useful [11–16]. 11 

Most biomedical language models still use Transformer self-attention, which has quadratic cost in 12 

sequence length [17]. This cost becomes restrictive when biomedical inputs are long, terminology-13 

dense, and distributed across multiple sentences or sections. State space models, especially structured 14 

state space models [18], offer an alternative. Mamba introduced selective state space layers for 15 

linear-time sequence modeling, and later work further strengthened the connection between state 16 

space models and attention while preserving favorable scaling for long contexts [19, 20]. 17 

Despite this progress, biomedical adaptation of Mamba models remains limited. Biomedical 18 

language modeling is still dominated by Transformer families and related open medical language 19 

models [2, 4, 5, 21–24], and the few reported Mamba-based biomedical adaptations have focused on 20 

narrower settings such as clinical notes [25]. Second, domain adaptation risks catastrophic forgetting: 21 

a model may improve on biomedical text while losing general-language-modeling fluency, which is a 22 

particular concern since biomedical writing often mixes specialized terminology with broader scientific 23 

language. This trade-off has been documented in domain-adaptive pretraining literature [26, 27], and 24 

in work explicitly targeting general knowledge preservation [28]. 25 

We address this gap with BioMamba, a family of biomedical models built by continued pretraining 26 

of released Mamba2 checkpoints. Our contribution is a reproducible biomedical continued pretraining 27 

recipe together with a family of open-weight BioMamba checkpoints at 130M, 370M, 780M, 1.3B, 28 

and 2.7B parameters; the architecture itself is unchanged from Mamba2. The main focus is therefore 29 

the adaptation strategy, its empirical effects, and the accompanying resource release rather than an 30 

2Downloaded from https://spj.science.org on May 15, 2026 architectural modification. This study makes three contributions: 31 

• A public release of the training and evaluation code and all five checkpoints at https://github. 32 

com/LeoYML/BioMamba under the Apache-2.0 license, so that the biomedical natural language 33 

processing community can directly use or extend the models. 34 

• A systematic investigation of the continued pretraining pipeline showing that an appropriate 35 

80%/10%/10% PubMed/C4/Wikipedia mixture can enhance biomedical language modeling 36 

while preserving general-language-modeling fluency and mitigating catastrophic forgetting. 37 

• Controlled base-versus-adapted experiments showing that BioMamba achieves consistent im-38 

provements across downstream tasks, including biomedical question answering (PubMedQA, 39 

BioASQ), clinical note completion, and discharge summary generation on MIMIC-IV. 40 

# 2 Methods 41 

## 2.1 Overview 42 

This study tests whether biomedical continued pretraining [26, 27] can improve Mamba language 43 

models while retaining general-language-modeling fluency. Figure 1 shows the overall workflow. We 44 

started from public Mamba2 checkpoints, continued pretraining on a mixed corpus of PubMed, C4, 45 

and Wikipedia, evaluated language-model quality on fixed in-domain and out-of-domain validation 46 

sets, and then fine-tuned the resulting models on clinical note completion, discharge summary 47 

generation, and biomedical question answering. 48 

Before analysis, we specified which comparisons would be treated as primary. The primary 49 

comparison is between each base model and its BioMamba counterpart under a shared tokenizer and 50 

fixed evaluation sets. These comparisons isolate the effect of biomedical adaptation most directly. 51 

Comparisons with public checkpoints are included only for context because tokenizers and prior 52 

training histories differ across released models. 53 

## 2.2 Model family and training data 54 

BioMamba was obtained by continued pretraining of released Hugging Face Mamba2 checkpoints 55 

from state-spaces/mamba2-* [19, 20] at five model scales spanning 130M–2.7B parameters. To 56 

keep tokenization fixed across internal comparisons, all models use the shared GPT-NeoX tokenizer 57 

[29] from state-spaces/mamba-2.8b-hf , which has a vocabulary of 50,280 tokens. 58 

All BioMamba variants were trained autoregressively with the standard next-token objective, 59

## 2.3 Training procedure 68 

Continued pretraining was performed for 3 epochs on the mixed PubMed/C4/Wikipedia corpus 69 

using AdamW [33] optimization with weight decay 0.1, BF16 mixed precision, a maximum sequence 70 

length of 1,024 tokens, gradient clipping of 1.0, and random seed 42. To keep optimization roughly 71 

comparable across scales, we adjusted per-device microbatch size and gradient accumulation so that 72 

the effective batch size stayed approximately constant, at 240–256 sequences for the 130M–1.3B 73 

models and 192 sequences for the 2.7B model. This yielded 5,967–7,953 optimizer steps over the 74 

full 3-epoch training budget. Exponential moving average of model weights was enabled throughout 75 

training, and the checkpoint with the lowest validation loss was kept for downstream evaluation. 76

## 2.4 Evaluation setup 87 

Internal language-model evaluation. The primary language-model evaluation used fixed val-88 

idation sets of 1,000 held-out sequences for PubMed [30] and C4 [31], drawn from the same data 89 

pools as continued pretraining but excluded from the training split, and tokenized with the shared 90 

tokenizer. For the Wikipedia held-out set, we used the Wikitext-103 raw split, referred to throughout 91 

this paper as the Wikipedia-style held-out set for brevity, and tokenized it with the same shared 92 

tokenizer. This is the most controlled comparison in the study because the tokenizer is held constant 93 

across all internal evaluations and each benchmark split is fixed in advance. 94 

Contextual comparison with public checkpoints. To place BioMamba alongside released 95 

biomedical and medically adapted checkpoints, we also evaluated public models on the same raw 96 

text using each model’s native tokenizer. Because perplexity depends on tokenization, these results 97 

are contextual rather than strictly comparable and are interpreted only as secondary model-level 98 

evidence. Exact checkpoint sources are listed in Supplementary Section S3. 99 

## 2.5 Downstream tasks 100 

We evaluated downstream transfer to clinical text on two complementary supervised generation tasks 101 

constructed from de-identified discharge summaries in MIMIC-IV-Note v2.2 [34] within the broader 102 

MIMIC-IV resource [35]. Note completion evaluates continuation ability: the model receives the first 103 

half of a discharge note and generates its continuation. Discharge summary generation evaluates 104 

5Downloaded from https://spj.science.org on May 15, 2026 summarization ability: the model receives structured admission sections and generates discharge 105 

sections. To avoid information leakage, splits were defined at the patient level, and quantitative 106 

evaluation used 500 randomly sampled held-out test notes per task. These clinical generation 107 

experiments were conducted at all five model scales. The two clinical generation settings are closely 108 

related to prior work on hospital-course summarization and discharge-note generation [36–40]. Full 109 

data construction, fine-tuning settings, and qualitative examples are provided in Supplementary 110 

Sections S5 and S6. 111 

We also evaluated downstream transfer on biomedical yes/no question answering [1] because it 112 

provides a simple and interpretable test of whether the model can use biomedical knowledge after 113 

continued pretraining. Supervised fine-tuning used a mixture of Biomedical Semantic Indexing and 114 

Question Answering (BioASQ) yes/no training examples from the BioASQ challenge series [41–43] 115 

aggregated from BioASQ 7B [44] and 13B [45] (1,114 samples after class balancing) and a binary 116 

yes/no split derived from the PubMed Question Answering (PubMedQA) dataset [46] (800 training 117 

samples). Held-out evaluation was conducted on BioASQ 13B and the corresponding 200-sample 118 

binary PubMedQA test split. For both downstream settings, supervised fine-tuning updated all 119 

model parameters rather than using parameter-efficient adapters. 120

# 3 Results 138 

## 3.1 Biomedical language modeling 139 

Quantitative results. Under the tokenizer-controlled internal comparison, continued pretraining 140 

lowered PubMed perplexity at every model scale (Table 2). Continued pretraining yielded consistent 141 

reductions in PubMed perplexity across all scales, dropping from 9.41 to 8.42 in the 130M model and 142 

scaling down to 5.28 in the 2.7B model. The relative PubMed perplexity reduction ranged from 7% 143 

to 11% across scales, demonstrating that biomedical adaptation yields consistent gains even at the 144 

largest model size. Wikipedia-style held-out perplexity also decreased at every scale, consistent with 145 

the inclusion of Wikipedia in the continued pretraining mixture. By contrast, C4 perplexity changed 146 

by at most about 1%, so PubMed gains did not come at the cost of out-of-domain C4 performance 147 

before any task-specific fine-tuning. 148

## 3.3 Clinical note completion and discharge summary generation on 179 

## MIMIC-IV 180 

We next asked whether biomedical adaptation learned from PubMed would transfer to clinical 181 

documentation. We therefore evaluated the models on MIMIC-IV-Note v2.2, using the patient-level 182 

split and 500-sample held-out evaluation described in the Methods and in Supplementary Section S5. 183 

9Downloaded from https://spj.science.org on May 15, 2026 Clinical supervised fine-tuning. Starting from both the Mamba2 base checkpoints and the 184 

BioMamba checkpoints, we performed clinical supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on two generation tasks 185 

constructed from the discharge notes. Training data comprised up to 20,000 note-completion samples 186 

and approximately 12,893 discharge-generation samples (32,893 total; 90% train, 10% validation). 187 

Instruction masking was applied so that only response tokens contributed to the loss. BioMamba+SFT 188 

uses a lower learning rate and more training epochs than Mamba2+SFT because aggressive fine-tuning 189 

tends to overwrite the information acquired during continued pretraining; to verify that this design 190 

choice does not create an unfair comparison, we additionally ran a matched-budget control in which 191 

Mamba2 was fine-tuned with the exact BioMamba SFT recipe, and the BioMamba schedule did 192 

not close the gap (Supplementary Section S9). We also found that validation loss was not a reliable 193 

proxy for downstream ROUGE in the BioMamba setting, which further motivates the conservative 194 

BioMamba SFT schedule. Full SFT data construction, hyperparameter configurations, and tuning 195 

details are provided in Supplementary Section S5. 196 

Evaluation tasks. We evaluated two clinical generation tasks that reflect different capabilities. 197 

In note completion, the model receives the first half of a discharge note and generates a 128-token 198 

continuation (greedy decoding), scored against the ground-truth second half by ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, 199 

and ROUGE-L. In discharge summary generation, the model receives structured admission sections 200 

and generates discharge sections, again scored by ROUGE. 201

## 3.7 Ablation studies 310 

Data-mixture ablation. The first ablation examined the composition of the continued pretraining 311 

corpus. As shown in Table 13, PubMed-only continued pretraining improved in-domain perplexity 312 

but worsened both Wikipedia and C4 perplexity, consistent with forgetting. Most mixed-corpus 313 

settings retained the PubMed gain, but many still weakened at least one held-out benchmark. The 314 

selected 10% C4 + 10% Wikipedia setting was the most balanced configuration: it achieved the lowest 315 

PubMed perplexity, the largest Wikipedia improvement, and essentially unchanged C4 perplexity. 316 

These results support the final 80% PubMed, 10% C4, and 10% Wikipedia mixture used for the main 317 

BioMamba checkpoints. A complementary 370M PubMed-only control, reported in Supplementary 318 

Table S13, confirms that this mixture remains load-bearing at a larger scale: PubMed-only continued 319 

pretraining worsens Wikipedia-style held-out perplexity by 69% and C4 perplexity by 15%, and is 320 

also slightly worse on held-out PubMed itself (9.32 → 9.91), consistent with the 130M pilot where 321 

the 80%/10%/10% mixture already matched PubMed-only on PubMed (8.41 vs. 8.47); the gap is 322 

larger at 370M, indicating that domain-only continued pretraining incurs a larger forgetting penalty 323 

at scale even on in-domain text. 324

# 4 Discussion 348 

This study examined whether biomedical continued pretraining can improve Mamba language models 349 

without substantially weakening general-language-modeling fluency. The results support this goal 350 

across both biomedical literature and clinical text. Under tokenizer-controlled internal evaluation, 351 

BioMamba lowered PubMed perplexity at every model scale, improved Wikipedia-style held-out 352 

perplexity, and left C4 performance largely unchanged; a six-task out-of-domain multiple-choice suite 353 

and an evaluation-budget stability check (Section 3.2) further support preservation on the benchmarks 354 

tested. After supervised fine-tuning, the models also transferred across three downstream tasks: 355 

note completion, discharge summary generation, and biomedical yes/no question answering. Taken 356 

together, the findings support balanced domain adaptation as a practical strategy for biomedical 357 

state space language models. 358 

## 4.1 Practical biomedical and clinical applications 359 

The most immediate use of BioMamba is workflow support rather than autonomous bedside decision 360 

making. The PubMed, BioASQ, and PubMedQA results are most relevant to tasks such as literature 361 

screening, evidence extraction, abstract triage, biomedical question answering, and drafting structured 362 

evidence summaries [48, 49]. The MIMIC-IV results extend this picture to clinical documentation 363 

support, especially note completion and discharge summary generation, which are closely related 364 

to hospital-course summarization and discharge-note generation tasks studied previously [36–39, 365 

50]. These tasks require models to handle biomedical terminology together with broader scientific 366 

or clinical language, which fits the balanced adaptation strategy used here. Recent studies of 367 

medical large language models report strong performance on clinical knowledge and medical question 368 

answering benchmarks [51–54], but discussions of clinical deployment similarly suggest that the most 369 

credible near-term role of such models is workflow support with retrieval and human review rather 370 

19 Downloaded from https://spj.science.org on May 15, 2026 than autonomous clinical decision making [55, 56]. 371 

The scale analysis also has practical implications. Continued pretraining produced its largest 372 

downstream gains in the smaller models, suggesting that useful biomedical adaptation is not limited 373 

to the largest checkpoint. This is relevant for institutions that require lower inference cost, local 374 

deployment, or more reproducible fine-tuning under limited computational budgets. At the same time, 375 

the present results do not justify direct use for patient-specific diagnosis, prognosis, or treatment 376 

recommendation. BioMamba was trained mainly on PubMed-centered text and evaluated on 377 

benchmark question answering and clinical generation rather than on prospective clinical tasks. Its 378 

most suitable role is therefore as an assistive component in literature-grounded or documentation-379 

support pipelines with retrieval, source attribution, and expert oversight [55, 57]. 380 

## 4.2 Related Work 381 

Biomedical language modeling has been dominated by Transformer-based architectures, including 382 

SciBERT [3], BioBERT [2], PubMedBERT [4], BioGPT [5], SciFive [6], BioBART [7], and BioMedLM 383 

[21], which established the value of domain-specific pretraining for biomedical natural language 384 

processing. Text-to-text pretraining paradigms such as T5 [58] and link-aware literature pretraining 385 

approaches such as LinkBERT [59] further expanded the design space for biomedical language 386 

modeling. Clinical-domain models such as ClinicalBERT [8], GatorTron [9], ClinicalT5 [10], and 387 

broader EHR-oriented language models [60, 61] extended domain adaptation to clinical notes [62]. 388 

Work on hospital-course summarization and clinical text generation has also expanded, including 389 

dataset construction, model adaptation, and shared-task evaluation [36–40, 50]. Biomedical question 390 

answering has a mature benchmark ecosystem centered on BioASQ [41–45] and PubMedQA [46]. More 391 

recent open medical language models such as PMC-LLaMA [22], BioMistral [23], MEDITRON-70B 392 

[24], and Clinical Camel [63] still rely mainly on Transformer-style backbones. In parallel, structured 393 

state space models [18], Mamba [19], and state space duality refinements [20] have provided an 394 

alternative framework with favorable scaling for long inputs [64]. However, biomedical adaptation of 395 

these models remains limited, and published work has so far focused more narrowly on settings such 396 

as clinical text modeling [25]. 397 

The present study differs from much of this literature in two ways. First, the main evidence is based 398 

on tokenizer-controlled comparisons between each released base checkpoint and the corresponding 399 

checkpoint after continued pretraining. This design isolates the effect of biomedical adaptation more 400 

directly than comparisons across unrelated public models. Second, the adaptation strategy explicitly 401 

addresses the trade-off between biomedical specialization and retention of general-language-modeling 402 

fluency. That trade-off matters in biomedical literature workflows, where specialized terminology is 403 

often mixed with broader scientific language, and it has been emphasized in continued pretraining 404 

studies [26–28]. The question also relates to ongoing debate about whether generalist foundation 405 

models can replace specialized biomedical or clinical models [65, 66]. Recent benchmark studies 406 

in biomedical NLP and real-world clinical practice text [67, 68], together with cautionary few-shot 407 

results in the biomedical domain [69], suggest that conclusions depend strongly on task formulation 408 

and evaluation protocol. Recent methodological work has also argued that benchmark gains should 409 

20 Downloaded from https://spj.science.org on May 15, 2026 be interpreted cautiously and connected to realistic biomedical use settings [57]. In that context, 410 

the present study aims to provide controlled and reproducible evidence on biomedical continued 411 

pretraining for Mamba-family models rather than to claim clinical readiness. 412 

## 4.3 New Findings 413 

Several findings merit emphasis. 414 

First, the mixture ratio mattered more than the raw inclusion of non-biomedical data. At 130M, 415 

PubMed-only continued pretraining worsened Wikipedia-style held-out perplexity by 5.2% and C4 416 

perplexity by 5.2% relative to the base model, and most alternative mixtures we tested retained 417 

the PubMed gain but still weakened at least one held-out benchmark. At 370M, the PubMed-only 418 

control was worse than the 80/10/10 recipe on Wikipedia (+69%), C4 (+15%), and even held-out 419 

PubMed itself (9.32 → 9.91; Supplementary Table S13), indicating that the forgetting penalty grows 420 

with scale and that adding general-domain data did not trade off against in-domain performance. 421 

The 80/10/10 recipe therefore appears effective because it strikes a specific balance rather than 422 

because it simply adds more data, and this balance is load-bearing beyond the original 130M pilot. 423 

Second, the downstream effect of continued pretraining after supervised fine-tuning was jointly 424 

task- and scale-dependent: PubMedQA gains were positive at every scale, MIMIC-IV gains were 425 

positive at every scale on both completion and discharge generation, while BioASQ gains were 426 

non-monotonic and the 370M and 2.7B deltas fell within the 1.22 pp single-question resolution and 427 

the seed-level variance reported in Supplementary Table S16. A likely explanation is that smaller 428 

models benefit more from additional domain exposure, whereas larger models may already contain 429 

enough transferable knowledge for some supervised tasks—yet biomedical adaptation and scaling 430 

remained complementary, since the BioMamba+SFT advantage over Mamba2+SFT persisted at 431 

every evaluated scale even where absolute ROUGE plateaued at 1.3B on MIMIC-IV. 432 

## 4.4 Limitations 433 

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting these results. The continued pretraining 434 

corpus was restricted to PubMed abstracts and did not include full-text biomedical articles, clinical 435 

narratives, electronic health records, or multimodal clinical data [70]. The downstream evaluation, 436 

although broader than yes/no question answering alone, remained limited to three task families—note 437 

completion, discharge summary generation, and yes/no question answering—and therefore does not 438 

cover the full range of biomedical language model utility. Additional tasks such as information 439 

extraction, retrieval, evidence attribution, broader summarization, and long-context reasoning were 440 

not evaluated, despite their prominence in recent biomedical and clinical benchmark suites [67, 68]. 441 

The mixture-ratio search was performed primarily in a 130M pilot study, and the selected mixture 442 

was then applied across all model scales, with a single 370M cross-scale ablation confirming that the 443 

mixture is load-bearing at larger scales. Although the cross-scale results were broadly consistent, 444 

this design does not exclude the possibility that different scales would benefit from different data 445 

proportions or training schedules. 446 

21 Downloaded from https://spj.science.org on May 15, 2026 Our comparisons with publicly released biomedical checkpoints (Tables 3 and 11) are contextual 447 

rather than controlled, as discussed in the Methods and Results sections, and should not be read 448 

as leaderboard-style performance claims. In addition, the benchmark datasets were modest in size, 449 

especially the BioASQ yes/no evaluation set, so small absolute differences should be interpreted 450 

cautiously. 451 

A further limitation is that the study did not assess calibration, uncertainty estimation, hallucina-452 

tion behavior, robustness to distribution shift, or safety in real clinical workflows [71]. Our factuality 453 

analysis (Section 3) uses NLI contradiction as a proxy for source faithfulness; this does not replace a 454 

clinician audit, and the current study does not establish clinical safety, uncertainty calibration, or 455 

workflow readiness. A prospective clinician audit with a structured error taxonomy is left to future 456 

work. The evaluation scope of this study is in-domain biomedical QA and clinical generation, together 457 

with general-language-modeling fluency; broader capabilities such as retrieval-grounded reasoning, 458 

evidence-grounded citation, structured information extraction, long-context clinical reasoning, and 459 

prospective deployment were not evaluated and are therefore hypothesized rather than demonstrated. 460 

Improvement on benchmark question answering or clinical generation does not by itself establish 461 

clinical validity or deployment readiness [57]. The present results should therefore be interpreted as 462 

evidence about language-model adaptation and transfer, not as evidence that BioMamba is suitable 463 

for autonomous clinical use. 464 

## 4.5 Future Work 465 

Several directions follow directly from these limitations. An immediate next step is to expand the 466 

continued pretraining corpus to include full-text biomedical articles, clinical guidelines, trial reports, 467 

and carefully governed clinical narratives when available. It would also be useful to study whether 468 

the optimal PubMed/C4/Wikipedia balance changes with model size, training budget, or target 469 

task. More systematic ablations on data mixture, training duration, and learning-rate schedules may 470 

further refine the adaptation strategy. 471 

Future evaluation should extend beyond the present tasks of note completion, discharge summary 472 

generation, and yes/no question answering to tasks that are closer to biomedical and clinical 473 

workflows [72], including retrieval-augmented question answering, evidence extraction, literature 474 

screening, broader summarization, uncertainty estimation, and source-grounded generation [55, 56]. 475 

It will also be important to assess robustness, calibration, and error patterns in settings with expert 476 

review. More broadly, the present results suggest that biomedical Mamba models may be most useful 477 

as components in retrieval-grounded and clinician-supervised systems rather than as standalone 478 

models. Testing that hypothesis in end-to-end health data science pipelines is an important next 479 

step. 480 

# 5 Conclusion 481 

BioMamba shows that biomedical domain-adaptive continued pretraining can strengthen public 482 

Mamba2 checkpoints without changing the underlying architecture. Across five model scales from 483 

22 Downloaded from https://spj.science.org on May 15, 2026 130M to 2.7B, BioMamba improves biomedical language modeling, preserves general-language-484 

modeling fluency through a balanced 80%/10%/10% PubMed/C4/Wikipedia training corpus, and 485 

transfers effectively to three downstream tasks: clinical note completion, discharge summary gen-486 

eration, and biomedical question answering. Beyond the empirical findings, our main contribution 487 

is a reproducible biomedical continued pretraining recipe together with a family of open-weight 488 

BioMamba checkpoints (130M / 370M / 780M / 1.3B / 2.7B), released publicly with the full training 489 

and evaluation code to provide the biomedical natural language processing community with a reusable 490 

resource. 491

The NLI-ensemble factuality proxy reported in the main text (Table 10) uses two MNLI checkpoints 844 

(roberta-large-mnli and microsoft/deberta-v2-xlarge-mnli ), applied across 500 2.7B MIMIC 845 

discharge generations per arm and per decoding setting. For each (generation, source) pair we scored 846 

entailment, neutral, and contradiction probabilities under each MNLI checkpoint. A generation is 847 

flagged as contradictory only when both checkpoints independently assign contradiction probability 848 

> 0.7 (unanimous-agreement rule, equivalent to an AND over the two scorers); this is the tie-849 

break-free specialization of plain majority voting for a two-model ensemble and is conservative, 850 

since contradiction is the minority class. Alternative rules—e.g. averaging the two contradiction 851 

probabilities and thresholding at 0.7, or OR-agreement with any one checkpoint flagging contradiction— 852 

yield higher baseline contradiction rates but do not change the relative ordering of the two arms 853 

at the decoding settings tested. The source passed to the NLI scorers is the input admission text 854 

only—chief complaint, history of present illness, past medical history, medications, allergies, social 855 

and family history, physical examination, and laboratory/imaging summaries—i.e. the same sections 856 

used as the instruction for discharge summary generation. Discharge-section text (hospital course, 857 

discharge diagnosis, discharge medications, discharge instructions) is never passed to the scorer as 858 

source, so this is an input-grounding check rather than a comparison against the reference discharge. 859
