Abstract 

Training medical vision -language models (VLMs) typically demands millions of image -text pairs to achieve versatility 

and reasoning, posing significant challenges in data acquisition. We propose ConceptVLM, a novel data -efficient fine -

tuning paradigm that transforms general -domain VLMs into specialized medical ones with minimal labeled data, 

integrating medical knowledge without disrupting the model ’s existing general capabilities. Central to our approach 

is a key concept -aware training strategy, building a structured medical concept dictionary and emplo ying masked 

attention to guide the model ’s focus toward essential clinical concepts. This focused fine -tuning enhances domain -

specific comprehension while preserving the model ’s reasoning abilities and response diversity. Experiments across 

multimodal med ical benchmarks show ConceptVLM achieves state -of -the -art results using only 1% of the original 

training data, outperforming traditional methods reliant on large -scale QA datasets. These findings challenge the 

prevailing reliance on extensive annotated cor pora, demonstrating key concept -guided tuning as a viable path to 

developing cognitively capable medical VLMs.

ARTICLE IN PRESS Introduction 

Vision -language models (VLMs), which integrate visual encoders with large language models (LLMs), hold 

transformative potential for medical image understanding and clinical decision support .1,2 By interpreting medical 

images and responding to text -based queries, these models can function as interactive assistants for clinicians and 

patients, enhancing diagnostic accuracy and improving healthcare delivery .3-5

Despite their promise, training multimodal medical VLMs presents significant challenges . Assembling a privacy -

compliant, large -scale dataset with visual –language alignment is time -consuming and requires substantial clinical 

expertise, manual annotation effort, and institutional coordination. This greatly limits the feasibility of private 

custo mization, which is especially critical in medical scenarios due to stringent patient privacy requirements. 

Moreover, medical VLMs trained in this way underperform in multi -step reasoning, clinical judgment , and 

comprehension compared to general -purpose mod els in non -medical fields such as DeepSeek, GPT -4, and Qwen ,6-8

largely because most medical QA data are templated and lack high -quality datasets capturing complex chains of 

thought. Currently, most of the medical VLMs 3,9 tend to provide only final answers or “quasi -explanations” der ived 

from pre -training pattern matching without step -by -step reasoning . This limitation reduces model transparency and 

clinician trust, potentially leading to errors if the model’s attention is not properly directed. Medical diagnosis often 

relies on subtle findings or specific regions in an image , for example , a tiny hemorrhage, drusen, or neovascularization 

may be decisive in diagnosing wet age -related macular degeneration in ophthalmology. Therefore, a key objective is 

to equip medical VLMs with the ability to focus on the most relevant visual evidence and articulate the reasoning 

behind their predictions, much like a human specialist would. 

In this work, we propose ConceptVLM, a novel multi -modal medical question -answering framework that leverages 

key concept awareness to enhance its domain expertise (Figure 1) . Rather than following the conventional three -stage 

training , ConceptVLM directly fine -tunes the general -purpose VLM and incorporates domain knowledge through a

high -quality QA corpus without compromising the generality and reasoning capabilities of the VLM. Note that 

domain -specific optimization can potentially degrade the general reasoning capacity and response diversity , and 

therefore we introduce a key concept -aware adaptation mechanism to overcome the catastrophic forgetting caused by 

limited and less diverse data fine -tuning. 

To establish such a pipeline for the medical question answering , first, w e collect and construct an instruction -tuning 

dataset, including Slake 10 , PathVQA 11 , and PolyOphthalmology, a multimodal ophthalmic dataset with stan dardised 

captions and QA pairs . We then use this dataset to develop ConceptVLM and benchmark our proposed model on 

seven multimodal VQA datasets in ophthalmology, radiology, and pathology and four reasoning sets. Figure 2 shows 

the study design. Our results demonstrate that ConceptVLM achieves higher accuracy and better handles complex, 

multi -step questions compared to the baseline model , while utilizing merely 1% of the training data required by state -

of -the -art counterparts . Notably, the proposed framework exhibits robust multitask proficiency in clinically relevant 

capabilities including radiology report generation, differential diagnosis rationalization, and interactive medical 

consultation without additional tuning . Overall, our approach demonstr ates that it is feasible to construct a robust 

medical VLM with reasoning ability using low -cost, small -scale data, thereby providing possibilities for personalized 

clinical adaptation for a broad range of researchers and hospitals. ARTICLE IN PRESS

Training data set description 

Unlike conventional training approaches for large foundation models that rely on extensive medical image -text 

instruction datasets, our method focuses on fine -tuning a general -purpose vision -language model (VLM) using a 

limited set of high -quality medical instruction data. 

ConceptVLM employs three specialized medical vision -language datasets: Slake 10 , PathVQA 11 , and 

PolyOphthalmology. Slake is a public radiology VQA dataset comprising computed tomography (CT), magnetic 

resonance imaging (MRI), and radiography (X -ray) modalities, containing 9,835 training question -answer (QA) pairs; 

PathVQA is a pathology image understanding dataset with 19,755 annotated QA pairs; PolyOphthalmology is a 

proprietary multimodal ophthalmology dataset curated by us, encompassing color fundus photography (CFP) , optical 

coherence tomography (OCT), and fundus fluorescein angiography (FFA) imaging modalities paired with domain -

specific clinical queries, totaling 13,536 training instances. The combined instruction -tuning corpus (43,126 QA pairs) 

represents a substantially more compact scale compared to conventio nal medical VLMs (Figure 3c ). Specifically, our 

training data volume aligns with LLaVA -med 3 (42k samples) while constituting merely 1% of RADFM 12 (around 3M 

samples). ARTICLE IN PRESS

Architecture of ConceptVLM 

Key concept -aware fine -tuning is the central innovation of this work. We start with a pre -trained vision -language 

model and adapt it to the medical QA domain by training on an instruction -tuning dataset . As shown in Figure 2 , the 

proposed framework comprises t hree key components. First, we leverage the pre -trained VLM 43, which has been ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS trained on a broad spectrum of natural image -text pairs. Second, we introduce an algorithmic approach to construct a 

medical key concept dictionary, enabling the extraction of cr itical medical terms from Q&A or classification datasets. 

Third, we incorporate a key concept -aware loss function, ensuring precise responses without compromising the 

diversity of output formats. 

The VLM follows the “ViT -MLP -LLM ” architecture, consisting of an image encoder, an MLP projector, and a large 

language model (LLM). Our approach diverges from traditional methods that compute cross -entropy loss directly 

between predicted and ground truth t okens. Instead, we construct a medical key concept dictionary and sample tokens 

from the LLM predictions that align with this dictionary before computing the loss. A token mask is created using the 

predicted tokens and the dictionary, setting the contribut ion of non -matching tokens to zero during loss computation. 

This ensures that only predictions matching the medical key concepts influence the loss, prioritizing medical relevance 

over format discrepancies and reducing overfitting. 

Additionally, we differ entiate between closed -ended and open -ended question -and -answer (Q&A) data in our training 

process. For closed -ended Q&A data, characterized by short, structured answers, we apply the key concept -aware loss 

across all samples. For open -ended Q&A data, whic h exhibit diverse formats and cover multiple key concept 

categories, constructing a unified dictionary is challenging. To address this, we introduce a length threshold criterion: 

if the ground truth answer is shorter than the threshold and contains at leas t one concept from the medical key concept 

dictionary , we apply the key concept -aware loss; otherwise, we revert to conventional cross -entropy loss. This dual -

strategy approach enables the model to effectively handle both structured simplicity and unstruct ured complexity. 

Multi -modal visual question answering 

We evaluated the visual question answering performance of ConceptVLM on multiple benchmarks across various 

modalities. These benchmarks included not only the in -domain test sets from radiology dataset Slake 10 , pathology 

dataset PathVQA 11 , and our private dataset PolyOphthalmology, but also out -of -domain (OOD) datasets that lie 

outside the training data domains. The OOD datasets were primarily constructed by selecting subsets from the large -

scale OmniMedVQA benchmark 9 according to modalities, comprising the Omni med_Pathology dataset, 

Omni med_CT dataset, Omni med_Ophthalmology dataset, and Omni med_Xray dataset . Figures 4a and 4b summarize 

the results, comparing our model to multiple state -of -the -art methods : (1) Intern vl -v2 -8B 43 , the baseline general -

purpose VLM model without co ncept fine -tuning, and (2) existing specialized medical VLMs: Huatuo -vision 13 , Llava -

med 3, RadFM 12 , Med -flamingo 14 , and Health -GPT 15 . For Huatuo -vision, we utilized the 7B ve rsion, and for Health -

GPT, we employed the Health -GPT -L14 model. We also demonstrate the rich medical question -answering capabilities 

of our model, providing visual examples in Figure 5a and Supplementary Figure 1 .

Overall, ConceptVLM achieved a higher overall accuracy than the baseline Intern vl -v2 in all the datasets. Specifically, 

ConceptVLM improved Intern vl -v2 by 17.9% on Omnimed_Ophthalmology, 2.5% on Omnimed_CT, 2.7% on 

Omnimed_Pathology, and 5.9% on Omnimed_Xra y dataset . Notably, the advantage of concept -aware fine -tuning was 

most pronounced on the open -ended questions . In some cases, questions requiring the identification of a specific 

abnormality or the description of a finding were answered correctly by Conce ptVLM, whereas the baseline often ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS produced incomplete or incorrect answers (Figure 5b ). While closed -ended questions were already reasonably well 

addressed by the baseline model, ConceptVLM further reduced the error rate, indicating that concept guidance helps 

the model focus on the relevant visual features when making decisions. 

Ablation results further demonstrate that each design (key concept -aware loss, medical key concept dictionary and 

dual loss strategy ) effectively improves the accuracy of medical question answering (Figure 4c ). The ‘Baseline ’ in 

Figure 4c refers to Intern vl -v2 -8B fine -tuned using standard LoRA with full answer supervision. The medical key 

concept dictionary plays a foundational role by distilling clinically salient terms from trai ning data, allowing the model 

to prioritize diagnostically relevant information rather than learning concepts implicitly from sparse QA pairs. The 

key concept -aware loss builds on this by selectively supervising only those tokens associated with entries in the 

dictionary, which sharpens attention to critical visual evidence while mitigating overfitting and preserving general 

reasoning capabilities during low -data adaptation. Meanwhile, the dual loss strategy handles the diversity of answer 

formats: it appli es concept -guided supervision to short, structured responses where medical precision is essential, and 

reverts to standard cross -entropy loss for longer, open -ended answers to maintain linguistic fluency. The necessity of 

this dual loss strategy is confirm ed in a ablation ( Supplementary Table 12 ), which shows that relying solely on the 

key concept loss for all answer types degrades performance on open -ended questions. Notably, the construction of the 

medical key concept dictionary significantly enhances the model ’s focus on medical knowledge, leading to accuracy 

improvements of 6.1%, 4.6%, and 4.5% on S lake , P ath VQA, and PolyOphthalmology, respectively. Importantly, 

qualitative analysis confirms that while the dictionary guides attention, it does not restric t the model's vocabulary. As 

illustrated in Supplementary Figure 6 , ConceptVLM maintains high semantic coherence and accurately generates 

complex clinical descriptions even when specific key medical concepts are absent from the concept dictionary 

(highligh ted in yellow). For instance, the model dynamically formulates precise pathological signs such as 

“neuroretinal rim loss ” in a glaucoma case ( Supplementary Figure 6b ) and correlates “punched -out areas ” with 

“multiple myeloma ” in gross pathology ( Supplement ary Figure 6e ). Moreover, t o verify the generality of our method, 

we have added comparative experiments with two different backbones to evaluate the compatibility of our framework 

with stronger backbones. Specifically, as shown in Supplementary Table 7, we adopted the Qwen2.5 -vl -7B 13 model, 

which outperforms Internvl -v2 -8B on two out -of -domain dataset s. The experimental results demonstrate that our 

approach achieves a 5.8% improvement on Omnimed_Ophthalmology and a 1.6% improvement on Omnimed_Xray. 

Therefore, our training framework effectively enhances the model ’s question -answering accuracy when combin ed 

with two different backbone architectures. To assess the effectiveness of concept -guided supervision, we also 

compare d ConceptVLM with a Direct Preference Optimization (DPO ) reinforcement learning baseline on 

PolyOphthalmology test set (Supplementary Ta ble 11 ). Our method outperforms DPO across all metrics —by +4.4% 

in recall , +9.6% in accuracy , and +3.6% in ROUGE -L.

For the in -domain dataset , ConceptVLM achieved mean accuracy of 76.7%, significantly outperform ing the previous 

state -of -the -art method Huatuo -vision ’s 32.4% on the PolyOphthalmology dataset (P<0.001). On Slake and PathVQA

Complex reasoning ability of ConceptVLM 

We evaluated the complex reasoning capabilities of ConceptVLM on five complex reasoning and clinical generation 

datasets: MCQs_CN 16 , MCQs_EN 16 , MedMCQA_radiology 44 , MedMCQA_ pathology 44 and MIMIC -CXR -RRG 17 .

Among these, MCQs_CN consists of ophthalmology questions in Chinese, while MCQs_EN contains ophthalmology 

questions in English. As shown in Figure 6a, Supplementary Figure 2 and Supplementary Figure 3, ConceptVLM 

can provide detailed analyses of the questions and options while giving answers , d emonstrating its ability to perform 

logical reasoning. The numerical results in Figure 6b and Supplementary Table 3 also clearly indicate that our 

training approach effectively enhances the reasoning capability of the baseline model, Internvl -v2 (all P<0.05). 

Additionally, our model achieves an accuracy of 0.692 on the Chinese reasoning dataset MCQs_CN, reaching a level 

comparable to powerful general reasoning models such as OpenAI o1 and OpenAI o3 -mini (P>0.05). However, 

regarding English reasoning capabilities, our ConceptVLM still lags behind general reasoning models, limited by the 

capacity of the baseline mo del. To deeply understand these cross -lingual performance variations, we conducted a 

granular qualitative error analysis ( Supplementary Figures 7 -8). We observed that the model's failure modes are 

highly language -dependent: the English model often suffers from "single -choice collapse" (ignoring multi -select 

instructions) and flawed exclusion logic, whereas the Chinese model tends towards "defensive over -selection" 

(sacrificing precision for recall) and false causal anchoring on historical keywords. These di stinct heuristic biases 

highlight the inherent complexities of multilingual medical alignment beyond mere domain knowledge. For 

MedMCQA_radiology and MedMCQA_ pathology datasets, we further provide experiments in Supplementary Table 

8 to validate the reason ing capabilities of ConceptVLM on radiology and pathology questions. Experimental results 

show that our method enhances Internvl -v2's reasoning capabilities in radiology and pathology, while also surpassing 

a state -of -the -art medical vision -language model. For MIMIC -CXR -RRG dataset, we evaluated the zero -shot report 

generation performance in Supplementary Table 9 . Since ConceptVLM was not explicitly fine -tuned on large -scale 

report templates, it maintained competitive, albeit statistically comparable, perfo rmance across exact -match metrics 

(BLEU, METEOR, 𝑝 > 0.05 compared to baselines). Importantly, however, it achieved a statistically significant 

improvement in the ROUGE -L metric ( 𝑝 < 0.05 ). Because ROUGE -L evaluates the longest common subsequence, 

this suggests that our concept -guided tuning helps preserve and slightly enhance the structural coherence and logical 

sequencing of clinical narratives, demonstrating the method's potential to generalize beyond short -form VQA. ARTICLE IN PRESS

Discussion 

In this study, we propose a novel paradigm for constructing large medical question -answering models: efficiently fine -

tuning a general -purpose foundational VLM to a specialized one by constraining the optimization objective through a 

key concept -aware adap tation mechanism. This approach enables the injection of critical medical information while 

preserving the generality and reasoning capabilities of the VLM. Our training strategy is specifically designed to 

address the data scarcity and limited task divers ity commonly found in medical settings, achieving strong 

generalization and reasoning capabilities using only simple classification datasets or monotonous question -answering 

datasets. 

A major advantage of ConceptVLM lies in its ability to efficiently utili ze small amounts of data, as demonstrated in 

our multimodal VQA tasks and comparisons across different ratios of training samples . In contrast, traditional medical 

VLMs typically require a large volume of instruction data and aim to diversify the forms of question -answering to 

ensure the model exhibits diversity and good generalization. 18 The difficulties in dataset construction significantly 

limit the feasibility of privatizing medical VLMs. Given the sensitivity of medical data, models that can be privat ely 

deployed at a low cost are essential. Therefore, our proposed training strategy provides a key medical concept -based 

training mode, which leverages the inherent knowledge of general -purpose VLMs and adapts them to private data, 

greatly reducing the req uirements for training data. 

Another key finding of ConceptVLM is that injecting domain -specific knowledge in the form of key concepts can 

substantially improve a general -purpose VLM ’s performance and reasoning capabilities within the medical domain. 

Importantly, our framework differs fundamentally from both conventional masking strategies and selective ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS supervision strategies. The conventional masking models mask input tokens or patches and trains the model to 

reconstruct them via self -supervision, our method operates during supervised fine -tuning and involves no input 

masking or content reconstruction. Instead, it selectively computes the training loss only over output tokens that match 

clinically salient concepts from a curated dictionary. Moreover, w hile typical selective supervision relies on heuristic 

criteria (e.g., token position, answer length, or model confidence) to downweight predictions, our selection is grounded 

in domain semantics, making it a knowledge -guided mechanism rather than a generi c efficiency trick. This approach 

differs from most existing adaptation techniques for large multimodal models. 19 Previous works on medical VLMs 

often focused on either pre -training on medical image -text data or fine -tuni ng with supervised QA pairs alone. While 

those methods capture correlations between images and answers, they do not explicitly teach the model how to reason 

about the question. In ConceptVLM, by contrast, we guide the model to consider “What is the questio n really asking? ”

through the concept prediction task. We validated the improved reasoning capability of ConceptVLM compared to the 

baseline model in medical complex reasoning tasks. This small addition has an outsized effect: it acts as an implicit 

reason ing step inserted into the training process. The model learns to break down the question into a core concept, 

mirroring how a clinician would approach a problem by identifying the relevant finding or condition before 

formulating an answer. Essentially, we align the model ’s latent reasoning by providing domain -specific simple 

question -answering data, thus avoiding the need for expensive manual annotations of full reasoning chains. In contrast 

to conventional supervised fine -tuning that applies uniform loss o ver all answer tokens, our approach focuses learning 

exclusively on high -value clinical concepts. By doing so, it not only avoids overfitting to superficial answer patterns 

but also implicitly preserves general -domain knowledge, which manifests as retained response diversity even under 

extreme data scarcity. This makes the construction of multi -task VLMs much simpler. 

Additionally, ConceptVLM demonstrates good domain generalization, achieving performance improvements on test 

sets outside the training data. The concept -aware approach essentially injects domain knowledge into the model ’s

training objective. We are leveraging the fact that the model does not need to discover from scratch which visual 

features matter , and it is pointed to the likely relevant fea ture . This guided learning is especially useful in medicine, 

where some findings can be subtle or easily overlooked by a gene ral model. Our approach shows that the key concept -

aware adaptation could not only be effective for question -answering tasks includ ed in the training data but also 

indirectly enhances the model ’s sensitivity to medical keywords. Even medical keywords not present in the training 

data exhibit a degree of probabilistic enhancement, leading to improved accuracy in answering out -of -domain 

questions. This indicates that the concept -aware approach serves as an effective means of incorporating expert 

knowledge, particularly in scenarios with limited data availability. The mechanism is analogous to instructing the 

model during training to "focu s on specific concepts in the image," which proves especially valuable for addressing 

rare or complex clinical cases. 

Moreover, ConceptVLM is more efficient and cost -effective. Many existing approaches to enhancing reasoning 

capabilities involve heavy reso urce consumption and depend on refined feedback data obtained through reinforcement 

or active learning processes, while others rely on extensive chain -of -thought datasets for supervised training —both of 

which result in significantly higher costs. By compar ison, our concept -aware adaptation training employs essential 

medical concepts to steer general -purpose vision -language models toward more accurate reasoning and responses.

ARTICLE IN PRESS and affordable solution. Furthermore, preliminary results on radiology report generation (Supplementary Table 9) 

suggest our framework offers a promising direction for mitigating structural data limitations in broader cl inical tasks. 

The statistically significant improvement in ROUGE -L points to enhanced logical flow and structural coherence. 

While VQA remains our primary focus, this initial evidence of transferability suggests that grounding models in key

Our study has several limitations. First, despite the improved response flexibility, some failure cases still exhibit 

misdiagnosis or overly simplistic answe rs, primarily due to limited data quality and pathological coverage. Second ,

our training paradigm relies on the capabilities of existing universal VLMs. For example, in English medical complex 

reasoning question -answering experiments, although our method effectively improves the baseline model ’s

performance, it still falls short of the best reasoning models. More importantly, our method inherits any weaknesses 

of the base model, such as poor image -text alignment or limited clinical knowledge, which undersc ores the need for 

foundation models better suited to healthcare. Third , due to the lack of multimodal medical knowledge, our current 

method for constructing the key concept dictionary is relatively rudimentary, relying on deduplication of answers and 

lengt h-based filtering, which may introduce noise. Finally, all evaluations are conducted on retrospective benchmark 

datasets under controlled settings. We have not yet tested the system in real clinical workflows, where factors like time 

constraints, user inte raction patterns, and integration with electronic health records would critically influence its utility 

and safety. In future work, we will evaluate the training paradigm on more capable foundation models, test the 

dictionary derived from structured medica l ontologies, explore retrieval -augmented reasoning to dynamically access 

external knowledge, and collaborate with clinicians to conduct human –AI co -evaluation studies in realistic care

Data curation and preprocessing for training 

To construct the PolyOphthalmology dataset , w e first collected 22 publicly available ophthalmology datasets, 

comprising a total of 73k images . To construct image captions, we standardized all captions using our self -developed 

condition standardized dictionary, creating captions that include information on laterality, modality, diagnosis, and

question -answer pairs from these captions, using the instruction prompts specified in Supplementary Figure 5. These 

question -answer pairs were then filtered through human validation: two ophthalmology -trained annotators 

independently reviewed each pair for clinical relevance, factual accuracy, and alignment with the source caption. Pairs 

with ambiguous, incorrect, or hallucinated content were discarded. Ultimately, we sampled 13,536 QA pairs for the ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS training set, including 1,853 on mod ality, 2,000 on laterality, 7,023 on diagnosis, and 2,660 open -ended image 

description pairs.

ARTICLE IN PRESS Model design and training details 

ConceptVLM employs pretrained InternViT -300M 45 as the vision encoder. The InternViT -300M model consists of 0.3 

billion parameters, 24 layers, a hidden size of 1024, and 16 attention heads. It employs standard LayerNorm without 

QK -Norm and is also pre -trained with the Next Token Prediction (NTP) loss. The language model used in 

ConceptVLM is the InternLM2 -Chat -7B model 46 , which has been trained on extensive multimodal da tasets such as 

ShareGPT4V 47 , LLaVA -150K 48 and VQAv2 49 . InternLM2 -Chat -7B is a 7 -billion -parameter decoder -only Transformer, 

featuring Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) and Grouped Query Attention (GQA) to optimize memory efficiency 

while supporting 200K -token context windows. Each transformer layer features pre -layer normalization for stability 

and SwiGLU activation functions in feed -forward networks for enhanced nonlinear modeling. The model supports 

multi -image and video data alongside single -image and text -only data, enhancing its scalabili ty for high -resolution 

processing .

Medical key concept dictionary 

To accurately capture medical knowledge related to image -based questions while preventing the model from 

overemphasizing the form of responses, we have designed an algorithm for constructing a medical key concept 

dictionary. 5,50,51 For closed -end Q&A data, which typically require a specific word or phrase as an answer, such as 

identifying the modality of an image or categorizing a disease, we compile the c losed -end key concept dictionary by 

directly tallying all unique answers across the dataset. This involves counting each distinct response and then removing 

duplicates, ensuring that the resulting dictionary encompasses all possible closed -form answers. Re garding open -end 

Q&A data, which can vary widely in format and content, we employ a length threshold criterion to distinguish between 

simple and complex responses. For answers containing fewer than K words, we construct an open -end key concept 

dictionary. This is motivated by the observation that very short open responses (e.g., “cystoid macular edema ”) are 

term -dense clinical phrases rather than free -form narratives, and thus benefit from focused supervision on high -value 

tokens. We set K as 5 based on parameter investigation experiments in Supplementary Table 4 on the 

PolyOphthalmology validation set. These shorter responses are likely to contain critical terms that can be effectively 

captured using our key concept -matching approach. Conversely, for res ponses exceeding the K-word threshold, 

indicating more complex or nuanced answers, we apply the traditional cross -entropy loss without additional 

processing. This allows the model to learn from diverse and detailed responses without imposing restrictive co nstraints.

The model architecture is an extension of the Intern vl -v2 -8B foundational model, featuring a frozen backbone and 

LLM parameters, while keeping the MLP layers trainable. To ensure parameter -efficient tuning, a LoRA rank of 256 

is applied to the LLM component. The len gth threshold K in medical key concept dictionary construction algorithm 

is set as 5. The training setup utilizes 2 GPUs, with each processing 4 samples per batch; gradients are accumulated 

over 8 steps to achieve an effective global batch size of 64. A dy namic resolution mechanism is employed to adaptively 

scale input images from a base size of 448 pixels, supporting up to 6 patch divisions and 50% downsampling, alongside 

thumbnail optimization to minimize computational overhead. Optimization is carried ou t using AdamW, with an initial 

learning rate of 1e -5, incorporating cosine decay scheduling, a 1% warmup phase, and a weight decay of 0.01. The 

training regimen covers 6 epochs, employing gradient checkpointing and limiting sequence lengths to 2,048 tokens .

The total training time was approximately 32 GPU -hours, with a peak GPU memory consumption of approximately 

69 GB. 

We utilized Python version 3.9.20, alongside a suite of open -source libraries that enabled comprehensive 

experimentation and analysis. For the core of our training efforts, single NVIDIA A800 GPUs, each equipped with 

80GB of VRAM, were employed. These GPUs were configured for multi -GPU training through 

DistributedDataParallel module (PyTorch version 2.2.1, CUDA 12.1), facilitating efficient p arallel processing and 

significantly reducing training time. The underlying hardware also included an AMD EPYC 7713 64 -Core Processor, 

providing robust computational support necessary for handling auxiliary tasks and data preprocessing. To manage 

large -sca le model training more effectively, we incorporated DeepSpeed (version 0.13.5), which helped in optimizing 

memory usage and enabling the training of larger models than would otherwise be possible. Image input processing 

leveraged the Pillow library (versio n 11.0.0), ensuring high -quality image data handling. Additionally, TensorBoard 

(version 2.18.0) was used for visualization and monitoring of the training process, allowing us to track performance 

metrics and adjust parameters as needed. For specific funct ionalities within our pipeline, we employed Timm (version 

0.9.12) for vision model architectures, torchvision (version 0.17.2) for additional image transformations and utilities, 

and OpenCV -Python (version 4.10.0.84) for advanced image processing tasks. Th is combination of hardware and 

software components created a powerful and flexible environment tailored to the demands of our ConceptVLM model 

training, enabling both efficiency and effectiveness in our research endeavors. 

Statistical Analysis 

Two -sided t -tests were used to compare the accuracy and recall of ConceptVLM with those of other models. We used 

nonparametric bootstrapping to construct 95% confidence intervals (CI) calculated using 1.96 × standard error, 

sampling with the size of the te st set. The P value was the proportion of differences in model performance greater than 

the observed difference in terms of absolute value. The null hypothesis was that there was no difference in model 

performance for the given test set and evaluation metric .
