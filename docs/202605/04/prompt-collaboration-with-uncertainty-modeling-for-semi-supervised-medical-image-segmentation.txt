## Abstract

Deep learning models for medical image segmentation typically require extensive annotations that are labor-intensive to obtain. While the segment anything model (SAM) has been adopted to facilitate semi-supervised learning via prompt-driven pseudo-labeling, its performance is often hindered by confirmation bias. This issue arises because prompts are conventionally derived only from model predictions in high confidence regions, causing the network to reinforce its existing knowledge while failing to correct errors in ambiguous regions. In this work, we present the prompt collaboration with uncertainty modeling (PCUM) framework to facilitate robust segmentation under extremely limited supervision. Unlike previous methods, our approach employs a dual-prompting mechanism that simultaneously leverages prompts from reliable high confidence regions and informative high uncertainty regions. This strategy enables the model to maintain structural stability while explicitly refining its performance on challenging decision boundaries. Furthermore, we fuse the predictions from these complementary views to provide rectified supervision and apply consistency-regularization to ensure robust feature alignment. Extensive experiments on the BraTS and ACDC datasets validate the superiority of our method, where PCUM achieves a Dice score of 73.12% using only 10 labeled data on BraTS and 85.42% using a single labeled case on ACDC.

## Introduction

Medical image segmentation, as a core task in intelligent diagnostic systems, plays a fundamental role in modern healthcare. It supports various clinical applications, including tumor identification [1], [2], organ modeling [3], [4], preoperative planning [5], [6], and disease monitoring [7], [8]. In recent years, driven by advances in deep learning, medical image segmentation has achieved remarkable progress [9], [10], [11], particularly with the emergence of models such as U-Net [12], [13], [14] and Transformer-based architectures [15], [16], [17], which have significantly enhanced the capability of modeling complex anatomical structures. However, these methods still encounter significant challenges in real-world deployment. One of the most fundamental issues is their reliance on large-scale, high-quality, pixel-level annotated datasets.

Semi-supervised learning (SSL) has emerged as a promising approach to address the challenge of limited annotated data in medical image segmentation. Its core objective is to fully exploit the latent semantic information in unlabeled data with limited supervision, thereby enhancing model performance. Mainstream semi-supervised segmentation approaches can be broadly categorized into two strategies: consistency-regularization [18], [19], [20], [21] and pseudo-labeling [22], [23], [24], [25]. Consistency-regularization is grounded in the consistency assumption, which posits that a model’s predictions should remain consistent under different perturbations to its input. This strategy encourages the network to learn robust representations. Pseudo-labeling follows a self-training paradigm, where the model generates pseudo-labels for unlabeled data and incorporates them into the training process.

Even though pseudo-labeling demonstrates promising scalability in practice, its performance is heavily dependent on the reliability of the generated pseudo-labels. Given the limited segmentation capability of early-stage models, introducing low-quality pseudo-labels can lead to semantic drift and unstable training, thereby impeding convergence. To address this issue, recent studies have increasingly integrated confidence estimation and uncertainty modeling techniques aimed at enhancing the accuracy and robustness of pseudo-label selection. For example, Luo et al. introduced an efficient pseudo-labeling strategy by estimating uncertainty from multi-scale predictions in a single pass, reweighting the pseudo-labels for more robust semi-supervised segmentation [26]; Su et al. proposed a mutual learning method with reliable pseudo-labels, which selects and weights them via confidence comparison and intra-class consistency between subnetworks [27].

Recent studies have explored foundation models, such as the segment anything model (SAM) [28], [29], to generate more reliable pseudo-labels for semi-supervised training [30], [31]. These methods utilize prompts derived from model predictions or task-specific priors to guide the model’s focus, reduce pseudo-label bias, and better exploit unlabeled data. However, existing prompt generation mechanisms often fall into a confirmation bias loop. Prompts generated from the model’s own predictions tend to over-concentrate on easy, high confidence regions, while failing to explore challenging or uncertain regions, leading to biased supervision and error accumulation.

To address these challenges, we introduce a novel prompt collaboration with uncertainty modeling (PCUM) semi-supervised framework for robust medical image segmentation, as shown in Fig. 1. Our approach tackles the problems of unreliable pseudo-labels and consistent regularization based on the pre-trained foundation model through two core contributions: First, instead of relying solely on potentially biased predictions, our mechanism leverages prompts extracted from multiple (i.e., confidence and uncertainty) regions to guide pseudo-label generation. This dual-prompting encourages the model to learn from both reliable and ambiguous regions, thereby improving the robustness and diversity of the generated pseudo-labels. Second, we introduce a cross-prompt consistency strategy, which enforces alignment between the model’s predictions with different prompts, using the fused pseudo-labels as robust supervision. This collaborative optimization framework ensures effective pseudo-supervision throughout the training process. The main contributions of this study are summarized as follows:

*   •
We propose the PCUM framework, which integrates an uncertainty-guided prompt generation strategy with a cross-prompt consistency supervision mechanism, systematically enhancing the quality and robustness of pseudo-labels under limited annotation conditions.

*   •
We design a novel dual-prompting strategy that extracts prompt points from both confidence and uncertainty regions, thereby facilitating the generation of pseudo-labels.

*   •
We present a series of consistency supervision mechanisms, where fused pseudo-labels serve as a bridge to enforce consistency constraints among different segmentation decoders.

*   •
Extensive evaluations on BraTs [32] and ACDC [33] medical image segmentation tasks demonstrate that our method outperforms state-of-the-art methods in terms of segmentation accuracy, uncertainty region modeling, and pseudo-label robustness, exhibiting strong ability and practical potential.

## Conclusion

In this paper, we addressed the critical challenge of medical image segmentation under limited data supervision. We introduced PCUM, a novel uncertainty-guided collaborative framework built upon the SAM. The core of PCUM is a dual-prompting strategy that leverages both confidence and uncertainty regions to generate diverse and informative prompts. These prompts guide the collaborative creation and dynamic fusion of pseudo-labels, yielding highly robust supervisory signals for unlabeled data.
