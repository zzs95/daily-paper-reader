## Abstract

## INTRODUCTION AND OBJECTIVES:

In the modern MRI era, PSA density (PSAD) derived from multiparametric MRI has become an accessible biomarker for risk stratification in localized prostate cancer. Although widely used, the optimal PSAD threshold for predicting adverse pathology and long-term outcomes after radical prostatectomy remains uncertain. This study is to determine the prognostic utility and optimal cutoff of MRI-based PSA density in predicting adverse pathology and oncologic outcomes among men undergoing robot-assisted radical prostatectomy (RARP).

## METHODS:

This study analyzed a prospectively maintained MRI-era cohort of 812 consecutive patients who underwent RARP between 2013 and 2023. All patients had preoperative multiparametric MRI with volumetric assessment, biopsy and pathologic grade group data, and long-term follow-up for biochemical and metastatic progression.

## RESULTS:

Median age was 63 years, median PSA 7.4 ng/mL, median prostate volume 42 ml, and median MRI PSAD 0.16 ng/mL/cm 3. Adverse pathology occurred in 43% of patients, with 16% harboring positive margins, 7% SVI, and 4.6% nodal involvement. On multivariable analysis, MRI PSAD independently predicted adverse pathology (OR 14.1, 95% CI 4.8–41.6, p<0.001), SVI (OR 2.7, p=0.036), PSM (OR 3.4, p=0.004), and PSA persistence (OR 4.7, p=0.003). DCA demonstrated that PSAD ≥0.10 provided the greatest overall net benefit, while ≥0.15 achieved better specificity and performed best in men with PI-RADS <3. Elevated PSAD was associated with significantly reduced BCRFS and MFS (log-rank p<0.001).

## CONCLUSIONS:

MRI PSA density is a strong, independent predictor of adverse pathology and oncologic outcomes in the MRI era. A threshold of 0.15 ng/mL/cm 3 offers the most balanced clinical utility overall, whereas 0.10 ng/mL/cm 3 may be more appropriate in men with PI-RADS≥3 above lesion.
