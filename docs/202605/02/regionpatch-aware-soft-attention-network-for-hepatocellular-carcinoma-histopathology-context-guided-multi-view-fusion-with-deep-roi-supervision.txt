## Abstract

Due to ultra-high resolution, irregular lesion morphology, and limited background information in local regions, the accurate classification of hepatocellular carcinoma based on hematoxylin-and-eosin (H&E) whole-slide images remains challenging. To address this, we propose the Region-to-Patch Aware Soft Attention Network (RPA-SAN), which adopts a dual-input framework that simultaneously integrates local details with semantic information from surrounding regions to simulate the macro-to-micro reasoning process in pathological diagnosis. Specifically, RPA-SAN injects semantic information from surrounding regions into local patch representations via inter-region cross-attention and learns soft attention maps aligned with patch of interest under Dice-based deep supervision, enhancing the discriminative power and interpretability.

On the internal EHBH dataset, RPA-SAN achieved accuracy of 0.977 [0.964–0.990], an F1 score of 0.956 [0.943–0.969], and an AUC of 0.963 [0.947–0.983], on the external TCGA dataset, its F1 score and AUC reached 0.948 [0.945–0.952] and 0.953 [0.951–0.956], respectively, indicating that the model demonstrates excellent classification performance and generalization capabilities across different datasets.

Furthermore, to evaluate the applicability of RPA-SAN under WSI-level supervision only, we incorporated pseudo-labels generated by weakly supervised methods into the training process and systematically compared the performance of different weak supervision strategies in this setting. The results show that all weak supervision methods yield performance improvements when combined with RPA-SAN. Specifically, Pseudo-RPA-SAN under the best weak-supervision model achieved ACC and AUC values of 0.943 and 0.921, respectively, approaching the performance ceiling of the fully supervised RPA-SAN. These results demonstrate that RPA-SAN not only performs excellently under fully supervised conditions but also exhibits strong discriminative power and robustness in weak-supervision scenarios.The source code is available at https://github.com/dirtydog0811/RPA-SAN.

## Introduction

Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is one of the most common malignant tumors worldwide, with high incidence and mortality rates; early detection and accurate diagnosis are crucial for improving patient prognosis [1], [2], [3]. In clinical practice, histopathological examination remains a key basis for the diagnosis of HCC [4], [5]. With the advancement of digital pathology and artificial intelligence technologies, researchers have begun to explore the integration of various observation and imaging methods into pathological identification tasks. These include digital pathology analysis based on conventional hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) slides [6], [7], as well as technical approaches such as hyperspectral imaging [8], [9] and immunohistochemistry (IHC)-assisted imaging [10], [11]. Among these, hyperspectral imaging provides additional spatial–spectral information, while immunohistochemistry offers supplementary evidence at the molecular biomarker level. H&E sections, however, remain the most commonly used method in histopathological observation due to their well-established preparation processes, widespread application, and relatively uniform diagnostic criteria. Against this backdrop, intelligent analysis of whole-slide H&E images has become a major focus in current computational pathology research [12], [13], [14].

Since H&E whole-slide images typically have gigapixel-level resolution, most existing computational models require dividing them into smaller image patches before performing feature learning and result prediction. Within this framework, several representative approaches have gradually emerged. One category, represented by the MobileNetV2 [15], VGG11 [16], and ResNet [17] series, primarily relies on convolutional neural networks to extract local texture and cellular morphological features; another category, represented by ViT [18] and Swin-Transformer [19], places greater emphasis on modeling cross-regional relationships and long-range dependencies. Additionally, some studies have attempted to address the issue of insufficient information in a single patch through multi-scale fusion or local–global joint modeling. For example, MV-ViT [20] integrates features of different resolutions via multi-scale stages and pooling attention, while Fu.2025 [21] employs dual branches—a CNN and a Transformer—to encode local and global information, respectively; Other methods enhance the model focus on lesion area by introducing additional spatial supervision, such as GuSA [22], which utilizes ROI masks to constrain the training process. Overall, these methods have advanced the analysis of H&E pathological images from the perspectives of local representation, global dependencies, multi-scale fusion, and spatial supervision. However, for the classification of HCC, the semantic relationship between local patches and surrounding tissue remains under-modeled. In fact, pathologists typically follow a macro-to-micro interpretation process: first, they quickly scan the entire section under low magnification to locate suspicious areas, and then make a final diagnosis under high magnification by integrating cellular morphological features. In contrast, many existing patch-based methods still rely primarily on local patch discrimination or post-processing feature aggregation [23], [24], [25], making it difficult to fully capture this “macro-to-micro” diagnostic logic; simultaneously, the area of interest learned by the models may not consistently correspond to actual lesion areas, thereby affecting classification stability and interpretability. How to establish a closer connection between local discrimination and regional semantics, and how to better align the model attention patterns with pathological lesions, remains a topic worthy of further research.

Based on the above considerations, we propose the Region–Patch Aware Soft Attention Network (RPA-SAN). This method uses region–patch paired views, which conform to the logic of pathological interpretation, as the basic input units. Through the region –patch cross-attention mechanism to explicitly inject semantic context from surrounding tissue into local patch representations. By applying Dice-based deep supervision on soft attention maps aligned with lesion ROIs, the method enhances the model ability to focus on key area, reduces interference from mixed tissue, and improves classification stability and interpretability. Unlike modeling approaches that rely solely on local patches or post-processing aggregation, RPA-SAN places greater emphasis on the synergistic modeling of macroscopic tissue structure and microscopic cellular morphology.

To validate the effectiveness of this method, we first conducted a systematic evaluation of RPA-SAN on an internal EHBH cohort and an external TCGA cohort to examine its classification performance and generalization ability under different data distributions. Furthermore, considering the high cost of obtaining fine-grained ROI annotations in real-world pathology settings, we conducted additional experiments using pseudo-annotations generated by weakly supervised methods [26], [27] on the Camelyon16 dataset [28] to evaluate the framework applicability under annotation-constrained conditions. Specifically, we trained RPA-SAN using pseudo-labels generated by weakly supervised methods to assess its classification capability in the absence of fine-grained annotations. The results demonstrate that the framework can achieve effective classification even without the use of true fine-grained annotations during training, indicating that RPA-SAN is not only suitable for fully annotated scenarios but also possesses a certain degree of annotation adaptability and practical application potential.

Overall, this study aims to propose a patch-level classification framework that integrates pathological interpretation logic, spatial alignment capabilities, and cross-scale semantic modeling, providing a robust and interpretable solution for hepatocellular carcinoma identification in complex histological structures. Additionally, this study demonstrates that the framework retains a certain degree of usability under annotation-constrained conditions, offering new insights for intelligent pathological analysis in real-world clinical settings.
