## Introduction

Radiology Report Generation (RRG) aims to automatically generate detailed and meaningful diagnostic reports from medical images (Chen et al., 2020). The traditional manual reporting process is often inefficient and time-consuming, with report quality strongly influenced by individual radiologist experience (Li et al., 2024, Zhao et al., 2025). With the rapid growth of medical imaging data, radiologists face an increasing burden of image interpretation and report writing, which further constrains improvements in clinical workflow efficiency (Cao and Cheng, 2025, Hou, Li, Wang et al., 2025, A. Liu et al., 2024, Zhang, Yang et al., 2024). Therefore, RRG has emerged as a critical application of artificial intelligence in medical imaging, playing an important role in improving healthcare service efficiency and diagnostic quality (Bu et al., 2024, Ding et al., 2024, Hou, Li, Lu et al., 2025, Mei et al., 2024).

Breakthroughs in image captioning have significantly advanced the field of RRG, establishing encoder–decoder architectures as the dominant paradigm. These frameworks typically employ Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to hierarchically extract discriminative visual features from medical images, coupled with Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) to autoregressively generate structured textual descriptions. Extensive research (Jing et al., 2018, Xue et al., 2018) has demonstrated the effectiveness of such encoder–decoder architectures in generating preliminary medical reports. Building upon this foundation, subsequent studies (Hou, Liu et al., 2023, Hou, Sang et al., 2023, Huang et al., 2023, Liang et al., 2024) have incorporated domain-specific knowledge and medical annotations to improve the semantic richness and clinical relevance of the generated text. Additionally, given the inherently multi-modal nature of RRG, recent research (Chen et al., 2021, Hou, Li, Liu et al., 2025, Li et al., 2022, Song et al., 2026) has prioritized sophisticated cross-modal fusion techniques, with particular emphasis on memory-enhanced mechanisms to facilitate the alignment of heterogeneous visual and textual representations. Recent advances in RRG have mainly focused on improving visual encoding or cross-modal interaction, including knowledge-enhanced frameworks, memory-based architectures, and diffusion-based extensions. However, as summarized in Table 1, these methods still largely rely on global or generic visual representations that lack sensitivity to fine-grained pathological details, and often adopt coarse or implicit cross-modal interaction mechanisms that fail to establish fine-grained semantic alignment. This limitation restricts their ability to accurately capture subtle pathological patterns and establish precise visual-textual correspondence. This observation suggests that effective radiology report generation requires not only stronger pathology-aware visual refinement, but also a more explicit cross-modal alignment framework.

Despite significant advancements in representation learning, existing methods for radiology report generation still face two fundamental challenges:

*   •
**Low semantic pathological representation:** Accurate lesion characterization remains challenging due to the inherent complexities of radiological images. Pathologies such as pulmonary nodules or cardiomegaly often exhibit small spatial footprints, low intensity contrast, and indistinct boundaries, all of which significantly hinder precise localization and semantic encoding. Although recent methods leverage CNNs (Jing et al., 2018, Xue et al., 2018) and incorporate medical knowledge (Hou, Sang et al., 2023, Huang et al., 2023, Liang et al., 2024) to enhance visual representations, CNN-based architectures rely on repeated downsampling and local receptive fields. This design tends to suppress weak signals from small or low-contrast lesions, making them poorly suited for detecting early-stage abnormalities. In addition, knowledge-based methods rely on static, predefined knowledge structures, this limits adaptability to subtle or previously unseen pathological patterns.

*   •
**Heterogeneous modality interaction barrier:** The modality gap between continuous spatial visual features (encoding local anatomical structures) and discrete textual semantics (conveying clinical abstractions) remains under-addressed. Although cross-modal memory networks (Chen et al., 2021, Hou, Li, Liu et al., 2025, Li et al., 2022) have improved model performance, their static memory architecture inherently limits adaptive refinement of pathological features, while rigid cross-modal operations fail to establish hierarchical semantic alignment.

In this paper, we propose a Latent Diffusion-augmented Cross-modal Representation learning framework (LDCR) for radiology report generation. The framework incorporates a Latent Diffusion Enhancement Module (LDEM), which leverages a diffusion-based degradation-reconstruction process in latent space to refine disease-relevant semantics and enhance subtle pathological features. Building upon the refined visual representations, a Heterogeneous Information Fusion Module (HIFM) is introduced to align visual and textual modalities in a shared semantic space through bidirectional interaction, enabling fine-grained cross-modal semantic fusion. Experiments on two public RRG benchmarks demonstrate that LDCR achieves state-of-the-art performance against strong baselines. Ablation studies and hyperparameter analyses further validate the effectiveness of the proposed components. Our contributions are summarized as follows:

*   •
We propose a Latent Diffusion-augmented Cross-modal Representation learning (LDCR) framework that enhances subtle lesion representation and bridges visual-textual modality gaps through latent diffusion-enhanced pathology modeling and deep cross-modal alignment.

*   •
LDCR introduces two core innovations: a latent diffusion enhancement module that captures subtle lesions via degradation-reconstruction learning, and a heterogeneous information fusion module that aligns diffusion-enhanced visual features with clinical texts through shared semantic projection and bidirectional interaction.

*   •
We conduct extensive experiments on IU X-ray and MIMIC-CXR, where LDCR achieves consistently competitive and overall superior performance across standard natural language generation and clinical efficacy metrics.
