1 INTRODUCTION 

Liver cancer remains one of the leading causes of cancer-related mortality worldwide. Accurate segmentation 

of liver tumors from medical images plays a crucial role in clinical diagnosis, treatment planning, and disease 

monitoring. While contrast-enhanced CT (CECT) improves lesion visibility and is widely used in clinical 

practice, non-enhanced CT (NECT) is often preferred in certain scenarios because it is safer, more 

economical, and suitable for patients for whom contrast agents are contraindicated [9] . However, the absence 

of contrast enhancement significantly reduces lesion – parenchyma contrast, making automatic tumor 

segmentation from NECT images considerably more challenging. 

Recent advances in deep learning have substantially improved the performance of medical image 

segmentation. In particular, encoder –decoder architectures such as U-Net and its variants have become the 

dominant paradigm [1], [2] . More recently, the nnU-Net framework has demonstrated strong and consistent 

performance across a wide range of biomedical segmentation tasks by automatically configuring 

preprocessing strategies, network architectures, and training pipelines based on dataset characteristics [3] .

Despite these advances, segmentation of liver tumors in NECT images remains difficult due to several 

inherent challenges. 

First, severe foreground –background class imbalance exists, as tumor voxels typically occupy only a small 

fraction of the entire image volume. Standard loss functions may therefore be dominated by background 

regions, leading to insufficient learning of small or low-contrast lesions. Second, boundary ambiguity caused 

by low contrast and intensity inhomogeneity makes accurate delineation difficult. In practice, segmentation 

models may achieve reasonable overlap metrics while still producing large boundary errors due to a small 

number of distant false-positive regions, which can disproportionately affect boundary-based metrics such as 

the 95th percentile Hausdorff distance (HD95). These challenges highlight the importance of designing 

training objectives and evaluation protocols that are robust to class imbalance and sensitive to clinically 

meaningful detection performance. 

To address these issues under small-data conditions, this work presents a compact and reproducible 

baseline based on nnU-Net v2 (3D full-resolution). We introduce ComboLoss, a weighted combination of Dice 

loss and focal loss that emphasizes difficult foreground voxels while remaining fully compatible with nnU-Net 

training mechanisms, including deep supervision and ignore-label masking. In addition, we adopt a

standardized evaluation protocol that incorporates both voxel-level metrics and lesion-level detection 

performance in order to provide a more comprehensive assessment of model behavior. 3

The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows: 

1. An imbalance-aware training objective. 

We propose ComboLoss, a simple Dice-plus-Focal objective that improves learning of rare tumor voxels 

without modifying the nnU-Net architecture. 

2. A controlled comparison of loss functions. 

We evaluate Dice+CE, ComboLoss, and Focal Tversky Loss under an identical nnU-Net v2 configuration, 

enabling a fair analysis of loss design under severe class imbalance. 

3. A reproducible NECT segmentation baseline. 

We provide a compact and standardized training and evaluation pipeline for small-data NECT liver tumor 

segmentation. 

2 RELATED WORK 

2.1 Loss Functions for Class Imbalance 

Class imbalance is a fundamental challenge in medical image segmentation, particularly in tumor detection 

tasks where foreground voxels typically occupy only a very small fraction of the total image volume. Under 

such conditions, standard pixel-wise loss functions such as cross-entropy tend to be dominated by easy 

background samples, leading to suboptimal learning of rare and clinically important structures. 

To mitigate this issue, Dice-based losses have been widely adopted because they directly optimize region 

overlap between prediction and ground truth. Variants such as soft Dice loss and generalized Dice loss 

improve robustness to class imbalance by reducing the influence of background-dominated gradients [12] .

However, Dice-based objectives alone may still be insufficient to emphasize hard examples, especially for 

very small or low-contrast lesions. 

Focal loss was originally introduced to address class imbalance in dense object detection by down-

weighting easy samples and focusing on difficult examples [5] . This idea has been extended to medical image 

segmentation to improve sensitivity to small lesions. In addition, the Tversky loss introduces asymmetric 

weighting of false positives and false negatives, enabling explicit control over precision –recall trade-offs [6] .

Focal Tversky loss further combines focal weighting with Tversky imbalance modeling to enhance 

performance under extreme class imbalance [7] .

In this work, we adopt a simple yet effective strategy by combining Dice loss and focal loss into a unified 

objective, referred to as ComboLoss. Compared with more complex formulations, this design maintains 

compatibility with standard training pipelines while improving learning of difficult foreground voxels. 

2.2 Deep Learning for Liver Tumor Segmentation 

Deep learning has significantly advanced automatic liver and tumor segmentation in recent years. Encoder –

decoder architectures, particularly U-Net and its variants, have become the dominant paradigm due to their 

ability to capture both local and global contextual information [1] . Extensions such as 3D U-Net further 

improve volumetric feature representation [2] , while attention mechanisms have been introduced to enhance 

focus on relevant anatomical structures. Public liver tumor segmentation benchmarks such as LiTS have 

substantially advanced algorithm development in CT imaging [8]. 4

More recently, transformer-based architectures have been explored to model long-range dependencies in 

medical images. Although these approaches can improve representation capacity, they often require large-

scale datasets and careful tuning, which may limit their effectiveness in small-data scenarios. 

Despite these advances, segmentation of liver tumors from non-enhanced CT images remains challenging. 

The absence of contrast enhancement reduces lesion visibility and increases ambiguity in tumor boundaries. 

In addition, small lesions are particularly difficult to detect, making the segmentation task highly sensitive to 

training objectives and evaluation strategies. 

2.3 nnU-Net as a Reproducible Baseline 

The nnU-Net framework has emerged as a strong and widely adopted baseline for medical image 

segmentation [3] . It automatically configures preprocessing, network architecture, training schedules, and 

inference procedures based on dataset-specific characteristics, enabling consistent and reproducible 

performance across a wide range of tasks. 

Because nnU-Net standardizes most components of the segmentation pipeline, it provides an ideal 

platform for controlled comparisons of different training objectives. In this work, we adopt nnU-Net v2 (3D full-

resolution) as the backbone and focus on improving loss design under severe class imbalance without 

modifying the network architecture. This allows us to isolate the impact of loss functions while maintaining a

consistent and reproducible experimental setup. 

3 METHODS 

3.1 Backbone and Pre-processing 

We adopt nnU-Net v2 (3D full-resolution) as the backbone for liver tumor segmentation , following the self-

configuring nnU-Net paradigm [3] . nnU-Net provides a self-configuring framework that automatically 

determines preprocessing strategies, network architecture, and training configurations according to dataset 

properties such as voxel spacing, image resolution, and intensity distribution. 

The preprocessing pipeline includes resampling to a target spacing, foreground cropping, and z-score 

intensity normalization. During training, standard nnU-Net data augmentation is applied, including spatial 

transformations and intensity scaling. The network architecture follows a 3D encoder –decoder design with 

skip connections, and deep supervision is introduced at multiple decoder stages. 

In this work, we keep the nnU-Net architecture and training pipeline unchanged, and focus only on 

improving the loss design to address severe foreground –background imbalance in NECT liver tumor 

segmentation. The overall framework, including nnU-Net v2 preprocessing, ComboLoss-based training, and 

optional connected-component post-processing, is illustrated in Fig. 1. 5

3.4 Comparison with Alternative Loss Functions 

To evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed objective, we compare ComboLoss with several commonly 

used loss functions under the same nnU-Net v2 configuration. 

First, we include Dice + Cross-Entropy (Dice+CE), which is the default objective in nnU-Net and serves as 

the baseline. Second, we evaluate Tversky Loss [6] , which introduces asymmetric penalties for false positives 

and false negatives and is often used in highly imbalanced segmentation tasks. Third, we include Focal 

Tversky Loss [7] , which further emphasizes hard examples under severe class imbalance. 

All loss functions are trained and evaluated under identical preprocessing, backbone, training, and 

inference settings, so that the comparison focuses only on the effect of loss design in NECT liver tumor 

segmentation. 

3.5 Inference and Post-processing 

During inference, we follow the standard nnU-Net sliding-window strategy with Gaussian weighting. The 

predicted probability map is thresholded at 0.5 to obtain the final binary segmentation mask. Unless otherwise 

specified, test-time augmentation is not applied. 

Boundary-based metrics such as HD95 can be highly sensitive to isolated false-positive regions. Even a

small number of distant components may disproportionately increase the measured boundary distance. To 

improve robustness, we evaluate a simple connected-component (CC) post-processing step as an ablation. 

Specifically, small disconnected components can be removed according to a volume threshold, or only the 

largest connected components can be retained. 

This post-processing strategy does not change the network itself and provides a lightweight way to reduce 

boundary outliers in the final segmentation results. 

4 EXPERIMENTS 

4.1 Dataset and Data Split 

We conduct experiments on an institute-provided non-enhanced CT (NECT) liver tumor segmentation dataset 

consisting of 40 volumetric scans with binary tumor annotations. To ensure consistency across all 

experiments, we follow a fixed split of 28 cases for training and 12 cases for testing. 

All scans are de-identified prior to analysis. No manual post-processing or case-specific correction is 

applied to the model outputs unless explicitly stated in the post-processing ablation. This setting allows a

controlled comparison of different loss functions under the same backbone and data conditions. 7

Because the dataset is relatively small and tumors often occupy only a limited fraction of the image volume, 

it provides a suitable benchmark for evaluating the effect of imbalance-aware loss design in NECT liver tumor 

segmentation. 

4.2 Implementation Details 

All experiments are implemented within the nnU-Net v2 framework using the 3D full-resolution configuration. 

The preprocessing, network architecture, augmentation strategy, optimizer, and inference pipeline are kept 

identical across all compared methods. In this way, the only experimental variable is the loss function. 

The default preprocessing includes resampling, foreground cropping, and intensity normalization as 

determined automatically by nnU-Net. During training, standard nnU-Net data augmentation is applied. Deep 

supervision is enabled following the default training configuration. 

For the proposed ComboLoss, the hyperparameters are set to 𝐀 = 0.3 , 𝐀 = 0.3 , and 𝐀 = 2.5 . The final 

model checkpoint is selected according to the highest pseudo-Dice score on the validation stage within the 

nnU-Net training process. 

During inference, we use the standard sliding-window strategy with Gaussian weighting. The probability 

threshold is set to 0.5 to generate binary segmentation masks. Unless otherwise stated, test-time 

augmentation is disabled in order to keep the evaluation setting compact and reproducible. 

4.3 Compared Loss Functions 

To assess the impact of loss design under severe class imbalance, we compare three training objectives 

under the same nnU-Net v2 backbone and identical experimental settings. 

The first objective is Dice + Cross-Entropy (Dice+CE), which is the default loss in nnU-Net and serves as 

the baseline. The second is the proposed ComboLoss, which combines Dice loss and focal loss to improve 

learning of hard foreground voxels. The third is Focal Tversky Loss, which further emphasizes hard examples 

by combining focal modulation with Tversky-style imbalance control. 

This comparison is designed to isolate the influence of loss formulation while avoiding confounding effects 

from changes in architecture, preprocessing, or inference. 

4.4 Evaluation Metrics 

We evaluate segmentation performance from three complementary perspectives: overlap accuracy, boundary 

fidelity, and lesion-level detectability. 

For overlap-based evaluation, we report Dice similarity coefficient (DSC), intersection-over-union (IoU), 

precision, and recall. These metrics reflect volumetric agreement between predicted and ground-truth masks 

and are widely used in medical image segmentation. 

To evaluate boundary quality, we report the 95th percentile Hausdorff distance (HD95) in millimeters. 

Compared with the maximum Hausdorff distance, HD95 is less sensitive to extreme outliers and is commonly 

used as a more robust boundary metric [10] .

In addition, we report lesion-level recall at 𝐀🀀𝐀 ≥ 0.1 . Under this criterion, each ground-truth connected 

component is counted as detected if any predicted component overlaps with it at an IoU of at least 0.1. This 

metric is intended to better reflect clinically relevant detectability, especially for smal l lesions whose exact 

boundaries may be difficult to delineate. 8

All results are reported as mean ± standard deviation over the test cases. 

4.5 Statistical Analysis 

For pairwise comparisons between methods evaluated on the same test set, we adopt a two-sided Wilcoxon 

signed-rank test [11] . This non-parametric test is suitable for small sample settings and paired experimental 

observations. 

A significance threshold of 𝐀 < 0.05 is used to indicate statistically significant differences. When 

appropriate, the statistical analysis is used to support comparisons between the baseline Dice+CE objective 

and the proposed ComboLoss under the same nnU-Net configuration. 

4.6 Post-processing Ablation 

Although overlap-based metrics can provide a reasonable summary of segmentation quality, boundary 

metrics such as HD95 may be disproportionately affected by a small number of distant false-positive 

components. To investigate whether this effect can be mitigated without modifying the network itself, we 

further evaluate a simple connected-component (CC) post-processing step as an ablation on top of 

ComboLoss predictions. 

In this setting, small disconnected components can be removed based on a predefined size threshold, or 

only the dominant connected components are retained. The purpose of this ablation is to examine whether a

lightweight post-processing strategy can improve boundary robustness while preserving overlap performance 

and lesion detectability.

prediction should be interpreted together with boundary-oriented post-processing rather than by overlap 

metrics alone. 

5.2 Effect of ComboLoss Relative to the Baseline 

Compared with the default Dice+CE objective, ComboLoss provides a consistent improvement in 

segmentation overlap and lesion detectability. The gain in DSC, IoU, and recall indicates that the proposed 

combination of Dice loss and focal loss is effective for handling the severe foreground –background imbalance 

in NECT images. 

A plausible explanation is that the Dice term stabilizes region-overlap optimization, while the focal term 

increases the contribution of hard and under-represented foreground voxels during training. This combination 

appears particularly beneficial in cases where tumor regions are small or exhibit weak contrast relative to 

surrounding liver tissue. 

From a practical perspective, ComboLoss also has the advantage of being easy to integrate into the 

standard nnU-Net training framework. It does not require architectural modification, additional supervision 

signals, or complex post-hoc refinement. This makes it a lightweight and reproducible alternative to the default 

objective.

5.4 Discussion of Trade-offs Across Loss Functions 

The results reveal a clear trade-off among the compared objectives. Dice+CE provides the weakest 

performance overall, indicating that the default objective is insufficient for this highly imbalanced segmentation 

task. ComboLoss improves both overlap and lesion detectability relative to the baseline while remaining 

simple and easy to reproduce. Tversky Loss further improves recall-oriented performance, suggesting 

stronger sensitivity to tumor voxels, but at the expense of larger boundary outliers. Focal Tversky Loss 

achieves competitive performance but does not consistently surpass Tversky Loss or ComboLoss across all 

metrics. 

These findings suggest that no single metric is sufficient to characterize performance in NECT liver tumor 

segmentation. A method with higher recall may detect more lesions but also produce more false-positive 

extensions or isolated components, which can degrade boundary fidelity. Conversely, a method with more 11 

stable boundaries may not necessarily maximize lesion sensitivity. For this reason, it is important to evaluate 

methods jointly using overlap, boundary, and lesion-level metrics. 

Within this context, ComboLoss should be interpreted as an overlap- and detection-oriented objective 

rather than a complete solution to all segmentation-quality criteria. It substantially improves DSC, IoU, recall, 

and lesion-level recall over the Dice+CE baseline, but the raw predictions may contain isolated false-positive 

regions that worsen HD95. Therefore, the main value of ComboLoss lies in improving rare-lesion learning 

under severe class imbalance, while lightweight CC post-processing provides a complementary mechanism 

for controlling boundary outliers. This combination offers a practical balance between effectiveness, simplicity, 

and reproducibility under the small-data conditions considered in this study.

5.5 Limitations 

This study has several limitations. First, the dataset is relatively small and single-institute in nature, consisting 

of 40 NECT volumes with a fixed split of 28 training and 12 test cases. Therefore, the reported results should 

be interpreted as evidence from a controlled small-data setting rather than as proof of broad clinical 

generalization. The performance of ComboLoss may vary when applied to more heterogeneous populations, 

different scanners, different acquisition protocols, or external clinical institutions. Future work should validate 

the proposed pipeline on larger multi-center NECT datasets and include external test cohorts to assess 

generalizability more rigorously. 

Second, although multiple loss functions are compared under a unified nnU-Net v2 framework, we do not 

perform extensive hyperparameter tuning for each objective. More comprehensive tuning may further improve 

the performance of some methods and may change the balance between lesion sensitivity and false-positive 

control. Third, the current study focuses on loss design and lightweight connected-component post-

processing, while architectural modifications, multi-stage detection-segmentation pipelines, and uncertainty-

aware refinement strategies are not explored. 12 

Despite these limitations, the present study provides a compact and reproducible baseline for NECT liver 

tumor segmentation and offers a controlled analysis of loss design under severe class imbalance .

6 CONCLUSION 

In this work, we presented a compact and reproducible nnU-Net v2 baseline for non-enhanced CT liver tumor 

segmentation under small-data conditions. By introducing ComboLoss, a weighted combination of Dice loss 

and focal loss, we improved segmentation overlap and lesion detectability relative to the default Dice+CE 

objective without modifying the network architecture. 

In addition, we compared several imbalance-aware loss functions, including Focal Tversky Loss, under an 

identical nnU-Net v2 configuration. The experimental results showed that imbalance-aware objectives 

consistently outperform the default baseline, while also revealing a trade-off between lesion sensitivity and 

boundary robustness. A simple connected-component post-processing step further reduced boundary outliers 

and improved HD95 without materially affecting overlap performance. 

Overall, the proposed framework provides a transparent and reproducible reference pipeline for NECT liver 

tumor segmentation in a controlled small-data setting. The results suggest that ComboLoss can improve 

overlap-based segmentation and lesion-level detectability, while connected-component post-processing is 

useful for reducing boundary outliers caused by isolated false-positive regions. Future work should extend this 

study to larger multi-center datasets, include external validation cohorts, and investigate the combination of 

imbalance-aware objectives with more advanced segmentation architectures.
