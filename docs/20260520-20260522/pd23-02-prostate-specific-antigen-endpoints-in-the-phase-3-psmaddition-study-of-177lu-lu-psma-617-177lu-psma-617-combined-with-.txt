# PD23-01 

UTILITY OF PROSTEST, A NOVEL BLOOD-BASED MRNA ASSAY, 

FOR PREDICTING OUTCOME TO  177 LUPSMA RADIOLIGAND 

THERAPY 

Mark Kidd, Abdel Halim, New Haven, CT 

INTRODUCTION AND OBJECTIVES: Treatment of metastatic 

castration-sensitive (mCSPC) and -resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC) 

has recently advanced with novel targeted radioligand therapies (RLT) 

such as  177 Lu-PSMA. While treatment eligibility is traditionally based on 

positive  68 Ga-PSMA-PET imaging this can only identify whether there 

are targetable lesions. Predicting patient response remains critical 

unmet needs. This study investigated whether PROSTest, a blood -

based molecular assay utilizing prostate cancer-speci fi c transcripts, 

could predict treatment response in mCRPC patients receiving 

multiple cycles of  177 Lu-PSMA. We hypothesized that a decrease in 

PROSTest score after fi rst cycle of treatment from baseline would be 

associated with response (PFS e PSA nadir) and overall survival 

(OS) in later cycles. 

METHODS: We audited the results from two hundred and sixty -

eight patients (median age: 73 years, range: 58-89) with mCRPC from 3

different European centers (Munster, Basel and Essen). Subjects were 

treated with a median of 2 (range 1-6) cycles. Samples for PSA 

assessment using standard procedures were collected prior to every 

cycle. Response (PFS) was categorized by PSA nadir ( -50% 

decreases from baseline). PROSTest scores (on scale of 0-100) were 

measured at baseline and after one treatment cycle. Changes in 

PROSTest (after 1 cycle vs. baseline) were correlated with PFS or 

OS using AUROC, Kaplan-Meier survival analysis and hazard ratios. 

RESULTS: A total of 133 (49.6%) responded to treatment 

based on PSA nadir and 135 were non-responders. Median PFS was 

15.0 months [95% CI: 8.7 e15.0] in responders vs. 4.4 months 

[3.4 e6.1] in non-responders. One hundred and thirty (48.5%) 

perished from disease. The OS was 8.6 months [7.4 e11.2] in 

deceased patients and undetermined in survivors (n[135). 

PROSTest was positive (50 scores) in all subjects. Changes in 

PROSTest correlated with both PFS and OS. Patients with a

decrease in PROSTest score had a markedly better response (mPFS 

15.0 months [8.7-15]) compared to those with increased scores 

(mPFS: 4.4 months [3.4-6.1]) after 1 cycle of therapy. PROSTest 

demonstrated a strong association with PFS (HR [0.37; [0.36 e0.71]). 

PROSTest similarly exhibited a strong association with OS (HR [0.26 

[0.18 e0.37]). We identi fi ed that 90.4% (122/135) of patients who 

clinically responded with a demonstrable survival advantage (mOS 

unde fi ned) showed a decrease in PROSTest, while 97 of 130 (74.6%) 

patients who progressed had an increase in PROSTest score (mOS: 

8.6 months [8.8-14.3]). 

CONCLUSIONS: This audit identi fi ed that early changes in 

PROSTest are signi fi cantly associated with PFS and OS in patients with 

mCRPC undergoing  177 LuPSMA RLT. These multi-center fi ndings 

identify the test ’s utility as a sensitive, early predictor of long-term 

therapeutic bene fi t and suggest that the PROSTest could be used for 

treatment personalization in RLT.

INTRODUCTION AND OBJECTIVES: In the phase 3 PSMAd -

dition study (NCT04720157), combining  177 Lu-PSMA-617 with 

androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) and an androgen receptor pathway 

inhibitor (ARPI) signi fi cantly improved rPFS vs ADT + ARPI in patients 

with PSMA+ mHSPC (HR 0.72; 95% CI 0.58, 0.90; p=0.002), at interim 

analysis 2 (IA2) for rPFS (data cutoff Jan 13, 2025). We present 

prostate-speci fi c antigen (PSA)-related endpoints at rPFS IA2. 

METHODS: Eligible male patients had treatment-naive/ 

minimally treated (45 days before consent) mHSPC and 1 PSMA+ 

metastatic lesion on [68 Ga]Ga-PSMA-11 PET/CT. Patients were 

randomized 1:1 to  177 Lu-PSMA-617 (7.4 GBq  10% q6w, 6 cycles) 

+ ADT + ARPI (177 Lu-PSMA-617 arm), or ADT + ARPI (control arm). 

The primary endpoint was rPFS (BIRC-con fi rmed rPD per PCWG3/ 

RECIST v1.1 or death). Secondary endpoints included PSA90 

response rate (PSA90-RR), PSA nadir <0.2 ng/mL at 12, 24 and 48 

weeks, and time to PSA progression. 

RESULTS: In total, 1144 patients were randomized. Median 

duration of study treatment was 20.6 and 19.9 months in the  177 Lu -

PSMA-617 and control arms, respectively. PSA90-RR and the 

proportion of patients achieving a PSA nadir of <0.2 ng/mL at weeks 

12, 24 and 48 were numerically higher in the  177 Lu-PSMA-617 arm vs 

the control arm (Figure 1). The risk of PSA progression was reduced 

by 58% (HR 0.42; 95% CI 0.30, 0.59) in the  177 Lu-PSMA-617 arm vs 

the control arm; medians were not reached (Figure 2). Among 

patients with baseline and 1 post-baseline PSA measurements, 

PSA levels decreased from baseline in 98.2% and 98.7% of patients 

in the  177 Lu-PSMA-617 arm (n [ 557) and control arm (n [ 553), 

respectively. 

CONCLUSIONS: Combining  177 Lu-PSMA-617 with ADT +

ARPI increased the frequency and depth of PSA responses and pro -

longed time to PSA progression, on top of the rapid and substantial 

decline in PSA levels with ADT + ARPI alone. Taken together with 

previously reported rPFS, safety and quality of life results, these fi nd -

ings indicate that combining  177 Lu-PSMA-617 with ADT + ARPI pro -

vides clinically meaningful bene fi t to patients with PSMA+ mHSPC.

RLT moves into earlier lines and combination settings, it becomes 

increasingly important to understand who is most likely to respond, and 

why resistance develops. De fi ning the molecular patterns of response 

and resistance will be essential to improve patient selection, guide 

therapy sequencing and optimize the use of RLT in clinical practice.

response to Lu-177-PSMA-617 using harmonized real-world data and 

liquid biopsy genomics.
