Abstract 

Purpose: To compare the cross -site generalization of radiomic features and deep learning embeddings for MRI prediction 

of substantial lymphovascular space invasion (LVSI) in endometrial cancer. 

Materials and Methods: This retrospective two -center study included 206 women (mean age, 59.8 years) with 

endometrial cancer who underwent preoperative 3 -T MRI from March 2016 to March 2023. Hospital A (n = 130) was 

used for development and Hospital B (n = 76) for strict externa l testing. T2 -weighted, reduced field -of -view diffusion -

weighted, and apparent diffusion coefficient images were manually segmented. Radiomic features and seed -pooled 

embeddings from 3D ResNet18, DenseNet121, and U -NEXtractor were modeled with elastic -net logistic regression or 

XGBoost. Out -of -fold Platt calibration and sensitivity -targeted thresholds were estimated using development data only. 

AUCs were summarized with 95% bootstrap confidence intervals. 

Results: External radiomics with elastic -net achieved an AUC of 0.609 (95% CI: 0.464, 0.740) and sensitivity of 0 of 12 

(0%). DenseNet121 with elastic -net had the highest external AUC (0.685; 95% CI: 0.538, 0.822) but sensitivity of 3 of 12 

(25%). U -NEXtractor with elastic -net detected 10 of 12 positive cases (83.3%) with specificity of 32 of 64 (50.0%) and 

balanced accuracy of 0.667. XGBoost showed higher apparent development performance but weaker external operating 

behavior. 

Conclusion: Under real -world cross -site MRI acquisition shift, DenseNet121 and U -NEXtractor embeddings showed 

better external generalization than handcrafted radiomic features for substantial LVSI prediction. 

Keywords: Endometrial cancer; Lymphovascular space invasion; Magnetic resonance imaging; Radiomics; Deep learning

Introduction 

Substantial lymphovascular space invasion (LVSI) is associated with nodal metastasis, 

recurrence, and poorer survival in endometrial cancer and now contributes to 2023 International 

Federation of Gynecology and Obstetrics staging [1] . Because LVSI is usually established only on final 

hysterectomy histopathology, a noninvasive preoperative predictor could support risk stratification. Pelvic 

MRI is the reference standard for local staging, and standard MRI sequences have shown promise f or 

LVSI prediction [2 -5] .

Radiomics and deep learning can extract prognostic information from preoperative MRI, and 

prior LVSI studies have reported encouraging discrimination [5 -8] . Translation remains limited by feature 

instability, flexible preprocessing, small -sample optimism, and limited external validation [9 -12] . These 

concerns are particularly important when scanners, acquisition planes, coils, and institutional workflows 

differ between development and deployment sites. 

Most studies select a single best model under relatively controlled conditions rather than asking 

which representation remains useful under acquisition shift. In small, high -dimensional imaging cohorts, 

downstream complexity and unstable splits can inflate apparent performance, whereas informative 

representations paired with regularized classifiers may transfer more reliably [13 -16] .

We hypothesized that learned deep features, particularly when pooled across random seeds and 

coupled to elastic -net logistic regression, would generalize better than handcrafted radiomics under cross -

site scanner and protocol shift. The study design follow ed principles for rigorous external validation and 

transparent prediction -model reporting [17, 18] . Therefore, the purpose of this study was to compare the 

cross -site generalization of radiomic features and deep learning embeddings for MRI prediction of 

substantial LVSI in endometrial cancer.

Materials and Methods 

Study design and patient cohort 

This retrospective two -center secondary analysis included 206 women with histologically 

confirmed endometrial cancer who underwent preoperative 3 -T pelvic MRI between March 2016 and 

March 2023. Institutional review boards approved the study and waived cons ent. The cohort was 

previously reported in a radiomics feasibility study [4]; this analysis adds deep/fusion representations, 

multiseed training, calibration, thresholding, and external testing. Hospital A (n = 130) was the 

development site and Hospital B (n = 76) the external test site. Hospital A was split by patient into a 

training cohort (n = 117) and supportive internal test cohort (n = 13). Feature selection, preprocessing, 

model selection, calibration, and thresholding used training data only; both t est cohorts were evaluated 

after model locking. No additional cases were excluded from the source cohort, and no formal power 

calculation was performed. Images and tables used coded identifiers. Exclusion criteria were invisible 

tumor, poor image quality, missing required sequences or LVSI information, MRI -to -surgery interval 

greater than 6 weeks in LVSI -positive cases, and preoperative chemotherapy or hormone therapy. Cohort 

characteristics are shown in Table 1. 

MRI acquisition and tumor segmentation 

Analyses used T2 -weighted imaging, reduced field -of -view diffusion -weighted imaging 

(FOCUS), and apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) maps. Scanners, coils, planes, fields of view, and 

timing parameters differed by site (Table 2 and Supplementary Table S1) and were retained as the 

external -test condition. Reader 1, a radiologist with 10 years of gynecologic oncology MRI experience, 

manually segmented each tumor in three dimensions. Reader 2, with 20 years of experience, 

independently repeated segmentations for the reproducibility subset. Readers knew the diagnosis but were 

blinded to LVSI status. Reader 1 masks defined lesion -centered deep -learning crops.

Outcome definition and clinical variables 

The primary outcome was substantial/extensive LVSI on the final hysterectomy pathology report, 

selected because histopathology is the clinical reference standard. LVSI was modeled as 

substantial/extensive versus negative/focal; imaging readers did not assi gn the outcome. Age and serum 

CA125 were candidate covariates. Missing CA125 values were median -imputed within each training fold, 

and the fitted value was applied unchanged to held -out data. 

Radiomic feature set 

Radiomic features were extracted from tumor regions with an Image Biomarker Standardization 

Initiative -oriented workflow [12] implemented in PyRadiomics [19] and SimpleITK. Boundary 

perturbations, fixed -bin -width discretization, isotropic resampling, three -dimensional texture, wavelet, 

and Laplacian -of -Gaussian features are detailed in the Supplementary Methods [5] .

Radiomic feature filtering for the present study 

Within training data, features were retained when Reader 1 -Reader 2 Spearman correlation was at 

least 0.80 in the reproducibility subset, pruned at pairwise absolute correlation greater than 0.95, and 

screened by Mann -Whitney U testing and repeated elastic -net selection; features selected in at least 50% 

of repeats were retained. Age and CA125 were appended. 

Deep -learning input construction 

Each image -mask pair was cropped around the lesion, resized to 96 × 96 × 96 voxels, and z -score 

normalized; ADC values were constrained to nonnegative values. Three -dimensional ResNet18 [20] ,

DenseNet121 [21] , and U -NEXtractor -style [22] backbones were initialized randomly and trained from 

scratch. ResNet18 and DenseNet121 could receive image and mask channels; U -NEXtractor used image -

only input with classification and auxiliary segmentation heads. Penultimate -layer embeddings were 

export ed.

Deep backbone training 

Within the 117 -patient training cohort, each seed used a stratified 80:20 fit/validation split. Three 

seeds (42, 43, and 44) were trained per backbone with AdamW (learning rate, 3 × 10^ -5; weight decay, 1 

× 10^ -4) for up to 50 epochs and 10 -epoch early sto pping; validation PR AUC selected the checkpoint. 

Training -only augmentation included spatial and intensity perturbations, noise, blur, low -resolution 

simulation, and modality dropout. No augmentation was applied during evaluation or embedding 

extraction. 

Embedding extraction and seed pooling 

Best -checkpoint embeddings were averaged elementwise across seeds. Radiomics -only, deep -

only, and fusion views were matched by patient identifier. 

Blockwise preprocessing and dimensionality control 

Within each fold, radiomics were robust -scaled; deep embeddings were standardized and reduced 

to 24 principal components. Fusion blocks were processed separately and concatenated. Elastic -net 

logistic regression was primary. XGBoost with constrained random search and early stopping was a 

secondary ablation. No internal -test or external data informed preprocessing or tuning. 

Out -of -fold calibration and threshold selection 

Repeated out -of -fold predictions from the training cohort were Platt -calibrated with logistic 

regression on logit -transformed probabilities. A bootstrap -stabilized threshold targeting sensitivity of 0.80 

was selected from development predictions and applie d unchanged to the training, internal, and external 

cohorts. 

Performance metrics and reporting 

Primary external metrics were receiver operating characteristic AUC with 95% bootstrap 

confidence intervals, precision -recall AUC, sensitivity, specificity, balanced accuracy, predictive values, 

F1 score, Matthews correlation coefficient, and confusion -mat rix counts. Decision -curve analysis 

estimated net benefit. Continuous cohort variables were compared with Mann -Whitney U tests and

Analyses used Python 3.11; PyRadiomics 2.2; SimpleITK 2.5; Pingouin 0.5; PyTorch 2.12;

Results 

Of 206 women (mean age, 59.8 years), 130 were assigned to development (17 of 130 [13.1%] with 

substantial/extensive LVSI) and 76 to external testing (12 of 76 [15.8%]). Age, LVSI prevalence, 

histologic type, CA125, and FIGO stage did not differ significant ly between sites (all P ≥ .079) (Table 1). 

Radiomics -only models generalized poorly to the external site. Radiomics with elastic -net 

achieved external AUC 0.609 and PR AUC 0.200 but detected 0 of 12 positive cases (0.0% sensitivity), 

with 63 of 64 negative cases correctly classified (98.4% specific ity; balanced accuracy, 0.492). Radiomics 

with XGBoost also detected 0 of 12 positive cases (Table 3). 

Among deep -only models, DenseNet121 with elastic -net achieved the highest external AUC 

(0.685; PR AUC, 0.252), detecting 3 of 12 positive cases (25.0%) and 45 of 64 negative cases (70.3%). 

U-NEXtractor with elastic -net achieved AUC 0.671 and detected 10 of 12 positive cases (83.3%), with 

specificity of 32 of 64 (50.0%) and balanced accuracy of 0.667. ResNet18 with elastic -net detected 0 of 

12 positive cases. External ROC and precision -recall curves are shown in Figure 2. 

Fusion did not consistently improve performance. Fusion U -NEXtractor with elastic -net achieved 

AUC 0.669 and detected 5 of 12 positive cases (41.7%), with specificity of 45 of 64 (70.3%) and 

balanced accuracy of 0.560. Fusion DenseNet121 with elastic -net d etected 6 of 12 positive cases (50.0%) 

but reduced specificity to 37 of 64 (57.8%). Calibration, score distributions, and decision -curve results are 

shown in Figures 3 and 4.

XGBoost showed greater apparent development performance but weaker external behavior. 

ResNet18 with XGBoost had training AUC 1.000 but external AUC 0.447 and detected 0 of 12 positive 

cases; fusion U -NEXtractor with XGBoost had training AUC 0.993 but exter nal AUC 0.531 and detected 

1 of 12 positive cases. Training -to -external AUC gaps are summarized in Supplementary Table S6. 

The internal test set contained only 2 positive cases, so threshold metrics were highly discrete and 

were considered supportive (Supplementary Table S2). In external error analysis, radiomics with elastic -

net missed all 12 positive cases while producing 1 false -positive prediction; deep U -NEXtractor with 

elastic -net reduced false negatives to 2 but produced 32 false positives. Thus, its sensitivity gain was 

accompanied by lower specificity rather than uniform calibration improvement. 

Discussion 

This study provides evidence that deep learning features can generalize better than handcrafted 

radiomics under real -world cross -site MRI acquisition shift. With scanner and protocol differences 

deliberately retained, DenseNet121 embeddings provided the hi ghest external discrimination and U -

NEXtractor embeddings the strongest sensitivity -oriented detection, whereas the radiomics baseline 

retained modest ranking ability but failed at the locked threshold. Fusion did not consistently improve 

performance, indi cating that the advantage arose from particular learned representations rather than 

simply adding more features. Calibration and threshold analyses supported this central finding by 

showing that the representation difference affected external decision beha vior, not only AUC. 

Recent LVSI studies have reported higher headline AUCs for clinical -radiomics and integrated 

radiomics -deep learning models [5 -8] . The present study addressed a different question: which 

representation and downstream regularization strategy best resists transfer failure when external positives 

are sparse and acquisition shift is retained. This emphasis on calibration and operating -point 

transportability helps explain why the observed AUCs were more modest. 

Radiomics did not lose all signal; external AUCs remained above chance. The failure was 

threshold robustness: calibrated decision rules predicted essentially no positive cases at the external site.

This matters because clinical deployment requires not only ranking but also stable probabilities and 

operating thresholds under protocol shift. 

Elastic -net transferred more consistently than XGBoost in this cohort. Several boosted -tree heads 

achieved near -perfect training AUCs yet reverted externally to highly specific, low -sensitivity behavior. 

In this low -event, high -dimensional feature space, n onlinear flexibility appeared easier to overfit than to 

transport, whereas regularized linear heads produced more useful external thresholds. 

Multi -seed pooling may have helped U -NEXtractor. Although this backbone had the widest seed -

to -seed variability, pooled embeddings became the strongest sensitivity -oriented external model, 

suggesting that complementary latent structure can be stabilized by seed averaging [23] .

These results emphasize why discrimination alone is insufficient for externally validated clinical 

prediction. A model with acceptable AUC may still be unusable if its calibrated threshold does not 

identify positive cases. Calibration, risk distributions, and prespecified operating thresholds should 

therefore be reported alongside AUC in radiology AI studies [24 -28] .

Limitations include the modest sample size and only 12 external positive cases, which widened 

confidence intervals and made threshold metrics coarse. The study included two centers, so Hospital B 

provides a realistic stress test rather than a full sampling of scanner, vendor, and protocol diversity. The 

broad benchmark also introduces implicit multiplicity despite training -only feature selection, out -of -fold 

calibration, and strict external testing. Future validation should use larger multicenter cohorts, t emporal 

holdouts, and clinical comparators such as structured MRI features, clinical -only models, molecular 

markers, or combined radiogenomic risk models. 

In summary, DenseNet121 and U -NEXtractor embeddings paired with elastic -net generalized 

better than handcrafted radiomic features under real -world cross -site scanner and protocol shift. 

Calibration and locked -threshold analyses showed that this representat ion advantage affected external 

decision behavior, not only AUC. These findings support learned deep features as a more robust basis for 

multisite MRI prediction.
