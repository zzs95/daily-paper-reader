## Abstract 

Constrained decoding is essential for serving LLMs, ensuring that generated outputs follow specific structures such as JSON schema-formatted function calls. Existing systems are designed for autoregressive models and assume left-to-right generation, masking out invalid next tokens at each step. Diffusion language models, however, break this assumption: they sample multiple positions simultaneously from a fully-factorized mean-field distribution at each denoising step. In this paper, we present an exact and tractable algorithm for sampling from the constrained mean-field posterior under any constraint expressible as a finite automaton. Viewing finite automata as graphical models, we obtain tractable representations of the constrained distribution that enable efficient inference. The approach guarantees constraint satisfaction by construction, supports both greedy and sampling-based decoding, and is compatible with parallel and block-wise decoding under arbitrary remasking schedules. Applying depth-reduction techniques from arithmetic circuit theory, we further reduce sampling depth from linear to logarithmic in the sequence length. Empirical evaluations on Dream-7B and LLaDA-8B show substantial accuracy gains across various tasks including function calling (xLAM, BFCL), planning (Sudoku, Countdown), text-to-SQL (Spider), and math reasoning (GSM-Symbolic), with little inference overhead relative to unconstrained decoding. For example, on BFCL-Live, our approach improves Dream-7B’s greedy decoding accuracy from 63.9% to 71.5%, and stochastic sampling accuracy from 22.3% to 69.0%, where the unconstrained baseline collapses, with under 5% wall-clock overhead. 

## 1 Introduction 

A wide range of LLM applications require outputs to strictly adhere to structural constraints. Examples include generating function calls conforming to a given JSON schema [ 16 , 17 ] and producing syntactically correct SQL queries [ 8]. Autoregressive LLMs enforce such constraints at inference time via constrained decoding , where invalid next tokens violating the constraint are masked out at each generation step [ 32 ]. This approach is now a standard component of LLM inference frameworks such as XGrammar [3], Guidance [5] and SGLang [38]. Recent advances in discrete diffusion language models (dLLMs) have established them as competitive alternatives to autoregressive LLMs [ 1, 10 , 22 ]. Open-weight models such as LLaDA-8B [ 13 ] and Dream-7B [ 34 ] have demonstrated performance comparable to similarly sized autoregressive models on code and math reasoning. Closed-source models including Mercury [ 7 ], Gemini Diffusion [ 4], and Seed Diffusion [27] further demonstrate excellent inference efficiency. Existing constrained generation frameworks [ 3, 5, 38 ] are specifically designed for left-to-right au-toregressive generation and adapting them to dLLMs poses unique challenges. Unlike autoregressive LLMs, dLLMs sample multiple tokens simultaneously from a fully-factorized mean-field distribution at each step. Because constraints operate globally, masking tokens independently at each position is not enough: locally valid tokens can still combine into invalid sequences.

To bridge this gap, we introduce an exact and tractable inference algorithm that, at each diffusion step, samples from the constrained mean-field posterior : pθ (x0 | xt, C) ∝ pθ (x0 | xt) · 1[x0 ∈ C ]

where the constraint C is expressed as a finite automaton, pθ (x0 | xt) is the model’s fully factorized mean-field prediction, and the indicator enforces the constraint globally on the complete sequence x0.Viewing the automaton as a graphical model encoding constraint C as its support, we are able to obtain a tractable representation for the constrained mean-field prediction pθ (x0 | xt, C), which enables efficient sampling. Our approach guarantees constraint satisfaction by construction, supports both greedy decoding and stochastic sampling, and applies to parallel and block-wise decoding under arbitrary remasking schedules. It also computes per-token marginals under the constrained distribution, which we use as a confidence signal for remasking. Naive sampling from the chain-structured graphical model induced by a finite automaton requires 

O(L) sequential forward/backward steps for a sequence of length L. Inspired by depth-reduction techniques for arithmetic circuits [ 30 ], we restructure this linear computation graph into a binary tree of prefix–suffix state pairs, reducing inference steps from O(L) to O(log L) while preserving the same sampling distribution. This yields substantially more parallelism on modern GPUs. Empirically, we evaluate our method on LLaDA-8B and Dream-7B comparing against the uncon-strained base models. We consider diverse constraints and tasks, including JSON-schema constraints for function calling (xLAM, BFCL), format constraints for planning (Sudoku, Countdown), SQL syntax and schema constraints (Spider), and symbolic expression constraints for math reasoning (GSM-Symbolic). Our method guarantees constraint satisfaction and substantially improves accuracy. For example, on BFCL-Live, our approach improves Dream’s greedy decoding accuracy from 63.9% to 71.5%, and remains robust under stochastic sampling: while the unconstrained Dream collapses to 22.3%, our method achieves 69.0%, with under 5% wall-clock overhead. 

## 2 Related Work 

Constrained decoding for autoregressive LLMs. There is an extensive line of work on locally constrained decoding [ 26 , 23 , 20 , 29 , 15 ], with a primary focus on enforcing hard constraints efficiently during autoregressive generation. These approaches typically operate by masking out invalid next tokens based on the current prefix, thereby ensuring local constraint satisfaction at each decoding step. Existing frameworks leverage different constraint representations and develop different parsing algorithms correspondingly: for example, Outlines [ 32 ] uses regular expressions and their corresponding finite automata representations, while more recent systems such as XGrammar [ 3]and Guidance [5] rely on context-free grammars. 

Constrained decoding for dLLMs. DINGO [ 28 ] is one pioneering work exploring inference algorithms over finite automata to support constrained decoding for dLLMs. In particular, DINGO leverages a dynamic programming algorithm to perform MAP inference on deterministic finite automata with their inputs weighted by the mean-field predictions from dLLMs, supporting block-wise greedy decoding from dLLMs. Our approach can be viewed as a strict generalization of DINGO, supporting greedy decoding and sampling under arbitrary masking strategies constrained by (possibly nondeterministic) finite automata (Section 4). Mündler et al. [12] and Zhang et al. [37] target richer constraints expressed as context-free grammars (CFGs). Both adopt a per-step rejection-sampling scheme that draws candidate tokens and then verifies whether the intermediate output remains acceptable; Zhang et al. [37] additionally introduce a lookahead step that uses the mean-field distributions to reliably verify proposed tokens. These methods do not guarantee constraint satisfaction within a finite sampling budget , as acceptance rate can be extremely low in practice.

In this work, we consider constraints expressible as finite automata [6].

A (nondeterministic) finite automaton (FA) is a tuple M = ( S, V, δ, s 0, F) with states S, alphabet V (often corresponding to a dLLM vocabulary), transition function δ : S × V → 2S ,initial state s0, and accept states F ⊆ S . E := {(s, s ′) ∈ S × S | ∃ v s.t. s′ ∈ δ(s, v )}.

For each edge e = ( s, s ′), we write src( e) := s, dst( e) := s′ and label( e) := {v | s′ ∈ δ(s, v )}.Under this view, a string v1: L is accepted iff there exists a path e1: L such that src( e1) = s0, dst( ei) = src( ei+1 ) for all i < L , dst( eL) ∈ F , and vi ∈ label( ei) for all i.

## 4 Method 

4.1 Problem Statement 

Our goal is to sample from a dLLM’s generative distribution pdLLM restricted to a constraint C,i.e., pdLLM (x0 | x0 ∈ C ). However, sampling exactly from this distribution is intractable, and one naive resort is rejection sampling against the full reverse process, which is infeasible in practice due to extremely low acceptance rates. For autoregressive LLMs, a common practice is to mask out next tokens that would violate the constraint as generation proceeds left-to-right [ 32 ]. Following an analogous strategy, we enforce that samples drawn from each diffusion step must satisfy the constraint. Recall from Equation (2) that a standard diffusion step samples ˆx0 from the model’s mean-field distribution. We instead sample from this distribution restricted to C, ensuring ˆx0 ∈ C at every step, so the final output is guaranteed to satisfy the constraint by construction. Sampling exactly from Equation (4) therefore requires reasoning about the joint distribution induced by the constraint.

Parallelization vs. FLOPs tradeoff. Compared to ancestral sampling, log-depth sampling increases the message-passing FLOPs by a factor of |S| , but reduces the depth of the com-putation graph from O(L) to O(log L). This tradeoff is favorable on modern GPUs: the additional FLOPs are spent on larger matrix operations, while the number of sequential GPU calls is reduced from O(L) to O(log L). Since each step of ancestral sampling performs relatively little work, its runtime is often dominated by synchronization and kernel-launch overhead, while the log-depth sampler exposes substantially more parallelism at each layer, leading to higher GPU utilization and lower wall-clock latency. 

## 5 Experiments 

5.1 Tasks, Models and Setups Tasks We evaluate our method on five tasks across six datasets. Constraints are encoded as finite automata (DFA or NFA); full prompts and example outputs are provided in Appendix C. • Function Calling (xLAM, BFCL). Task: Given a user query and a set of available function schemas, generate the corresponding function call. Data: xlam-function-calling-60k (xLAM) [ 9 ]and the single-turn split of the Berkeley Function Calling Leaderboard (BFCL) [ 17 ], including both non-live and live settings. Metric: Exact match against the ground-truth function call. Constraint: 

A DFA enforcing that the output conforms to the given function schema. We evaluate two output formats: Python format (the BFCL standard, e.g., get_weather(location="NYC") ) and JSON format (e.g., {"name": "get_weather", "location": "NYC"} ). • Sudoku. Task: Solve a 4 ×4 Sudoku puzzle [ 33 ]. Metric: Exact match against valid Sudoku solutions. Averaged across number of prefilled cells {4, . . . , 12 }. Constraint: A DFA enforcing the 4 ×4 output format and preserving prefilled input cells; row, column, and box constraints are not enforced by the DFA, which would require an exponentially larger DFA. • Countdown. Task: Reach a target number using arithmetic operations on three given numbers [ 33 ]. 

Metric: Whether the expression evaluates to the target. Constraint: A DFA enforcing A op B = C

at each step, with operands from the input or prior results and the final result equal to the target. • Math reasoning (GSM-Symbolic). Task: Solve grade-school math word problems. Data: GSM-Symbolic [ 11 ], where numerical values and names are replaced by symbolic variables. Metric: 

Exact match on the final answer. Constraint: A DFA enforcing that symbolic expressions appear only within « and », while the remaining text is unrestricted natural language. • Text-to-SQL (Spider). Task: Given a user query and a database schema, generate the corresponding SQL query. Data: Spider [ 35 ]. Metric: Execution accuracy. A query is correct if it returns the same results as the ground truth on the test database. Constraint: An NFA encodes SQL grammar restricted to the columns and tables in the provided schema. 

Models We evaluate two open-weight dLLMs, LLaDA-8B [ 13 ] and Dream-7B [ 34 ], each with 

Base and Instruct variants. We use the Base variants for Sudoku and Countdown and the Instruct 

variants for the remaining tasks [ 34 ]. For position selection, LLaDA selects the highest-probability positions, while Dream selects the lowest entropy ones. Our method computes marginal probabilities under the constrained distribution at each step and uses them as the confidence signal for both models.

## 6 Conclusion 

We propose a constrained decoding algorithm for diffusion language models under constraints expressible as finite automata. Viewing finite automata as graphical models, our approach performs exact and tractable sampling/inference over the constrained mean-field posterior at each denoising step, guaranteeing constraint satisfaction. We further propose a log-depth sampling strategy to unlock greater parallelization, yielding low overhead in practice. Our approach substantially improves model performance across a variety of downstream tasks where unconstrained models struggle. 

## 7 Limitations and Societal Impacts 

Our method focuses on finite automata, and does not cover richer constraints such as context-free grammars. Constrained decoding can improve the reliability of LLM systems used for function calling and other structured-output applications. However, if misused, this method could enable the generation of syntactically valid but malicious tool calls or database queries, increasing the risk of unintended or harmful system actions. 9References

## B Parallel Sampling Algorithm 

We show that Algorithm 1 produces samples distributed exactly as the constrained posterior p(x0 |

xt, C), despite using unnormalized conditionals during sampling. 

Proposition B.1. Let p(x0 | xt) denote the diffusion model’s mean-field predictions, and let C be a constraint encoded as a finite automaton with state space S. Algorithm 1 returns a sample x0

distributed exactly as 

p(x0 | xt, C) ∝ p(x0 | xt) · 1[x0 ∈ L (C)] .

Proof. We proceed by induction on the recursion depth. 

Base case. At a leaf of the recursion (segment with boundary states zℓ, z r satisfying r − ℓ ≤ 1), the algorithm samples a single token xℓ from the conditional p(xℓ | zℓ, z r ). Since xℓ ranges over a finite vocabulary, this categorical distribution can be normalized exactly by summing over its support, and the resulting sample is exact. 

Inductive step. Consider a segment [ℓ, r ] with boundary states zℓ, z r and midpoint m. The algo-rithm performs three steps: 1. Sample zm from p(zm | zℓ, z r ) ∝ p(zm | zℓ) · p(zr | zm).13 2. Recursively sample xℓ:m−1 from the left subproblem with endpoints (zℓ, z m).3. Recursively sample xm:r−1 from the right subproblem with endpoints (zm, z r ).Step 1 is a categorical sampling problem over the finite state space S, and the unnormalized scores can be summed and normalized exactly in O(|S| ). By the inductive hypothesis, steps 2 and 3 produce exact samples from their respective conditional distributions. By d-separation in the hidden Markov model induced by p(x0 | xt, C) (Figure 1), conditioning on zℓ, z m, z r renders xℓ:m−1 and xm:r−1

independent. Hence the joint distribution over the segment factors as 

p(xℓ:r−1, z m | zℓ, z r ) = p(zm | zℓ, z r ) · p(xℓ:m−1 | zℓ, z m) · p(xm:r−1 | zm, z r ),

which is precisely the distribution sampled by steps 1–3. Thus the segment-level sample is exact. 

Composition. The boundary states z1, z L+1 at the root of the recursion are sampled from their joint marginal p(z1, z L+1 ), which is computed exactly from the precomputed skip-transitions. Combined with the inductive argument above, the full sample x0 is distributed exactly as p(x0 | xt, C).

Remark on unnormalized conditionals. Although each conditional p(zm | zℓ, z r ) is written up to a (zℓ, z r )-dependent normalization constant, this poses no issue for exactness: at sampling time, the constant is computed by summing the |S| unnormalized scores, and the resulting categorical distribution is exact. Different segments use different normalization constants, as they should—each subproblem is conditioned on different endpoint values, and the chain-rule decomposition above ensures these segment-local normalizers compose into the correct joint distribution.
