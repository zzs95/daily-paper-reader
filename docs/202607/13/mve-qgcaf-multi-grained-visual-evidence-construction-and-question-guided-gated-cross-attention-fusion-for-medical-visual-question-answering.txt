Journal of King Saud University Computer and Information Sciences https://doi.org/10.1007/s44443-026-00936-8 Received: 03 May 2026 Accepted: 07 Jun 2026 Yuhai Yu, Lin Lu, Jiana Meng, Jing Liu, Shan Lu & Long Li ARTICLE IN PRESS MVE-QGCAF: Multi-grained Visual Evidence Construction and Question-guided Gated Cross-Attention Fusion for Medical Visual Question Answering 

Abstract 

Medical Visual Question Answering (MedVQA) aims to generate accurate answers to clinical questions based on medical images. Although recent generative vision-language models have improved open-ended answer generation, two key challenges remain insufficiently addressed. First, many medical questions depend on small lesions, organ-level structures, or subtle imaging findings, which may be weakened when the model relies only on whole-image representations. Sec-ond, question semantics are not always effectively used to determine which local regions are relevant and how much they should contribute to answer generation. To address these issues, we propose MVE-QGCAF, a generative MedVQA framework based on Multi-grained Visual Evidence Construction (MVE) and Question-guided Gated Cross-Attention Fusion (QGCAF). MVE constructs a global-local visual representation by selecting question-conditioned regions of interest from automatically generated candidate medical regions while retaining the whole-image context. QGCAF further decomposes multimodal fusion into two sequential operations: cross-attention-based retrieval of multi-grained visual evidence and question-guided gating for local evidence contribution calibration. The calibrated visual representation is then projected into the input space of a generative language model for concise medical answer generation. Experiments on VQA-RAD, SLAKE, and VQA-Med-2019 show that MVE-QGCAF achieves overall accuracies of 80.50 ±0.21%, 89.22 ±0.20%, and 82.17 ±0.11%, respectively. Further ablation studies, local-region quality analysis, gating-weight visualization, computational cost analysis, and failure case analysis demonstrate that the performance gains mainly stem from question-conditioned evidence selection and adaptive local evidence calibration, rather than from the simple addition of local image crops. These results indicate that organizing and weighting local visual evidence according to question semantics is an effective strategy for generative MedVQA.

Medical Visual Question Answering (MedVQA) aims to answer natural-language ques-tions based on medical images. Unlike conventional medical image analysis tasks that usually focus on predefined labels or specific abnormality categories, MedVQA requires a model to understand both the visual content and the intention of the question. A reliable MedVQA system should identify the anatomical structures, lesion regions, or imaging findings that are relevant to the current question and then generate a clinically meaningful answer. With the development of public datasets such as VQA-RAD [1], SLAKE [2], and VQA-Med-2019 [3], MedVQA has gradually evolved from closed-form answer classification toward more flexible short-answer generation. Recent advances in medical vision-language pre-training and multimodal large language models have promoted the development of MedVQA. Early studies mainly focused on extracting visual and textual features and fusing them for answer pre-diction [1, 4–6]. Later, domain-specific pre-trained models, such as PubMedCLIP [7], M3AE [8], and BioMedCLIP [9], improved image-text representation learning in the medical domain. Generative frameworks, including LLaVA-Med [10], MedBLIP [11], FAVP [12], LaPA [13], and Med-MoE [14], further connected medical visual encoders with large language models through instruction tuning, visual prompts, latent prompts, or generative decoding. Beyond MedVQA, recent medical image analysis studies have also shown the value of deep learning in complex clinical tasks, such as multi-task glioma profiling from anatomical and genomic information [15]. These stud-ies indicate that robust visual representation learning and effective vision-language alignment are important for medical image understanding. Despite this progress, existing MedVQA methods still face two important limita-tions. First, many answers rely on localized and fine-grained visual evidence. Small abnormalities, organ boundaries, catheter tips, lesion regions, and subtle imaging signs may occupy only a small part of the image. When a model mainly relies on whole-image representations, these local cues can be diluted by surrounding anatomical background. Some recent methods introduce local prompts or multi-scale features [12, 13, 16, 17], but the selected regions are not always explicitly matched to the current question. Second, question semantics are often used mainly during feature fusion or answer gen-eration, while their role in earlier visual evidence construction remains limited [16–19]. However, different questions on the same image may require different evidence. For example, a modality or plane question may depend more on global image character-istics, whereas an abnormality detection or lesion localization question may require specific local regions. Cross-attention and multimodal fusion mechanisms have been widely adopted to enhance image-text interaction. Recent studies in fine-grained visual understanding further show that cross-fusion, cross-transformer modeling, and prompt-aware adap-tation can strengthen feature interaction across different visual or visual-language representations. For example, C2T-Net [20] introduces a channel-aware cross-fused transformer framework for pedestrian attribute recognition, while CLEAR [21] com-bines cross-transformer modeling with a pre-trained language model for person attribute recognition and retrieval. In medical image analysis, MergeSlide [22] explores ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS continual model merging and task-to-class prompt-aligned inference for lifelong learn-ing on whole slide images. Although these studies reflect a broader trend toward explicit feature interaction and language- or prompt-guided adaptation, they are not direct MedVQA baselines and do not directly address question-conditioned local evidence selection or regional contribution calibration in generative MedVQA. To address these issues, we propose MVE-QGCAF, a generative MedVQA frame-work based on Multi-grained Visual Evidence Construction and Question-guided Gated Cross-Attention Fusion. The framework follows a question-conditioned visual evidence pipeline. First, the MVE module constructs a global-local visual repre-sentation by selecting question-relevant local regions from automatically generated candidate medical regions while preserving the whole-image context. Second, the QGCAF module uses cross-attention to retrieve multi-grained visual evidence from global image context, local regions, and question semantics. Third, a question-guided gating mechanism further calibrates the contribution of local evidence, enabling the model to suppress weakly related regions and emphasize evidence that is more rele-vant to the current question. Finally, the fused representation is projected into the input space of a generative language model for concise medical answer generation. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows: (1) We propose a question-conditioned multi-grained visual evidence construction strategy for generative MedVQA. By combining global image context with automati-cally selected question-relevant local regions, the model can better capture fine-grained medical cues required by different questions. (2) We design a question-guided gated cross-attention fusion module that decouples evidence retrieval from local evidence contribution calibration. Cross-attention first models interactions among global context, local evidence, and question semantics, while the gating mechanism further adjusts the contribution of local regions according to the current question. (3) We conduct extensive experiments on VQA-RAD, SLAKE, and VQA-Med-2019. In addition to performance comparisons, we provide component-level and mechanism-level ablation studies, local-region quality analysis, gating-weight visu-alization across question categories, computational cost analysis, and failure case analysis to evaluate the effectiveness, interpretability, and limitations of the proposed framework. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Research status of medical visual question answering 

MedVQA combines medical image analysis with natural language questioning, requir-ing models to generate answers based on visual evidence and relevant questions. It has potential value in diagnostic assistance, report interpretation, and intelligent med-ical interaction. Early MedVQA studies usually followed a discriminative paradigm. Visual features were extracted by convolutional or pre-trained visual encoders, textual features were encoded by recurrent or attention-based models, and the two modalities were fused for answer prediction. MEVF [23], for example, addressed the data scarcity problem by combining pre-trained visual features with recurrent text representations ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS through bilinear fusion. QCR [24] introduced conditional reasoning to strengthen question-driven joint modeling, while CMSA [25] used cross-modal self-attention to improve image-question interaction. These methods built the early foundation of MedVQA, but their visual inputs were still largely coarse-grained. With the development of multimodal large language models, MedVQA has grad-ually moved toward generative modeling. LLaVA-Med [10] and Med-Flamingo [26] explored medical visual instruction tuning and few-shot generation, showing the poten-tial of large generative frameworks in biomedical scenarios. In parallel, domain-specific visual encoders such as BioMedCLIP [9] improved medical image-text representa-tion and provided a stronger basis for downstream question answering. This shift has changed MedVQA from a task dominated by answer classification to one that increasingly emphasizes open-ended generation and cross-task transfer. Recent medical image analysis studies further show that robust visual repre-sentation learning is important for complex clinical reasoning tasks. For example, Abdusalomov et al. [15] applied multi-task deep learning to comprehensive glioma pro-filing from anatomical and genomic information, demonstrating the value of learning clinically informative representations from heterogeneous medical data. This perspec-tive is consistent with MedVQA, where reliable answer generation also depends on identifying visual evidence that is relevant to the given question. However, the core difficulty of MedVQA has not disappeared. Medical images often contain weak, local, and question-dependent evidence. The relevant region may change even when the input image remains the same. Existing models have improved repre-sentation learning and language generation, but many still organize visual evidence through whole-image features or fixed local enhancement. The role of question seman-tics in selecting and weighting local evidence is also limited. These issues reduce the reliability of MedVQA models when answering fine-grained clinical questions. 

2.2 Multi-granularity visual representation and local evidence modeling 

The use of local visual evidence is important for MedVQA, because many answers depend on small lesions, organ boundaries, or subtle imaging signs. Early methods mainly used whole-image features as the dominant visual input. MEVF [23] improved multimodal fusion under limited data, QCR [24] enhanced question-conditioned rea-soning, and MMBERT [6] introduced multimodal pre-training for medical image-text representation. Although these methods established useful baselines, their dependence on global visual representations makes it difficult to capture fine-grained local cues in some clinical questions. To address this limitation, later studies began to incorporate local regions, multi-scale features, and local-global joint representations. FAVP [12] uses fine-grained adaptive visual prompts to enhance organ-level and lesion-level information. LaPA [13] introduces latent prompts and knowledge fusion to improve implicit local semantic rep-resentation. LGMTNet [27] and related local representation methods [28] further show that multi-scale and local-global visual cues are complementary. Recent studies have also shifted attention to evidence organization and interpretability. Context-Guided MedVQA [16], Answer Semantics-enhanced MedVQA [29], GVII [18], R-LLaVA [30], ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS and Med-CoE [17] all attempt to improve how visual evidence is organized or explained. These studies show that local evidence and local-global modeling are useful for MedVQA. Still, most existing methods focus on enhancing local features or learning local-global representations after regions have been determined. The question of how to dynamically select local regions according to the current question is less explored. This is important because static prompts or fixed region sets may not consistently cover the most informative lesion-related regions, especially in presence detection, localization, and fine-grained attribute recognition. This motivates the proposed MVE-QGCAF framework, which first constructs question-relevant multi-grained visual evidence and then uses question-guided fusion to make better use of that evidence. 

2.3 Multimodal Feature Fusion and Visual-Language Alignment 

Multimodal fusion determines how visual perception, question understanding, and answer generation are connected in MedVQA. Early studies focused on explicit inter-action between image and text features. MEVF [23] modeled higher-order correlations through bilinear pooling. BAN [31], a representative method in general VQA, used bilinear attention to build finer-grained visual-linguistic associations. QCR [24] and CMSA [25] further introduced conditional reasoning and cross-modal self-attention into MedVQA. These approaches confirmed that explicit image-question interaction is useful, but they still relied mostly on whole-image or coarse visual features. Medical vision-language pre-training has provided another route for improv-ing alignment. PubMedCLIP [7], M3AE [8], and BioMedCLIP [9] learn domain-specific image-text representations from medical data, improving semantic consistency between visual and textual modalities. In generative frameworks, InstructBLIP [32], MedBLIP [11], and LLaVA-Med [10] connect visual encoders with language mod-els through visual queries, bridging modules, or instruction tuning, which supports more flexible answer generation. Other recent methods, such as Context-Guided MedVQA [16], GVII [18], QPMF [19], and Med-CoE [17], further introduce con-textual constraints, global visual intervention, progressive fusion, or evidence-based reasoning to improve multimodal fusion. Beyond MedVQA, recent cross-fusion and cross-transformer studies also provide useful references for fine-grained feature interac-tion. C2T-Net [20] explores channel-aware cross-fused transformer-style modeling for pedestrian attribute recognition, and CLEAR [21] combines cross-transformer model-ing with a pre-trained language model for person attribute recognition and retrieval. In medical image analysis, MergeSlide [22] studies continual model merging and task-to-class prompt-aligned inference for lifelong learning on whole slide images. These works are not direct MedVQA baselines, but they support the broader motivation for explicit feature interaction and language- or prompt-guided adaptation. Although existing methods improve alignment and generation, they do not fully solve the problem of question-conditioned local evidence utilization. In many cases, question semantics are introduced mainly during feature fusion or answer genera-tion, while local region selection and local evidence weighting remain relatively static. In contrast, MVE-QGCAF uses question semantics throughout the visual evidence ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS pipeline: question information is used to select question-relevant local regions, guide cross-modal evidence retrieval, and calibrate the contribution of local evidence. There-fore, the focus of this work is not to replace the visual or language backbone, but to organize and use multi-grained visual evidence in a question-conditioned manner. 

## 3 Method 

The proposed framework consists of two main modules as shown in Figure 1: Multi-grained Visual Evidence Construction (MVE) and Question-Guided Cross-Attention Fusion (QGCAF). The MVE module constructs multi-granularity visual evidence by selecting question-relevant local regions from candidate regions while retaining global image context. Based on this representation, the QGCAF module uses question seman-tics to guide the retrieval, weighting, and fusion of global, local, and textual features. The resulting fused representation is then mapped to a generative language model through a vision-language bridging module for concise medical answer generation.

The proposed method does not aim to redesign the underlying visual encoder or the general-purpose language model. Instead, it focuses on constructing visual evi-dence representations that are better suited to MedVQA and on strengthening the collaborative modeling of global and local visual information during multimodal fusion. In MedVQA, open-ended answers often rely on fine-grained visual features, includ-ing specific organs, lesion regions, and subtle imaging findings. These diagnostically relevant cues are usually distributed in local image regions and may be obscured by complex anatomical backgrounds. Using the entire image as the sole visual input may cause local abnormalities to be diluted by extensive background structures, thereby weakening question-relevant fine-grained visual evidence. This limitation is particu-larly evident in tasks such as presence detection, localization, and lesion attribute recognition, where a single global image representation often fails to provide sufficiently precise visual support. To address this issue, the proposed method adopts Multi-grained visual evidence construction as a core design principle. While preserving global image context, it introduces question-relevant local regions of interest and expands the visual input from a single whole-image representation to a multi-granularity evidence set composed of global context and local key regions. In this way, global and local information can complement each other during subsequent question answering, providing a more discriminative visual basis for cross-modal fusion and answer generation. 4.2 Implementation Details 

We adopt BioMedCLIP as the medical vision-language pre-training backbone. BioMedCLIP consists of a ViT-B/16 [40] visual encoder and a PubMedBERT [41] text encoder, and is pre-trained on the PMC-15M dataset. In this study, the model is initialized with publicly available pre-trained weights. Considering the limited scale of MedVQA data, the parameters of the visual encoder are kept frozen during training. On the language generation side, Vicuna-7B [42] is used as the generative language model. Local candidate regions are first generated by SAM-Med2D and then filtered according to question relevance, with the top K=3 regions retained as local visual evidence. To reduce computational cost, this step is performed and cached during pre-processing, and the cached results are directly used during training and inference. All input images are resized to 224 ×224. To improve cross-modal alignment, training is conducted in two stages. In the first stage, the QGCAF module, the vision-language projection layer, and the learnable query vectors are pre-trained on the PMC-VQA [43] dataset. In the second stage, the model is fine-tuned on the downstream VQA-RAD, SLAKE, and VQA-Med-2019 datasets. Owing to the limited size of MedVQA datasets, LoRA [44] is adopted on the generation side for parameter-efficient training, with the rank set to r=16. For parameter statistics, we report the number of trainable param-eters involved in optimization rather than the total number of parameters in the full vision-language system. Specifically, LoRA introduces 66.41M trainable parameters, while the QGCAF module, the vision-language projection layer, and the learnable ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS query vectors contribute an additional 3.8M parameters, resulting in 70.21M train-able parameters in total. The frozen visual encoder and the backbone language model parameters are excluded from this count. Training is performed using the AdamW [45] optimizer with an initial learning rate of 1 ×10 −5, 100 warm-up steps, and a batch size of 16. All experiments are conducted on four NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 GPUs, each equipped with 24 GB of memory, with Python 3.9.19, CUDA 11.8, and PyTorch 2.0.0. To further analyze the computational cost of MVE-QGCAF, we use VQA-RAD as a representative benchmark and report the estimated forward computational cost, runtime, GPU memory usage, and inference latency. Under the reported input setting, the estimated forward computational cost of the main MVE-QGCAF inference model is approximately 3.46 TFLOPs per query, excluding offline SAM-Med2D candidate region generation. In a four-GPU distributed setup, training the model for 10 epochs takes approximately 23.2 minutes. The peak GPU memory consumption during train-ing is approximately 22.53 GB per GPU. During inference, evaluating 451 VQA-RAD test samples takes approximately 39 seconds, corresponding to an average end-to-end inference latency of approximately 86.5 ms per sample. Since SAM-Med2D candidate regions are generated and cached during preprocessing, this offline candidate-region generation step is not included in the reported FLOPs or inference latency. 

4.3 Comparative Experiments 

To comprehensively evaluate the proposed model, we compared it with several repre-sentative methods on three widely used MedVQA benchmark datasets. 4.4 Ablation Experiment 

To evaluate the contributions of the Multi-grained Visual Evidence Construction (MVE) module and the Question-guided Gated Cross-Attention Fusion (QGCAF) module, we conducted stepwise ablation studies on three datasets, and the results are reported in Table 3. In addition to the component-level ablation in Table 3, we reorganized and strengthened the ablation analysis from four aspects: local region quality and selection strategy, question-guided gating design, two-stage training strat-egy, and LLM adaptation configuration. These analyses are further detailed in the following subsections and summarized in Tables 4–7. They are intended to examine not only whether each component is useful, but also why the key design choices in MVE-QGCAF contribute to the final performance. Overall, both modules improve performance, and the best results are obtained when they are combined. Specifically, after incorporating the MVE module into the baseline, the overall accuracy increases from 80.05% to 80.16% on VQA-RAD, from 87.65% to 88.18% on SLAKE, and from 80.32% to 80.83% on VQA-Med-2019. These improvements indicate that introducing question-relevant local evidence enhances the model’s ability to capture and utilize fine-grained visual information. When QGCAF is introduced alone, the overall accuracy further improves to 80.29%, 88.70%, and 81.75% on the three datasets, respectively, showing more consistent gains. This suggests that targeted interactions among global image context, local evidence, and question semantics help the model exploit multi-grained visual information more effectively. The combination of MVE and QGCAF results in the best performance on all three datasets, achieving overall accuracies of 80.50%, 89.22%, and 82.17% on VQA-RAD, SLAKE, and VQA-Med-2019, respectively. These results indicate that local evidence construction and question-guided fusion are complementary rather than independent: multi-grained local evidence provides a richer visual basis for subsequent fusion, while the question-guided mechanism ensures that such evidence is used more effectively.

To further analyze the internal design of QGCAF, we compare three fusion variants: cross-attention only, cross-attention with question-free gating, and cross-attention with question-guided gating. When question semantics are further involved in the gating process, the model can more effectively identify the local evidence most relevant to the current question, resulting in more stable performance improvements. 4.5.3 Sensitivity analysis of the number of local regions K 

In the MVE module, the number of local regions K determines the scale of local visual evidence available to the model. To evaluate the influence of this parameter, we performed sensitivity analysis with different values of K while keeping the remaining settings fixed. As shown in Figure 4, the overall accuracy on VQA-RAD, SLAKE, and VQA-Med-2019 consistently increases as K rises from 1 to 3, indicating that incorporating an appropriate number of local regions helps enrich fine-grained visual evidence and improves the perception of key lesion areas and anatomical structures. When K is increased to 4 or 5, the performance gains plateau, and slight degradation is seen in some datasets. This indicates that an excessive number of local regions can introduce redundant information and irrelevant noise, which may diminish the effectiveness of the subsequent fusion. Based on these results, K=3 is adopted in this study.

4.7 Training Phase and Backbone Influence Analysis 

To assess the effectiveness of the two-stage training strategy, we conducted ablation studies, as summarized in Table 6. Using only Stage 1 yields overall accura-cies of 51.12%, 62.01%, and 54.72% on VQA-RAD, SLAKE, and VQA-Med-2019, respectively, suggesting that pre-training on PMC-VQA provides an initial level of cross-modal alignment. Using only Stage 2 improves the results to 73.95%, 74.76%, and 68.30%, indicating that downstream fine-tuning is effective for task adaptation. When the two stages are combined, the model achieves 80.50%, 89.22%, and 82.17%, respectively, outperforming Stage 2 alone by 6.55, 14.89, and 13.87 percentage points. This confirms that pre-training and downstream fine-tuning are complementary and jointly contribute to improved performance. To evaluate the impact of different LLM backbones and LoRA rank settings, we compared Vicuna and LLaMA-2 with r ∈ { 0, 2, 4, 8, 16 }, as shown in Table 7. Overall, Vicuna achieves better performance than LLaMA-2 under most rank settings. At r =16, Vicuna reaches 80.50% and 89.22% overall accuracy on VQA-RAD and SLAKE, respectively, exceeding that of LLaMA-2 (79.41% and 87.09%). Although LLaMA-2 performs marginally better on VQA-Med-2019 (82.31% vs. 82.17%), the overall comparison indicates that higher LoRA ranks are beneficial for adapting the model to MedVQA tasks. Based on the results across all three datasets, Vicuna with r = 16 is selected as the default configuration. ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 5 Conclusion and Limitations 

This work addresses two key challenges in medical visual question answering: local discriminative cues may be weakened by complex global image backgrounds, and the contribution of local regions is not always well aligned with the question. To address these issues, we propose MVE-QGCAF, a generative MedVQA framework based on multi-grained visual evidence construction and question-guided gated cross-attention fusion. The MVE module builds a global-local representation by selecting question-relevant local regions while preserving whole-image context. The QGCAF module further uses question semantics to guide multi-grained evidence retrieval and local contribution calibration. Experiments on VQA-RAD, SLAKE, and VQA-Med-2019 show that the proposed method achieves competitive performance across different datasets. Ablation studies, local-region quality analysis, gating-weight visual-ization, computational cost analysis, and qualitative failure analysis further show that question-conditioned evidence construction and adaptive local evidence calibration contribute to the effectiveness and interpretability of the proposed framework. Despite these improvements, several limitations remain. First, although the Ran-dom ROI and SAM without question-relevance filtering analyses provide an initial evaluation of local region quality, the current framework still relies on SAM-Med2D for candidate region generation. We did not systematically evaluate alternative medical segmentation or detection backbones in this study, and the influence of different pro-posal backbones remains to be further investigated. Second, MVE-QGCAF is mainly designed and evaluated for short-answer MedVQA. Its direct applicability to long-form clinical reasoning, report generation, and multi-turn medical dialogue has not been experimentally validated. These tasks require additional capabilities, such as long-context modeling, factual consistency control, clinical knowledge grounding, and dialogue history tracking. Third, although experiments were conducted on three public MedVQA benchmarks, robustness under broader domain shifts remains an open issue. Differences in imaging modality, scanner protocol, institution, disease distribution, and annotation style may affect both candidate region generation and visual-language alignment. Future work will explore more robust region proposal strategies, external clinical validation, and extensions to more complex medical vision-language generation scenarios.
