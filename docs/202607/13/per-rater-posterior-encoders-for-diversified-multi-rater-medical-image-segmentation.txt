## Abstract:

Multi- rater medical image segmentation should produce one plausible output per annotator style, not collapse all the dis- agreement into one prediction. Most probabilistic models handle this by learning a shared posterior over all annota- tors' labels, sometimes supplemented with per- rater layers for personalisation. But training one shared encoder on all raters at once creates a problem: gradients from different raters average out rather than reinforce, so the latent space ends up encoding image content rather than rater style. No subsequent personalisation stage can recover what the shared encoder never encoded. We replace the shared encoder with N independent encoders, one per rater, each trained only on its rater's mask. The training objective becomes a sum of N per-rater ELBO terms; the shared decoder gets N clean, sepa- rate gradient signals instead of one blurred average. Inference is unchanged. On LIDC-IDRI (4-fold cross- validation), our Stage 1-only model achieves GED 0. 1444±0.0141(−4.2%) and Dice match 0.9112±0.0061(+2.28%).

### I. Introduction

When four radiologists look at the same lung nodule CT scan, they often draw four different boundaries, not because three of them are wrong, but because the boundary really is uncertain. Tissue transitions at nodule margins are gradual. What counts as the right delineation depends on clinical experience and judgment. A model that collapses all four opinions into one averaged prediction discards exactly the variation that clinical decisions depend on. The right model should instead produce one plausible output for every annotator style, so the full range of clinical disagreement is preserved.
