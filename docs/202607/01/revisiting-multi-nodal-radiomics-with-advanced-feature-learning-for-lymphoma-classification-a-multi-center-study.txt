Abstract 

Objective. Develop and evaluate a patient-level framework for discriminating Hodgkin lymphoma (HL) from non-HL (NHL) on baseline 18 F-FDG PET by aggregating radiomic descriptors from a variable number of involved lymph nodes in a multi-center setting. Approach. Each patient was modeled as an unordered set of lesion-level PET radiomic feature vectors. PET images were con-verted to standardized uptake values (SUV), resampled to a uniform voxel spacing of 2 × 2 × 2mm 3, and radiomic features were extracted using PyRadiomics from the original SUV images with a fixed bin width of 0.25 SUV units, following definitions consistent with the Image Biomarker Standardisation Initiative. A total of 107 first-order, texture, and shape features were extracted per lesion and optionally augmented with demographic variables and normalized node center-of-mass coordinates after rigid registration to a common reference space. We compared statistical pooled tabular baselines with learnable set-based aggregation models, including Transformer, Deep Sets, multiple instance learning, Set Transformer, and a graph-based model. Models were trained using five-fold cross-validation on Center 1 (151 patients; 85 HL, 66 NHL) and evaluated on an inde-pendent external Center 2 cohort (80 patients; 36 HL, 44 NHL) under direct transfer and unsu-pervised test-time adaptation (TTA). Main results. Combining radiomics with demographic and spatial features consistently improved discrimination. On Center 1, the best-performing deep aggregation models achieved ROC-AUC (mean ± standard deviation) up to 0 .91 ± 0.05 and accuracy up to 0 .84 ± 0.07. External evaluation on Center 2 revealed a measurable domain shift, with reduced performance under direct transfer. Unsupervised TTA, particularly TENT, improved cross-center generalization for deep aggregation models; for example, the Transformer improved from ROC-AUC 0 .81 ± 0.05 and F1-score 0 .60 ± 0.23 to ROC-AUC 0 .87 ± 0.01 and 

F1-score 0 .75 ± 0.01. Feature attribution analysis indicated that both demographic and radiomic descriptors contributed to the predictions. Significance. These results show that multi-nodal PET radiomics combined with demographic and spatial dissemination cues is a promising strategy for patient-level HL versus NHL prediction. The findings also highlight the importance of robustness considerations in multi-center PET radiomics and suggest that TTA may help mitigate cross-center variability.

## 1. Introduction 

Lymphoma comprises a diverse group of hematologic malignancies arising from lymphoid cells within the lymphatic system. Contemporary disease taxonomy separates lymphomas into two overarching enti-ties, Hodgkin lymphoma (HL) and non-HL (NHL), that differ in cellular origin, biological behavior, dissemination patterns, and therapeutic paradigms. HL is classically defined by the presence of Reed– Sternberg cells within a characteristic inflammatory microenvironment and often demonstrates a com-paratively predictable, contiguous nodal spread. NHL, in contrast, includes a broad spectrum of B-cell, T-cell, and NK-cell neoplasms with markedly heterogeneous clinical courses, ranging from indolent sub-types to highly aggressive disease requiring urgent treatment (Swerdlow 2008, Alaggio 2022, Cree 2022). Lymphoma poses a significant global health burden. According to GLOBOCAN 2022, NHL ranks among the most prevalent hematologic malignancies worldwide, with an age-standardized incidence rate of approximately 5.8 per 100 000 and a mortality rate of 2.6 per 100 000, with notable geographic variation (Sung et al 2021). In the United States, the age-adjusted incidence of NHL is around 18.7 per 100 000, with a mortality rate of 4.9 per 100 000 (Races and Males and Howlader 2020). The estimated 5 year survival rate is approximately 74% for NHL and 88%–90% for HL, although these values vary considerably depending on the disease stage and lymphoma subtype (American Cancer Society 2025a, 2025b). From a clinical standpoint, early discrimination between HL and NHL is critical because treatment selection, prognosis, and response monitoring strategies differ substantially across entities and subtypes. While histopathology from biopsy remains the reference standard, biopsy can be invasive, resource-intensive, and occasionally limited by sampling constraints, particularly in disseminated disease where multiple nodal stations may harbor heterogeneous biology. Moreover, visual assessment of medical images alone is generally insufficient for reliably distinguishing HL from NHL, as the imaging appear-ances of these entities often overlap and lack distinctive macroscopic features that allow consistent sub-type differentiation by human observers. As a result, there is growing interest in complementary, non-invasive virtual biopsy strategies that leverage imaging-derived biomarkers to support diagnostic workup and risk stratification (Barrington 2014, Hasanabadi 2025).

F-FDG PET/CT plays a central role in the management of most FDG-avid lymphomas, serving as the standard modality for baseline staging and treatment response assessment in HL and many aggres-sive NHL subtypes. Consensus guidance and widely adopted criteria recommend PET/CT for evaluat-ing disease extent and metabolic response, reflecting PET’s sensitivity to tumor glycolytic activity and its ability to capture whole-body disease burden (Barrington 2014, Cheson et al 2014). Although CT can provide complementary morphological information, in this study we deliberately focused on PET-derived radiomics in order to assess whether the metabolic phenotype and dissemination patterns captured by baseline PET are sufficient for patient-level HL/NHL discrimination. This choice also provided a more controlled multi-center setting, as the CT component was acquired as low-dose attenuation-correction CT with center-specific acquisition and reconstruction differences, which could introduce additional variability in CT radiomics. Accordingly, we restricted the present analysis to PET-only features. PET radiomics is sensitive not only to disease biology but also to imaging physics and protocol vari-ability. Factors such as voxel size, reconstruction algorithm, smoothing, and uptake time can influence SUV distributions and texture descriptors, while partial volume effects can degrade feature reliability in small lesions. These issues are particularly relevant in multi-center studies, where scanner and protocol related differences may introduce non-biological variation into radiomic features. Therefore, multi-center PET radiomics studies should consider robustness and harmonization alongside standardized preprocess-ing and external validation (Soret et al 2007, Adams et al 2010, Zwanenburg 2020). Radiomics and classical machine learning have been widely explored for lymphoma characterization. PET radiomics converts uptake patterns within segmented lesions into quantitative descriptors spanning first-order statistics, texture, and shape, enabling downstream predictive modeling. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses indicate that PET(/CT) radiomics can provide measurable diagnostic and prognos-tic value across lymphoma applications, while also highlighting recurring issues around heterogeneity, reproducibility, and validation design (Hasani et al 2022, Liu et al 2025). However, a distinctive chal-lenge in lymphoma is that disease is frequently multi-nodal; a single patient may present with a variable number of involved lymph nodes distributed across anatomical regions. In many radiomics pipelines, this variability is handled using handcrafted lesion-selection rules (e.g. largest or hottest lesions) or statistical pooling (e.g. mean/max/median across lesions). Such strategies are operationally simple and have demonstrated utility, but they can discard lesion-level heterogeneity and impose rigid assumptions about which nodes matter most (Triumbari et al 2023, 2025). Within this pooled-table paradigm, sev-eral studies have demonstrated that quantitative imaging features can support lymphoma discrimination 2Phys. Med. Biol. 71 (2026) 125014 R Karimzadeh et al 

tasks. Hasanabadi et al conducted a multicenter study using FDG PET radiomics with machine learning, including analyses relevant to lymphoma type and treatment-decision surrogates, and examined nodal versus extranodal information within a tabular modeling framework (Hasanabadi 2025). Complementary efforts include Malagi et al who explored multiparametric texture analysis from PET and DWI for HL– NHL discrimination (2025), and Erturk et al who reported radiomics-based neural network prediction of lymphoma types using low dose CT radiomics from PET/CT protocols (2023). Taken together, these studies support the premise that imaging-derived features contain discriminative signal; yet, they largely rely on fixed aggregation choices rather than learning how multiple nodes should be combined. Importantly, statistical pooling is not merely an implementation detail; it determines what infor-mation the model is allowed to use. By aggregating lesion-level descriptors into a small set of global summaries, statistical pooling can be effective for capturing overall disease burden, but it may under-represent variability in uptake, texture, and spatial distribution across nodal sites. Similarly, selecting a single representative lesion is attractive for simplicity, yet it assumes that one node sufficiently cap-tures the patient-level subtype signal and that the remaining lesions contribute little additional infor-mation. In lymphoma, where disease often presents with multi-site dissemination, modeling multiple nodes may therefore provide complementary information on lesion-level imaging variation and spatial spread beyond global summary biomarkers alone. Likewise, patient-level radiomics approaches based on a single combined region of interest (ROI) can capture overall burden and some degree of heterogeneity, but they do not explicitly model lesion-to-lesion interactions or adapt node importance across patients (Triumbari et al 2023, Cui 2025). A principled alternative is to treat each lymph node as an element in an unordered set and learn a permutation invariant aggregation function in a fully integrated pipeline. multiple instance learning (MIL) provides a direct formulation, each lesion/node becomes an instance, the patient is a bag, and the model learns how to pool instance features, often with attention that yields interpretability via instance importance scores. This paradigm has been successfully applied to radiomics in multifocal disease set-tings, showing that attention-based pooling can outperform fixed summaries by learning when a small subset of lesions dominates the outcome and when broader heterogeneity matters (Chen et al 2021, 2022). Conceptually, this maps cleanly onto disseminated lymphoma, where the number of nodes varies and a diagnosis may depend on a combination of dominant and supportive lesions rather than a single representative ROI. Beyond set pooling, disseminated lymphoma can also be modeled as a relational structure, where lesions/nodes are vertices and edges encode relationships (e.g. anatomical proximity, co-occurrence pat-terns, or feature similarity). Graph neural networks (GNNs) then enable message passing that captures inter-lesion dependencies, while attention mechanisms can fuse lesion representations with clinical vari-ables in a context-aware manner. Recent lymphoma work has operationalized this concept by construct-ing PET-derived lesion graphs and combining them with clinical data using attention-based fusion, illus-trating that explicit multi-lesion representation learning is feasible and can provide interpretability at the lesion level. Such approaches strengthen the argument that multi-nodal lymphoma is naturally suited to learnable aggregation that can capture heterogeneity and interaction effects more faithfully than hand-crafted pooling (Thiery et al 2025, Zhang et al 2025). These observations suggest a coherent methodological hierarchy for multi-nodal lymphoma radiomics; statistical pooling remains a strong, transparent baselines that enable classical ML, but they may be fundamentally limited by the information they discard. Modern architectures (Transformers, Deep Sets, Set Transformers, MIL, and GNNs) offer a complementary path in which aggregation is learned from data while respecting the set/graph structure inherent to multi-nodal disease. In parallel, when one deliberately commits to pooled tabular representations for reproducibility and comparability, TabPFN (Hollmann et al 2025a) provides a competitive tabular foundation-model baseline for patient-level radiomics tables. This framing naturally motivates evaluating both families: pooled-table models (including TabPFN) versus learnable multi-node aggregation that preserves lesion-level heterogeneity. Therefore, we can summarize our contributions in this work as follows: 

• Patient-level HL/NHL prediction is formulated as set-based learning from a variable size collection of PET lymph node radiomic descriptors, explicitly capturing inter-node heterogeneity within each patient. 

• Learned set aggregation models are introduced/evaluated to map node-level radiomics to a single patient-level subtype prediction with adaptive node importance. 

• Generalization under domain shift is assessed via a multi-center protocol, training on Center 1 and testing on an external Center 2 cohort. 3Phys. Med. Biol. 71 (2026) 125014 R Karimzadeh et al 

• We release a reproducible end-to-end pipeline for PET radiomics extraction, multi-node aggregation, and evaluation, designed to facilitate future research on multi-lesion modeling 6.

## 2. Material and methods 

This section presents our framework for patient-level lymphoma subtype prediction (HL vs NHL) by aggregating radiomic descriptors extracted from multiple involved lymph nodes on baseline PET imag-ing. Each patient is modeled as a variable-size set of node-level feature vectors, capturing both the het-erogeneous metabolic phenotype of individual nodes and the overall disease distribution across the body. As illustrated in figure 1, we extract node-level radiomics (e.g. intensity, texture, and shape features) and optionally augment them with normalized spatial coordinates and demographic variables. We then map the set of node descriptors to a single patient representation using either classical statistical pool-ing or deep set aggregation models, followed by a patient-level classifier that outputs the final HL/NHL prediction.

2.3. Patient-level classification models 

Following statistical aggregation, each patient is represented by a fixed-length feature vector zp ∈ R6d.These patient-level representations are used as input to a set of classical and modern tabular classifiers (gθ in equation (3)). In this setting, all models operate exclusively at the patient level and do not explic-itly model node-level structure or inter-node relationships. Logistic Regression is employed as a linear baseline classifier. It models the conditional class proba-bility as a linear function of the aggregated features followed by a logistic activation. Despite its simplic-ity, Logistic Regression provides a strong and interpretable reference point for evaluating more complex models (Hosmer et al 2013). Support vector machines (SVM) with a radial basis function kernel are used to capture non-linear decision boundaries in the aggregated feature space. By implicitly mapping inputs into a high-dimensional reproducing kernel Hilbert space, SVMs can model complex class separations while main-taining strong generalization properties (Hsu 2003). 5Phys. The k-nearest neighbors classifier assigns patient labels based on the majority class among the k

closest samples in the aggregated feature space. This non-parametric approach relies on local similarity structure and serves as a simple distance-based baseline (Hastie 2009). Tree-based ensemble models are extensively used due to their ability to capture non-linear feature interactions and robustness to feature scaling. We consider Random Forest and Extra Trees as bagging-based ensembles, as well as boosting-based methods including AdaBoost, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost (Freund and Schapire 1997, Breiman 2001, Friedman 2001, Geurts et al 2006, Chen 2016, Ke et al 2017, Prokhorenkova et al 2018). These methods construct an ensemble of decision trees to improve predictive performance and are particularly well suited for radiomics-based tabular data. In addition to conventional classifiers, we include TabPFN as a foundation model for tabular data. TabPFN is a transformer-based model pre-trained on a large corpus of synthetic tabular classification tasks, enabling it to approximate Bayesian inference over functions without task-specific training. Given patient-level representations zp, TabPFN directly produces class probabilities by conditioning on the full training set at inference time. Unlike traditional models, TabPFN does not require hyperparameter tun-ing or iterative optimization and is particularly well suited for low to medium sized tabular datasets (Hollmann et al 2023, 2025a, Grinsztajn 2025). By incorporating TabPFN, we aim to assess whether a pre-trained foundation model for tabular data can outperform or complement classical machine learning approaches in the context of statistically aggregated radiomic features. This comparison provides a strong reference point for evaluating the benefits of deep learning-based aggregation strategies introduced in the subsequent sections.

2.5. Dataset 

This cohort study included patients imaged between 2014–2024 at two institutions: Center 1 (Masih Daneshvari Hospital, Tehran, Iran) and Center 2 (Razavi Hospital, Mashhad, Iran). The study was approved by the Medical Ethical Review Committee of Shahid Beheshti University of Medical Sciences (IR.SBMU.NRITLD.REC.1402.060) and was conducted in accordance with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and local statutory requirements. Given the non-interventional use of routinely acquired clinical data, the committee waived informed consent. All patient data were fully anonymized prior to analysis to ensure confidentiality and protect patient privacy. Patients were eligible if they had (i) a new diagnosis of lymphoma, (ii) a baseline 18 F-FDG PET/CT performed before any treatment, and (iii) histopathological confirmation of the initial diagnosis. Exclusion criteria included incomplete imaging or clinical data, missing pathology confirmation, prior therapy before baseline PET/CT, negative/inconclusive diagnostic results, suspected concurrent infection, concurrent or recent malignancy, and scans with substantial motion or artifacts affecting pathological regions. We extracted age and gender from clinical/pathology records. The ground-truth label was defined using histopathology according to the WHO classification of hematopoietic and lymphoid tissues (Sabattini et al 2010). The prediction task in this work was restricted to binary classification: HL vs NHL. All subjects underwent routine whole-body 18 F-FDG PET/CT (vertex to mid-thigh). PET images were attenuation and scatter corrected using CT-based corrections provided by the vendor reconstruction. Acquisition parameters followed institutional clinical protocols: 

• Center 1: PET/CT examinations were performed on a GE Discovery 690 scanner (GE Healthcare, USA) equipped with time-of-flight (TOF) capability and a 64-slice CT. 18 F-FDG was administered intravenously at a weight-based dose of approximately 3 MBq/kg, with a mean administered activity of approximately 331 MBq (range: 99–521 MBq); the administered activity was calculated by sub-tracting the residual syringe activity from the calibrated dose. The uptake time ranged from 45 to 75 min (mean approximately 60 min), and each PET bed position was acquired for 2–3 min. PET images were reconstructed using GE VUE Point OSEM-based algorithms with TOF, using 2 itera-tions, 24 subsets, a 6.4 mm FWHM Gaussian post-reconstruction filter, and a 256 × 256 matrix (pixel size: 2 .73 × 2.73 mm; PET slice thickness: 3.27 mm). A subset of cases was reconstructed with VUE Point HD (OSEM + TOF, without PSF), while another subset was reconstructed with VUE Point FX (OSEM + TOF + PSF, GE SharpIR); all other reconstruction parameters were identical. PET images were corrected for scatter using model-based correction and for attenuation using CT-derived atten-uation maps. The low-dose CT was acquired at 120 kVp with automatic tube-current modulation (Smart mAs, 50–150 mA), pitch factor 0.9, and slice thickness 1.33–2.5 mm. 

• Center 2: PET/CT examinations were performed on a Siemens Biograph 6 TrueV scanner (Siemens Healthineers, Germany) equipped with a 6-slice CT and without TOF capability. 18 F-FDG was admin-istered intravenously at a weight-based dose of 0.1 mCi/kg (3.7 MBq/kg). The uptake time ranged from 50 to 70 min (mean approximately 60 min), and each PET bed position was acquired for 2.2 min. PET images were reconstructed using OSEM with point-spread-function modeling (Siemens TrueX/HD PET), using 2 iterations, 21 subsets, a 5 mm FWHM 3D Gaussian post-reconstruction fil-ter, and a 168 × 168 matrix (pixel size: 4 .07 × 4.07 mm; PET slice thickness: 5 mm). PET images were corrected for scatter using model-based correction and for attenuation using CT-derived attenuation maps. CT images were reconstructed using filtered back-projection, with a pitch factor of 0.55 and slice thickness 3 mm. These differences reflect realistic multi-center variability and motivated our external validation and adap-tation experiments. PET/CT studies were initially reviewed by an experienced nuclear medicine physician to confirm eligibility and image quality. Pathological lesions were delineated using a semi-automated graph-based segmentation approach (Beichel 2016) implemented in 3D Slicer (Fedorov 2012), followed by manual refinement as needed. Each patient was represented as a variable-size set of segmented lesions/nodes; the number of nodes varied substantially across patients, reflecting heterogeneous disease burden. All PET images were converted to SUV prior to analysis. Radiomic features were extracted on a lesion-wise basis using PyRadiomics (Van Griethuysen et al 2017) from the original SUV images and corresponding binary segmentation masks. Images were resampled to a uniform voxel spacing of 2 × 2 × 2 mm 3 using B-spline interpolation to ensure spatial consistency. Intensity discretization was per-formed using a fixed bin width of 0 .25 SUV units without intensity shifting. To improve computational 8Phys. Med. Biol. 71 (2026) 125014 R Karimzadeh et al 

efficiency, region-of-interest cropping available in PyRadiomics was applied prior to feature extraction. A total of 107 features per lesion were extracted, including shape, first-order, and texture features, follow-ing definitions consistent with the Image Biomarker Standardisation Initiative (IBSI) (Zwanenburg 2020). These features were used as node-level descriptors for patient-level aggregation. Given the multi-center nature of the dataset, scanner and protocol related variability was partially mitigated through standardized preprocessing, including SUV conversion, spatial resampling to a com-mon voxel spacing, fixed-bin-width intensity discretization, and IBSI-consistent feature extraction. No explicit feature-level harmonization method, such as ComBat (Johnson et al 2007), was applied in the present study. Instead, we treated cross-center variability as a central aspect of the evaluation and assessed robustness through a strictly held-out external test cohort, feature-level domain-shift analysis, and unsupervised test time adaptation (TTA). We acknowledge that these analyses characterize cross-center distributional differences but do not replace a dedicated radiomics repeatability or harmonization study. 

2.6. Implementation details 

In this study, the task-specific loss function (equation (4)) is defined as the binary cross-entropy loss applied to the two-logit output (HL vs NHL), followed by a softmax activation. The deep learning model’s parameters were optimized using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov and Hutter 2017). The class distribution is moderately imbalanced; therefore, no explicit class weighting was applied during training. Instead, model performance was evaluated using threshold-independent and imbalance-aware metrics, including ROC-AUC and F1-score. Hyperparameter tuning was performed separately for each model within the training folds to avoid data leakage. For classical machine learning models, we employed a grid search strategy over predefined hyperparameter ranges using only the training data of each fold. For deep learning models, a subset of the training data was held out as a validation set, and model selection was based on the lowest valida-tion loss, with early stopping applied to mitigate overfitting. The final model for each fold was evaluated on the corresponding held-out test split. Besides, Center 2 was not used at any stage of model devel-opment, including hyperparameter tuning, model selection, or feature engineering. After finalizing the models on Center 1, they were applied directly to Center 2 as a fully independent external test cohort. For the graph-based model, each patient is represented as a graph, where each node corresponds to a lymph node described by its feature vector and associated spatial location. Edges are constructed to encode spatial relationships between nodes. Second, incorporating simple clinical context (age and gender) and spatial information (normalized node coordinates) systematically improved performance and reduced variability. A key result of the ablation analysis was that radiomics-only formulations were consistently subopti-mal compared with configurations that additionally incorporated demographics and nodal coordinates. The demographic effect is clinically plausible: HL and NHL exhibit distinct age distributions in both centers, and gender composition is broadly comparable, making age a particularly informative covari-ate. Spatial coordinates further improved performance, supporting the hypothesis that lymphoma sub-type is reflected not only in node-level intensity/texture patterns but also in dissemination topology. Importantly, in our formulation coordinates are not used to encode patient identity or acquisition site; rather, all cases were rigidly registered to a common reference frame and each node was represented by its normalized center-of-mass coordinates, allowing the model to exploit relative anatomical patterns in a standardized manner. The class-conditional node distribution maps (figure 7) provide additional support for this premise. The node-percentage sweep experiment (figure 8) highlights another important property of multi-instance inference; performance and uncertainty depend strongly on the number of available nodes per patient. As progressively larger fractions of nodes were provided at inference time, ROC-AUC increased while variance across runs decreased. This behavior is expected for set aggregation, with more nodes, the model receives a more complete representation of disease burden and becomes less sensitive to any single lesion, reducing sampling noise and stabilizing patient-level decisions. The effect was most pronounced in low-node regimes, where subsampling can omit diagnostically informative lesions and amplify stochasticity in which nodes are presented. Notably, feature augmentation with demographics and coordinates tended to yield more stable performance under partial node availability, suggesting that global context (age/gender) and anatomical priors can compensate when lesion evidence is sparse. These findings have practical implications, naming, segmentation completeness is a relevant factor for deploy-ment, and training strategies such as node dropout or random subset sampling may further improve robustness to incomplete lesion sets. Despite strong Center 1 performance, direct transfer to Center 2 resulted in a degradation consistent with domain shift. Differences in scanner vendors, reconstruction algorithms, slice thickness, and cohort composition can all alter SUV distributions and texture statistics, and these effects are known to impact radiomics reproducibility. In this setting, TTA provided an effective mitigation. Simple normalization-based adaptation (Norm), which updates normalization statistics on target data, produced limited improvements, whereas TENT consistently improved performance on Center 2. This pattern suggests that the shift affects not only feature scaling but also the model’s confidence calibration and decision boundary; by minimizing prediction entropy on unlabeled target samples, TENT encourages confident predictions and adapts normalization parameters to better align target representations with the source-trained model. From an imaging physics perspective, the observed model behavior is likely influenced by several known limitations of PET radiomics. In particular, lesion size affects feature reliability because small lesions are more vulnerable to partial volume effects, which can blur lesion boundaries and underesti-mate tracer uptake, thereby reducing the stability of intensity and texture based descriptors. In addition, SUV-related measurements are sensitive to scanner hardware, reconstruction settings, uptake time, and acquisition protocol, all of which can introduce variability in first-order and higher-order radiomic fea-tures across centers. This is especially relevant in our multi-center setting, where table 1 shows differ-ences in SUV max , MTV, and TMTV between cohorts, and may partly explain the domain shift observed between Center 1 and Center 2. In a multi-nodal framework, these effects are compounded by within-patient heterogeneity in lesion size and metabolic activity: larger, more avid lesions tend to yield more stable radiomic signatures, whereas smaller or low-contrast lesions may contribute noisier features (Orlhac et al 2018, Traverso et al 2018). This interpretation is consistent with figure 8, where perfor-mance improved and variability decreased as more nodes were included, suggesting that aggregating information across multiple lesions can partially mitigate lesion-level noise, partial volume effects, and cross-center SUV variability. Overall, these imaging physics considerations provide a plausible explana-tion for the greater sensitivity of radiomics-only models to domain shift and for the improved robust-ness observed with multi-node aggregation and TTA (Soret et al 2007, Adams et al 2010, Zwanenburg 2020). We also performed a thorough study on learnable aggregation models against pooled tabular base-lines. For clarity in the main manuscript, we reported TabPFN and AdaBoost as representative strong baselines. TabPFN serves as a modern tabular foundation model, while AdaBoost provided a competi-tive classical reference among conventional ML methods; the full set of tabular baselines is provided in the Supplementary Material. In general, deep aggregation approaches offer an advantage when node-level evidence is heterogeneous and when lesion-to-lesion interactions and adaptive node weighting are 18 Phys. Med. Biol. 71 (2026) 125014 R Karimzadeh et al 

important. Conversely, statistical pooled feature baselines can be more stable under shift when the aggre-gation compresses noisy lesion-level variability, which motivates reporting both paradigms. This work has limitations. First, we focused on binary HL versus NHL prediction, NHL is biolog-ically heterogeneous, and subtype level stratification (e.g. DLBCL and indolent lymphomas) was out-side the scope of this study. Second, results depend on lesion segmentation quality and completeness; node counts vary widely across patients and centers, and incomplete lesion coverage may affect perfor-mance, as indicated by the node-percentage sweep. Third, rigid registration and normalized center of mass coordinates provide only a coarse approximation of anatomical correspondence; future work could incorporate atlas-based region encoding or more expressive spatial graphs. Another limitation is the rel-atively high feature dimensionality relative to sample size. Although tuning and model selection were restricted to training data within each cross-validation fold, pooled tabular models remain more sus-ceptible to overfitting than the learnable aggregation frameworks. Finally, we did not incorporate CT-derived radiomics. While CT may provide complementary morphological information, we prioritized PET because it is the primary functional modality in lymphoma assessment and because low-dose CT acquisition differed across centers, potentially reducing the robustness of CT radiomics in this retrospec-tive multi-center setting. Future work should investigate whether multimodal PET +CT feature integra-tion further improves subtype discrimination and cross-center generalization. Furthermore, several methodological limitations should be emphasized. First, although standardized preprocessing and IBSI-consistent feature extraction were used, we did not perform explicit radiomics harmonization or feature stability selection. Therefore, some extracted features may remain sensitive to scanner, reconstruction, uptake-time, and lesion-size effects. Second, the strong age imbalance between HL and NHL represents a potential confounder. The demographic-only baseline and ablation experi-ments indicate that radiomics and spatial features provide information beyond demographics, but they do not fully replace age-matched or age-adjusted validation. Third, the graph construction used a fixed Gaussian distance kernel with σ = 0.1; future work should evaluate sensitivity to this parameter and explore anatomically informed graph definitions based on nodal stations or atlas-derived regions. Future research should evaluate multi-center training and harmonization strategies to further improve robustness, extend the framework to multi-class subtype prediction, and integrate richer clin-ical context when available. From a modeling perspective, incorporating anatomically-informed graph structures, improving calibration under domain shift, and explicitly training for robustness to missing nodes are promising directions. Overall, our results indicate that combining node-level PET radiomics with simple demographic cues and spatial dissemination information provides a principled and effective approach for patient-level lymphoma subtype prediction, with improved generalization when coupled with TTA methods. 

## 5. Conclusion 

In conclusion, we proposed a patient-level framework for HL versus NHL prediction from baseline 18 F-FDG PET by aggregating node-level radiomic features from a variable number of segmented involved nodes. On Center 1, deep aggregation models achieved strong discrimination, and the ablation study showed consistent improvements when demographic variables and normalized node coordinates were included. On external evaluation in Center 2, direct transfer revealed a measurable domain shift, while TTA improved generalization without requiring target labels. Overall, these findings suggest that combin-ing radiomics with demographic and spatial dissemination cues is a promising approach for patient-level lymphoma subtype prediction. However, given the modest size of the external cohort, further validation in larger independent multi-center datasets is needed before drawing conclusions about clinical utility or deployment.
