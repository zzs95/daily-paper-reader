ABSTRACT 

Accurate survival prediction is critical for guiding individualized radiotherapy in 

locally advanced cervical cancer (LACC), yet most existing models rely solely on 

pre -treatment imaging and overlook tumor changes during therapy. In this study, we 

develope d and validated a multivariable Cox model to predict disease -free survival 

(DFS) in 971 LACC patients undergoing concurrent chemoradiotherapy. The model 

incorporated clinical variables, radiomics features from pre - and post -radiotherapy 

CT scans, and their changes during treatment, thereby quantifying treatment -

associated imaging changes . It achieved C -indices of 0.713 and 0.693 in internal and 

external validation cohorts, with AUCs up to 0.859, showing improved overall 

performance compared with single -time point imaging models in most evaluation 

settings. By integrating dynamic imaging and clinical data, the model improved 

prognostic accuracy, enhanced risk stratification, and showed strong potential for 

supporting personalized radiotherapy planning.

Introduction 

Cervical cancer is one of the most prevalent malignancies among women 1. The 

standard radiotherapy for locally advanced cervical cancer (LACC) is concurrent 

chemoradiotherapy (CCRT) 2. In the era of precision oncology, the goal is to 

personalize radiotherapy according to the unique needs of each patient. For patients 

with LACC who are at high risk of radiotherapy failure, intensified therapies —

immunotherapy 3,4 and induction chemotherapy 5, in addition to CCRT —are 

employed to improve patient survival outcomes. In contrast, for low -risk patients, 

radiotherapy intensity is reduced through strategi es such as lowering the radiation 

dose, thereby minimizing the risk of both acute and chronic radiation -induced 

toxicities. The success of personalized radiotherapy strategies depends on accurate 

prognostic risk stratification. 

Currently, risk stratificat ion for LACC primarily relies on staging and basic clinical 

characteristics. However, staging alone is insufficient for accurate prognostication, 

as significant heterogeneity exists in the outcomes of patients with the same stage 6.

Additionally, cancer s themselves exhibit considerable heterogeneity, including 

varying radiation sensitivit y among patients 7. This variability is likely linked to 

genomic differences within the tumors 8. For patients with LACC, the combined dose 

of external beam radiation therapy (EBRT) and brachytherapy typically exceeds 85 

Gy 2. However, some patients with lower radiation sensitivity may not achieve 

complete remission at this dose, while others with higher sensitivity may not require 

such high radiation levels. 

Due to the limitations of current risk stratification and the considerable heterogeneity 

among tumors and patient responses, more effective methods are needed for 

individualized prognosis. Radiomics, as an advanced imaging tool, can capture these ARTICLE IN PRESS

complex tumor characteristics and help guide personalized radiotherapy decisions 9-

. Radiomics is a method that transforms medical imaging data into high -

dimensional quantitative features, including intensity distribution, shape, texture, 

and spatial heterogeneity, which may not be readily captured by visual assessment 

alone 9,10 . Through systematic analysis of these features, radiomics may provide 

ind irect information related to tumor heterogeneity, aggressiveness, and treatment -

associated imaging changes 12 -14 , thereby supporting personalized radiotherapy 

decision -making 9,15 . Several radiomics models, utilizing CT, MRI, or PET/CT scans, 

have been developed to predict the efficacy of radiotherapy in cervical cancer 

patients 16 -18 . These models predominantly rely on pre -radiotherapy imaging, which 

limits their ability to explore radiomic features re lated to radiotherapy sensitivity. In 

contrast, delta radiomics enables the comparison of medical images obtained before, 

during, or after radiotherapy , allowing for the extraction of dynamic changes in 

radiomic features 19 . This approach p rovides a quantitative description of radiomic 

changes occurring during radiotherapy 19,20 . The tumor ’s response to radiotherapy 

can, to some extent, reflect its inherent sensitivity to radiation. Currently, studies on 

delta radiomics in cervical c ancer remain limited. In a study by Small C et al., 39 

patients with LACC were included, and MRI images before and after brachytherapy 

were analyzed, demonstrating that delta radiomics could predict tumor progression 

after radiotherapy 20 .

In this study, we integrated radiomic features extracted from both pre - and post -

rad iotherapy imaging, as well as their respective differences, and combined these 

with clinical variables to develop a delta radiomics -based survival prediction model 

for patients with cervical cancer undergoing definitive radiotherapy. The robustness 

and cli nical applicability of the model were further validated using both internal and 

external cohorts, thereby ensuring its reliability across different clinical settings. ARTICLE IN PRESS

Patient Characteristics 

A total of 854 patients from center 1 were included, with 512 in the training cohort 

and 342 in the internal validation cohort. Most patients had squamous cell carcinoma 

(90.2%) and FIGO stage IIB (43.2%), with 28.9% showing pelvic lymph node

year and 5 -year DFS rates were 63.9% and 31.9%, respectively ( Table 1).

Discussion 

Previous prognostic studies in cervical cancer have predominantly relied on 

radiomic or cl inical data obtained at a single time point —either before or after 

radiotherapy 16 -18,2 1. Such static modeling often fails to capture the temporal 

dynamics of tumor evolution and radiotherapy response, limiting predictive 

accuracy and clinical utility. In this study, we proposed a novel survival prediction 

framework that integrates clinical data and radiomic features from both pre - (R1) 

and post -radiotherapy (R2) imaging, along with explicitly defined difference (Diff) 

features, which quantify the tempor al changes between the two phases. These Diff 

features were found to be particularly informative in improving the prediction of 

DFS .

From a modeling standpoint, conventional Cox models treat R1 and R2 as 

independent variables, ignoring the inherent temporal relationship between them 16 -

> 18, 21

. Diff features bridge this gap by embedding longitudinal changes directly into 

the feature space, allowing Cox models to incorporate temporal imaging information 

while maintaining interpretability . While deep learning models may implicitly learn 

such dynamics from raw sequences, they often lack transparency. The inclusion of 

explicit Diff featur es represents a more practical and interpretable strategy, providing 

a practical and interpretable strategy for incorporating longitudinal imaging changes 

into prognostic modeling. 

In this study, we identified four pre -to -post -radiotherapy delta radiomic f eatures 

associated with DFS. These features may reflect treatment -associated phenotypic 

changes, including tumor regression, changes in lesion shape, spatial heterogeneity, 

necrosis, edema, stromal remodeling, or alterations in the tumor 

microenvironment 13,14,2 2,2 3. Nevertheless, the biological interpretation of delta ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS 

radiomic features remains indirect. Radiotherapy response is a complex and non -

linear biological process involving DNA damage repair, hypoxia, apoptosis, immune 

activation, vascular changes, and microenvironmental remodeling. Therefore, our 

findings should be interpreted as prognostic associ ations rather than evidence that 

delta radiomics directly measures intrinsic radiosensitivity . Future studies 

incorporating matched pre - and post -radiotherapy tissue samples, genomic 

sequencing, transcriptomic profiling, hypoxia markers, immune profiling, and 

apoptosis -related biomarkers are needed to clarify the biological mechanisms 

underlying these imaging changes. 

Another methodological consideration is the use of CTV rather than GTV for 

radiomic feature extraction. Although GTV more directly reflects the visible tumor 

burden, CTV is the clinically actionable target volume in definitive radiotherapy and 

incorporates regions at risk for microscopic extension. This may be particularly 

relevant in cervical cancer, where local extension and peritumoral tiss ue 

involvement are closely related to treatment response and recurrence risk. Moreover, 

CTV is routinely delineated during radiotherapy planning, which improves the 

practical applicability of the model without requiring additional GTV segmentation 

solely f or radiomics analysis. 

Despite promising results, our study has several limitations. First, the dataset was 

collected from only two centers with a relatively small sample size, which may 

restrict generalizability. Although the model showed favorable perfor mance in the 

internal validation cohort, its risk stratification performance in the external 

validation cohort should be interpreted with caution. In the external cohort, the 

Kaplan -Meier curves showed a consistent trend toward poorer DFS in the predicted 

high -risk group, but the difference did not reach statistical significance. This may be 

partly explained by the smaller sample size of the external cohort, which reduced the ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS 

statistical power for detecting survival differences between risk groups. In addit ion, 

baseline clinical characteristics differed between the internal and external cohorts, 

including FIGO stage distribution, SCC antigen level, pelvic lymph node metastasis, 

and para -aortic lymph node metastasis. These differences suggest greater clinical 

heterogeneity in the external cohort and may have weakened the separation between 

the predicted high - and low -risk groups. Potential center -specific differences in 

imaging acquisition, treatment planning, contouring practice, and follow -up may 

have furthe r contributed to performance variation. Therefore, larger multicenter 

cohorts with harmonized imaging and clinical protocols are needed to further 

validate and refine the model ’s risk stratification performance. In addition, the 

multimodal design of the mo del may introduce further methodological complexity, 

because integrating clinical variables with pre -radiotherapy, post -radiotherapy, and 

delta radiomic features can amplify the effects of heterogeneity in imaging 

acquisition, segmentation, preprocessing, and follow -up across centers. Future 

studies with harmonized imaging protocols and standardized feature extraction 

pipelines are needed to confirm the robustness and generalizability of this 

multimodal framework. 

Second, although the Diff features showed a dditional prognostic value, their 

underlying biological mechanisms remain incompletely understood. Radiotherapy 

response is a complex and highly non -linear biological process involving DNA 

damage repair, hypoxia, apoptosis, immune activation, vascular remo deling, stromal 

changes, and tumor microenvironmental adaptation 24,2 5,2 6. Therefore, the observed 

CT -based radiomic changes should be interpreted cautiously as treatment -associated 

phenotypic imaging biomarkers rather than as direct evidence of intrinsic 

radiosensitivity 24,2 7. Changes in shape and texture features may reflect macroscopic 

consequences of tumor regression, spatial heterogeneity, necrosis, edema, stromal ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS 

remodeling, or alterations in the tumor microenvironment; however, these 

interpretations r emain indirect 25,2 7. Future radiogenomic and multi -omics studies 

incorporating matched pre - and post -radiotherapy tissue samples, genomic 

sequencing, transcriptomic profiling, hypoxia -related markers, apoptosis -related 

biomarkers, and immune profiling are needed to clarify the biological basis of delta 

radiomic features and to determine whether specific imaging changes are linked to 

intrinsic radiation response. 24,2 7

Third, the Cox proportional hazards model used in this study has inherent 

methodological li mitations. The Cox model assumes proportional hazards and an 

approximately log -linear association between covariates and the hazard. Although 

this model was selected because of its interpretability, clinical transparency, and 

ability to provide hazard rati os with confidence intervals, it cannot fully capture the 

complex non -linear relationships that may exist between high -dimensional radiomic 

features and survival outcomes. In the present study, we did not explicitly model 

non -linear effects using spline tr ansformations or non -linear survival models. Future 

studies with larger multicenter datasets should compare Cox -based models with 

more flexible survival modeling approaches, such as random survival forests, 

gradient boosting survival models, and neural net work -based survival models, while 

balancing predictive performance, interpretability, and clinical applicability. 

To reduce the risk that hazard ratio estimates were distorted by redundancy among 

pre -radiotherapy, post -radiotherapy, and Diff features, we a pplied correlation -based 

feature filtering before model construction. Highly correlated features with Pearson 

correlation coefficients greater than 0.7 were removed, and variance inflation factors 

were calculated for the final predictors. The VIF analysis did not indicate severe 

multicollinearity among the retained variables. In addition, the proportional hazards 

assumption was assessed using Schoenfeld residuals. A small number of radiomic ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS 

predictors, including FOS_RMS_pre, GLDM_LDHGLE_post, and FOS_Entr_p re, 

showed potential deviations from the proportional hazards assumption. Therefore, 

the model should be interpreted primarily as a prognostic prediction model rather 

than as a causal inference model. 

Finally, causal interpretation remains limited by the r etrospective study design and 

the availability of only two predefined imaging time points. Although delta radiomic 

features quantify imaging changes between pre - and post -radiotherapy CT scans, 

these changes should not be interpreted as direct causal deter minants of survival. 

Potential time -dependent confounders, such as treatment response dynamics, 

treatment interruptions, systemic therapy variation, and patient -specific biological 

factors, may influence both radiomic changes and DFS. Because repeated 

long itudinal imaging was not available, formal stationarity testing or Granger 

causality analysis was not feasible. Future prospective studies with repeated imaging 

at multiple treatment time points and prospectively collected treatment -response 

variables are needed to more rigorously evaluate temporal relationships, potential 

causal pathways, and the long -term utility of dynamic multimodal survival models 

in cervical cance 

Methods 

Study design and patients 

As illustrated in Figure 3, this study developed a multimodal Cox proportional 

hazards model, referred to as the Delta model, which integrates pre - and post -

radiotherapy CT imaging with clinical features to predict disease -free survival (DFS) 

in cervical cancer patients undergoing definitive radiotherapy. The model 

construction involved three key steps: First, predefined handcrafted radiomic 

features, including shape, first -order intensity, and texture features, were extracted ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS 

from the CTV delineated on pre - and post -radiotherapy CT scans ; Second, these 

radiomic features were combined with key clinical variables to form a

multidimensional hybrid feature set, followed by rigorous feature selection to 

identify the most informative predictors; Third, a multivariate Cox proportional 

haza rds model was built based on the selected features to perform survival prediction. 

Model development was based on a training cohort of 512 patients from Dongdan 

Branch of Peking Union Medical College Hospital (Center 1) , integrating 

comprehensive imaging and clinical data. For model validation, two independent 

cohorts were employed: an internal validation cohort (n = 342) from the same 

institution to evaluate model stability and consistency, and an external validation 

co hort (n = 117) from the Xidan Branch of Peking Union Medical College Hospital 

(Center 2) to assess generalizability across heterogeneous populations. Detailed 

patient inclusion criteria are provided in Figure S1. Model performance was 

comprehensively evalu ated using multiple metrics, including the Brier score, area 

under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC), Harrell ’s concordance 

index (C -index), and Kaplan –Meier survival analysis. 

The Institutional Review Board of Peking Union Medical College Hospital approve 

this study with the ethical principles outlined in the Declaration of Helsinki. The 

ethical committee process number is K5231. The requirement for informed consent 

was waived by Institute Review Board. All patients had previously provided general 

consent for the potential research use of their medical records and imaging data.

Imaging Acquisition and Region of Interest 

In this study, pelvic CT images for all patients were acquired using a high -

performance multi -slice spiral CT scanner at the two participating cohorts . Pre -

radiotherapy CT was acq uired 1 -2 weeks before treatment. Post -radiotherapy CT 

was scanned after 20 f ractions of EBRT and 1 fraction of brachytherapy. The 

imaging data were standardized and stored in DICOM format. The clinical target ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS 

volume (CTV) was considered the region of interest (ROI) in this study, which 

included the primary tumor, cervix, uterus, p arametrium, upper vagina, and pelvic 

lymph nodes region ( including common iliac, external iliac, internal iliac, obturator, 

and presacral lymph node regions ). The CTV was selected as the ROI rather than the 

gross tumor volume (GTV) for both clinical and methodological considerations. 

From a radiotherapy perspective, the CTV represents the standard treatment target 

volume in definitive radiotherapy for cervical c ancer and includes not only the gross 

tumor but also adjacent regions at risk for microscopic disease. Therefore, CTV -

based radiomic features may capture prognostically relevant information from both 

the tumor and the peritumoral high -risk microenvironment . In addition, the boundary 

of the GTV on CT images can be difficult to define precisely, especially in locally 

advanced cervical cancer and after treatment -related tumor regression, which may 

introduce inter -observer variability and reduce the reproducibi lity of radiomic 

features. In contrast, CTV delineation follows standardized radiotherapy planning 

principles and was routinely available in the treatment planning workflow. Using 

CTV as the ROI therefore enhances the clinical feasibility, reproducibility, and 

translational potential of the proposed model. The upper boundary of the CTV was 

defined as the aortic bifurcation. The segmentation process combined automatic 

segmentation with manual correction to ensure the accuracy and consistency of the 

results. Initial CTV contours were generated using an in -house deep learning -based 

automatic segmentation network based on 3D nnU -Net, rather than commercial 

software. After automatic segmentation with the pretrained model, two board -

certified radiation oncologists (W.W. and X.L., each with more than five years of 

experience in cervical cancer CTV delineation) manually reviewed and modified the 

automatically segmented target areas when necessary. Any disagreements were 

adjudicated by a chief physician in radiation o ncology (Xiaorong Hou, with over 20 ARTICLE IN PRESS

years of experience). While all the aforementioned physicians were aware that the 

patients were undergoing radical radiotherapy for cervical cancer, they were blinded 

to other clinical characteristics, such as clinical s tage, pathological type, and lymph

Model Development 

In this study, radiomic features were extracted from pre - and post -radiotherapy CT 

images using PyRadiomics (version 3.1.0). Before feature extraction, all CT images 

were preproc essed using the same pipeline across all cohorts. Spatial resolution was 

standardized by resampling all images to a voxel spacing of 1  × 1 × 1 mm ³. To 

reduce inter -patient and inter -scanner gray -level variation, including variation 

potentially related to contrast enhancement, image intensity harmonization was 

performed using a histogram matching -based method , Specifically, histogram 

matching was performed using a reference histogram generated from the training 

cohort, and the same reference was applied to the internal and external validation 

cohorts. This approach was intended to improve preprocessing consistency across 

cohorts while minimizing the risk of information leakage . A total of 107 handcrafted 

radiomic features were extracted in 3D from each pre - and post -radiotherapy CT 

image within the CTV ROI. These features included shape features, first -order 

intensity features, gray -level co -occurrence matrix (GLCM) features, gray -level 

dependence matrix (GLDM) features, gray -level run length matrix (GLRLM) 

features, gray -level size zone matrix (GLSZM) features, and neighboring gray tone 

difference matrix (NGTDM) features. PyRadiomics implements standardized 

definitions for many commonly used radiomic features in accordance with the Image 

Biomarker Standardisa tion Initiative (IBSI) . To improve reproducibility, the same ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS 

preprocessing and feature extraction settings were applied to all cohorts. Gray -level 

discretization was performed using a fixed bin width of 25 HU , and the same 

discretization setting was applie d to all images. No intensity -based re -segmentation 

was applied, and all voxels within the delineated CTV were included for feature 

extraction. Subsequently, the differences in radiomic features between pre - and post -

radiotherapy CT images were calculated. The features from pre - and post -

radiotherapy CT , their differences, and clinical variables were combined, resulting 

in a total of 330 features. Next, a univariate Cox regression model (based on lifelines, 

version 0.27.8) was used for preliminary feature s election, retaining features 

significantly associated with DFS (p < 0.05). On this basis, Pearson correlation 

coefficients were calculated, and highly correlated features (correlation coefficient > 

0.7) were removed to reduce redundancy and the impact of m ulticollinearity. 

Variance inflation factors (VIFs) were further calculated for the final predictors to 

evaluate residual multicollinearity, with VIF > 10 considered indicative of severe 

multicollinearity. No severe multicollinearity was observed among the final 

predictors. 

As shown in Figure 4A, a total of 28 features were ultimately selected for modeling, 

including 10 radiomic features from pre -radiotherapy CT images, 7 from post -

radiotherapy images, 4 difference features, and 7 clinical features. A multi variate 

Cox regression model (lifelines 0.27.8) was then constructed to predict DFS based 

on these filtered features. The proportional hazards assumption of the final Cox 

model was assessed using Schoenfeld residuals. Due to the excessive length of 

feature names, abbreviations were used, and the corresponding relationships are 

provided in Table S 2. The correlations among features included in the model are 

presented in Figure S2. The feature engineering and correlation analysis were 

conducted using scikit -learn (version 1.2.2). This modeling process combines ARTICLE IN PRESS 

ARTICLE IN PRESS 

clinical factors with multi -timepoint imaging information, providing a reliable tool 

and methodological foundation for individualized prediction of DFS. The consistent 

distribution of selected fe atures across cohorts supports the robustness and 

generalizability of the model in cross cohort validation (Figure S 3). 

Evaluation of the Prognostic Prediction Model for Radiotherapy Outcomes 

We systematically validated the prognostic accuracy of the feature set that 

incorporated radiomic features and their differences derived from the pre - and post -

radiotherapy CT images for predicting DFS in cervical cancer patients undergoing 

CCRT , and asses sed its discriminative ability and predictive probability accuracy. 

Discrimination was quantified using the C -index, which measures the accuracy of 

the model in ranking individual patient outcomes. Predictive probability accuracy 

was evaluated using the Br ier score and calibration curves, comparing the model ’s

predicted survival probabilities with actual observed outcomes. To further assess the 

model ’s discriminative ability, patients in the training cohort were divided into high -

and low -risk groups based on the median predicted survival score. The same cutoff 

was applied to the validation cohort to ensure the robustness of the model on the test 

dataset. In addition, we compared the predictive performance to that of feature sets 

without incorporating the di fferences in radiomic features between pre - and post -

radiotherapy CT images.
