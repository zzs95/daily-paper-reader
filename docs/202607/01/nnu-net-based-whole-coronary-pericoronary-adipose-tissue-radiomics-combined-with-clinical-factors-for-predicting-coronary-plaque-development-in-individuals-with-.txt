Abstract 

Background  Effective early prediction of coronary artery disease remains 

challenging. This study aims  to investigate whether radiomic features of pericoronary 

adipose tissue (PCAT) derived from the whole -coronary tree using  nnU -Net, 

combined with PCAT metrics and clinical factors, can predict coronary plaque 

development during follow -up in individuals with normal baseline coronary arteries. ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS Materials and  Methods  In this retrospective study, 210 patients with normal baseline 

coronary CT angiography (CCTA) findings who underwent follow -up CCTA were 

included and classified into plaque -positive and plaque -negative groups.  Baseline 

clinical data and PCAT metrics ( fat attenuation index [FAI] and fat volume [FV] )

were collected.  Radiomic features were extracted from whole -coronary PCAT  based 

on nnU -Net. Patients were randomly  divided  into training and  validation set s (7:3). 

Five logistic regression models (clinical, PCAT, clinical –PCAT, radiomics, and 

combined) were constructed and  compared using receiver operating characteristic 

analysis, calibration curves, and decision curve analysis. 

Results  During follow -up, 77 of 210 patients (36.7%) developed coronary plaques. 

One clinical factor, three PCAT metrics, and 20 radiomic features were selected.  The 

combined model demonstrated the best performance,  with training/ validation set  AUC 

values  of 0.931 (95% CI: 0.889 –0.973) and 0.896 (95% CI: 0.821 –0.970).  Calibration 

and  decision curve analyses demonstrated good agreement and clinical utility. 

Conclusion  An automated nnU -Net -based whole -coronary PCAT radiomics model, 

integrated with clinical factors, demonstrates potential for early risk stratification of 

plaque  development in individuals with normal coronary arteries.

Coronary artery disease (CAD) is a common cardiovascular disorder in clinical 

practice. With the aging of the population, the prevalence of CAD has increased 

annually  [1]. Moreover, as plaques progress, the risk of major adverse cardiovascular 

events (MACE) rises accordingly. Although recent advances in novel 

technologies —such as  hybrid cell membrane -coated nanoparticles, targeted drug 

delivery systems, and multimodal imaging —have yielded promising results in the 

diagnosis and treatment of CAD  [2 -4] , the early prediction, detection, and 

intervention of coronary plaques remain of pa ramount importance for preventing 

plaque formation, retarding disease progression, and ultimately improving patient 

prognosis. 

Common clinical risk factors for coronary plaque occurrence or progression include 

age, body mass index (BMI), hypercholesterolem ia, hypertension, diabetes mellitus, 

elevated triglycerides, etc.  [5,6] . However, these systemic factors alone provide 

limited information about the local vascular inflammatory microenvironment and 

have suboptimal performance in predicting early plaque dev elopment.  In recent years, 

the concept of pericoronary adipose tissue (PCAT) has been proposed, defined as 

adipose tissue located in the perivascular space of the coronary artery, extending 

radially outward to a distance equal to the inner diameter of the  vessel  [7] . Due to its 

close proximity to the vascular wall, PCAT shares tight anatomical and physiological 

connections with the vessel wall and can act as a "sensor" reflecting the degree of 

vascular wall inflammation, accurately indicating inflammatory s tatus. Common ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS PCAT metrics include the fat attenuation index (FAI) and fat volume (FV)  [8,9] ,

which primarily reflect computed tomography  (CT ) attenuation and volume changes 

of fat within the PCAT region.  Numerous studies have shown that FAI and FV are 

val uable for predicting plaque progression  [10,11 ] and MACE occurrence  [12 -15]  in 

CAD patients.  Importantly, vascular and perivascular inflammation may already be 

present before visible plaque formation, suggesting that PCAT metrics could enable 

early risk as sessment in individuals with initially normal coronary arteries. However, 

evidence in this specific population remains limited. 

Additionally, limitations exist for FAI and FV, as they are based on fat voxel 

intensity. Compared with conventional imaging met rics, radiomics (or texture 

analysis) can deeply mine internal tissue information, visualize and quantify subtler 

differences within lesions or tissues, thereby improving diagnostic efficacy  [16] .

Studies have shown that radiomics significantly enhances  the predictive performance 

for coronary plaque occurrence, progression, and MACE  [5 ,1 7, 18] .

Nevertheless, most previous investigations focused on PCAT surrounding proximal coronary segments or established plaques [5,19], largely due to the lack of reliable tools for whole-coronary artery segmentation. However, analyzing only proximal vessels fails to capture the systemic inflammatory burden of the entire coronary tree. For individuals with normal baseline coronary arteries, future plaque locations are unpredictable and may occur at any segment. Consequently, a comprehensive assessment of the entire coronary tree using radiomics features is theoretically superior to proximal-only analysis in predicting plaque development. The nnU-Net ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS framework is a self-configuring deep learning segmentation pipeline that has demonstrated robust performance across various medical imaging tasks [20]. By enabling efficient and accurate automatic segmentation, nnU-Net provides an opportunity to perform whole-coronary PCAT radiomics analysis in a reproducible manner. 

Therefore, the aim of this study was to evaluate the predictive value of 

whole -coronary PCAT radiomics features extracted using nnU -Net, in combination 

with conventional PCAT metric s and clinical risk factors, for predicting plaque 

development during mid -term follow -up in individuals with normal baseline coronary 

arteries. We further sought to construct and validate a multimodal predictive model to 

improve early risk stratification a nd support personalized preventive strategies. 

Materials and Methods 

Study Population 

This study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki (revised in 

2013). It was approved by the Medical Ethics Committees of Bozhou People's 

Hospital  (Center 1 ) and The First Affiliated Hospital of Anhui Medical University 

(Center  2)  (ethical approval numbers: 2025 -273, 2025 -11 -85) , clinical trial number: 

not applicable . As this was a retrospective analysis, individual informed consent was 

waived. 

Initi ally, 971 patients who underwent at least two coronary computed tomography 

angiography (CCTA) examinations at Center 1 and Center  2 from July 2019 to March ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 2025 were enrolled. Based on the following inclusion and exclusion criteria 

(Supplementary Material  1 and  Supplementary Fig. 1 ), 210 patients were finally 

included (195 from Center 1 and 15 from Center 2).  This study was designed as a single-center model development and internal validation study. Although data from two centers were pooled to increase sample size, the proportion of cases from Center 2 (n=15) was insufficient to constitute an independent external validation cohort. Therefore, the dataset was randomly split into a training set (70%) and a validation set (30%) for internal validation. 

Baseline clinical data, including age, sex, BMI, smoking status, alcohol 

consumption, hypertension, diabetes mellitus, and hyperlipidemia, were collected 

from medical records  (flowchart shown in  Suppleme ntary  Fig . 1) .

Instruments and Methods 

CCTA examinations were performed using a GE Revolution CT scanner.  The scan 

range extended from the tracheal carina to 2 cm below the diaphragm. A total of 60 –

80 mL of iohexol (350 mg I/mL) was injected intravenously  at 5 mL/s, followed by 

40 mL of saline.  Scanning was triggered using bolus tracking with a threshold of 120 

Hounsfield unit ( HU ) in the ascending aorta. Scanning parameters included 

retrospective ECG gating, tube voltage of 100 kV, Smart mA (300 –545 mA),  and 

slice thickness and interval of 0.625 mm.  Optimal images from the two examinations 

were selected and transferred to the  Deepwise  vascular CT  imaging  diagnostic  system 

(abbreviated as  Deepwise  artificial  intelligence  (AI) , version 3.0.0.1, Hangzhou ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS Deep wise Bolian Technology Co., Ltd., China) for AI post -processing. This system 

has been certified by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA). 

Automated Segmentation of Coronary Artery Tree PCAT 

This study constructed the mo del based on the open -source framework nnU -Net (code 

repository address:  http://github.com/MIC -DKFZ/nnUNet ). The default configuration 

of nnU -Net v2 was adopted, and the training process was executed using the 3D 

full -resolution U -Net architecture (network  structure detailed in  Fig . 1). Model 

training and internal validation data were derived from the ImageCAS database, 

which contains 86 sets of CCTA images and their manually annotated segmentation 

labels. Hyperparameter optimization and performance evaluat ion were simultaneously 

performed via a five -fold cross -validation strategy, with evaluation metrics including 

the Dice similarity coefficient (DSC), Intersection over Union (IoU), Precision, and 

Recall. To verify the model’s generalization ability, an add itional 26 cases from 

Center 1  (distinct from our study cohort)  were introduced as an external  validation set 

for final performance validation ( Supplementary Material  2).  Crucially,  none of the 

images from our study's prediction cohort (n=210) were involve d in the training, 

validation, or fine -tuning of the segmentation model.  The segmentation model was 

used solely for region -of -interest (ROI) extraction and did not participate in outcome 

prediction. ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS Figure  1. Schematic diagram of the overall network  architecture of 3D full -resolution U -Net .

Radiomic Feature s Extraction of PCAT 

Based on the extracted  whole -coronary  tree ( Supplementary  Fig . 2a–b), axial 

expansion was performed with a range equal to the vessel diameter. A segmentation 

threshold (CT  attenuation threshold set at  –190 to  –30 HU) was applied, and voxels 

within this region were defined as the PCAT region of interest ( Fig . 2). Following the 

Image  biomarker  standardization  initiative (IBSI) guidelines, PyRadiomics (version 

v3.0.1) was u sed to extract radiomic features from the PCAT region.  To ensure robust 

and comparable feature extraction while reducing the computational burden of 

whole -coronary PCAT analysis, we performed isotropic resampling ( 3 × 3 × 3 mm, 

nearest -neighbor  interpolati on ), intensity discretization using a  fixed bin width of 5 

HU , and image normalization.  A total of 1,561 features were initially extracted, 

including shape, first -order, and texture features ( Supplementary Material  3). ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS Figure  2.  Schematic diagram of the  whole -coronary  artery tree PCAT ROI ( purple  region) .

Evaluation of CCTA Images and Measurement of PCAT  Metrics 

The images processed by the Deepwise AI system were evaluated by two board-certified radiologists, each with 15 years of experience specializing in cardiovascular disease imaging diagnosis. Patients were divided into a positive group and a negative group based on whether newly developed coronary plaques were detected on follow-up CCTA. When the assessment conclusions of the two physicians were inconsistent, a final decision was made by a senior radiologist (with 20 years of work experience). ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS To ensure consistency in anatomical landmark identification, PCAT metrics (FAI 

and FV values)  [21]  were measured by a single board -certified radiol ogist on the 

initial CCTA examination. Measurements were performed according to a predefined 

standardized anatomical protocol, covering the proximal 0 –40 mm segments of the 

LAD and LCX and the 10 –50 mm segment of the RCA.  The FAI metrics of the three 

main  coronary arteries were denoted as LAD -FAI, LCx -FAI, and RCA -FAI, and the 

FV metrics as LAD -FV, LCx -FV, and RCA -FV, respectively. The CT attenuation 

value range for measuring FAI and FV was set as  -190 to  -30 HU. 

Screening of Clinical Factors, PCAT Metrics , and Radiomic Features and Model 

Construction 

Univariate followed by multivariate analysis was used to screen clinical factors and 

PCAT metrics. A total of 1 ,561 radiomic features were sequentially screened using 

Pearson correlation ( |r|  ≥ 0.90), SelectKB est (score function: f_classif), and  least 

absolute shrinkage and selection operator ( Lasso ) (maximum iterations: 3 ,000, 

stopping iteration threshold: 0.0001).  To prevent data leakage and avoid optimistic 

bias, all feature selection and hyperparameter optimization procedures were 

conducted exclusively within the  training set, the validation set was used exclusively 

at the final stage to evaluate the performance o f the trained model .

Five logistic regression (LR) models were constructed: (1) clinical model, (2) 

PCAT model, (3) clinical -PCAT model, (4) radiomics model, and (5) 

clinical -PCAT -radiomics combined model.  Model performance was evaluated using ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS area under t he curve (AUC), sensitivity (SEN), specificity (SPE), accuracy (ACC), 

harmonic mean of precision and recall (F1 score), positive predictive value (PPV), 

and negative predictive value (NPV).  Calibration curves (CC) and decision curve 

analysis (DCA) were use d to assess model calibration and clinical utility. 

Model Interpretability 

Bar charts, hexagonal plots, force plots, waterfall plots, etc., were constructed based

model based on  Shapley values —to quantify the contributions of each predictive

Statistical  analysis was performed using SPSS 26.0 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA) 

and Python 3.6.5 (including packages such as imblearn, sklearn, xgboost, lifelines, 

and SHAP). For measurement data, normality was tested using the 

Kolmogorov -Smirnov test. Data conforming  to a normal distribution were expressed 

as mean ± standard deviation (SD), while non -normally distributed data were 

presented as median (interquartile range, IQR).  With the presence of plaques on 

follow -up CCTA as the dependent variable and clinical facto rs and PCAT metrics as 

independent variables, univariate logistic regression analysis was performed. 

Independent variables with  P < 0.05 in univariate analysis were included in a 

multivariate logistic regression model to screen for independent risk factors . For 

comparison of quantitative data between the negative and positive groups: t -test was 

used for normally distributed data, and Mann -Whitney U test for non -normally 

distributed data; chi -square test was applied for qualitative data. 

The predictive perfo rmance of the five models for plaque occurrence was evaluated 

using the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve, and the AUC was calculated. 

To determine the optimal decision threshold, the Youden Index (maximizing 

sensitivity + specificity) was appl ied to the  training set ROC curve . The selected 

cutoff was subsequently applied to the  validation set  to calculate confusion matrices 

and derive classification metrics, including SEN, SPE, ACC, PPV, and NPV.  DeLong ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS test was used to compare the AUC values o f each model. A two -tailed  P < 0.05 was

Results 

Baseline Characteristics of Clinical and PCAT Data 

Among the 210 patients, 77 (36.7%) developed newly formed coronary plaques 

during follow -up, while 133 remained plaque -free. The age ranged from 30 to 85 

years, mean age was 53.83 ± 9.26 years, and 86 patients (41.0%) were male. The 

median follow -up interv al was 23.72 months (range, 8.52 –51.36 months).  The 210 

patients were divided into a training set (n  = 147) and a validation set (n  = 63) at a 7:3 

ratio. Among them, the positive cases in the training set and validation set were 54 

and 23, respectively, an d the negative cases were 93 and 40, respectively.  There were 

no significant differences in clinical characteristics or PCAT metrics between the 

training and validation sets  (all  P > 0.05) ( Supplementary  Table 1 ).  Supplementary 

table 2  shows the demographi c data of the 210 patients at the first CCTA examination 

and the results of univariate and multivariate logistic regression analyses in the 

training set. Sequential univariate and multivariate logistic regression analyses 

revealed that age, LAD -FAI, LAD -FV , and RCA -FAI were independent risk factors 

for plaque occurrence (all  P < 0.05) ( Supplementary  Table 2 ).

Demonstration of Model Interpretability 

To enhance model interpretability, SHAP analysis quantified the contribution of each 

feature  to the combined model's predictions ( Fig. 8a -b). The results indicated that 

LAD -FAI, RCA -FAI, wavelet -LHH_firstorder_Median, and age were key predictors

reflecting substantial in ter -individual variability and potential for higher specificity,

showed slightly lower influence but narrower SHAP value distributions, indicating 

greater stability acro ss individuals.

Discussion 

In this study, we developed an nnU-Net-based automated pipeline for whole-coronary PCAT extraction and multimodal predictive models for plaque development during follow-up in individuals with normal baseline coronary arteries. The main findings are twofold. First, whole-coronary PCAT radiomics alone outperformed conventional PCAT metrics and clinical factors. Second, the combined model achieved the highest discriminative performance (AUC: 0.931 in training; 0.896 in validation). However, the lack of a statistically significant improvement over the radiomics-only model (Delong test, P=0.761) suggests that the incremental clinical value of adding clinical and PCAT metrics may be limited. 

Relationship Be tween Coronary Plaque Formation, PCAT, and Inflammation ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS During plaque formation in the coronary artery, the upregulation of mediators triggers 

the formation of a pro -inflammatory microenvironment, thereby promoting leukocyte 

loc alization, chemotaxis, and activation  [24] . Meanwhile, inflammatory factors within 

the vessel wall diffuse outward, causing PCAT to be exposed to this inflammatory 

microenvironment and leading to PCAT dysfunction. When PCAT is dysfunctional, it 

prompts PCA T to secrete inflammatory mediators (e.g., interleukin -6, interleukin -8, 

and monocyte chemoattractant protein -1) via paracrine and vasocrine pathways; these 

mediators directly diffuse to the coronary artery wall, resulting in smooth muscle cell 

proliferati on and leukocyte migration  [25, 26] . Therefore, atherosclerosis is essentially 

a chronic disease characterized by lipid -driven inflammation, and meanwhile, 

inflammation in both the vessel wall and PCAT is also a key factor driving coronary 

plaque formation  [21 ,2 7-29] .

PCAT is closely related to the vessel wall, with its commonly used metrics being 

FAI and FV.  FAI reflects the CT attenuation index (i.e., CT value) within PCAT, while 

FV reflects the total volume of fat in the PCAT region. When inflammation inc reases 

within PCAT, it inhibits lipid synthesis in adipocytes and reduces lipid content. The 

inflammatory environment also increases intracellular and extracellular fluid, and 

further progression of inflammation can lead to adipose tissue fibrosis  [7,30] —a shift 

from the "lipid phase" to the "water phase". These changes cause an increase in CT 

attenuation value within PCAT (i.e., elevated FAI values) and a reduction in fat 

content (i.e., decreased FV values). Therefore, PCAT can serve as an inflammatory 

"se nsor" for the vessel wall, and the FAI and FV metrics can be used to reflect the ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS inflammatory status of the vessel wall. 

Predictive Value of Clinical Factors and PCAT Metrics for Plaque  Occurrence 

During Follow -up in Individuals with Normal Coronary Arter ies 

Numerous studies have shown that male gender, age, hypertension, diabetes, smoking, 

obesity, and hyperlipidemia are all independent risk factors for the occurrence and/or 

progression of CAD  [31,32] , among which age is the strongest clinical independent 

predictor of CAD  [33] . In this study, age, as the only clinical risk factor, had a certain 

predictive value for plaque occurrence (AUC : 0.601 ,0.605, respectively). SHAP plots 

showed that its importance in predicting plaque occurrence was second only to  the 

indicators LAD -FAI, RCA -FAI, and wavelet -LHH_firstorder_Median, which is 

consistent with the view that age is the strongest independent clinical predictor among 

CAD -related factors  [33] . However, other clinical factors had no predictive value for 

plaqu e occurrence  during follow -up , which may be related to the short follow -up 

interval. 

Among the PCAT metrics, LAD -FAI, LAD -FV, and RCA -FAI are independent 

risk factors for plaque occurrence.  The combination of these three metrics showed 

good diagnostic perf ormance for predicting plaque occurrence  during follow -up 

(AUC : 0.759 , 0.767, respectively), indicating that the FAI and FV metrics have 

significant importance for predicting plaque occurrence.  The mechanism may be that 

atherosclerosis is essentially a pro cess driven by lipid -induced inflammation, which in 

turn promotes plaque formation. Before plaque occurrence, inflammation already ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS exists in both the vessel wall and PCAT. The FAI and FV metrics can accurately and 

real -time reflect the inflammatory status  of PCAT, thereby enabling the prediction of 

future plaque occurrence. This is consistent with the view of Yuvaraj J et al.  [24]  that 

increased vascular inflammation promotes plaque formation and rapid progression of 

MACE. 

Although FAI and FV metrics have important predictive value for plaque 

occurrence, differences in predictive value still exist among different vessels (LAD, 

LCx, and RCA) and between different metrics (FAI vs. FV).  Xu et al.  [34]  found that 

FAI metrics dif fer among different coronary arteries and are influenced by different 

clinical factors. In a previous study of ours, we also found that FAI metrics have a 

better relative response to inflammation within PCAT compared to FV metrics  [35] .

In this study, LAD -FAI, LAD -FV, and RCA -FAI were risk factors for plaque 

occurrence, whereas LCx -FAI, LCx -FV, and RCA -FV were not, which may be 

related to differences in PCAT metrics among different vessels. Because LCx is 

adjacent to the myocardium and the PCAT region is to o small, this may lead to severe 

distortion of FAI and FV metrics, thus failing to accurately reflect the inflammatory 

status within PCAT. Additionally, compared with FAI metrics, FV has a poorer 

inflammatory response, which may also be the potential reaso n why RCA -FV was not 

an independent risk factor  [35] .

Predictive Value of the  Radiomics model  for Plaque Occurrence ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS Some traditional plaque features (e.g., plaque nature, plaque vulnerability) or PCAT 

metrics have certain predictive value for plaque  progression  [11] . However, compared 

with these traditional metrics, some radiomic features can reflect more subtle 

information within PCAT (such as internal fat morphological changes and 

heterogeneity), showing superior predictive performance for plaque oc currence and 

progression compared to traditional metric -based models  [5 ,1 9] . Zhang et al. 

predicted early plaque occurrence based on proximal radiomic features (279 in total) 

of the LAD, LCx, and RCA, with AUC values of 0.830 for both the training and 

vali dation sets  [5] , which were slightly lower than those of the  radiomics model  in 

this study (0.898 and 0.870). This may be attributed to the following two reasons: ①

The radiomic features in this study were extracted from the PCAT of the 

whole -coronary  tree , enabling a more comprehensive reflection of the radiomic 

features of the  whole -coronary  PCAT, whereas the former study only extracted 

features from the proximal segments (4 cm) of the three coronary arteries, which has 

limitations; ②This study extracted  a total of 1 ,561 radiomic features from the PCAT, 

which is more comprehensive compared to the former (279 features), further 

improving the predictive performance of the  radiomics model .

Although radiomics can significantly improve the predictive performance for 

plaque occurrence, efficient and accurate segmentation methods for the 

whole -coronary  tree and PCAT region were lacking in previous studies.  To address 

this issue, we developed an  automatic segmentation model based on nnU -Net. This 

model showed stable performance and excellent segmentation effect, enabling ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS efficient and accurate segmentation of the  whole -coronary  tree and its PCAT region. 

It greatly improved the segmentation efficie ncy of PCAT and served as a valuable 

tool for subsequent PCAT radiomic feature extraction and analysis. 

Although SHAP analysis showed that certain PCAT metrics had strong individual 

influence, radiomics features demonstrated greater stability across patien ts, suggesting 

that these two types of information play complementary roles. Their integration in a 

combined model resulted in the best overall performance. 

Evaluation of Predictive Performance of Each Model for Plaque Occurrence 

Among the three single mo dels, the  radiomics model  showed the best predictive 

performance for early plaque occurrence,  outperforming both the PCAT and clinical 

models.  This indicates that although PCAT metrics can reflect the situation within 

PCAT to a certain extent, they have limitations due to being restricted by grayscale 

features. In contrast to PCAT metrics, radiomics can reflect more subtle information 

within the  PCAT of the  whole -coronary  tree, provide more comprehensive diagnostic 

information, and further improve their predictive performance. As systemic factors, 

clinical metrics have a weaker impact on the vessel wall and a less close association 

with vascular w all inflammation compared to PCAT metrics. Additionally, modifiable 

clinical factors such as BMI, diabetes, and hyperlipidemia may change due to lifestyle 

modifications or drug treatments, thereby losing their impact on the coronary arteries. 

Although the  combination of clinical factors and PCAT metrics improved the 

predictive performance for early plaque occurrence, there was no statistically ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS significant difference in predictive performance compared with the single PCAT 

metric model.  However, the clinical -PCAT -radiomics combined model uniquely 

integrated clinical factors, traditional PCAT metrics, and radiomic features, allowing 

for a more comprehensive assessment of plaque occurrence. While the combined 

model achieved the highest AUC values (0.931 and 0.89 6, respectively), the 

incremental value over  radiomics model  was limited, warranting further validation 

before clinical implementation. 

Beyond assessing diagnostic performance, establishing an appropriate operational 

threshold is pivotal for translating mo del predictions into actionable clinical strategies. 

While a default threshold of 50% is conventionally utilized, it warrants dynamic 

adjustment based on the risk -benefit ratio of interventions within a specific clinical 

context. For instance, if clinical  consensus deems that the net benefit of preventive 

treatment outweighs its costs and potential harms only when the risk exceeds 30%, 

this value serves as the operational threshold. Applying this criterion, Patient A is 

accurately stratified into the high -priority intervention group, whereas Patient B is 

spared from overtreatment. This individualized, risk -stratified approach 

exemplifies —and reinforces —the clinical utility established by DCA. 

Limitations 

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the retrospective design with variable follow-up intervals introduces inherent biases. Although we defined the outcome as plaque presence at the last follow-up, this approach does not fully account ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS for temporal heterogeneity and right-censoring. While the LR model effectively stratifies baseline risk, it may underestimate the true hazard associated with time-varying factors. Future prospective studies employing time-to-event analyses (e.g., Cox regression) are warranted to capture these temporal dynamics. Second, this study was essentially a single-center investigation lacking independent external validation, and the generalizability of the model remains to be validated. Third, variations in tube voltage [36], imaging protocols, and AI-assisted platforms may affect PCAT metrics. Finally, although LR was chosen for its interpretability, more advanced machine learning models may further improve performance and should be explored in future multicenter prospective studies. 

Conclusion 

An nnU-Net –based automated whole-coronary PCAT radiomics approach, combined with PCAT metrics and clinical factors, enables effective prediction of plaque development during follow-up in individuals with normal coronary arteries. The proposed multimodal model shows potential as a tool for early risk stratification and may support personalized preventive strategies in clinical practice.
