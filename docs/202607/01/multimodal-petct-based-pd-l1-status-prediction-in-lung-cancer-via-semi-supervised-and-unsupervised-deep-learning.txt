ABSTRACT 

Deep learning (DL) techniques have been applied in lung cancer screening, assessing drug effectiveness, and enhancing prognosis prediction. Within this context, the combination of 18FDG PET/CT images with DL has demonstrated promising results, particularly in predicting programmed death ligand-1 (PD-L1) expression in lung cancer, improving overall prediction accuracy and offering a viable non-invasive complementary imaging biomarker to support clinical decision-making and patient stratification. An effective way to improve the performance of deep neural networks in most tasks is to increase the quantity of labeled data and the quality of labels. However, in medical imaging, high-quality annotations and large datasets are both challenging to obtain due to the need for expert knowledge and tedious procedures including regulatory obstacles. In this context, we propose a semi-supervised and unsupervised deep neural networks (USSLNet) using early fusion multi-modal PET/CT images within the context of predicting PD-L1 expression. By alternately running two tasks, label information is propagated to the unlabeled data, enabling the model to extract semantic information and mitigating the risk of overfitting to limited labeled data. Model performance was evaluated using the area under the receiver operating characteristic curves (AUCs) and 95% confidence intervals (CIs). Compared with current methods, our framework demonstrates improved robustness, reducing the impact of outliers and yielding superior performance in PD-L1 status classification. Moreover, the framework consistently outperformed current approaches when utilizing various types of unlabeled PET/CT images. These findings highlight the effectiveness of our approach in predicting PD-L1 expression through the use of limited in size and partially annotated multi-modal PET/CT datasets. 

Introduction 

Despite significant therapeutic advances in recent years, lung cancer continues to be one of the leading causes of cancer related mortality worldwide. Among therapeutic options, immunotherapy has gained recognition for its ability to control localized and locally advanced tumors by activating the immune system. Immune checkpoints, especially related to lymphocyte activity, are expressed on the surface of cancer cells and play a crucial role in the regulation of the immune response. Immunotherapy works by blocking these inhibitory checkpoints, enabling a more effective anti-tumor immune response. One of the key immune checkpoint inhibitors is programmed death ligand-1 (PD-L1), and its evaluation, often performed by immunohistochemistry (IHC), has become a critical factor in the treatment of non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) therapy using immunotherapy. A correlation between PD-L1 and the efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) in NSCLC has been well established 1, 2 .In the first-line treatment setting, pembrolizumab monotherapy has shown improved progression-free survival (PFS) and overall survival (OS) benefits compared to chemotherapy in NSCLC patients with PD-L1 expression in ≥ 50% of tumor cells 1, 2 . While a recent study reported significantly longer PFS and OS in the pembrolizumab-treated group compared to the chemotherapy-treated group in the overall NSCLC cohort with PD − L1 ≥ 1% , a clear benefit in PFS and OS was only 

## ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS evident in the subgroup of patients with PD − L1 ≥ 50% , with higher PD-L1 expression correlating with greater benefit 3–5 .Consequently, the FDA has approved an IHC assay for PD-L1 expression, using a cut-off of 50% tumor proportion score (TPS) for first-line pembrolizumab treatment. PD-L1 through IHC is usually determined by surgical or biopsy tumor samples, both of which carry risks of associated morbidities 6. In addition, standard IHC for determining PD-L1 expression is labor-intensive and requires a high level of expertise from pathologists. The precision and reproducibility of the PD-L1 assessment through IHC are further complicated by the presence of tumor heterogeneity. In addition, studies have found evidence of differences in PD-L1 expression during treatment 3–5 . Finally, intratumoral heterogeneity in PD-L1 expression, coupled with variations in immunohistochemistry analysis using different antibodies, platforms, and scoring criteria, complicates the interpretation of results 7–9 .In this context, there is a clear need for a non-invasive, whole-tumor-based biomarker. The development of a non-invasive strategy to assess PD-L1 status could enhance clinical decision support, particularly in situations where tissue access is restricted or when IHC proves insufficient 10, 11 . Integrating multiple imaging modalities, such as Positron Emission Tomography (PET) and Computed Tomography (CT), may provide an alternative method to assess PD-L1 expression status, according to the emerging correlation between imaging findings and lymphocytic infiltration. Importantly, there is no direct molecular mapping behind the association between imaging biomarkers like 18F-FDG PET and PD-L1 expression. Instead, FDG uptake represents glucose metabolism and glycolytic activity, while PD-L1 is an immune checkpoint protein controlled by tumor and immune cell interactions. The tumor immune–metabolic microenvironment, which combines metabolic reprogramming, inflammatory signaling, and immune cell invasion, is responsible for their indirect and probabilistic relationship. These partially overlapping biological processes can produce correlated imaging phenotypes that can be captured by high-dimensional learning models without requiring an explicit mechanistic linkage. Traditionally, the use of medical imaging for tumor assessment is based on basic metrics, such as size (diameter) or volume, and intensity or activity concentration in the case of CT and PET/CT images respectively. Radiomics strategies 12 have allowed the description of more complex patterns with image features in the form of image intensity histograms, texture matrices, and morphological descriptors. The potential benefits of radiomics have been well explored in lung cancer 13–16 , where machine learning algorithms for patient classification have already proven very effective against the large number of parameters of a predefined cohort of patients. Recent studies have shown promising results in the use of radiomics and machine learning procedures to predict PD-L1 expression from PET/CT images 17–19 . However, several challenges remain on the analysis of radiomic features since feature extraction and computation are very labor-intensive and time-consuming. Moreover, changes in imaging protocols and scanner/patient positioning affect the reproducibility of radiomic features 20, 21 . Therefore, more robust algorithmically automatic methods for the extraction and analysis of radiomic features are urgently needed for practical implementation in clinical routine. With the advancements of deep learning (DL) technology, deep neural networks (DNNs) have been widely used in the field of medical image processing. In the treatment of lung cancer patients, DNNs found applications from screening to evaluating drug efficiency, thus improving the precision of prognostic predictions 22–24 . More recently, DNNs have been increasingly integrated into radiomic frameworks. This integration is achieved through the automation of lesion delineation tasks with deep convolutional encoder-decoder architectures 25–28 through which deep features are extracted from intermediate hidden layers. In the context of NSCLC, several studies have revealed that deep features are effective in evaluating patient survival or detecting recurrence 27–30 . Also in NSCLC, some studies have investigated the potential of deep learning models in predicting PD-L1 expression with CT images. Recently, studies have used multimodality data, demonstrating improved performance through fusion of PET and CT images within a DL based framework for the prediction of PDL-1 expression 26, 31 . Despite good performance with an AUC up to 0,85 most of these studies are suffering from relatively small patient cohorts. This is often due to a lack of large patient cohorts with exhaustive genetic profile characterization and systematic data labeling outside the context of prospective clinical trials. In addition, with retrospective studies, one is often confronted with large parts of unlabeled data. Within this context, semi-supervised learning, which utilizes both labeled and unlabeled data, provides distinct advantages. In this study, we propose a unique semi-supervised learning framework that integrates unsupervised learning techniques (USSLNet). The USSLNet framework is designed to enhance the model’s capacity to extract meaningful insights from unlabeled data while mitigating the risks of overfitting and effectively reducing the influence of outliers. The potential impact of this new approach was evaluated within the context of DL based radiogenomics exploiting the use of multimodality (PET/CT) for predicting PD-L1 expression in NSCLC. This study utilized two large single center cohorts to validate the performance and generalizability of the proposed model. The first consisted of a 384 NSCLC patients (189 labeled and 195 unlabeled cases) and a second dataset of 464 patients (289 labeled and 175 unlabeled cases).

Training and validation cohorts 

The training dataset consists of 384 patients with [18F]FDG PET/CT scans acquired between 2012 and 2019 in the Nuclear Medicine department of the University Hospital of Poitiers. All patients were subjected to biopsy for histological confirmation of NSCLC. At the same time, the status of PD-L1 was assessed. Two experienced lung pathologists examined all hematoxylin and eosin to determine histological classification according to the World Health Organization Classification in lung tumors 32 .The pathological stage was established based on the eighth edition of the TNM classification 33 . For the PD-L1 IHC assay, we utilized the QR-1 clone (Diagomic) exclusively for all patient samples to ensure consistency in data characterization. The slides were stained on a Ventana BenchMark ULTRA automated staining platform. The interpretation was based on a tumor proportion score (TPS). TPS was defined as the number of membranous positive cells regardless of intensity, divided by the total number of viable cells multiplied by 100 to express the score in %. [18F] FDG PET/CT scans were performed 60+/-5 minutes after intravenous administration of 2.25 to 3 MBq/kg of [18F]FDG. Images were obtained using a Biograph mCT40 ToF scanner (Siemens Healthcare, Erlangen, Germany)), using a standard routine protocol based on EANM guidelines (skull base to mid thighs, fasting for at least 6 hours, glucose level <2 g/L). A low-dose CT was acquired for attenuation correction and anatomical correlation of PET abnormalities (tube current 120 kV, CARE Dose 4D current modulation system, reconstruction with 5 mm slice thickness). PET acquisitions were performed using 3.5 minutes per bed position, and the images were reconstructed using the OSEM-TrueX-TOF algorithm, 3 iterations and 21 subsets, with a voxel size of 4.073 x 4.073 x 2.072 mm3. The largest lung lesion was semi-automatically segmented using the fuzzy locally adaptive bayesian (FLAB) algorithm 34, 35 . Tumor segmentation in CT images was performed by applying the PET volume of interest (VOI) in CT images using the 3D Slicer 36 .In addition to the images, the dataset includes the patients’ sex, stage, age, and PD-L1 expression levels, among other clinical characteristics. In the context of the current study looking at PD-L1 expression, of the 384 patients 189 were labeled and 195 unlabeled. Amongst the labeled cases, 105 (56%) were PD-L1 positive, while the remaining 84 cases (44%) were categorized as PD-L1 negative. The external validation dataset consists of 464 NSCLC patients with T1 (<3 cm) and T2 (3–5 cm) tumors clinically or pathologically diagnosed and treated with stereotactic beam radiotherapy (SBRT) between 2010 and 2020 in the University Hospital of Liege. Age, date of diagnosis, gender, WHO performance status (PS), clinical T stage, tumor size measured on CT, smoking history, histology and mutational status were available. In addition, the status of PD-L1 was evaluated. To maintain consistency in data characterization for the PD-L1 IHC assay, we only used the QR-1 clone for all patient samples. [18F] FDG PET/CT studies were acquired using a Philips Gemini TF (Philips Healthcare). The [18F] FDG PET exams were imported into a MIM workstation (MIM Software, Cleveland, OH, USA). A gradient-based method (PET-Edge.) 37 was used by a skilled radiation oncologist to semi-automatically segment the 3D primary tumor volume using the PET images. Each CT was independently segmented using a previously validated semi-automatic approach that made use of 3D Slicer and the GrowCut algorithm 38 . In the context of this study, of the 464 patients, 289 were labeled and 175 were not based on their PD-L1 expression. 164 (57%) of the cases labeled had positive PD-L1 expression, while the remaining 125 cases (43%) were classified as having a negative PD-L1 expression. The current study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and received approval from the Institutional Review Board (IRB)/ Ethics Committee of the University Hospital of Poitiers.

## ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS Semi-Supervised Classification Combined with Unsupervised Learning 

In this context, we introduce USSLNet, which uses the same network architecture in both unsupervised and semi-supervised phases for feature extraction, as illustrated in Figure 2. USSLNet cycles between semi-supervised classification and unsupervised clustering. During the semi-supervised learning phase, the use of label information facilitates unsupervised clustering, simplifying the task. Conversely, in the unsupervised phase, the model disregards label information, enhancing its generalization capabilities and mitigating the risk of overfitting to the limited labeled data. Moreover, the overclustering approach decreases the effect of low-confidence targets or outliers on network parameters. Following experimentation with different hyperparameters, and considering the final experimental results, the model is initially trained four times in a semi-supervised training, followed by one iteration of unsupervised training. 

Figure 2. Overview architecture of our our proposed deep USSLNet framework, illustrating the alternating semi-supervised and unsupervised training cycles. 

Implementation details and evaluation metrics 

In this work, the 3D ResNet model and multimodal early fusion using PET and CT modalities that showed the best performance in our previous study 31 , within the context of radiogenomics to determine PDL-1 expression from multimodality imaging, was used as the baseline network for all experiments using the same parameter settings as FixMatch 40 in the semi-supervised phase. In the unsupervised clustering phase, we used the SGD (stochastic gradient descent) optimizer with a batch size of 64. The learning rate and weight decay were set to 0.01 and 0.0002, respectively. All methods trained for 200 epochs apart from the proposed methodology that trained for 80 epochs in the semi-supervised phase and 20 epochs in the unsupervised phase, with a batch size of 1. The Poitiers cohort (n = 384) was used exclusively for model development where we carried-out a 5-fold cross-validation. In each fold, we used 232 out of 384 samples for training, 76 for validation, and 76 for testing, resulting in a split of 70% and 30%, separately. Within each training subset only, we further constructed the semi-supervised learning setting. Specifically, a variable proportion of the training data (ranging from 20% to 100%) was treated as labeled data, while the remaining samples were considered unlabeled. The labeled/unlabeled partitioning was performed independently within each fold, ensuring that no information from validation or test sets was used during training, pseudo-labeling, or clustering. To address class imbalance in the dataset , the Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique (SMOTE) 42 was applied on the labeled training set during model training. This strategy was used to reduce potential bias toward the majority class and to improve model generalization across PD-L1 positive and negative categories. For the definition of the prediction target, PD-L1 expression was binarized using the tumor proportion score (TPS). Specifically, patients were classified as PD-L1-positive when TPS ≥ 1% and PD-L1-negative when TPS < 1% , in accordance with clinically used thresholds in NSCLC immunotherapy stratification. This definition was applied consistently across both training and external validation cohorts. Different metrics were utilized to assess the performance of the classifiers: Area Under the Curve (AUC) metric and Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve analysis, accuracy, sensitivity and specificity. The DeLong strategy was used to 

/

## ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS calculate the 95% confidence intervals (CI). The median and interquartile range (IQR) with 95% CI was utilized to address continuous variables. All statistical tests were two-tailed, with statistical significance set at p < 0.05. AUC gives an exhaustive measure of the model’s capacity to discriminate PD-L1-positive and PD-L1-negative examples. The AUC values range from 0 to 1, with higher values demonstrating a superior power. On the other hand, the ROC curve outwardly addresses the trade-off between sensitivity (true positive rate) and specificity (1 - false positive rate) across various classification thresholds. We benchmark our approach against existing semi-supervised techniques, such as UDA 39 and the original FixMatch 40 . In addition, we perform an ablation analysis by varying the ratio of labeled data utilization, ranging from 20% to 100%, and unlabeled data utilization from 50% to 100%. Furthermore, a formal statistical comparisons were conducted between the proposed USSLNet model and baseline semi-supervised methods (UDA 39 and FixMatch 40 ). Specifically, paired statistical testing was performed across cross-validation folds to assess whether observed differences in AUC were statistically significant. All tests were two-tailed, with statistical significance set at p < 0.05. To further assess the generalizability of the model, we validated the trained model on an external PET/CT dataset from Liège, comprising 464 patients (289 labeled and 175 unlabelled). The contribution of each component of the proposed framework is inherently captured through the reported comparisons. In particular, Tables 1–4 include a fully supervised baseline (3D ResNet), semi-supervised learning methods (UDA and FixMatch), and the full proposed USSLNet framework under identical experimental settings. 

Results 

Table 1 compares our proposed method (USSLNet) with two semi-supervised learning methods, UDA and FixMatch, as well as with the supervised baseline approach using ResNet in the testing datasets. Evaluation metrics such as AUC, Accuracy, Specificity, and Sensitivity were used to capture the performance of the model for various ratios of labelled data. For lower ratios of labeled data (20% and 40%), the baseline supervised approach shows moderate performance, achieving an AUC of 0.63 and an accuracy level of 0.64. With the percentage of labeled data increasing to 100%, the performance of the baseline method improves significantly, reaching an AUC of 0.83 and an accuracy of 0.84. This trend indicates as expected that as the proportion of labeled data increases, the performance of the model improves. In contrast, the UDA method consistently outperforms the supervised baseline approach performance for all labeled data ratios. As expected this improved performance is more important when one is using a smaller fraction of labelled to unlabelled data ratio. Indeed, even with 20% labeled data: UDA reaches an AUC of 0.76 and a precision of 0.77, largely outperforming the supervised baseline method. When one considers the use of all labeled data, the performance of UDA reaches an AUC of 0.87 and accuracy of 0.87. UDA’s ability to effectively utilize labeled and unlabeled data allows to enhance model training and generalization capabilities. The capabilities of semi-supervised methods in improving predictive performance are further demonstrated by the FixMatch and USSLNet methods’ performance. FixMatch achieves an AUC of 0.77 with 20% labeled data and a significant increase to 0.88 with 100% labeled data, demonstrating higher performance even at higher labeled data ratios. Meanwhile, USSLNet achieved the best performance amongst all semi-supervised algorithms tested with an AUC and an accuracy of 0.90 and 0.91, respectively, when using all labeled data. The USSLNet’s ability to use unlabeled data to improve model learning and prediction accuracy is demonstrated by its robust performance even at all labeled ratios, as shown in Table 1 and Figure 3. Table 2 presents a comparative analysis of our proposed USSLNet method with two semi-supervised learning meth-ods—UDA and FixMatch approach in various proportions of unlabeled data in testing datasets. Metrics such as AUC, accuracy, specificity, and sensitivity, each with standard deviations indicating the variation between experiments, were used to evaluate the effectiveness of each method as the proportion of unlabelled data increases. The findings show that UDA clearly leads in an increase in performance metrics. In particular, UDA has an accuracy of 0.87 ± 0.07 and an AUC of 0.87 ± 0.03 at 100% unlabeled data, both of which indicates consistent improvement as more labeled data are made available. This in turn shows that UDA uses unlabeled information effectively to improve performance metrics in all scenarios, exhibiting robustness in separating between classes (sensitivity and specificity). FixMatch also shows comparative performance to UDA. FixMatch has an accuracy of 0.87 ± 0.08 and an AUC of 0.88 ± 0.03 at 100% unlabeled data. Discussion 

One of the major bottlenecks in any machine learning algorithm and its practical application in each field is the availability of well annotated training datasets but also its subsequent adaptability to heterogeneous and quite frequently incomplete databases. This challenge is particularly present in the medical domain, where annotation suffers from both inter- and intra-observer variability, and clinical protocols differ substantially between institutions and patient populations 43 . In the medical field, one is quite frequently confronted with both issues, given both the inter- and intra-observer variability in data annotation in addition to the large variability of both diagnostic and therapeutic protocols employed for a given disease across the healthcare system. Within this context, semi-supervised learning offers a viable alternative to the time-consuming and costly process of labelling images. Furthermore, utilizing unlabeled medical information for unsupervised learning becomes feasible with the abundance of daily generated medical information. Several studies have examined the relationship between 18F-FDG PET/CT-derived metabolic parameters and PD-L1 expression in NSCLC, with differing degrees of correlation reported. Overall, multiple cohorts has been shown to be statistically significant but moderate correlation between tumor metabolic activity and PD-L1 status. Specifically, higher standardized uptake values (SUVmax) and increased total lesion glycolysis have been linked with PD-L1 positive in several NSCLC populations 44–46 . Additionally, SUVmax has been found as an independent predictor of PD-L1 expression in multivariate analyses in selected studies 47 . Increased metabolic tumor burden has also been associated with higher PD-L1 expression and worse clinical outcomes 45 .However, the current studies remain heterogeneous and still inconsistent. Several studies have reported weak or non-significant correlations between FDG uptake and PD-L1 expression, especially when analyses are stratified by histological subtypes such as adenocarcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma 48, 49 . Existing evidence suggests a context-dependent and non-uniform association between FDG metabolic parameters and PD-L1 expression, with variability across cohorts, histologies, and imaging protocols. Furthermore, reported associations differ based on methodological factors, including the choice of PD-L1 immunohistochemistry cut-off thresholds, antibody clones, imaging feature definitions, and radiomic feature selection strategies 50–52 . Additionally, some cohorts show a strong association between SUV metrics and PD-L1 expression only in particular molecular or clinical categories, while others fail to observe any statistically meaningful relationship 48 .In this work, we have explored the potential of unsupervised learning in the context of tumor genetic expression charac-terization through the analysis of medical multimodality images. More specifically, we are interested in predicting PD-L1 expression status without the need for interventional tumor biopsies and subsequent histopathological characterisation. These procedures are both invasive and costly to any healthcare system and, as such, an approach that allows one to obtain such information through standard medical imaging procedures is of clear medico-economical interest. As with any other medical application the availability of well annotated and complete databases for training such deep learning models is essential 53, 54 .

As such, we propose exploring available imaging datasets with partially available PD-L1 expression status in combination with a new unsupervised learning model developed in this work. We formulated a semi-supervised classification model that coordinates unsupervised learning (USSLNet) in combination with medical image clustering and performed a comparative analysis with semi-supervised learning techniques utilizing variable proportions of labeled and unlabeled data. Our proposed method improved each performance metric, demonstrating its superior classification capabilities. This ability in distinguishing between positive and negative samples can be attributed to the synergistic interaction of semi-supervised and unsupervised classification methods. Even with a few labeled samples (20%), our proposed approach efficiently makes use of the available data to improve performance by combining the strengths of both approaches, clearly demonstrating its potential to outperform classical semi-supervised learning models, irrespective of the various data conditions. In addition, by employing cross-comparison and auxiliary overclustering during unsupervised learning, we increase the efficiency of unsupervised clustering, leading to an overall improved model performance. More specifically, these methodologies help the model to frame more exact and significant clusters, which in turn works on the nature of the semi-supervised learning process. Within this framework, the objective of our model is not to impose a predefined mechanistic mapping between FDG uptake and PD-L1 expression, but rather to learn latent representations that may encode complex, non-linear correlations emerging from tumor biology. The proposed deep learning approach therefore treats imaging data as a high-dimensional proxy of underlying tumor state, allowing the model to identify predictive patterns without requiring explicit biological causality assumptions. Performance evaluation in multiple labeled data ratios (Table 1 and Table 3) shows that USSLNet consistently outperforms baseline and existing semi-supervised methods. For example, with only 20% labeled data, USSLNet achieves an AUC of 0.79 and an accuracy of 0.81 surpassing UDA (AUC: 0.76, Accuracy: 0.77) 53 and FixMatch (AUC: 0.77) 54 . When using the full set of labeled data, USSLNet maintains its performance with an AUC of 0.90 and accuracy of 0.91, compared to 0.87/0.87 for UDA and 0.88/0.87 for FixMatch. Furthermore, Table 2 and Table 4 illustrate the robustness of the method in varying proportions of unlabeled data. Even with 100% unlabeled data, USSLNet achieves the highest AUC and accuracy compared to other methods. It also maintains high specificity and sensitivity, underscoring its ability to distinguish positive and negative cases in the absence of full supervision. Although UDA and FixMatch show consistent improvements in data splits 53, 54 , USSLNet exceeds both metrics and conditions, confirming its synergistic design and robustness. Current methods for semi-supervised learning have a few limitations, such as their vulnerability to overfitting in the absence of sufficient labelled data and their inability to adequately address class imbalance. These limitations highlight the need for more robust and adaptive methods that can handle the complexities of medical imaging data effectively. As the proportion of labelled data decreases, the performance of our technique becomes more pronounced, showing its ability to avoid overfitting by intermittently ignoring labelled data during neural network training. This in turn allows the model to generalize better to unseen information, improving its robustness. Our work shows how unsupervised clustering can be consistently integrated with semi-supervised learning to improve overall performance. The proposed method helps the semi-supervised model better classify by grouping similar categories into a single cluster and extracting meaningful semantic information from a large pool of unlabelled data. This integration improves the accuracy of the model as well as reduces its dependence on labelled information, making it a practical solution. In general, our method works in conjunction with conventional semi-supervised learning to reduce the model’s reliance on manually labelled data. This decrease in dependency on labelled information can fundamentally reduce the cost and time burden associated with annotation, making the practical use of machine learning models more accessible and reliable. Despite these promising results, there are some limitations with our method that need to be addressed in future studies. The high initialization requirements of clustering algorithms, which currently necessitate the use of a pre-trained network like ImageNet, are a notable limitation. Our approach is heavily based on appropriate initialization conditions, as evidenced by the suboptimal performance seen with random initialization. To guaranty consistent performance, future research should focus on improving the adaptability of the model to various initial conditions or developing more advanced initialization techniques 55, 56 .Also, residual confounding from acquisition protocols / scanner heterogeneity and population-specific effects cannot be fully excluded and may partially contribute to predictive performance. From a clinical perspective, it is crucial to emphasize that the proposed framework is meant to serve as a companion to histological evaluation rather than as a replacement for histopathological evaluation. Patients who cannot undergo biopsy because of medical contraindications or architecturally inaccessible tumor areas, as well as situations where tissue samples are insufficient or non-representative because of intratumoral heterogeneity, are the most suitable use cases. Furthermore, since PET/CT imaging is already often obtained for NSCLC staging, the suggested approach may offer additional non-invasive phenotypic information without requiring additional procedures. It may also be used as a pre-screening or stratification technique to assist in prioritizing molecular tests or guiding biopsy sample strategies. Crucially, the results of the model should always be viewed as supporting data rather than as a stand-alone indicator of immunotherapy eligibility. Although the model

Conclusion 

In conclusion, USSLNet, our semi-supervised classification model that incorporates unsupervised clustering signals, represents a significant step forward in bridging semi-supervised and unsupervised learning approaches. Its superior capability in classification tasks, particularly when labeled data is scarce, is demonstrated by the substantial improvements across various performance metrics. Beyond methodological contributions, this work highlights the potential of leveraging both labeled and unlabeled multimodal PET/CT data for non-invasive tumor characterization, while explicitly positioning the approach as a complementary decision-support tool rather than a replacement for histopathological assessment. In the future, we will further enhance and broaden its applicability by addressing the limitations that have been identified by concentrating on potential improvements. The application of semi-supervised learning in a variety of fields, including the classification of medical images and other domains where there is a lack of labeled data, will be strengthened by this ongoing developments.
