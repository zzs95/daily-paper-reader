## Introduction

Computed tomography (CT) is one of the most widely used diagnostic tools for tumor evaluation in clinical practice. It provides clear delineation of tumor morphology, size, location, and spatial relationships with surrounding tissues, thereby offering essential information for staging and treatment planning [1]. However, CT alone often fails to distinguish benign from malignant lesions in the early stages, because metabolically active tumors may arise without substantial anatomical alterations. In contrast, positron emission tomography (PET) characterizes tissue metabolic activity using radiotracers such as 18F-FDG, enabling early detection, lesion characterization, and treatment response assessment [2]. Nevertheless, PET inherently suffers from low spatial resolution, limiting the precise localization of small tumors. Integrating PET and CT has thus become an effective strategy for leveraging their complementary strengths in tumor segmentation [3].

Early computational approaches to PET/CT tumor segmentation mainly relied on handcrafted feature extraction combined with classical machine learning models. For example, Wu et al. leveraged PET/CT-derived intensity statistics, texture measures, and spatial/contextual cues to automatically detect and delineate nasopharyngeal carcinoma, where a support vector machine (SVM) classifier was used to discriminate tumor from normal high-uptake structures [4]. Another example is the random forest (RF)-based strategy proposed to learn discriminative voxel- or region-level descriptors from both modalities for lung tumor co-segmentation on clinical three-dimensional PET/CT scans [5]. Beyond supervised classifiers, clustering-driven formulations such as fuzzy C-means (FCM) combined with probabilistic classification terms (e.g., Bayesian information) were employed to alleviate inter-modality intensity heterogeneity and address fuzzy boundaries in PET/CT tumor co-segmentation [6]. Despite promising results in specific cohorts, these feature-engineered pipelines often exhibited limited robustness to scanner and acquisition protocol variability, physiological uptake patterns, and tumor heterogeneity, which ultimately motivated the shift toward representation-learning-based deep models.

With the advent of deep learning, convolutional neural networks (CNN) and Transformer-based architectures have demonstrated outstanding performance in medical image analysis, overcoming many limitations of traditional machine learning approaches. CNN-based segmentation frameworks such as U-Net [7] employ an encoder–decoder topology with skip connections to fuse fine-grained spatial detail from shallow layers with semantically rich representations from deeper layers, enabling accurate boundary recovery while maintaining strong multi-scale context modeling for PET/CT tumor delineation. Building on this paradigm, nnU-Net [8] further improves practical performance by providing a self-configuring training pipeline that automatically adapts preprocessing, network configuration, optimization schedules, and post-processing to a given dataset, making it a widely adopted and highly competitive baseline for volumetric medical segmentation. However, CNNs have a restricted receptive field, which limits their ability to capture long-range dependencies in PET/CT images. To better model global dependencies, recent studies have increasingly explored Transformer-based segmentation frameworks that leverage self-attention for long-range contextual reasoning. TransUNet [9] represents a hybrid CNN-Transformer design in which a CNN backbone first extracts hierarchical feature maps and a Transformer encoder then models global relationships among tokenized representations before dense decoding, improving robustness in cases with fragmented lesions or weak boundaries. In a similar spirit, CSWinUNet [10] introduces a cross-shaped window self-attention mechanism that balances global context aggregation with computational efficiency, enabling stronger long-range modeling without the prohibitive memory cost of full attention on high-resolution medical images. Such Transformer-driven contextual reasoning is particularly useful in PET/CT segmentation scenarios involving low-contrast or diffuse PET regions, where long-range consistency and anatomical context can help suppress false positives and stabilize tumor delineation.

Despite these advances, PET/CT segmentation still faces several intrinsic obstacles. PET provides functional information with low spatial resolution and highly variable uptake, while CT captures detailed anatomy without metabolic cues. Their heterogeneous feature distributions often become entangled during joint modeling, weakening the complementarity of the two modalities. How to effectively integrate CT and PET has become a key challenge. Furthermore, tumor boundaries may remain ambiguous, especially in low Standardized Uptake Value (SUV) or infiltrative cases. As a result, the lack of reliable uncertainty estimation limits the clinical interpretability of model predictions.

To address these limitations, we propose an Anatomical Consistency Segmentation Network (ACSN) that explicitly disentangles anatomical structures from modality-specific textures and incorporates hierarchical uncertainty modeling to guide multimodal fusion. In this network, a dual-branch Feature Disentanglement Module (FDM) is first developed to extract shared anatomical features and PET/CT-specific textural features in a novel way upon adversarial learning, where an anatomical discriminator and weight-sharing architecture are employed to ensure structural consistency. Then, we develop a Multi-receptive field Segmentation Backbone (MSB) to enhance contextual feature extraction, a Feature Space Adjustment module (FSA) to optimize encoder–decoder feature fusion, and a Layer-wise Uncertainty Calibrator (LUC) to enable fine-grained uncertainty-aware fusion. Finally, a decision-level fusion strategy inspired by Dempster-Shafer Theory (DST) is proposed to adaptively integrates single-modality and fused predictions, providing uncertainty information that may support model reliability assessment.

The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:

*   •
We introduce a dual-branch adversarial disentanglement framework that separates shared anatomical structures from PET- and CT-specific textures, reducing redundancy and improving robustness.

*   •
We propose a Layer-wise Uncertainty Calibrator that mitigates miscalibration issues common in single-scale uncertainty modeling and enhances multimodal feature fusion.

*   •
We develop a Feature Space Adjustment module to better align anatomical and tumor-related features and employ a Multi- receptive field Segmentation Backbone to compensate for resolution reductions caused by feature disentanglement.

*   •
We validate ACSN on the AutoPET and Hecktor datasets, demonstrating competitive performance compared with representative PET/CT segmentation methods under the adopted 2D axial slice-wise evaluation protocol.
