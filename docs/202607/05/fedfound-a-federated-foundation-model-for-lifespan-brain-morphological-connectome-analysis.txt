# FedFound: A Federated Foundation Model for Lifespan Brain Morphological Connectome Analysis

Abstract 

The brain morphological connectome derived from structural MRI reflects inter-regional morphological relationships, providing a powerful representation for characterizing individual variability and detecting sub-tle abnormalities across the lifespan. However, these abnormal alterations are subtle and complex, posing significant challenges for accurate and generalizable diagnosis using machine learning. Here, we present Fed-Found, the first federated foundation model inspired by the structured educational and residency training pathway of radiologists, designed for robust and scalable analysis of lifespan brain morphological connec-tomes. Integrating heterogeneous datasets across sites and disorders (22,911 subjects aged 0 to 100 years), our FedFound combines self-supervised pre-training and supervised federated refinement, establishing a collaborative and privacy-preserving healthcare AI. Across nine diagnostic tasks spanning neurodevelop-mental, neuropsychiatric, and neurodegenerative disorders, FedFound demonstrates superior performance and interpretability, revealing both shared and disorder-specific morphological patterns across etiologies. Furthermore, FedFound serves as a reliable diagnostic aid that complements clinical expertise, supporting cross-institutional and privacy-aware applications. 

Introduction 

The human brain is a highly complex organ with large inter-subject variability and undergoes extremely complicated changes during development and aging, alongside alterations associated with various neuro-logical disorders. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) enables non-invasive investigation of brain anatomy, offering critical insights into these neurological conditions across the human lifespan [1–3]. In particular, individualized brain morphological connectome derived from MRI comprehensively delineates important re-gional relationships based on cortical features, including cortical thickness, surface area, cortical volume, mean curvature, and sulcal depth [4, 5]. With merits in robustness, reproducibility, and comprehensiveness, the brain morphological connectome has been demonstrated to reflect underlying genetics and cytoarchi-tectonics, providing discriminative information to identify early biomarkers in brain development, aging, and disorders [3, 6–14]. Although existing studies have substantially advanced morphological connectome analysis for differentiating brain disorders, an inevitable challenge remains to design robust algorithms to generalize across diverse diagnostic tasks during the human lifespan. Recently, foundation models have shown remarkable potential in medical imaging, demonstrating strong generalization and robustness across tasks such as segmentation [15, 16], classification [17–20] and multi-modal representation learning [21–23]. Notably, these foundation models trained on large-scale and diverse datasets using self-supervised learning offer promising pathways to learn generalized but discriminative feature representations, which are effective in addressing the pivotal challenges such as limited annotations and small sample size in various downstream tasks. However, foundation models devoted to the brain morphological connectome are still underexplored. Meanwhile, despite their strengths in representation ARTICLE IN PRESS

learning and transferability, the existing foundation model paradigm for neuroimage analysis (especially in disease diagnosis) often neglected the disease information and the potential in fostering interdisciplinary collaboration to excavate the complicated patterns across disease etiologies. These limitations are primarily derived from the data heterogeneity in the enrolled datasets, upon which each cohort targets a specific disorder or population with a distinct age range. This suggests further effort in effective integration of datasets of various neurological conditions and robust representation learning to understand the shared and specific phenotypes between neurological conditions across the human lifespan [11, 24]. To address these critical issues, we propose an innovative Federated Foundation model (FedFound) for scalable morphological connectome analysis, inspired by the structured training pathway of radiologists. Clinically, radiologist training involves a rigorous educational foundation in human anatomy and pathology, followed by residency training for sub-specialization, enabling accurate interpretation and diagnosis under expert guidance. Analogously, FedFound integrates a self-supervised pre-training phase to ensure generaliz-ability and transferability across diverse populations, followed by a federated learning stage that emulates multidisciplinary collaboration in clinical. This design allows FedFound to capture the inherent variability of morphological connectomes and enhance diagnostic performance across a wide spectrum of neurological conditions ( Fig. 1 ). Specifically, to emulate foundational radiology education and effectively capture the inherent trajectories of morphological connectomes across the lifespan, the proposed FedFound framework employs a masked au-toencoder (MAE) in a self-supervised pre-training stage. This involves the training on a large-scale dataset that comprises over 12,000 subjects spanning the entire human lifespan from 14 datasets. Thereafter, to achieve expert supervision during radiologists’ residency training and enable unified modeling, we then ap-ply federated learning to integrate disease-specific datasets for representation refinement. This approach facilitates multidisciplinary collaboration and enhances the understanding of complex brain abnormalities within the morphological connectome through supervised learning. In general, federated learning framework allows distributed optimization across clients under limited communications and computation capacities, but is mostly designed for clients with similar or identical tasks [25–30]. In contrast, FedFound extends cross-institutional collaboration by integrating datasets across various neurological conditions, without prejudice to the objective of federated learning to address the heterogeneity in local clients. This enables the discovery of both disorder-specific and shared morphological features, thereby improving model generalizability and rep-resentation discriminability. Importantly, the supervised federated refinement retains the key advantages of decentralized federated learning—such as privacy preservation and robustness to data heterogeneity—while facilitating the aggregation of multidisciplinary knowledge through distributed optimization. Our proposed FedFound achieves promising diagnostic performance and interpretability across nine clas-sification tasks, including the diagnosis of autism spectrum disorder (ASD), schizophrenia (SCZ), AD, and PD. Its generalizability is further validated on four heterogeneous external datasets with tasks of preterm vs. term infants, healthy control (HC) vs. focal cortical dysplasia (FCD), HC vs. ASD, and HC vs. type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM). These results underscore FedFound’s potential to support collaborative and cross-institutional healthcare applications and to serve as a reliable diagnostic aid in conjunction with clinical expertise. 

Results 

Diagnostic Performance Across Multiple Conditions. We evaluated FedFound across nine classifica-tion tasks spanning from neurodevelopmental and neuropsychiatric disorders to neurodegenerative diseases under three cortical parcellations, i.e., Desikan-Killiany parccellation, Destrieux parcellation and HCP par-cellation ( Fig. 2a , supplementary material Fig. S1 and Fig. S2 ), including AD, frontotemporal lobar degeneration (FTLD), PD, SCZ, ASD, etc. These tasks encompass heterogeneous diagnostic targets and span diverse demographic and clinical subpopulations. FedFound was instantiated at three scales (Tiny, Small, and Base) with comparison to the state-of-the-art baselines, including multi-layer perceptron (MLP), convo-lutional neural network for brain networks (BrainNetCNN)[31], graph-based networks (GIN: graph isomor-phism network, BCGCN: brain connectivity based graph convolutional network)[32, 33], vision Transformer (ViT) [34] and contrastive learning methods (MoCo: momentum contrastive learning , SimCLR: simple contrastive learning of representations)[35, 36]. the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC). As shown in Fig. 2a , FedFound consistently achieved superior performances across all tasks, with the highest average AUC (FedFound-Base: 0.751, FedFound-Small: 0.750, FedFound-Tiny: 0.745), demonstrating both robust generalizability and low variability across tasks. In diagnostically challenging scenarios such as sMCI vs. pMCI, prodromal PD (PrPD) vs. PD, HC vs. SCZ, and HC vs. attention deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), FedFound maintained high AUCs, i.e., 0.781, 0.811, 0.710, and 0.717, respectively, indicating great potentials of multidisciplinary collaboration in identifying complex and subtle phenotypic differences. 

Preservation of Age-related Characteristics. To assess the fidelity of MAE-based pre-training, we conducted age-stratified analysis using the reconstructed morphological connectomes with the model pre-trained on the large-scale dataset ( Fig. 2b ). Averaged correlation matrices over subjects, across diseased cohorts (i.e., ABIDE-1, ADHD200, SchizConnect, A4, PPMI, ADNI, and NIFD), grouped by age in years, revealed a remarkable alignment between the original and the reconstructed connectomes, underscoring that FedFound preserves key age-related structural characteristics throughout the pre-training process. 

Improvement on Predicting Disease Progression with Clinical Assessments. We evaluated the clinical significance of FedFound by combining its derived deep features with standard clinical assessments for MCI conversion prediction. Four commonly used clinical assessment scores (i.e., clinical dementia rating-sum of box, mini-mental state examination, Alzheimer’s disease assessment scale 11 tasks, and Alzheimer’s disease assessment scale 13 tasks) were included for evaluation ( Fig. 2c ). Compared to predictions using clinical assessments alone or in combination with raw morphological connectome, integrating FedFound-derived features achieved the highest average AUC (0.844). Notably, statistical analysis (paired t-test) confirmed significant improvements over both baselines (vs. assessments alone t = -2.85, p = 0.023; vs. assessments + raw connectome: t = -2.54, p = 0.032). These results underscore the capacity of FedFound to improve traditional assessment pipelines in collaboration with neurologists. 

Interpretability for Brain Disorders. To uncover the neuroanatomical abnormality underlying diagnos-tic interpretability in various brain disorders, we applied diffeomorphic counterfactual visualization [37] to our proposed FedFound based on Desikan-Killiany parcellation, revealing disorder-specific and transdiagnos-tic alterations of brain morphologic connectome across multiple cohorts ( Fig. 3). In region-level analysis focusing on regionally changing degree, transdiagnostic regions, including the left caudal middle frontal cortex, left paracentral lobule, bilateral precuneus, and right inferior frontal regions, were consistently im-plicated across all disorders. The distinctive patterns in neurodegenerative disorders (AD, PD, and FTLD) involved orbitofrontal, temporal, and supramarginal cortices, while neurodevelopmental disorders (ASD and ADHD) were characterized by abnormalities in the superior temporal sulcus, fusiform gyrus, prefrontal cor-tex, and medial orbitofrontal regions. Disease-specific analysis further revealed cohort-aligned vulnerability profiles. For example, the inferior parietal and orbitofrontal cortices were most salient in AD, while post-central and paracentral gyri were prominent in PD. However, in SCZ, regions associated with sensory-motor integration and visual processing (e.g., medial orbitofrontal regions, middle temporal sulcus, precentral and postcentral cortices, and lingual gyrus) were frequently implicated. In edge-level analysis focusing on top 0.5% positive/negative connectivity, although brain disorders shared some regions in region-based counter-factual visualization, they presented distinct abnormalities in the dominant edges. For example, reduced connectivity existed in the paracentral gyrus, supramarginal and rostral middle frontal cortex associated with AD and FTLD, while elevated connectivity associated with PD. Reduced connectivity existed in the frontal pole and temporal pole associated with ASD, while elevated connectivity associated with ADHD. Moreover, specific elevation existed in the entorhinal cortex associated with AD and the superior frontal gyrus associated with SCZ. 

Promising Initialization for External Validation. To assess generalizability, we conducted external validation of FedFound across heterogeneous populations with four classification tasks: preterm vs. term infants, HC vs. FCD, HC vs. ASD, and HC vs. T2DM ( Fig. 4a ). Conventional supervised learning models, including MLP, BrainNetCNN, BCGCN, GIN, ViT, MoCo, and SimCLR struggled with consistent generalization across tasks, exhibiting degraded performance when applied to underrepresented or highly heterogeneous cohorts. In contrast, FedFound, including its variants initiated as different scales, demon-strated consistent superiority and stable generalization across external cohorts. FedFound-Base achieved the best overall performance with AUC of 0.854 (Preterm vs. Term), 0.762 (HC vs. FCD), 0.662 (HC vs. ASD), and 0.685 (HC vs. T2DM). Notably, even at smaller scales (FedFound-Tiny and FedFound-Small), ARTICLE IN PRESS

Diagnostic Improvement with Pre-training and Federated Learning. To elucidate the effectiveness of federated learning (FL), pre-training (PT), and adversarial training (AT), we conducted a comprehensive ablation study across nine classification tasks and applied AUC for evaluation obtained from FedFound at small scale under the Desikan-Killiany parcellation, Destrieux parcellation and HCP parcellation ( Fig. 4b ,and supplementary material Figs. S3-S10 ). In Fig. 4b , the baseline model, trained without any of the three components, achieved limited generalizability with an average AUC of 0.727. Integrating FL alone yielded moderate performance gains (‘+FL’: 0.729), which was further enhanced with the incorporation of PT (‘+ FL + PT’: 0.737), highlighting the critical role of large-scale, domain-specific pre-training in learning transferable neuroanatomical representations, especially in more challenging disease categories such as prodromal Parkinson’s disease and schizophrenia. Interestingly, although the model with the combination of PT and AT achieved a comparable performance to the model with the combination of FL and PT (‘+ FL + PT’: 0.737, ‘+ PT + AT’: 0.736), incorporating FL significantly improved the AUC to 0.750, suggesting the effectiveness of adversarial regularization in contributing to intra-task robustness and federated learning in interdisciplinary collaboration, thus enhancing its resilience to inter-domain variability. 

Robustness to Model Scales, Parcellations, and Masking Ratios. To assess the robustness and generalization capacity of FedFound under varying hyper-parameters, we systematically evaluated model performance across model scales (tiny, small and base), parcellations (Desikan-Killiany, Destrieux, and HCP) and masking ratios in MAE (0.0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, and 0.9) ( Fig. 4c ). The validation in selecting the mask ratio as 0.7 in pre-training stage is presented in supplementary material Fig. S11 . Across all scales and masking ratios, FedFound maintained stable performance, particularly under the Desikan-Killiany parcellation, where FedFound-Base achieved the highest AUC (0.743) with marginal fluctuations and retained high performance even at extreme masking ratio (90%: AUC = 0.736), underscoring strong resilience to input perturbation during federated refinement. Similarly, both variants at tiny and small scales (FedFound-Tiny and FendFound-Small) based on Desikan-Killiany parcellation retained competitive AUC across all ratios, suggesting effective representation even with limited model capacity. In contrast, models using Destrieux parcellation and HCP parcellation exhibited a modest drop in performance with increased masking ratio. For instance, performance in FedFound-Base based on the Destrieux parcellation declined from 0.740 at 10% to 0.708 at 90%, while FedFound-Base based on the HCP parcellation decreased from 0.740 to 0.717. These trends reflect parcellation-dependent robustness, with FedFound-Base based on Desikan-Killiany parcellation showing greater stability under MAE framework. Collectively, these findings demonstrate that FedFound is not only accurate and interpretable, but also resilient and generalizable across cohorts, diseases and modeling configurations. 

Discussion 

In this study, we present FedFound, an innovative Federated Foundation model tailored for morphological connectome analysis across the human lifespan. By integrating large-scale masked autoencoder pre-training with supervised federated refinement, FedFound effectively captures both shared and disorder-specific brain alterations across a diverse spectrum of neurological conditions. Inspired by the radiologist training pathway, the model simulates foundational learning and specialized clinical training, enabling robust generalization across complex diagnostic tasks while preserving sensitivity to disease-specific patterns. Through extensive experiments that include comparisons with state-of-the-art baselines, external validations, ablation studies, and model interpretability analysis, FedFound demonstrates consistent and statistically significant improve-ments in brain disorder classification performance. Compared with state-of-the-art deep learning methods, including conventional MLPs, GCNs (e.g., Brain-NetCNN and BCGCN), Transformers, and contrastive representation learning (e.g., SimCLR and MoCo), FedFound consistently achieves higher and more stable performance in identifying neurological conditions. These conditions range from neurodevelopmental and neuropsychiatric disorders (e.g., ASD, ADHD, and SCZ) to neurodegenerative diseases (e.g., AD, PD, and FTLD) in federated refinement. Despite the gap between the achieved performance and the absolute performance threshold in MCI conversion prediction (“FedFound-Base”: 0.781 vs. “Assessments Alone”: 0.813), integrating FedFound-derived deep features and cognitive assessments achieved a significant performance improvement (“Assessments + Deep Features”: ARTICLE IN PRESS

0.844). These findings underscore the effectiveness of FedFound in extracting discriminative representations that preserve clinically relevant information and supports its potential use as a diagnostic aid for clinical screening. In addition, external validation on four heterogeneous tasks (i.e., Preterm vs. Term, HC vs. FCD, HC vs. ASD, and HC vs. T2DM) has further demonstrated the generalizability of FedFound in morphological connectome analysis. Furthermore, comprehensive ablation studies have underscored the effectiveness of pre-training (PT), federated learning (FL), and adversarial training (AT). The inclusion of large-scale PT facilitates the learning of generalized but discriminative representations from morphological connectome across the lifespan, while AT enhances generalization by minimizing the divergence between the input and its corresponding adversarial sample. Notably, the integration of FL enables the aggregation of multidisciplinary knowledge from various neurological conditions, and the decentralized training process within the federated refinement supports privacy preservation, offering a more feasible approach to comply with medical data regulations. Extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of FL, PT, and AT have underscored the necessity to jointly address the problem of unlabeled conditions and heterogeneity across various etiologies. Importantly, our proposed FedFound demonstrates great resilience to experimental settings across model scale (FedFound-Tiny, FendFound-Small and FendFound-Base), masked ratio (ranging from 0 to 0.9) and the pre-defined parcellation (Desikan-Killiany parcellation, Destrieux parcellation, and HCP parcellation). The counterfactual analysis identifies morphological disruptions that contribute most strongly to the model’s predictions, rather than providing a comprehensive characterization of morphological disruptions or direct evidence of underlying neurobiological mechanisms. Nevertheless, these disruptions partially overlap with previously reported shared and disorder-specific alterations across brain disorders, supporting their potential neurobiological relevance. Therefore, these findings should be interpreted primarily as model-derived features that improve diagnostic interpretability by highlighting influential edgewise and regional patterns, while also motivating future investigations into their neurobiological mechanisms. In region-level analysis, shared abnormalities in the bilateral precuneus and inferior frontal gyrus align with their known roles in cognitive control, sensorimotor integration, and self-referential processing. This suggests a common neuroethological axis that underpins the broad cognitive and affective dysfunction across neuropsychiatric and neurodegenerative disorders. The distinctive patterns in the orbitofrontal, temporal, and supramarginal cortices demonstrate known vulnerability to proteinopathies and metabolic dysfunction associated with neu-rodegenerative disorders (AD, PD, and FTLD) [38–42]. In contrast, neurodevelopmental disorders (ASD and ADHD) are characterized by abnormalities in the superior temporal sulcus, fusiform gyrus, prefrontal cortex, and medial orbitofrontal regions [43–46]. Disease-specific analysis further reveals specific abnor-malities in the inferior parietal and orbitofrontal cortices associated with AD, and in the postcentral and paracentral gyri linked to PD, indicating the respective cognitive and motor phenotypes [38, 39, 47, 48]. In edge-level analysis, there are distinct alterations in each brain disorder in the dominant edges, verifying the heterogeneity across various etiologies. Although our proposed FedFound achieves promising performance in morphological connectome analysis, several limitations should be carefully considered. First, the observed improvements remain moderate and are not yet sufficient for standalone clinical deployment, especially for the diagnosis of ASD, ADHD, SCZ and A β

positivity. This limitation likely reflects the disparity between established clinical diagnostic criteria and the inherently complementary role of neuroimaging in capturing brain alterations associated with heterogeneous etiologies. Future integration of FedFound with additional sources of information, such as multi-modal connectomes and clinical assessments, may further enhance its clinical applicability. This is particularly valuable given that FedFound-derived features have demonstrated improved performance when combined with clinical assessments for MCI conversion prediction. Second, while the federated setting simulates the residency training of radiologists to aggregate the multidisciplinary knowledge for accurate diagnosis, the federated learning in this work assumes static client participation and the continuous learning in conjunction with upcoming clients remain unresolved. Future work should explore dynamically refining the server model with the upcoming clients with expanding neurological conditions across various etiologies or heterogeneous cohorts. Third, the utilization of longitudinal neuroimages for representation enhancement is important to evaluate the effectiveness of FedFound in representation learning, which can closely align to disease progression in clinical workflows. Methods 

Datasets. Our FedFound model was developed and validated using over 20 diverse brain MRI datasets, representing cohorts from infancy through older adulthood (over 22,911 subjects aged 0–100 years), with a comprehensive and structured three-phase workflow ( Table 1 ): (1) large-scale self-supervised pre-training, (2) supervised federated refinement, and (3) external validation. During the pre-training phase, we utilized large-scale, diverse datasets (i.e., Narratives, HCP[49], HCPA[50], HCPD[51], NKI[52], QTIM[53], AOMIC (including ID1000, PIOP1 and PIOP2)[54], HBN[55], CORR[56], IXI, GSP[57]) from over 12,000 individuals encompassing a broad spectrum of neuroimaging and clinical scenarios. This phase aimed to establish a robust foundation of generalizable feature representations by capturing the inherent variability across individuals, including differences in population demographics, thus facilitating the understanding of lifespan characteristics of morphological connectome. In the fine-tuning phase, task-specific datasets (ADNI[58], NIFD, PPMI[59], A4[60], SCZ[61], ADHD200[12], ABIDE-1[62]) were used to adapt the pre-trained model to neurodegenerative diseases, neuropsychiatric disorders, and other specialized neurological conditions. This phase focused on refining models to capture nuanced biomarkers associated with specific neurological conditions. Finally, the external validation phase incorporated external datasets (dHCP[63], Epilepsy[64], ARTICLE IN PRESS

and ABIDE-2[65], and an in-house dataset [66, 67]) to rigorously evaluate its generalizability and robustness of the fine-tuned model. The publicly available studies (BCP, HCPD, HCP-YA, HCPA, HBN, QTIM, GSP, Narratives, PIOP1, PIOP2, ID1000, CORR, NKI, IXI, ADHD200, ABIDE-1, ABIDE-2, A4, ADNI, NIFD, PPMI, SchizConnect, dHCP and Epilepsy) received ethics approval from their local institutional review board and obtained informed consent (and assent where appropriate). Cortical Morphological Connectome Construction. To construct a robust and meaningful cortical morphological connectome that aligns closely with cortical cytoarchitectonics, we employed the morpho-metric inverse divergence approach [8], which offers superior sensitivity to capture subtle morphological differences between brain regions ( Fig. 1). Self-supervised Pre-training with Masked Autoencoder. As a variant of autoencoder, the masked autoencoder (MAE) incorporates a masking strategy to capture token-wise dependencies and reconstruct unseen parts from the corresponding unmasked portions, thereby achieving robust and generalized repre-sentation learning [73]. To develop a scalable learner for capturing inherent subtle variability and improve the diagnostic performance across various neurological conditions, our foundation model was first initialized using an MAE for morphological connectome analysis. Notably, each row of the connectome was treated as a token, and we randomly masked a large portion of them with a mask ratio. Therefore, recovering masked rows from unmasked rows enforces the autoencoder to learn holistic information and discriminative depen-dency across the connectome. In detail, the unmasked rows of the morphological connectome are projected into high-dimensional representations using a linear layer: F = Linear ( Munmasked ), where Munmasked is the morphological connectome after masking. After encoding and decoding the unmasked rows of the con-nectome, L2 normalization and matrix multiplication were then applied on the learned features Fout , which were output from the decoder of MAE, to reconstruct the original connectome M:

Supervised Federated Refinement. To emulate residency training of radiologists, we integrate datasets of diverse neurological conditions and leverage federated learning for distributed optimization [26]. Unlike traditional federated learning, where each local model is trained on the same task, the federated refine-ment herein extends its application to multidisciplinary collaboration by integrating datasets across diverse neurological conditions. This enhances the foundation model to extract disease-specific knowledge ranging from neurodevelopmental and neuropsychiatric disorders to neurodegenerative diseases, advancing the un-derstanding of both shared and distinct characteristics across various neurological conditions. Notably, the server in federated refinement contains only a masked autoencoder, while each client contains the masked autoencoder and an auxiliary classifier for disease-specific optimization, mitigating the issue of unlabeled conditions across different datasets and addressing the data heterogeneity across diseases. Therefore, the classifier in each client remains private and inaccessible to the server for disease-specific representation learn-ing. Counterfactual Visualization for Interpretability.
