## Introduction

Medical image segmentation is essential for detecting, diagnosing, and quantifying diseases by precisely delineating lesions or anatomical structures of interest [1], [2]. It is typically formulated as a dense prediction problem, where each pixel or voxel is classified to generate segmentation maps for target objects [3]. Over the past decade, many deep learning-based methods [4], particularly those using convolutional neural networks (CNNs), have been developed to improve segmentation accuracy. Among them, the U-shape network (U-Net) [5] has become a milestone model, demonstrating strong performance across a wide range of segmentation tasks by effectively capturing pixel-level texture and spatial relationships through stacked convolutional layers and resampling operations. However, U-Net struggles to capture semantic and contextual features for small structures and fine boundaries [6], limiting its overall segmentation performance, particularly in object boundary regions.

To address U-Net’s limitations, many deep learning networks, such as UNeXt [7], Rolling-UNet [8], and U-KAN [9], have been developed for more accurate image segmentation. Early efforts focused on the U-Net with diverse skip connection schemes [10] to better fuse deep semantic features with shallow contextual features. A notable example, U-Net++ [11], introduced nested and dense skip connections to strengthen semantic representation but often suffered from feature redundancy, excessive network parameters, and high computational cost. Later, attention-based modules like ASPP [12], CBAM [13], and EMCAD [14] were proposed to improve the learning of both local- and long-range pixel dependencies using spatial and channel attention mechanisms combined with hierarchical image pyramids. While these methods reduce segmentation uncertainty, particularly at object boundary regions, they still struggle to effectively localize focus areas, leading to noticeable errors. Additionally, multi-branch networks and multi-task learning approaches [15] have been adopted to guide segmentation based on prior knowledge or anatomical relationships. For instance, Wang et al. extracted target objects and their boundaries simultaneously using a boundary-guided convolutional neural network (BG-CNN) [16]. Despite these advances, challenges remain in further improving segmentation accuracy and network generalization.

Recently, Transformer [17], [18] has gained significant attention for medical image segmentation due to its great promise to capture long-range dependencies through multi-head self-attention and their high model capacity when trained on large-scale datasets. A major breakthrough was the development of the Segment Anything Model (SAM) [19], which demonstrated remarkable zero-shot generalization across natural image datasets. SAM consists of an image encoder that maps inputs into a high-dimensional embedding space, a prompt encoder that integrates prompts (e.g., points, bounding boxes, and masks) via positional encoding, and a mask decoder to generate segmentation outputs with confidence scores. Thanks to its powerful image encoder pretrained on large-scale data, SAM can be adapted to medical images of varying modalities, helping to bridge domain gaps between natural and medical images. This capability has inspired medical foundational models like SAM-Med2D [20] and MedSAM [21]. However, SAM’s performance degrades substantially when applied to cross-domain or cross-modality images with complex and ambiguous boundaries. Its reliance on per-instance user prompts also limits its suitability for high-throughput applications that require fully automated, large-scale segmentation. Furthermore, SAM’s large model size and high computational demands hinder its fine-tuning and retraining, particularly in resource-constrained settings. As a result, SAM is often repurposed as a pseudo-label generator [22], transferring distilled knowledge from pretraining to support downstream segmentation tasks. While such pseudo-labeling can improve image segmentation accuracy, current utilization schemes remain relatively simple, typically using pseudo-labels generated by SAM for input augmentation [23] or network supervision [22], without accounting for their inherent prediction uncertainty. This limits the extent to which SAM’s knowledge can be fully leveraged for domain-specific medical segmentation tasks.

To improve segmentation accuracy and boundary reliability, we propose a novel image segmentation framework that combines a hierarchical spatial perception network (HSP-Net) and a SAM-assisted uncertainty suppression (SUS) strategy. HSP-Net is built by integrating a group pyramid attention (GPA) module into the classical U-Net to enhance segmentation accuracy. The GPA module combines a Channel Pooling Attention (CPA) block and a Spatial Hierarchical Attention (SHA) block. This design enables the network to capture both local and global context, thereby detecting key visual features of target objects and their boundaries more effectively. Complementing this, the SUS strategy generates boundary uncertainty labels by measuring discrepancies between MedSAM-generated pseudo-labels and their corresponding ground-truth annotations. These uncertainty labels guide the segmentation network to focus on challenging boundary regions, reducing prediction ambiguity and improving robustness in these regions. With HSP-Net and SUS, our proposed framework is expected to achieve high segmentation accuracy and reliable boundary delineation.

The main contributions of this work are: (1) we develop a flexible image segmentation framework that reduces boundary prediction errors and can be easily adapted to existing neural networks and foundational models. (2) we design HSP-Net by embedding the GPA module into U-Net, significantly boosting feature learning by capturing multi-scale semantic information. (3) We introduce the SUS strategy that identifies hard-to-segment pixels and generates uncertainty labels to improve boundary segmentation. (4) We validate the proposed framework through extensive segmentation experiments on multiple medical image datasets to demonstrate its superior performance over existing methods.
