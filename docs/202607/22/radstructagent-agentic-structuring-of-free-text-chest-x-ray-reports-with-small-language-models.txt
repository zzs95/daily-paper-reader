Abstract Purpose: Variability in chest X-ray (CXR) reporting hinders training and eval-uation of vision-language models (VLMs) for radiology report generation. Our previous study showed that Structured Radiology Report Generation (SRRG)-style supervision improves VLM-based CXR reporting over free-text supervision. Here, we examine this finding in a larger multi-institutional setting and address a key preprocessing challenge: reliable free-text-to-structured-report conversion using open-source small language models (SLMs). 

Methods: We evaluate Qwen2.5-VL-3B using free-text and SRRG-style struc-tured supervision across 10 institutions covering 97K samples. We then propose RadStructAgent, an agentic framework that converts free-text CXR reports into structured reports using SLMs, combining LLM-based orchestration, judge feedback, and feedback-guided revision. RadStructAgent is evaluated on 1,000 free-text CXR reports using Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B. 

Results: For the report-generation task, structured supervision improves Qwen2.5-VL-3B over free-text supervision, increasing GREEN from 0.494 to 0.539, with similar gains across other clinical metrics. For the structuring task, RadStructAgent improves both clinical quality (GREEN) and anatomy-level assignment (RadFAA): for Qwen3-4B, from 0.844 to 0.867 and 0.883 to 0.891; for Qwen3-8B, from 0.863 to 0.881 and 0.889 to 0.902. 

Conclusion: Structured supervision remains effective for VLM-based CXR report generation when evaluated in a larger multi-institutional setting. Rad-StructAgent shows that open-source SLMs can be strengthened through agentic verification and refinement, providing a resource-efficient approach for offline and privacy-preserving structured report preparation.

# 1 Introduction 

Medical report generation (MRG) aims to automatically generate diagnostic reports from medical images and has attracted increasing attention as a way to reduce radiolo-gists’ workload and improve clinical workflow efficiency. Chest X-ray (CXR) reporting is one of the most widely studied MRG tasks because of the high volume of examina-tions performed in routine clinical practice. Although recent vision-language models (VLMs) have shown promising progress in CXR report generation [ 20 ], their perfor-mance remains limited by the heterogeneous and unstructured nature of radiology reports [ 8]. Radiology reports vary across institutions in terminology, writing style, sentence structure, reporting preference, and anatomical organization [ 8]. This variability complicates both model training and evaluation [ 8, 5]. In particular, free-text supervi-sion requires models to learn irrelevant linguistic variation and makes anatomy-level assessment difficult. Structured Radiology Report Generation (SRRG) addresses this limitation by using language models to organize free-text findings into predefined anatomical regions [ 5]. This enables more consistent supervision and anatomy-aware evaluation, but the quality of the structured output depends strongly on the capability of the underlying LLM used for structuring. In our recent work [ 8], we investigated SRRG-style report structuring and its effect on the VLM-based CXR report generation downstream task. The results showed that structured supervision improves VLM performance compared with conventional free-text supervision. In this study, we further examine the generalizability of this finding using a larger-scale multi-institutional setting. However, broader use of structured supervision depends on a critical preprocessing step: reliable conversion of free-text reports into SRRG-style anatomical sections. Preparing structured report data at scale remains challenging because most CXR datasets are still available in free-text format. The SRRG-Benchmark shows that strong proprietary large language models (LLMs), such as Gemini, achieve sub-stantially better structuring performance than SLMs [ 8]. These results suggest that high-quality SRRG-style structuring of free-text reports remains difficult for small models under standard single-pass inference settings. Nevertheless, proprietary mod-els may not always be practical for institutional use because of cost, privacy concerns, regulatory restrictions, and dependence on external cloud-based services. In addition, many research and clinical environments may have limited computational resources for running larger language models locally. Consequently, there remains a practical need for reliable and computationally efficient approaches that improve structured report generation using open-source small language models (SLMs). 2Fig. 1 Overview of RadStructAgent for SRRG-style structuring of free-text CXR reports. A base LLM generates an initial candidate y0 from input report x, which is iteratively refined through shared state st, orchestrator decisions O(st), judge feedback, revision, candidate selection, and stop control. The framework outputs the final structured report ˆ y.

Therefore, the primary focus of this study is to improve SRRG-style structuring of free-text CXR findings using open-source SLMs. To this end, we propose RadStruc-tAgent, a model-agnostic agentic framework for SRRG-style structuring of free-text CXR reports. It uses orchestration, judge feedback, sequential revision, and candidate selection to iteratively improve structured outputs. Instead of relying on single-pass inference, RadStructAgent iteratively verifies and refines structured outputs to improve clinical faithfulness and anatomical consistency while remaining suitable for resource-efficient offline report structuring. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows: 

• We investigate the generalizability of SRRG-style structured supervision for VLM-based CXR report generation using a larger-scale multi-institutional dataset. 

• We introduce RadStructAgent, an agentic framework for SRRG-style radiol-ogy report structuring that combines LLM-based orchestration, LLM-as-a-judge feedback, sequential revision, candidate selection, and stop control. 

• We open-source the RadStructAgent pipeline so that other researchers can repro-duce the workflow, adapt the prompts, and use it for free-text-to-structured-report conversion. 32 Related Work 

Automatic medical report generation has evolved from early CNN–RNN caption-ing models to transformer-based and, more recently, multimodal vision-language approaches [ 10 , 15 , 18 , 13 ]. However, many systems still rely on heterogeneous free-text supervision and conventional evaluation metrics, limiting reliable assessment of clinical correctness and anatomy-level consistency [ 6, 20 ]. Structured reporting has long been promoted as a way to improve consistency, clarity, and clinical communication in radiology. It has evolved from the American College of Radiology guideline to the RSNA RadLex and RadReport initiatives and the European Society of Radiology white paper. Despite these efforts, structured reporting remains underused in routine practice. [ 3]SRRG-style reporting addresses this limitation by organizing CXR findings into predefined anatomical regions [ 5]. This structure supports consistent report represen-tation, anatomy-aware evaluation, and anatomy-level data analysis. Prior work has also investigated fine-tuning approaches for converting free-text reports into structured reports suitable for training multimodal models on image-to-structured-report gener-ation tasks [ 9]. However, our recent study showed that smaller fine-tuned models still struggle in the findings section, including duplicated findings and incorrect anatomical assignment [ 8]. Therefore, reliable free-text-to-structured-report conversion remains an open challenge for preparing structured supervision for multimodal model training. Agentic AI extends LLMs to multi-step workflows with planning, tool use, memory, reflection, and feedback [ 16 , 4]. Recent work suggests that SLMs can be effective in agentic workflows because many agent calls are specialized and constrained [ 1]. In healthcare, this is relevant because open-source models may be preferred when proprietary cloud systems raise concerns about cost, privacy, transparency, and data governance [ 4]. In radiology, Agentic AI has been discussed for role-based agents, retrieval, uncer-tainty estimation, and iterative reasoning, including the risk of hallucination [ 11 ]. Multi-agent report generation methods have also used specialized agents for drafting, refinement, and final synthesis, but target image-to-report generation [ 17 ]. However, existing work has not specifically investigated agentic workflows for SRRG-style struc-turing of free-text CXR reports using SLMs. RadStructAgent addresses this gap by introducing an agentic framework that verifies and refines SRRG-style structuring with SLMs. 

# 3 Method 

We propose RadStructAgent, an agentic framework for SRRG-style structuring of free-text CXR findings. Given an input report x, a base LLM structurer M first generates an initial structured candidate y0. The candidate is then iteratively verified and refined through a shared state st, an LLM orchestrator O, prompt-guided LLM tools including judges Jf , J a, revision R, candidate memory Ct, and stop control, as shown in Fig. 1.At iteration t, the shared state st stores the current candidate, judge feedback, whether each judge has evaluated the current candidate, candidate history, tool-call count, revision count, and remaining budget. The orchestrator O reads st and 4selects the next action, which may invoke findings judging, anatomy judging, revision, candidate selection, or finalization. All modules use role-specific prompts, enabling specialized structuring, orchestration, judging, revision, and candidate selection. 

3.1 Initial Structuring and Shared State 

Let x denote the input free-text findings section. A base LLM structurer M generates the initial SRRG-style candidate y0 using a structuring prompt Ps:

y0 = M (Ps(x)) .

The prompt instructs the model to organize findings into predefined anatomical sections, including Lungs and Airways , Pleura , Cardiovascular , Hila and Mediastinum ,

Tubes, Catheters, and Support Devices , Musculoskeletal and Chest Wall , Abdominal ,and Other . The initial candidate is stored in the candidate memory: 

C0 = {y0}.

The agentic loop is controlled by the shared state: 

st = {yt, F t, A t, ρ f (yt), ρ a(yt), Ct, n t, r t, b t},

where yt is the current candidate, Ft is findings-level feedback, At is anatomy-level feedback, ρf (yt) and ρa(yt) indicate whether the findings judge and anatomy judge have evaluated the current candidate, Ct is the candidate set, nt is the number of tool calls, rt is the number of revision rounds, and bt is the remaining budget. The shared state provides a compact memory of the loop and makes all decisions, revisions, and stopping events traceable. 

3.2 Orchestration and Action Space 

The orchestrator O is an LLM-based controller that selects the next action from the current state: 

ut = O(st), ut ∈ U .

We define the action space as: 

U = {uf , u a, u r , u s, u final },

where uf , ua, ur , us, and ufinal denote findings judging, anatomy judging, revision, candidate selection, and finalization, respectively. The orchestrator returns a JSON-formatted decision containing the selected action, reasoning, and certainty score. Invalid actions, malformed JSON outputs, or budget-violating decisions are handled using conservative fallback rules, such as continuing validation, selecting the best available candidate, or finalizing the current report. 53.3 LLM Tools: Judging, Sequential Revision, and Selection 

RadStructAgent uses complementary LLM tools to evaluate and improve the current candidate. The findings judge Jf (·) evaluates whether the structured report preserves clinical content from the source free-text report: 

Ft = Jf (x, y t),

where Ft contains missing findings and unsupported findings. Missing findings are observations present in x but absent from yt, while unsupported findings are generated statements not supported by the source free-text report (hallucinations). The anatomy judge Ja(·) evaluates anatomical placement and repeated content: 

At = Ja(yt),

where At contains feedback on wrong-section and duplicate findings. Wrong-section findings are statements placed under an incorrect anatomical section in yt, while dupli-cate findings are repeated statements appearing in multiple sections. Both judges return structured JSON feedback, which is parsed and written to the shared state. When revision is selected, the framework applies feedback-guided revision sequen-tially rather than combining all feedback in a single revision call. If findings-level feedback is available, the revision agent first updates the current candidate using the source report and Ft:

y(f ) 

> t

= Rf (x, y t, F t).

If anatomy-level feedback is available, the anatomy revision agent then updates the intermediate candidate using At:

yt+1 = Ra(y(f )

, A t).

If only one feedback type is available, only the corresponding revision step is applied. This sequential design allows the framework to first correct missing or unsup-ported clinical content and then correct anatomical placement or duplicated findings. The revised candidate is added to the memory: 

Ct+1 = Ct ∪ { yt+1 }.

When the orchestrator invokes candidate selection, the selector S chooses the final structured report from candidate memory. It selects the candidate that best aligns with the source free-text report x, considering source-supported content, anatomical placement, duplicate findings, and conciseness: ˆy = S(x, Ct).

If the candidate selection is unavailable or invalid, the framework falls back to the current candidate. 63.4 Stop Control 

Before each new orchestrator decision, the shared state summarizes whether the cur-rent candidate has been evaluated by both the findings and anatomy judges and whether any findings-level or anatomy-level feedback remains. The loop stops when the orchestrator selects finalization, when the candidate selector returns the best report from memory, when the maximum tool-call or revision budget is reached, or when revision produces an empty or unchanged candidate. These safeguards keep inference computationally bounded and prevent unnecessary refinement. 

# 4 Experimental Setup 

4.1 Dataset and Tasks 

We use ReXGradient-160K [ 19 ], a public multi-institutional CXR dataset with image-report pairs. Since the findings section contains the main anatomical and pathological observations [ 8, 7], all experiments focus on this section. We conduct two complementary experiments. First, we evaluate structured super-vision for VLM-based report generation by fine-tuning Qwen2.5-VL-3B [ 12 ] with either free-text or SRRG-style reports using 97K samples from 10 institutions. The data are split into 80% training, 10% validation, and 10% testing sets. This experiment uses the structured-supervision dataset from our previous study [ 7], where Gemini was used to convert free-text reports into SRRG-style reports. This provides a high-quality structured-supervision condition and reduces the risk that the VLM comparison is biased by structuring errors. Second, for the RadStructAgent experiment, we evaluate free-text-to-structured-report conversion on 1,000 free-text findings reports randomly selected across multiple institutions. This is the same evaluation set used in our previous SRRG-Benchmark study [ 8], enabling comparison with proprietary reference models under a consistent evaluation setting. 

4.2 Baselines and Models 

For the structuring task, we compare RadStructAgent with a standard single-pass inference baseline. In this setting, the same underlying model directly converts the input findings section into an SRRG-style report using the structuring prompt, representing conventional single-pass LLM inference without agentic refinement. We evaluate Qwen3-4B as the primary SLM and Qwen3-8B as an additional model from the same family. The single-pass and agentic settings use the same model checkpoint, structuring prompt, and local inference configuration. For the agentic set-ting, both models use the same action space, stopping rules, and candidate-selection mechanism. 

4.3 Implementation Details 

For the downstream VLM experiment, we follow the fine-tuning protocol of our pre-vious study [ 8], while scaling the evaluation to a larger multi-institutional dataset. 7Qwen2.5-VL-3B is fine-tuned to generate reports from CXR images, using paired CXR images with either free-text reports or SRRG-style structured reports as supervision. Both settings use the same data split, model configuration, and evaluation pipeline, allowing us to isolate the effect of supervision format. RadStructAgent is implemented directly in Python without an external agentic framework, enabling explicit control over state management, tool execution, fallback handling, candidate memory, stop control, and trace logging. All RadStructAgent experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU (40 GB). Model inference is served through vLLM using an OpenAI-compatible local API endpoint with a max-imum context length of 32,768 tokens, GPU memory utilization of 0.90, bitsandbytes 4-bit quantization, and one worker by default. Deterministic decoding is used with temperature set to 0.0 for reproducibility. The open-source structuring experiments in this study use a different inference setup from the original SRRG-Benchmark [ 8] open-source evaluation. For computa-tional efficiency and resource-efficient local use, RadStructAgent uses vLLM serving with 4-bit quantization to reduce GPU memory requirements. Therefore, proprietary model results are included as reference results from the prior benchmark, while the primary comparison is between single-pass and agentic inference under the same local configuration. Each report is processed with a maximum of 20 tool calls and 10 revision rounds, with candidate selection enabled by default. The implementation and full prompt templates are available in the public code repository .

4.4 Evaluation Metrics 

Because small wording changes can alter clinical meaning, surface-level similarity met-rics are insufficient for evaluating medical reports [ 2]. We evaluate both experiments using medical report evaluation metrics implemented in the RadEval framework [ 14 ]. GREEN is used as the primary metric for clinical report quality, while RadFAA [ 8] is used as a secondary metric for anatomy-level assignment and structured-report quality. F1-SRR-BERT, CRIMSON, and RaTEScore are reported as complementary metrics. For VLM-based report generation, all model outputs are evaluated against the original free-text reference report for the corresponding CXR image to ensure a con-sistent reference across supervision settings. Before evaluation, SRRG-style outputs are converted to plain free-text by removing anatomical section headers and extra line breaks, leaving only the medical findings content. For the report structuring task, following the setting used in our prior SRRG-Benchmark study [ 8], each structured report is evaluated against its corresponding source free-text report. 85 Results and Discussion

5.3 Orchestrator Behavior and Runtime Trade-off 

Fig. 2 summarizes the behavior of the RadStructAgent refinement loop. The revision-round distribution shows that most reports require only a small number of revisions, indicating that the framework adapts the amount of refinement to each case. The stop-reason analysis shows that judge-approved finalization occurs in 87% of Qwen3-4B cases and 81% of Qwen3-8B cases. The remaining cases terminate through controlled fallback pathways, including unchanged revisions, budget limits, or candidate selection from memory. These results show that RadStructAgent does not rely on a fixed number of itera-tions. Instead, it usually finalizes candidates after judge approval, while more difficult cases stop when revisions no longer change the output, the budget is reached, or the best candidate is selected from memory. Fig. 3 compares runtime and quality between single-pass inference and RadStruc-tAgent. For Qwen3-4B, the mean time per report increases from 0.7 s to 6.7 s, while GREEN increases from 0.844 to 0.867 and RadFAA from 0.883 to 0.891. For Qwen3-8B, the mean time per report increases from 1.0 s to 13.2 s, while GREEN increases from 0.863 to 0.881 and RadFAA from 0.889 to 0.902. Although the agentic work-flow increases inference time due to multiple LLM calls, structured report preparation is typically a one-time offline preprocessing step. Component Build-up 

Table 3 reports the build-up ablation from single-pass inference to the full RadStruc-tAgent configuration, with all gains measured relative to the single-pass baseline. Blind revision applies the revision step within our framework without judge feedback, to assess the contribution of the revisor alone. Compared with single-pass inference, blind revision does not change GREEN for either model, but increases RadFAA by 0.008 for Qwen3-4B and 0.015 for Qwen3-8B. Adding findings-judge feedback mainly improves GREEN, with gains of 0.023 for Qwen3-4B and 0.017 for Qwen3-8B. The blind-revision result suggests that, within RadStructAgent, the revision step alone provides limited standalone benefit: it improves anatomy-level assignment, as reflected by RadFAA, but does not improve clinical quality. 5.5 Limitations and Future Work 

Although RadStructAgent improves SRRG-style structuring with open-source SLMs, there remains substantial potential to further improve the proposed framework. This study relies on automatic metrics, and the LLM-as-a-judge tools may miss or incor-rectly flag findings. The single anatomy judge may also be limited in assessing correct anatomical assignment for ambiguous findings. Future work should include human expert validation, improved judge reliability analysis, prompt-sensitivity evaluation, per-anatomy feedback judges, adaptive tool-call budgeting, broader multi-institutional evaluation, and experiments with additional model families and sizes. Another impor-tant direction is assigning different SLMs to different agent roles instead of using the same model across all modules. 

# 6 Conclusion 

In this study, we examine SRRG-style structured supervision for VLM-based CXR report generation in a larger multi-institutional setting, and introduce RadStructA-gent, an agentic framework for SRRG-style structuring of free-text CXR reports using open-source SLMs. Our downstream VLM experiment shows that SRRG-style structured supervision improves Qwen2.5-VL-3B report generation. For the structuring task, RadStructA-gent improves both clinical report quality and anatomy-level assignment compared with single-pass inference. The ablation analyses further suggest that the proposed framework benefits from complementary judge feedback and sequential revision, while not being limited to a single model family. These findings indicate that agentic verification and refinement can improve the clinical accuracy of free-text report structuring with open-source SLMs. Future work will focus on expert validation, improved judge reliability, adaptive tool-call budgeting, 13 broader multi-institutional evaluation, and testing additional model families and role-specific LLM assignments.
