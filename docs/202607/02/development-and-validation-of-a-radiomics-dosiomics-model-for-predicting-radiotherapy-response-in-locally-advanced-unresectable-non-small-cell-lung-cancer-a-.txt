Abstract 

Background:  Early assessment of treatment response is essential for  optimizing 

cancer management, as it allows timely interventions during the course of therapy, 

potentially improving cancer control and clinical outcomes. In this study, we aimed to 

develop and validat e a machine learning model integrating radiomics, dosiomics, and 

clinical  characteristics  to predict  response  to  radiotherapy in patients with  locally 

advanced, unresectable  non -small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). 

Methods:  A total of 222 patients across multiple centers who received radiotherapy 

for NSCLC were enrolled and divided into training (n =  110 ), internal validation (n = 

28) , and  external  validation (n = 84) cohorts. Objective response rate (ORR) and 

progression -fre e survival (PFS) were predicted using models based on radiomics, 

dosiomics, and clinical  characteristics . Model performance was evaluated using 

receiver operating characteristic  (ROC)  curves,  DeLong test,  decision curve analysis 

(DCA) , Kaplan -Meier  survival analysis , and  Integrated Brier Score (IBS) .

Results : The clinical models , CORR  and C PFS  (both  based on  planning target volume 

[PTV ] and  lymphocyte count ) were compared with combined 

radiomics –dosiomics –clinical model s (RDC ORR  and RDC PFS ). For ORR prediction,

RDC ORR  achieved AUC s of 0. 901, 0. 894 , and 0.8 69 in the training , internal validation, 

and  external  validation cohorts, outperforming C ORR  (AUC s of  0.723,  0. 606 , and 

0. 72 3), with  p < 0.05 . DCA  indicate d that RDC ORR  outperform ed  CORR , providing  a

higher overall net benefit . For PFS prediction, RDC PFS  yielded higher concordance 

indices (0. 805 , 0. 730 , and 0. 743  in the training , internal validation,  and  external 

validation  cohort s) than C PFS  (0. 679, 0.699,  and 0.64 0, respectively ).  RDC PFS  showed 

the lowest IBS  across  all cohorts  (0. 09 2, 0. 107 , and  0. 093 , respectively ) compared 

with  CPFS  and  the  reference  model , indicating better predictive accuracy .

Conclusions:  The combined model integrating radiomics, dosiomics, and clinical 

characteristics  enhances the prediction of radiotherapy response in  locally  advanced, 

unresectable  NSCLC,  facilitating  improved  patient monitoring and  more effective 

adjuvant clinical trial  design .

1.  Introduction 

Non -small cell lung cancer (NSCLC)  accounts for approximately  75% –80% 

of all primary lung cancer cases and remains  a leading  cause of cancer -related death 

worldwide , with  a 5-year overall survival rate  of  around  20% 1,2 . Radiotherapy  plays a 

crucial role in the  management  of  NSCLC , especially for  patients with  locally 

advanced  or  unresectable  diseases 3-5. However,  despite  standard ized  treatment 

regimens , local failure  occurs  in up to 50% of patients , and  insufficient local control 

remains  a major  cause of poor  survival outcomes 6,7 . For patients with NSCLC who 

receive  radiotherapy , attention  should be paid  to the radiation response of the tumor, 

such as  near -term local control rate , objective response rate  (ORR),  and 

progression -free survival  (PFS) . Tumor radiosensitivity and treatment response vary 

considerably among individuals 8. Early identification of patients who may benefit 

from radiotherapy enables timely adjustments to the treatment plan, thereby 

improving clinical efficacy.  Conventional  clinical factors, such as age, smoking status, 

tumor stage, or mutation statu s,  hematological parameters,  systemic 

immune -inflammation index , tumor consolidation and necrosis , etc. , offer limited 

ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 5

predictive value 9-13 , highlighting the necessity of more reliable biomarkers .

Artificial intelligence (AI), particularly machine learning,  ha s created new 

frontiers in oncologic imaging.  Among these approaches,  radiomics , which  extracts 

high -dimensional quantitative features from medical images has shown great potential 

in  the field of tumor  imaging and has been widely applied to predict treatment 

response s and prognos es of  various cancers, including lung cancer  treated with 

chemotherapy or radiotherapy 14 -17 .

Radiotherapy  efficacy  is  also  closely related to dose distribution . Although 

features based on  dose –volume histogram (DVH)  have been  used to predict  PFS  in 

locally advanced nasopharyngeal carcinoma , pathological  complete  response in 

esophageal  cancer 18,19 , and radiation  pneumonitis  in  NSCLC 20 , they  capture  only 

two -dimensional dose distribution . In contrast, t he  emerging  dosiomics method can 

describe the  three -dimensional  dose distribution  in terms of  intensity,  texture, shape 

and other dose characteristics , thereby providing more accurate and  comprehensive 

spatial  characterization of  radiotherapy dose  patterns 21,22 .

In this study,  we used multi -institution data to build and validate a machine 

learning model based on radiomics , dosiomics , and clinical characteristics  to predict 

radiotherapy response in patients with  NSCLC .

2.  Material s and methods 

2.1  Patient characteristics 

This was a retrospective multi -center study approved  by the Ethics Committee 

of the Affiliated Hospital of Jining Medical University (Jining Medical University 

ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 6

Ethics Committee for Human Research), China , and  the requirement for  informed 

consent was waived . This study was conducted according to the Declaration of 

Helsinki. 

This  retrospective  study  included  138 patients with NSCLC who received 

intensity -modulated radiation therapy (IMRT) or volumetric -modulated arc therapy 

(VMAT) at the participating center between June 2016 and December 2022 .

Radiotherapy  was  delivered using conventional linear accelerators, including Varian 

Clinac  23EX and Varian  Clinac  IX systems (Varian Medical Systems, Palo Alto, CA, 

USA), and the XHLA1400 linear accelerator ( Shinva Med ical Instrument Co., Ltd., 

China) . All treatments were  delivered under free -breathing conditions, without the use 

of respiratory gating or breath -hold techniques. Patients were positioned and 

immobilized according to standard clinical protocols.  The study cohort was  randomly 

stratified  into a training cohort  (n =  110 ) and an internal validation cohort  (n =  28 ) at a 

ratio of 8 :2. Additionally, 84 patients from two other hospitals (49  from  Hospital 1 

and  35 from  Hospital 2) were  collected  as the  external  validation cohort, with data 

between January 2019 and  December 2022 . Radiotherapy  was  delivered once d aily at 

1.8 –3.0 Gy per fraction , five  fractions per  week, across 20 –30 fractions, for a total 

dose of 40 –60 Gy . In the PACIFIC era, following the establishment of consolidation 

immunotherapy after chemoradiot herapy in unresectable  NSCLC , immunotherapy has 

become a key prognostic factor 23 ; therefore, patients were stratified into 

immunotherapy (IO) and non -immunotherapy (non -IO)  groups according to treatment 

regimens . The inclusion  criteria  were  as follows : (i) Pathological diagnosis of  NSCLC .

(ii)  Patients with locally advanced, unresectable  NSCLC —defined as those not 

amenable to surgical resection due to tumor extent (e.g., T4 disease, N3 nodal 

involvement, or invasion of critical mediastinal structures). All patients had an 

Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status (ECOG PS)  of ≤2 to e nsure 

tolerance to definitive radiotherapy.  (iii)  Patient s who  completed radiotherapy and ha d

comprehensive  clinical data.  (iv) All patients underwent chest CT examination at 1, 3, 

6, and 12 months after radiotherapy,  or had  the  endpoint . The exclusion  criteria  were 

as follows : (i)  Patients whose primary lesion was surgically removed and th us did not 

undergo radiotherapy . (ii) Recurrent chest radiotherapy  or p atients treated with 

stereotactic body radiation therapy . (iii)  Patients  with other  concomitant  malignancies. 

(iv ) Patients with mediastinal primary tumors . (v) Patients who did not complete 

radiotherapy due to poor tolerance, severe complications, or personal/ economic 

reasons, and thus lacked complete follow -up data . The primary endpoint of this study 

was PFS,  and  the secondary endpoint was the ORR of the primary tumor , assessed  at 

3 months  after  completion of  radiotherapy.  PFS was defined as the time from the 

initiation of radiotherapy to the first occurrence of disea se progression or death from 

any cause, whichever occurred first.  Tumor response and disease progression were 

assessed according to the Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST )

version 1.1 . Imaging evaluations were performed at 3 months after  radiotherapy and 

subsequently every 3 months wit hin the first year of follow -up . All evaluations were 

independently performed by one experienced radiologist and one experienced 

oncologist, both blinded to the patients’ clinical information and model output s.

The clinical characteristics  of the patients were recorded, including age, 

gender, smoking history, pathological type,  T stage,  treatment  regimens,  and baseline 

hematological parameters (obtained  within 2 weeks before radiotherapy  initiat ion ), 

including  lymphocyte count, neutrophil count, monocyte count, 

neutrophil -to -lymphocyte ratio (NLR),  and  lymphocyte -to -monocyte ratio (LMR) .

Radiotherapy -related parameters were also collected,  including  biological effective 

dose (BED) , planning target volume (PTV),  mean dose to PTV (PTV -Dmean), dose 

received by 98% of  the  PTV (PTV -D98).  The BED, which accounts for both the total 

dose and fractionation schedule, was calculated using the linear -quadratic model to 

better reflect the biological effect of the delivered r adiation .

Univariable and multivariable regression analyses were performed to identify 

independent clinical predictors  of  ORR and PFS, using logistic regression for ORR 

and Cox proportional hazards regression for PFS. Variables with  P < 0.05 in the 

univariable analysis were included in the corresponding multivariable models.  The 

clinical models ( CORR  and  CPFS ) were constructed based on the independent predictors 

identified in  the multivariable analyses. 

2.2  CT  acquisition  and image segmentation 

All patients underwent tumour  localization  using  large -bore  CT  simulators 

(PHILIPS Brilliance CT Big Bore , and Canon Aquilion Prime TSX -303A ). Scanning 

was performed with a tube  voltage  of  120kV . Tube current  was  set  at  approximately 

250 mAs  according to institutional protocols, and automatic exposure control  was 

applied in some cases depending on the scanner configuration.  The  helical  pitch  was  1,

and slice thickness  was  5 mm.  CT images were reconstructed using a standard 

soft -tissue convolution kernel with a matrix size of 512 × 512, resulting in an in -plane 

voxel size of approximately 0.98 × 0.98 mm².  Experienced radiation oncologists 

delineated the gross tumor volume (GTV) of the primary lung tumor using the Eclipse 

or  Monaco treatment planning system (TPS), based on  contrast -enhanced CT images. 

The clinical target volume (CTV) include d the GTV and regions at risk of 

microscopic tumor spread, with expansion margins determined according to tumor 

location, anatomical boundaries, and institutional protocols. The planning target 

volume (PTV) was generated by further expanding the CTV to acco unt for setup 

uncertainties and internal organ motion, with the margin size tailored to individual 

ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 10 

patient conditions and immobilization techniques.  The  prescri bed  dose  was designed 

to cover  at least 95% of PTV. 

2.3  Features extraction and selection,  mode ls building 

In this study,  different regions of interest  (ROI s) were adopted for radiomics 

and dosiomics feature extraction, considering their distinct biological and clinical 

implications. Radiomic s features were extracted from  GTV to ensure that the analysis 

primarily reflect ed  intratumoral heterogeneity, which is widely recognized as the 

standard approach in radiomics  research . In contrast, dosiomics features were derived 

from the PTV, as dose distribution in radiotherapy is p lanned and prescribed based on 

this volume , making PTV -based dosiomics more  representative  of the actual delivered 

dose . No additional manual segmentation was performed . The planning  CT  images, 

ROI s, and  corresponding dosimetric  images were exported from  TPS  and imported 

into 3D Slicer (version 5.1.0 , https://www.slicer.org ).  All radiomic s and dosiomic s

features were  then  extracted in compliance with the Image Biomarker Standardization 

Initiative (IBSI) usin g the  SlicerRadiomics  extension  based on the  Pyradiomics  library. 

The original dose calculation grid size in the  TPS  was  2.5 mm  for all  patients.  To 

ensure spatial consistency  among  CT images, dose distributions, and  ROI s, both  CT 

and dos imetric  images were resampled to an isotropic voxel size of 1 mm  prior to 

feature extraction  using nearest -neighbor interpolation . Voxel intensities were 

discretized with a fixed bin width of 25 HU for CT an d 0.1 Gy for dose distributions .

These preprocessing ste ps partially mitigated variability  among  different scanners. 

Dosiomic s features were extracted from the 3D dose maps  within the  corresponding 

ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 11 

ROI (PTV) using the same feature categories and extraction settings as  those used  for 

CT radiomics.  A total of 1 ,834 radiomics and 1 ,834 dosiomics features were extracted 

from each ROI,  including  216  first -order  features , 876  texture  features , 14  shape 

features  and 728 w avelet features , respectively . To enhance feature robustness, all 

features were Z -score  normalized, and highly correlated features (|r| > 0.9) were 

removed. Feature selection was performed in two steps: first, features were ranked 

using the minimum redundancy maximum relevance (mRMR) algorithm, followed by 

least absolute shrinkage and selecti on operator (LASSO) regression for final feature 

selection .

A logistic regression  (LR)  model  was fitted using the  selected features to 

obtain regression coefficients for each feature , and  the radiomics  signature  (RS)  and 

dosiomics signature (DS)  for  ORR (R SORR , DS ORR ) were calculated for each patient .

For PFS  analysis , radiomics  and dosiomics  features were selected  using  the 

LASSO -Cox model with tenfold cross -validation , and  the corresponding RS and DS 

(RS PFS , DS PFS ) were calculated  for each patient . The s ignature s were  calculated as

2.4  Performance comparison between different models 

For  ORR , model  performance  (COR R, R ORR , D ORR , and RDC ORR ) was  evaluated 

using  the  area under the receiver operating characteristic curve  (AUC) . The optimum 

cut -off value  wa s determined  using  the  You den index , and  the  sensitivity, specificity, 

and  accuracy  were  calculated in the training , internal validation,  and  external 

validation cohort s. De Long test was used to compare the AUC s among  models .

Decision curve analysis  (DCA) was  performed  to  assess  the clinical  utility .

For PFS,  risk scores (linear predictors) were calculated from the Cox 

proportional hazards model s using  the  “predict ” function  in R software . The optimal 

cutoff values for  CPFS  and  RDC PFS  were determined using  the  “survcutpoint ” function ,

and patients were  subsequently  stratified into  high -risk and low -risk group s. Survival 

differences between groups were assessed using the log -rank test. Model 

discrimination was evaluated using the concordance index (C -index) derived from the 

Cox model s. Additionally, predictive accuracy over time was assessed using the 

Integrated Brier Score (IBS)  integrated up to  1 year, with lower IBS  values  indicating 

better  model performance.

4.  Discussion 

In this multicenter study, we  evaluated the ability of a combined model 

incorporating radiomics, dosiomics, and independent clinical characteristics  to predict 

radiotherapy -associated ORR and PFS in  patients  with  locally advanced unresectable 

NSCLC.  For ORR prediction, the combined  model  (RDC ORR ) achieved  AUC s of 

0. 901, 0. 894 , and 0. 869 in the training , internal validation , and  external  validation 

cohort s, respectively, which w ere  higher than  those  of the  CORR  (AUC s of 0. 723, 

0.606 , and 0. 72 3, respectively). For PFS  prediction , the  combined model  (RDC PFS )

achieved  C-ind ices  of 0. 805 , 0.7 30 , and 0. 743  in the training , internal validation,  and 

external  validation  cohort s, which w ere  also higher than th ose  of the  CPFS  (0. 679, 

0.699 , and 0.6 40, respectively).  These  results demonstrated  that  the  combined model s

outperformed the clinical model s alone , indicating  the  incremental  value of radiomic s

and dosiomic s features  beyond  traditional clinical factors in predicting  radiotherapy 

outcomes  in  NSCLC. 

Our study show ed  that PTV and lymphocyte count  were  independent 

predictors of ORR  and PFS , emphasizing the importance of tumor  burden  and 

immune  status  in treatment outcome s. Consistent with previous studies,  larger tumor 

volume  has been  associated with poorer local control and survival 24 -27 . This may be 

attributed to increased tumor proliferation, hypoxic microenvironments, and potential 

dose heterogeneity in larger tumors, which can reduce treatment effectiveness 26 .

Higher l ymphocyte count was  associated  with  improved  ORR and PFS,  which is 

consistent with previous studies  show ing  that  pretreatment  lymphocytopenia  is  linked 

ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 20 

to  higher mortality  across  multiple cancer types 28 -30 . Higher lymphocyte  levels  may 

reflect a stronger  antitumor  immune response,  supporting  treatment  eff icacy and 

highlighting  the role of  the  immune system in cancer progression 28 .

In addition to these clinical factors,  treatment -related variables may also 

influence outcomes . S ystemic  treatment regimens varied a mong patients , and g iven 

the prognostic relevance of immunotherapy in the PACIFIC trial era , patients were 

stratified into IO and non -IO groups.  Subgroup analysis showed no statistically 

significant association between treatment strategy and ORR or PFS. However, this 

finding should be interpreted with caution  because of  the imbalance in sample size 

between groups and the relatively limited number of patients receiving 

immunotherapy, particularly  during  the earlier years of the study period (2016 –2022), 

when immunotherapy was less commonly used.  Radiation dose,  represented by  BED ,

has been associated with improved local control and survival in previo us stud y31 . In 

our  multivariable analysis  for  ORR , only PTV and lymphocyte count remained 

independent predictors,  whereas  BED and other dosimetric  parameters lost statistical 

significance, likely due to collinearity and shared information among radiation dose 

distribution metrics.  Similarly,  in the PFS analysis,  although PTV, lymphocyte count, 

and  NLR  were significant in univaria ble  analysis, only PTV and lymphocyte count 

remained independent pr edictors in multivariable model . NLR has been reported as a 

strong prognostic indicator in cancer 32 ; however, its effect in this study may overlap 

with  that of  other immune -relate d variables, leading to its exclusion  from  the 

multivariable  model .

ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 21 

In our study,  the integration of  radiomics and dosiomics  features  improved the 

predictive performance  for  ORR and PFS . The combined  models ( RDC ORR  and 

RDC PFS ) achieved better discrimination than  conventional  clinical models ( CORR  and 

CPFS ),  with higher  AUCs and C -index values across  training , internal validation , and 

external  validation cohorts.  Radiomic s ha s been  widely  applied  in  AI -based models to 

predict  oncologic  outcomes such as  local control  and  PFS  by capturing tumor shape, 

intensity, and texture features that reflect intratumoral heterogeneity 17,33 . Dosiomics 

characterizes  radiati on dose and spatial dose distribution s during radiotherapy and 

describes dose heterogeneity  within tumors . Studies have shown that dosiomics can 

predict  radiotherapy  efficacy and survival  outcomes in  different tumors 22,34 -36 .

Radiomics and dosi omics  provide  complement ary  information reflecting tumor 

phenotype and dose distribution characteristics, respectively . In our study, f or  ORR 

prediction,  although the radiomic s-only  (R ORR ) and dosiomic s-only  (D ORR ) models 

exhibited comparable performance, the integrated model (RDC ORR ) achieved 

relatively higher  AUC values across the training, internal  validation , and external 

validation cohorts.  For PFS prediction,  RDC PFS  was further evaluated using the 1 -year 

IBS  and the C -index.  RDC PFS  showed  relatively low  IBS  values  across cohorts  and 

maintained  acceptable  discriminative  performance  (C -ind ex  > 0.7),  suggesting a 

potentially useful predictive signal for  PFS.  Th ese  suggest  that combining 

complementary information from radiomic s, dosiomic s, and clinical  characteristics 

may provide additional predictive value ; however, the improvement in model 

performance  was  modest, and the findings should be interpreted with caution  given 

ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 22 

the limited sample size and wide confidence i ntervals in  the  internal validation  cohort .

Notably, certain geometric features, such as LeastAxisLength, may help explain the 

observed predictive signal. Previous studies have shown that shape -related des criptors 

may  be associated with treatment response 37 , suggesting that such features —whether 

derived from imaging or dose distributions —may reflect underlying tumor 

aggressiveness beyond simple volumetric measures. 

Additionally , there is overlap between the features predicting ORR and PFS, 

suggesting that these two prognostic  endpoints  may be influenced by similar 

biological mechanisms . A previous  review summarized the co rrelation and regression 

analys es between different tumor response outcomes and PFS,  primarily focusing on 

the relationship between ORR and PFS or OS 38 . Regarding  the association between 

ORR and PFS, most reported correlation coefficients  for  absolute  outcome  values 

were >0.60 (range  -0.72 to 0.96). For the correlation  between  treatment effects  on 

ORR  and PFS, most regression R 2 values were >0.40.  One  possible  explanation  is that 

certain  features , such as gene expression  and  tumor microenvironment , may affect 

both tumor response and growth  dynamics , thereby  affect ing  ORR and PFS. 

While the results are  encouraging  and  suggest that integrating radiomics, 

dosiomics, and clinical features may improve model performance , several limitations 

should be acknowledged. The retrospective design  and  relatively small s ample size  in 

a high -dimensional setting  may hav e reduced statistical power and introduced 

potential confounding ; therefore , the  robustness of the model s should be interpreted 

with caution . P rospective  multicenter studies with larger and more homogeneous 

ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 23 

cohorts are  warranted  to further validate the  predictive performance and clinical 

applicability of the se  models.  GTV and  PTV delineation w ere  performed manually, 

which is time -consuming and subject to inter -observer variability. The use of 

semi -automatic or automatic delineation methods in future stud ies could improve 

efficiency and consistency while enhancing model generalizability.  Another limitation 

is that radiomics analysis was  restricted  to  the  GTV, without incorporating 

peritumoral regions.  Emerging evidence suggests that peritumoral areas may  provide 

complementary biological information related to tumor –microenvironment 

interactions, which may differ from intratumoral features 30 . Future studies 

incorporating multiple regions of interest, su ch as the GTV, peritumoral regions, and 

PTV, may  better  characteriz e tumor heterogeneity and treatment -related effects. In 

particular, further investigation into the distinct and temporal roles of intra - and 

peritumoral radiomic s features during radiothera py may improve the understanding of 

tumor behavior and potentially enhance prognostic modeling in NSCLC 39 . In addition, 

although standardized CT and dose acquisition protocols were used, including a 

uniform 2.5 mm dose grid, variations in exposure control and scanner settings may 

still  hav e existed. CT and dose maps were resampled to a 1 mm isotropic grid for 

spatial alignment; however, interpolation during upsampling may introduce 

smoothing effects and influence feature stability. 

5.  Conclusions 

This  study suggests that combining radiomics and dosiomics features  can 

provide  complementary information  beyond traditional clinical parameters  in  patients 

ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS ARTICLE IN PRESS 24 

with  locally advanced  unresectable  NSCLC . By  improving  predicti ve  accuracy, these 

models  may enhance risk stratification and  facilitate b etter clinical decision support .

Future research should focus on further refining these models and  validating  their 

applicability in real -world clinical settings.
