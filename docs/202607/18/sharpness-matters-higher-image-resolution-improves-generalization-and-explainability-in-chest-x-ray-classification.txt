## Introduction

Artificial intelligence (AI) has been rapidly adopted in radiology with a focus on using deep learning (DL) algorithms for the diagnosis of diseases[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR1 "Rothenberg, S. A. et al. Prospective evaluation of AI triage of pulmonary emboli on CT pulmonary angiograms. Radiology 309, e230702. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1148/radiol.230702
                  
                 (2023) (PMID: 37787676)."),[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR2 "Salim, M. et al. External evaluation of 3 commercial artificial intelligence algorithms for independent assessment of screening mammograms. JAMA Oncology 6, 1581–1588, 
                  https://doi.org/10.1001/jamaoncol.2020.3321
                  
                . https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamaoncology/articlepdf/2769894/jamaoncology_salim_2020_oi_200057_1619718170.78837.pdf (2020)."),[3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR3 "Savage, C. H. et al. Prospective evaluation of artificial intelligence triage of incidental pulmonary emboli on contrast-enhanced CT examinations of the chest or abdomen. Am. J. Roentgenol. 223, e2431067. 
                  https://doi.org/10.2214/AJR.24.31067
                  
                 (2024)."). Although medical images are routinely acquired at high resolutions to capture fine pathological details, DL models commonly down-sample medical images during training[4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR4 "Murphy, Z. R., Venkatesh, K., Sulam, J. & Yi, P. H. Visual transformers and convolutional neural networks for disease classification on radiographs: A comparison of performance, sample efficiency, and hidden stratification. Radiol. Artif. Intell. 4, e220012. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1148/ryai.220012
                  
                 (2022)."),[5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR5 "Zech, J. R. et al. Variable generalization performance of a deep learning model to detect pneumonia in chest radiographs: A cross-sectional study. PLOS Med. 15, 1–17. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1002683
                  
                 (2018)."),[6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR6 "Rajpurkar, P. et al. Deep learning for chest radiograph diagnosis: A retrospective comparison of the chexnext algorithm to practicing radiologists. PLOS Med. 15, 1–17. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1002686
                  
                 (2018).") to reduce computational costs, which may limit their ability to identify subtle diseases. This practice contrasts sharply with clinical workflows, where radiologists rely on high-resolution images to make accurate diagnoses.

Previous studies[7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR7 "Sabottke, C. F. & Spieler, B. M. The effect of image resolution on deep learning in radiography. Radiol. Artif. Intell. 2, e190015 (2020)."),[8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR8 "Haque, M. I. U. et al. Effect of image resolution on automated classification of chest X-rays. J. Med. Imaging 10, 044503–044503 (2023).") have shown that DL models trained using lower resolution medical images can still achieve high performance for certain disease diagnoses in medical imaging, despite the common assumption that fine-grained details are essential for accurate predictions. A seminal study published in 2020 by Sabottke et al.[7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR7 "Sabottke, C. F. & Spieler, B. M. The effect of image resolution on deep learning in radiography. Radiol. Artif. Intell. 2, e190015 (2020).") suggested that when training DL classification models for chest radiograph (CXR) diagnosis, model performance plateaued for most diseases at resolutions of 256$\times$256 and 320$\times$320 pixels, although some had peak performance with higher image resolutions, i.e., 512$\times$512 or 600$\times$600; note that Sabottke et al. did not evaluate higher resolutions, despite the fact that native CXR resolution is typically 1024$\times$1024 or above). Subsequent work in CXR DL models[8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR8 "Haque, M. I. U. et al. Effect of image resolution on automated classification of chest X-rays. J. Med. Imaging 10, 044503–044503 (2023).") demonstrated similarly variable conclusions with peak performance for some diseases on low resolutions, e.g., 256$\times$256, and others at higher resolutions, e.g., 1024$\times$1024. These variable conclusions leave an unanswered question: what is the optimal image resolution for training deep learning CXR classifiers?

Discrepancies in reported optimal resolutions for DL classification models in radiology may stem from two key factors. First, previous studies on the topic have not evaluated generalizability of models on an external testing set (i.e. out-of-distribution [OOD] testing), despite the well-known potential overestimation of model performance when evaluated on internal testing images only[5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR5 "Zech, J. R. et al. Variable generalization performance of a deep learning model to detect pneumonia in chest radiographs: A cross-sectional study. PLOS Med. 15, 1–17. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1002683
                  
                 (2018)."). Second, prior studies have not evaluated image resolution’s impact on DL model explainability as determined by saliency map localization of imaging abnormalities[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR9 "Arun, N. et al. Assessing the trustworthiness of saliency maps for localizing abnormalities in medical imaging. Radiol. Artif. Intell. 3, e200267. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1148/ryai.2021200267
                  
                 (2021)."),[10](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR10 "Liu, Z., Adeli, E., Pohl, K. M. & Zhao, Q. Going beyond saliency maps: Training deep models to interpret deep models. In Information Processing in Medical Imaging: 27th International Conference, IPMI 2021, Virtual Event, June 28–June 30, 2021, Proceedings 27. 71–82 (Springer, 2021)."),[11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR11 "Zhang, J. et al. Revisiting the trustworthiness of saliency methods in radiology AI. Radiol. Artif. Intell. 6, e220221. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1148/ryai.220221
                  
                 (2024)."),[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR12 "Wollek, A. et al. Attention-based saliency maps improve interpretability of pneumothorax classification. Radiol. Artif. Intell. 5, e220187. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1148/ryai.220187
                  
                 (2023)."). DL model generalizability and explainability are both important for rigorously assessing the impact and clinical implications of using images of different resolutions when training DL classification models for radiology, especially as computational capabilities have improved in recent years that can accommodate high resolution images.

In this study, we revisit the impact of imaging resolution on medical imaging classification by addressing previously overlooked aspects of model generalizability and explanability. Specifically, we evaluated the following questions: Does increasing image resolution for training DL CXR classifiers improve not only in-distribution accuracy, but also model generalizability to external testing sets and explainability? We hypothesized that training models with higher resolution images will result in models that have higher external testing performance (even if internal performance is similar between models trained with low vs. high resolution images) and better explainability performance in localizing radiographic abnormalities.

## Methods

**Fig. 1**

Overview of study design. Deep learning classification models were trained on chest X-rays of varying image resolutions to evaluate the impact of training data resolution on performance and disease localization. We trained separate convolutional neural networks (CNN) for each set of image resolutions (top), followed by testing on both internal and external testing datasets (bottom). Testing comprised classification performance, as well as localization of the abnormalities of interest.

### Study overview

Figure [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#Fig1) outlines the overall design for our retrospective study. We evaluated the performance of two widely used convolutional neural network (CNN) architectures, DenseNet-121[13](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR13 "Huang, G., Liu, Z. & Weinberger, K. Q. Densely connected convolutional networks. CoRR abs/1608.06993 (2016).") and ResNet-152[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR14 "He, K., Zhang, X., Ren, S. & Sun, J. Deep residual learning for image recognition. In Proceedings of the IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition. 770–778 (2016).") when trained on the same CXR images across six image resolutions (64$\times$64, 128$\times$128, 224$\times$224, 512$\times$512, 768$\times$768, and 1024$\times$1024) and two pathologies (pneumothorax and pneumonia). We included 224 $\times$ 224 because it is the canonical input size in many ImageNet-derived classification pipelines and therefore represents an important historical and practical baseline for medical imaging. Each model was evaluated on an internal holdout testing set and an external (OOD) testing set. Classification performance and quality of localization of saliency maps were evaluated across models trained at different resolutions for every CNN architecture and dataset.

Image preprocessing comprised Z-normalization, followed by min-max scaling to adjust pixel values between 0 and 1. The normalization code was implemented in Python=3.8.10 using NumPy. The images were then resized using bilinear interpolation, the default method in the torchvision library. All resizing operations were performed with the torchvision framework to ensure consistency across different resolutions.

### Training strategy

In our implementation, we used Python=3.8.10 and PyTorch=2.1.0 to train all models. All experiments were conducted on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU. Training implementation details can be found in Supplemental Section 1.

**Fig. 2**

Overview of training and evaluation strategy: **(A)** Training strategy: for each resolution, models were trained using five-fold cross-validation to account for variability in performance due to different training-validation splits. **(B)** Evaluation strategy: at inference time, classification predictions and saliency maps from the five folds were averaged to obtain more stable and generalizable results. Classification performance was evaluated using AUROC. Saliency maps were extracted using Grad-CAM, thresholded into binary masks, and evaluated against expert annotations using precision as the primary metric. Three thresholds were tested, and the average results were reported.

Since our main objective was observing trends across image resolutions and not optimizing models for a given dataset, we chose to use a five-fold cross-validation training strategy (as shown in Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#Fig2)A) to mitigate sensitivity of model performance to the training/validation split. All models were initialized with ImageNet-pretrained weights, and finetuned using a fixed learning rate of 0.0001 and standard hyperparameters (Supplemental Section 2). The decision to use these hyperparameters was based on empirical evaluation of the potential benefits from resolution-specific hyperparameter tuning, which proved to be inconsistent across different train-validation splits when evaluated using a five-fold cross-validation (Supplemental Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#MOESM1)). Code used for all model development and evaluation is available at https://github.com/YiLab-SJ/sharpness-matters.git.

### Evaluation strategy

We aggregated the outputs of the five models from the cross-validation strategy into an ensemble for final evaluation (as shown in Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#Fig2)A), using unweighted averaging as our aggregation strategy for both classification logits and saliency maps. For evaluating classification performance, we used the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic (AUROC) Curve as our metric. We also evaluated computational tradeoff of using higher resolutions. We report peak memory (MB) usage, inference time (milliseconds), and throughput (Images/second) (Supplemental Section 3).

#### Characterizing good explanations

We identified two features that make for good AI explanations in CXR diagnosis: _Conciseness_ and _Accuracy_. Previous literature[24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR24 "Gomez, T., Fréour, T. & Mouchère, H. Metrics for saliency map evaluation of deep learning explanation methods. In International Conference on Pattern Recognition and Artificial Intelligence. 84–95 (Springer, 2022).") has criticized saliency maps for their inability to provide useful explanations, noting that these maps can be inaccurate, as well as nonspecific (i.e., too large to be informative about where a disease is), thus failing to improve AI explainability. Therefore, it is important to choose a metric that captures both conciseness and accuracy to faithfully evaluate the usefulness of these explanations.

Unlike standard segmentation tasks (where false negatives are just as crucial as false positives), false positives are detrimental for explainability because highlighting the wrong regions can lead to immediate mistrust. Previous work has explored metrics that focus on the importance of precision in AI explanations, including _sparsity_[24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR24 "Gomez, T., Fréour, T. & Mouchère, H. Metrics for saliency map evaluation of deep learning explanation methods. In International Conference on Pattern Recognition and Artificial Intelligence. 84–95 (Springer, 2022)."), _hit rate_[25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR25 "Saporta, A. et al. Benchmarking saliency methods for chest X-ray interpretation. Nat. Mach. Intell. 4, 867–878 (2022)."), in which only one point is used as an explanation and a metric $M_{g t}$ which focuses on the overlap of the most salient pixels with the ground truth mask[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR26 "Szczepankiewicz, K. et al. Ground truth based comparison of saliency maps algorithms. Sci. Rep. 13, 16887 (2023)."). The latter metric can be reformulated as precision, a common metric that evaluates positive predictive power. In this study, we chose to use _precision_ as our primary metric for evaluating saliency maps. Precision quantifies the proportion of predicted salient pixels that overlap with the ground truth annotation. It is defined as the ratio of true positives (TP) to all predicted positives, i.e the sum of TP and false positives (FP):

$$
P r e c i s i o n = \frac{\left|\right. M^{G T} \cap M^{p r e d} \left|\right.}{\left|\right. M^{p r e d} \left|\right.} == \frac{T P}{T P + F P}
$$

where the $M^{G T}$ is the ground truth mask and $M^{p r e d}$ is the predicted explanation mask.

By focusing on precision, we account for both conciseness and accuracy, offering insights into specificity and reliability of salient regions. This is particularly important in radiology, where consistent and trustworthy saliency maps can be a critical component of AI-assisted clinical workflows.

In addition to precision, we also report _coverage_, which represents the average size of explanation masks for evaluating conciseness independent of accuracy. Coverage is simply defined as ratio of the size of the explanation mask to the size of the image.

#### Saliency map generation and evaluation

We used Grad-CAM[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR27 "Selvaraju, R. R. et al. Grad-cam: Why did you say that? Visual explanations from deep networks via gradient-based localization. CoRR abs/1610.02391 (2016).") to generate saliency maps. Since quantitative evaluation metrics typically compare binary masks, we thresholded the normalized Grad-CAM saliency maps to binary explanation masks. Since the choice of optimal thresholds can vary for different datasets and models, we chose to evaluate both precision and coverage on three different thresholds (t=0.25, t=0.5, t=0.75) and reported the average performance as shown in Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#Fig2)B (see Supplemental Section 4 for details and reasoning behind thresholding method). Since the ground truth annotations for pneumonia datasets are bounding boxes, we extracted bounding boxes (Supplemental Section 5) from the thresholded saliency maps for the pneumonia dataset for a fair comparison.

For our saliency map localization evaluations, saliency map localization precision is set to zero for all false predictions. This reflects clinical practice, where a saliency map would only be presented following a positive disease prediction. Consequently, no map would be shown for false negative results (i.e., the disease that is present is not localized), and for false positive results, no disease is present for the map to localize (i.e., the saliency map localization precision is zero).

### Statistical analysis

For each model and dataset, AUROC and Precision for every resolution was compared against every other resolution, while inter-model and inter-dataset comparisons were excluded. Results that were considered a statistically significant improvement needed to be significantly better than all lower resolutions. Results with a P-values lower than 0.05 were defined as statistically significant. Given the large number of comparisons, we applied the Benjamini/Hochberg False Discovery Rate procedure[28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR28 "Benjamini, Y. & Hochberg, Y. Controlling the false discovery rate: A practical and powerful approach to multiple testing. J. R. Stat. Soc. Ser. B (Methodological) 57, 289–300. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1111/j.2517-6161.1995.tb02031.x
                  
                . https://rss.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.1111/j.2517-6161.1995.tb02031.x (1995).") on a per-dataset basis to reduce the likelihood of false positives. For classification performance, we used Delong’s test[29](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR29 "DeLong, E. R., DeLong, D. M. & Clarke-Pearson, D. L. Comparing the areas under two or more correlated receiver operating characteristic curves: a nonparametric approach. Biometrics 837–845 (1988).") to compare AUROC values. We employed the Mann–Whitney U test[30](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR30 "Mann, H. B. & Whitney, D. R. On a test of whether one of two random variables is stochastically larger than the other. Ann. Math. Stat. 50–60 (1947).") for saliency localization metrics, since our data was unpaired and not normally distributed. Since models trained at different resolutions produce distinct sets of false positives and false negatives, each resolution yields a unique evaluation subset, making the data unpaired across resolutions.

## Results

### Classification performance

Although training models with higher-resolution images offered marginal gains in classification performance on internal test datasets, it led to a substantial improvement in performance on external (OOD) data. This enhanced external performance reached a plateau when models were trained with images at 512 $\times$ 512 pixels. Figure [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#Fig3) reports the absolute classification performance of ResNet and DenseNet, measured by the AUROC on both internal and external test sets for pneumothorax and pneumonia. On the internal test set, significant improvements plateaued at 512$\times$ 512 (ResNet: 0.9629 [0.95-0.97, 95% confidence interval (CI)]; DenseNet: 0.9682 [0.96–0.98]; P<0.001 for both) for both models on the pneumothorax dataset and at 128x128 (ResNet: 0.88 [0.87-0.89] P<0.001; DenseNet: 0.88 [0.86-0.89] P=0.0106) for both models on the pneumonia dataset. In contrast, on the external test set, the best significant results were observed at 512$\times$ 512 for both pneumothorax (ResNet: 0.86 [0.84–0.88]; DenseNet: 0.91 [0.89–0.92]; P<0.001 for both) and pneumonia (ResNet: 0.85 [0.84–0.86] )P<0.001; DenseNet: 0.85 [0.84–0.86; P<0.005).

As expected, we observed a drop in classification performance when evaluated on external (OOD) test sets of both the pneumothorax and pneumonia datasets. Table [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#Tab2) shows the performance drop as a percentage of the AUROC performance in the internal set. The degree of drop in classification performance on external test sets was greatest for models trained on lower resolution images. For example, models trained to detect pneumothorax on 64 $\times$ 64 resolution images dropped by 24.4 to 26.4% compared to 5.0 to 10.7% at 1024 $\times$ 1024 when tested on external data.

As a complementary analysis, we also evaluated classification performance using area under the precision-recall curve (AUPRC). These results showed trends broadly consistent with the AUROC analysis, with similar input resolutions yielding the strongest performance within each dataset (Supplementary Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#MOESM1)).

### Conciseness of explanations

Qualitative assessment of saliency explanations (Fig. [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#Fig4)) suggests that higher resolutions lead to more specific and precise saliency maps. To quantify this observation, Fig. [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#Fig5) compares coverage across different resolutions; recall that higher coverage indicates less conciseness of saliency maps. We found that increasing resolution resulted in smaller explanation masks (i.e., lower coverage) at all thresholds, indicating a consistent trend toward more focused and concise explanations when image resolution is increased, regardless of type of pathology or model architecture.

For the pneumothorax datasets, DenseNet had the highest coverage at 64 $\times$ 64 (internal: $\approx$26%; external: $\approx$22%) and the lowest at 1024 $\times$ 1024 ($\approx$3% for both internal and external) whereas, ResNet had the highest coverage at 128 $\times$ 128 (internal: $\approx$24%; external: $\approx$23%) and the lowest at 1024 $\times$ 1024 ($\approx$4% for both internal and external).

For the pneumonia datasets, DenseNet had the highest coverage at 64 $\times$ 64 (internal: $\approx$15%; external: $\approx$20%) and the lowest at 1024 $\times$ 1024 ($\approx$3% for both internal and external) whereas, ResNet had the highest coverage at 128$\times$128 ($\approx$10% for both internal and external) and the lowest at 1024 $\times$ 1024 ($\approx$4% for both internal and external).

Trends in localization quality of saliency maps across resolutions: quality of explanations tend to increase with increase in image resolution while conciseness (coverage) drops with increase in resolution. **(A)** Explanation quality: precision plotted against image resolution shows an overall trend of improved explanation quality at higher resolutions, particularly for pneumothorax, which is a finer pathology compared to pneumonia. **(B)** Conciseness: the percentage of the image covered by the saliency map decreases with increasing resolution, indicating more concise and focused explanations.

We found a general trend of higher image resolutions increasing saliency map localization precision for both datasets. This improvement was slightly more pronounced in the pneumothorax models, with little improvement up to 224$\times$224, but a steep rise in performance between 224$\times$224 and 512$\times$512, continuing to improve up to 768$\times$768 or 1024$\times$1024. The highest significant performance was at 1024$\times$1024 (0.14; P = 0.0213) and 768$\times$768 (0.15; P=0.0213) for ResNet and DenseNet respectively on the internal set, while they plateaued at 768$\times$768 on the external set for both models (ResNet: 0.26; DenseNet: 0.40) (P<0.001 for both). In contrast, pneumonia models showed a more model-dependent pattern: ResNet improved linearly up to 768$\times$768, while DenseNet exhibited diminishing returns beyond 512$\times$512. internal set performance plateaued at 512$\times$512 (0.27; P<0.001) and 224$\times$224 (0.28; P<0.001) while external performance plateaued at 768$\times$768 (0.34; P<0.001) and 512$\times$512 (0.33; P<0.005) for ResNet and DenseNet respectively.

### Computational tradeoff

Supplemental Tables [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#MOESM1) and [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#MOESM1) show trends indicating a non-linear relationship between image resolution and all computational benchmarking metrics. As image resolution increases, both ResNet and DenseNet exhibit a consistent upward trend in peak memory usage and inference time per batch, with minimal changes at lower resolutions (64–224) and sharp increases beyond 512$\times$512, indicating non-linear computational scaling. Throughput, in contrast, shows a steady decline with increasing resolution, with the most significant drop occurring between 224 and 512, marking a critical threshold for performance degradation. These results suggest that while low-resolution inputs maintain efficient runtime characteristics, higher resolutions impose substantial computational and memory demands.

## Discussion

In this study, we revisit the question of how image resolution impacts DL performance for CXR classification. The widespread use of 224 $\times$ 224 inputs in medical image classification is partly inherited from transfer learning practices established in natural image analysis, where ImageNet-pretrained models commonly use this resolution. While this convention has practical advantages, including compatibility with standard pretrained architectures, it has also encouraged the routine use of relatively low-resolution inputs in medical imaging. While prior work on this topic[7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR7 "Sabottke, C. F. & Spieler, B. M. The effect of image resolution on deep learning in radiography. Radiol. Artif. Intell. 2, e190015 (2020)."),[8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR8 "Haque, M. I. U. et al. Effect of image resolution on automated classification of chest X-rays. J. Med. Imaging 10, 044503–044503 (2023).") suggested little or modest gains on increasing resolution for many classification tasks, these studies did not perform external testing or explainability analysis, both of which are critical for clinical evaluations of AI in medical imaging. In this study, we evaluate these aspects of DL model performance and found that models trained with lower resolution images were more susceptible to poor generalizability and had less concise and accurate explanations. Although these trends were slightly more pronounced in pneumothorax, both diseases evaluated showed consistent improvements with increasing resolution, despite the varying levels of visual granularity required for their detection.

We found that training with low resolution can lead to poor generalizability to external data. While increasing training image resolution above 512$\times$512 did not lead to major gains in classification performance in internal or external testing, resolutions below this threshold resulted in a sharp performance decline, particularly for pneumothorax. Our findings align with prior observations[31](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR31 "Zech, J. R. et al. Variable generalization performance of a deep learning model to detect pneumonia in chest radiographs: A cross-sectional study. PLoS Med. 15, e1002683 (2018).") that DL models often show a performance gap between internal and external datasets. However, the role of image resolution in modulating this gap has received limited attention. We show that training at lower resolutions amplifies this generalization gap, suggesting that lower-resolution models fail to capture features essential for robustness to external data and revealing an added vulnerability of low-resolution training to distributional shifts. Strong in-distribution performance at low resolutions can be misleading, a limitation that may have gone unnoticed in prior work[7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR7 "Sabottke, C. F. & Spieler, B. M. The effect of image resolution on deep learning in radiography. Radiol. Artif. Intell. 2, e190015 (2020)."),[8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR8 "Haque, M. I. U. et al. Effect of image resolution on automated classification of chest X-rays. J. Med. Imaging 10, 044503–044503 (2023).") due to the lack of external testing. Although the exact optimal resolution may vary by pathology, our findings underscore the importance of maintaining at least 512$\times$512 resolution for reliable deployment, which offers a practical balance between generalizability and computational cost, particularly as computational capacity becomes less of a limiting factor over time.

We observed that increasing image resolution increased conciseness (lower coverage) in explanations, aligning with qualitative findings from our work and prior studies[8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR8 "Haque, M. I. U. et al. Effect of image resolution on automated classification of chest X-rays. J. Med. Imaging 10, 044503–044503 (2023)."), which attribute this effect to a reduced effective receptive field in CNNs at higher resolutions. However, while previous work primarily focused on qualitative observations, our study also provides quantitative evidence supporting this trend. In our experiments, higher resolutions produced more concise localizations of disease. These findings suggest that when conciseness of explanation is important, higher resolutions should be considered, as they consistently yield more focused saliency maps regardless of pathology type or model architecture.

In our experiments, quality of explanations, as defined by precision, improved as image resolution increased. Previous work[24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR24 "Gomez, T., Fréour, T. & Mouchère, H. Metrics for saliency map evaluation of deep learning explanation methods. In International Conference on Pattern Recognition and Artificial Intelligence. 84–95 (Springer, 2022)."),[25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR25 "Saporta, A. et al. Benchmarking saliency methods for chest X-ray interpretation. Nat. Mach. Intell. 4, 867–878 (2022)."),[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR26 "Szczepankiewicz, K. et al. Ground truth based comparison of saliency maps algorithms. Sci. Rep. 13, 16887 (2023).") emphasizes the importance of focused explanations to increase trust and adoptability of AI algorithms. Motivated by these insights, we use precision as an indicator of explanation quality in our evaluation. For both crisp-appearing imaging findings (pneumothorax, i.e., a line representing the pleural edge) and coarser-appearing pathology (pneumonia, which frequently presents as relatively ill-defined opacities compared to a pleural edge), higher resolution training data yielded better out-of-distribution explanations. This reinforces the notion that higher resolutions are more robust to external data, not only when it comes to classification performance, but also for the quality of explanations. To our knowledge, this is the first study to quantitatively examine how image resolution affects the quality of saliency map explanations. Given our findings, researchers should consider the importance of precise explanations and improved generalizability based on the level of granularity required for their application against other factors like computational trade-offs when choosing image resolutions for their AI pipelines.

The effect size of improvement in quality of explanations and classification with higher resolution training data was, however, higher for pneumothorax compared to pneumonia. One explanation is that pneumothorax is more challenging to localize than pneumonia for radiologists due to its subtle nature, and AI-models may benefit from the fine-details captured at higher resolutions for diseases that require more granularity. This is also consistent with clinical practice, where pneumothorax diagnosis often relies on careful inspection of the pleural line and other subtle radiographic details; in this sense, higher input resolution may function analogously to magnified visual inspection. Secondly, disease manifestation can influence the behavior of explanation metrics. Pneumonia has a more heterogeneous radiographic presentation, ranging from large dense consolidations to smaller and subtler infiltrative opacities, which may partly explain why improvements with increasing resolution are less pronounced than for pneumothorax. For example, pneumonia can span large portions of the lungs, making concise localization less critical. As a result, bounding boxes are commonly used for annotation. However, this annotation style can affect the interpretation of localization metrics. Specifically, large ground-truth boxes can inflate metric scores if the saliency maps have high coverage–even if they are imprecise, as often seen in low-resolution models. This may blur the gap between poor and meaningful explanations, inflating quantitative performance of low-resolution explanations for pneumonia.

This study has limitations. While we evaluated multiple CXR datasets and diseases, our findings may not generalize to other imaging modalities and pathologies. In addition, our experiments focused on CNN-based architectures, and do not explore transformer-based models. Evaluating whether the trends observed here extend across model families is an important direction for future work. In addition, our external pneumonia evaluation used a COVID-focused dataset, which may not fully capture the broader radiographic and clinical heterogeneity of non-COVID pneumonia. As a result, the observed external generalization trends for pneumonia should be interpreted with some caution and may not directly extend to all pneumonia subtypes or clinical settings. The scale of evaluation, particularly for pneumothorax, was also influenced by the limited availability of expert-annotated datasets; for example, resources like CANDID-PTX[32](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR32 "Feng, S. et al. Curation of the candid-ptx dataset with free-text reports. Radiol. Artif. Intell. 3, e210136, 
                  https://doi.org/10.1148/ryai.2021210136
                  
                 (2021).") exist but are no longer accessible for research use per updated governmental restrictions[23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR23 "Feng, S. Personal communication, November 25, 2024 (2024)."). From a technical standpoint, evaluating the quality of model explanations remains inherently challenging. In this work, we used quantitative metrics such as precision and coverage, focusing on conciseness, inspired by prior work[24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR24 "Gomez, T., Fréour, T. & Mouchère, H. Metrics for saliency map evaluation of deep learning explanation methods. In International Conference on Pattern Recognition and Artificial Intelligence. 84–95 (Springer, 2022)."),[25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR25 "Saporta, A. et al. Benchmarking saliency methods for chest X-ray interpretation. Nat. Mach. Intell. 4, 867–878 (2022)."),[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-49129-3#ref-CR26 "Szczepankiewicz, K. et al. Ground truth based comparison of saliency maps algorithms. Sci. Rep. 13, 16887 (2023)."). While these metrics offer a reproducible and interpretable way to assess explanation quality, it remains unclear whether they are optimally aligned with clinical expectations, which is a topic for future research. Future user studies involving clinicians could help validate the relevance of these metrics or inform the development of new ones better suited to clinical decision-making. Finally, while this study isolates the effect of input resolution, performance in medical image AI may also be improved through architectural innovation or ROI-based preprocessing. Exploring how these strategies interact is an important direction for future work.

In conclusion, we have revisited the question of whether higher image resolutions improve DL model classification performance in medical imaging. By expanding our evaluation to include external test performance using strong ground truth standards and disease localization, we present a rigorous evaluation of the benefits of increasing image resolution. We found that higher image resolution significantly improves both generalizability and explainability of CXR DL classification models. Based on our findings, we recommend starting with 512x512 image resolution as a reasonable balance between computational cost and performance.
